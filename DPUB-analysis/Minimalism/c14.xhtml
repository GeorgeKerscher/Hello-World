Understanding Digital Overload

Unpacking the challenges of having too much digital stuff

Using mindfulness to manage tech excess

Today, digital screen devices reflect a wide range of technologies and include iPhones and Android smartphones; iPads, Kindles, and other tablets; laptop and desktop computers; and streaming devices and smart TVs. These days, it’s very difficult to find something that isn’t directly or wirelessly linked to the Internet. All digital devices have the power to rob you of your time and attention, and this imbalance creates problems.

The Internet is neither good nor bad — it’s amoral. It has no feelings in its power to captivate you. However, you should always keep in mind that the only goal of everything online is to capture and hold your attention.

Figuring Out What’s Interrupting Our Zen

When it comes to digital, it’s very easy to have too much. Too many files, too many emails, too many photos, and too much time spent on digital consumption, such as social media and streaming services. This often leads to overwhelm and a sense of our digital lives being out of control. But you can reduce the digital noise in your life and bring more quiet to your everyday by understanding and reducing digital’s footprint in your life.

Understanding how files, photos, and emails can be problems

In the age of digital, it often seems there are no limits. The storage space on computers and devices is large, and if you’re storing items in the cloud, you have even more space at your fingertips. We don’t have limits on how much we can store the way we used to (you could only stuff so many papers in a file cabinet or photos in an album), so we no longer have the same motivation to be mindful about accumulation.

Why don’t we just get rid of our excess files and emails? Maybe you worry that someday you’ll need that Very Important File (which no one has ever asked for but you’re afraid to delete it just in case ), or you’ll want to remember the first few drafts of the book you’ve been working on (you likely won’t; you’ll be reveling in its final published version).

Instead of loathing to get rid of files, maybe you want to start deleting what you don’t need, but facing down 6,000 photos and 500 videos (a conservative amount for many) is a daunting task for even the most organized among us. Layer in our sentimentality about items (that pic is fuzzy, but grandma and grandpa look so cute; look, the org chart for the first team I managed; funny emails from the friend group you cherished in your 20s), and it’s not hard to understand why we hang on to what we’ve accumulated. And all that digital stuff can cause as much stress as physical clutter can.

Figuring out how sharing, watching, and playing can go wrong

Ok, forget for a moment the load of things you’ve accumulated. There’s also the ongoing use of our devices. How do you know whether your digital device has become your digital vice? This isn’t an easy question to answer, as it depends on your values about your time and how you want to spend it, and how much disruption is tolerable in your life. A steady diet of digital inputs (what you see, hear, read) can fragment your attention and steal your time from real-world pursuits.

The following sections talk about the content most of us engage with every day, often without realizing how much time we’re spending doing so.

Social media

S ocial media is a broad category of applications and websites that seek to connect people, organizations, or businesses around themes, content, or interest areas. Examples include Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok, Reddit, Snapchat, X (formerly known as Twitter), and BlueSky. Too much time on social media can turn it from fun to formidable: it can fuel comparisons with others, it can replace real connections with virtual interactions, and it can sabotage your ability to navigate discomfort like boredom, feelings, and fatigue.

Streaming audio and video

Most of the audio and video you consume is in streaming format. A few examples include Spotify, Pandora, and Apple Music; Netflix, Hulu, and a host of other tv/video services; and podcasts of all types. And we can’t forget YouTube, which not only offers videos and podcasts of all lengths as well as a robust music service but is the second-largest search engine on the Internet. A constant diet of podcasts or movies, particularly if they’re on when your brain is trying to concentrate, can make it hard for you to focus, and it can distort your time perception as hours disappear.

Video games

Video games, including handheld devices, consoles, and PC-based systems, can be a fun way to relax or engage with friends. In fact, many video game users find the social component really compelling, often using headsets and apps like Discord to talk to others, and the conversations aren’t always exclusively about the game being played. Some downsides, though, include a reliance on virtual connections instead of relationships with real people, and you can easily lose all sense of time as you constantly seek that next level-up.

Living Mindfully in the Digital Age

Most of us have smartphones, but they’re not very smart. Our phones send us text messages just when we’re getting settled into a concentration-intensive activity. Or it rings when we’re driving. Its addictive nature beckons us to check social media when we’re supposed to be drifting off to sleep.

The digital age has brought huge benefits: from saving lives in emergencies, to sharing information with the world, the advantages are countless. But without mindfulness, living in the digital age can drive you crazy! If you don’t turn your phone or computer off from time to time, your attention can be completely hijacked by websites, incoming messages, social media, games, and more. Gadgets are so compelling.

A mindful approach to your digital life can help quell the chaos and bring back calm. As with your physical possessions, the secret is being intentional about what you keep (files, photos, online recipes, and so on), getting rid of what’s not essential to you, and keeping the digital wolves at bay through new habits based on mindfulness about your decisions. But first, knowledge is power, and it’s useful to understand how compelling technology has become for you.

Assessing your level of attention to technology

Nowadays, people seem to use their phones a lot. A recent survey of over 4,000 users found that

  • Average smartphone users check their phones 47 times a day.
  • 85 percent of smartphone users will check their phone when speaking with friends and family.
  • 80 percent of smartphone users check their phone within an hour of waking up or going to sleep.
  • Almost half of all smartphone users have tried to limit their usage in the past.

When you find you’re spending more time on your phone than interacting with real people, it may be time to reassess your phone usage. Smartphone addiction is often fueled by Internet overuse, as it’s often the games, apps, and online worlds that are most compelling.

Here’s a quiz we’ve developed to get an idea of just how addicted you are to your phone:

  1. You’re doing some work and a phone rings in another room. Do you:
    1. Take no notice: it must be someone else’s; your phone is normally off.
    2. Ignore it and check it later.
    3. Walk casually to pick it up.
    4. Run to pick up the phone, sometimes tripping over or stubbing your toe in the process, and getting annoyed by anyone in the way.
  2. You’re planning a holiday, but the hotel has no Wi-Fi and no phone signal. Will you go?
    1. Yes, why not?
    2. Oh, I’d love the chance to get a break from my devices. Heaven!
    3. Probably wouldn’t go there.
    4. No way! How can I have a vacation without my phone and/or laptop — that doesn’t make sense. I need a good phone signal and superfast Internet 24/7.
  3. Where’s your phone right now?
    1. My what? Oh, phone … Err, no idea. I’m not sure if I have a phone actually.
    2. Somewhere around here.
    3. In this room.
    4. It’s right here — my beautiful, precious phone.
  4. What do you use your phone for?
    1. Phone calls, of course. What else is it for?
    2. Calls and texts from time to time. Mainly emergencies.
    3. Call and texts. And picking up emails sometimes too. A few pictures.
    4. Everything. It’s my life! Facebook, X, WhatsApp, Instagram, Snapchat, email, texting, photos, video, playing games, fitness, video chats. Oh yes, and occasionally phone calls, too.
  5. Do you keep your phone nearby as you sleep?
    1. No way!
    2. Sometimes. Or just for my alarm clock. Don’t really check last thing at night or first thing in the morning.
    3. Quite often. Send the odd text and maybe have a peek at my messages first thing in the morning too.
    4. Every night. I sleep with my phone. It’s the last thing I look at before falling asleep and the first thing I see when I wake up. It’s a compulsion.

Add up your score: letter a is 1 point, b is 2 points, c is 3 points, and d is four points.

  • 5–10 points: You’re not really addicted to your phone at all — you’re probably too busy meditating.
  • 11–15 points: You like your phone, but not that much. You’re still in control and can live comfortably without it.
  • 16–18 points: You’re pretty dependent on your phone for many things. You might like to take a little break from your phone from time to time.
  • 19–20 points: You love your phone, maybe more than your favorite band and definitely more than your favorite sneakers. Make sure you have some moments in the day where you take a break from your device and do some mindful walking or stretching. If you feel that your phone usage is out of control, try some of the tips in the following section, or consider getting professional help.

BALANCING TECHNOLOGY WITH REAL-TIME LIVING

Anything in excess can produce negative health issues, and the Internet and screen technologies are no exception. Health issues relating to screen use have been well established and may involve reduced and poor sleep, increased stress and elevated cortisol, repetitive motion injury, and neck and back problems. There have been reports of thumb, finger, and upper-back problems from excessive screen use, as well as eye strain and difficulties focusing. Some reports of more serious medical issues include elevated blood pressure, blood clots, weight gain from sedentary behavior, and heart rate issues from dehydration; in extreme cases, people have died.

Health is in large part determined by balance in your life; screens and all the content you endlessly consume online may interfere with that balance. But balance is key, and a minimalist approach can help.

Detaching yourself physically from your tech

When adopting a digitally minimalist life and trying to disengage from so many digital habits, we suggest you create a lower-tech living plan — a plan that includes things you like, activities you love to do, and the people you like to do them with. Time is limited, and digital consumption can eat what time you have without your even realizing it.

The way to live with less tech is to practice living life without it, or at least without as much of it. Start small, and try these tips:

  • Participate in a new hobby regularly, such as knitting, gardening, or playing an instrument. By paying mindful attention to your hobby and keeping your phones and computers out of the way, you’ll develop greater mindfulness.
  • Practice taking a walk, sitting in a waiting room, standing in line, or having a meal or a cup of coffee without your phone. Try to pair fewer activities with your phone, tablet, or laptop, and consciously look for opportunities to do it differently. This will feel odd at first, but remember when smartphones first came out, initially it felt weird to use them in public.
  • Consider leaving your phone in the car (secured in the glove compartment or console) when you meet your family or a friend for dinner. In fact, consider never having your phone out when eating or socializing; this conveys a mixed message to whomever you are with — that you are physically there, but not fully present.
  • When you go to bed, leave your phone charging in the other room. Buy an alarm clock. You’ll sleep better.

Making these changes feels strange at first. However, if you don’t practice creating some space from your technology, it’ll continue to eat up much of your time. Remember as you’re getting started — any change is a positive change.

Using tech functionality to support your mindfulness

If you’ve discovered that you’re using digital devices to the point that they’re having a negative impact on your work or social life, it’s time to get back in control.

You can manage overuse of digital devices by taking advantage of some of the technology that’s built into devices. Doing so isn’t as hard as you may think. In fact, once you start doing so, you may find that you don’t even want to look at your mobile devices. Well, at least not as often.

Here are some techniques to try:

  • Make good use of flight mode or Do Not Disturb, or switch your phone off. When we have an important task to do, we try to remember to keep our phone off or simply in flight mode. That way, we can’t be disturbed. Most digital phones, tablets, and even computers have a mode called “do not disturb.” This prevents calls or alerts from coming through. From 9 p.m. to 8 a.m. one of this book’s authors sets their phone to “do not disturb” to automatically prevent any more calls, messages, or notifications from coming in.

  • Set boundaries. Just before you go to bed, it’s important not to look at screens too much. Television, laptops, and phones emit a light that signals to your body that it’s still daytime. Seeing this light right before bedtime may make it hard to fall asleep and may have you wake up tired. Also, you may not want to be disturbed at other specific times in the day. For example, when walking through the park keep your phone off and enjoy nature and the people around you.

    And obviously, when you’re with friends or family or eating a meal, switch your phone off or leave it out of the way. If distancing yourself from your phone sounds like a challenge, just try it once and see how that goes. Eventually, it can feel freeing to leave your gadgets behind.

  • Switch off notifications. Does your computer beep each time an email comes through? Does your phone make a noise each time someone chats to you on social media or sends you a message? If so, you can end up with perpetual distraction. Every time you’re doing one task, you’re distracted by another. The more you keep switching your attention, the less your mindful awareness develops. Turn off as many notifications as you can. This way, you can focus on doing whatever you need to do with awareness.
  • Give spectator activities your full attention. When you sit down at night to watch a movie or TV show, resist the temptation to look up every actor or obscure fact, order an Amazon item, or do a social media check-in on your phone, tablet, or laptop. Focus on one task or activity at a time. iPhone has a “Focus” mode you can turn on; there are also apps you can download to help you avoid using some or all of your phone’s functionality for a period of time. (Two we like are Forest and Focus Friend.)
  • Adjust the appearance of your phone. Turn your smartphone screen to black and white (gray scale); this makes it less appealing and will quickly cut down your phone use. It’s an easy setting change (you may need to look online to see how to do it for your model).

Enhancing mindful awareness through apps

If you’re looking for a way to enhance your mindfulness, you may want to avoid technology altogether — and that’s understandable. Use of technology can distract your mind. But for most of us, using digital devices may be part of your everyday life. Switching them off for an extended period may seem impossible to achieve. In such a case, we encourage you to make good use of mindfulness apps, websites, and more.

You can download and use apps for mobile devices like phones and tablets. Simply search for “mindfulness” or “meditation” in the app stores and you’ll find lots of resources — take your pick. New apps come out every week! Apps we like include Insight Timer, Calm, and Headspace. But there are many more.

If you use social media a lot, following people or organizations that offer mindful images, tweets, and resources may help you. If you want to follow one of this book’s authors, just search for his name, Shamash Alidina, on your social media channel of choice. He usually shares mindfulness tips and quotes. And feel free to say “Hi” too!

You can also use software to help you focus mindfully on your work. This software helps you focus and be productive on your computer. For example, if you do your best creative work in the mornings and want to focus for a few hours, you can use an app to prevent you from using your phone until noon. You can block all your social media websites and your email, which can easily suck you in. With those areas blocked, you can mindfully focus on the activity that needs your attention or concentration. You can deal with emails, social media, and meetings in the afternoon.

It’s ironic that even though one of this book’s authors is a mindfulness specialist, he needs a self-control app to prevent him from using social media sites and email! It just goes to show how addictive they can be for us all and how we can use technology tools to help manage our usage.

OVERCOMING URGES, CRAVINGS, AND BOREDOM

Of all the triggers and urges we see with excess screen time, boredom seems to be at the top of the list. When we say boredom, we’re not talking about having hours of excess time with nothing to do. What we mean is that we have become a boredom-intolerant world, with barely the ability to sit for a few seconds without reaching for a screen.

To change boredom intolerance, first you need to resist the urge to scratch the digital itch. The only way to do this is to become consciously aware that you’re experiencing the urge, and to create enough of a conscious pause to hold off picking up a phone, laptop, or tablet. It isn’t easy, but it can be done with some effort. Even holding off a bit can create enough space to allow you to make a different choice. It is your choice. Ease of access increases the addictive potential of any substance or behavior, so find a way to create some space between the urge and the screen.
