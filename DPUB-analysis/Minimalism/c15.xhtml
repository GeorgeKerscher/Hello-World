With the ubiquity of streaming services and social media platforms, and the seemingly endless amount of storage space for our photos and files, it seems like the digital world is about as maximalist as can be. With so much incoming, our nervous systems are on high alert much of the time, and it can be hard to feel in control of things. A minimalist approach to your digital life can help.

Just as minimalist home and physical spaces make life feel less chaotic, your online world should be no different. Having hundreds of files on your computer’s desktop, multiple tabs open on your web browsers, and — yikes — hundreds of unread emails, can be very stressful.

Plus, digital clutter wastes time and creates inefficiency, so tasks take longer. How often have you searched for an important file on your desktop but didn’t remember the exact filename, so you hunted for a few minutes? Or you lose track of important emails because your inbox is so full. We encourage you to really take a good look at your entire online life and treat it with the same minimalist lens through which you see your closet or office.

Jump-Starting Your Minimalist Journey

When organizing digital stuff, many people begin with their computer’s files or their email inbox, both of which tend to have a large volume of items. If the thought of tackling hundreds (or more) of files stops you in your tracks, we get it. Sometimes wading into the shallow end of the pool is the best way to actually get going, and the little wins build confidence and momentum. Consider beginning with these quick tips that can make things feel more orderly:

  • Close browsers tabs while working. Having too many browser tabs open can be a huge distraction, it can make it difficult to quickly find what you’re looking for, and it can significantly slow down your computer.
  • Hide toolbars and close tabs while working on apps. This tidies your interface and helps give the illusion of a bigger working space. Your workspace feels less cluttered and you can find what you need more easily.
  • Delete videos, podcasts, and audio books that you’ve downloaded and finished watching/listening to. You can always go back and redownload these if you bought them, so there’s no reason they should be taking up valuable space in your digital life.
  • Clean out your inbox. We talk more about email decluttering later in this chapter, and this one is a must.
  • Get rid of saved music you no longer like. Yes, you may need some downloaded music if you lose cell service while on a remote camping trip in Montana or when flying to Aunt Jane’s house on the other coast, but most of the time, playlists on your favorite streaming service (that you or others have made) will give you access to the entertainment you like. Delete the downloads of all but your absolute favorites.
  • Focus on business with your business devices. If you have a work and a personal computer or phone, stick to work stuff on your work devices. Having dedicated tools and spaces for work can help minimize personal distractions during the workday, minimize the risk of work blurring into personal (especially risky when you work from home), and can make your personal digital space feel more like a welcoming respite.

Organizing Your Digital Files

Your computer is one of the most powerful tools that you have in your home and office, and there’s so much it can do to help you. It shouldn’t be a source of stress and overwhelm when you open it. Instead, if it’s a clean and organized space, it’ll help inspire you and keep you on task with minimal distractions.

Where to start when you have copious amounts of both digital files and emails in your inbox? We choose to start with files so that you can create an organizational system that works for you, and then you can move on to your inbox (possibly emulating the way you’ve organized your computer files). You also may be better able to tackle your inbox and delete emails once you know what files you’ve already saved and how you’ve organized them.

Even though your files are digital, treat them with the same minimalizing methods as you did your physical files and delete what you don’t need or use, and then organize what you keep.

Finding a home within the clouds

First things first — where should you store your files? If you’re not already doing so, store them in the cloud. If you currently keep your files on your local hard drive (that is, your computer), then you can only access them from that one device. And if your computer is ever stolen or damaged beyond repair, you may not be able to get back what you’ve stored there. Instead, store your files in the cloud; Google Drive and OneDrive are two examples. Files in the cloud can be accessed from any device (including your phone, your tablet, or your brother-in-law’s laptop), and cloud server providers implement robust backup strategies to keep your data safe.

If you don’t use the cloud but you do back up your hard drive, you’ve reduced your risk, but unless you back up your files daily, you could lose work if your computer became inaccessible between backups.

Whether you store your files in the cloud or you’re a hard-drive user who diligently backs up, either gives you a chance to organize your files so you can easily find them. No more saving to your computer’s desktop and struggling to find a Word doc in a sea of hundreds of unrelated files. The next section explains how to organize your files.

Deleting what you don’t need and sorting the files you do

Files that are well-organized are easy to find, and you minimize the chance of duplicates. But before you can categorize them and move them into folders for storage and easy retrieval, you need to delete the files you no longer need. As with physical goods, consider deleting anything you haven’t used in a year. This may seem too aggressive for some of you, so you could start by deleting anything you haven’t used in two years.

You may have some files you want to keep, perhaps as examples for your portfolio, or tools and templates you may need in the future. You may also want to keep final versions of important presentations, content pieces that are meaningful for a particular reason, performance reviews that sum up a year’s worth of projects, and so on. Delete anything that seems like a duplicate (you don’t need three versions of a deck as it evolved with feedback). If you’re hesitant to delete some files, put them into a holding area for a year (perhaps a folder called To Review for Deletion), and then critically evaluate these files in a year with an eye to deleting anything unnecessary.

After you’d selected the keepers, review all the retained files and sort them, grouping like with like. We use a nested approach, where we have just two main folders that everything falls within. Our main folders are Work and Personal, and we sort every file into one of those two categories (we group them further; see the next section on subfolders). Categorizing all your files into just two primary folders makes it easier to find what you want and to keep business and personal separate. You may prefer two or three different main folders (Marketing, Sales, Communication, for example); do what works for you. When sorting, we like to start with personal files because we have fewer of those than work files, and it’s also more fun.

Making use of subfolders

Subfolders are simply digital file folders nested within main file folders. You can have as many subfolders as you want (and subfolders within subfolders, and sub-sub within sub-sub, and so on). If you have more than ten related documents in a folder and aren’t using a subfolder for them, things can get cluttered. It’s much easier to find something nested within a clearly named subfolder than it is to find it among dozens or hundreds of files within a single main folder.

For example, you may have a main folder called Personal, and within it you may have a subfolder called Personal Finance, where you keep all files related to your money. Within the Personal Finance subfolder, you may have three sub-subfolders, called Budget, Retirement, and College Savings. This type of organization means you can easily find the spreadsheet related to your budget (within the Budget sub-subfolder) whenever you have life changes that cause you to adjust how you spend your money each month.

Naming your files and folders

Make sure you name your files and folders intuitively; nothing’s more difficult than trying to remember what Crncsrl.doc refers to (maybe a corn casserole recipe?). Practically speaking, it’s unlikely you’ll reach the maximum filename character limit (about 255 characters), so you don’t need to be cryptic when naming your files. And use the same naming structure for all your files if you can, or at least use the same naming structure for files within a folder. Two filename examples within a Budget subfolder might be Budget_Revised_Dec2025.xls and Vacation-Expenses_Planned_2026.xls.

Your digital filing system should be adapted and changed as your needs change. Nothing is set in stone, and that’s the beauty of digital files. You can easily change names and locations. Your minimalist system should make you feel calm and allow you to waste no time searching for documents.

Restoring Order to an Overwhelmed Inbox

They say there are two types of people in this world with regard to our inboxes — check out Figure 15-1 . This is a joke, but we do find people tend to fall into the “no emails unread” or the “I’ll get to it someday” camps. One isn’t better than the other, although an inbox teeming with emails is often a source of discomfort and even anxiety for some people.

We’re passionate about inbox maintenance — not because we want you to delete all your emails to get to zero, but because emails can become a total waste of time if not managed properly. As with other minimalist principles, we want you to determine what’s important to you, and have that drive your decisions. Maybe you get the most calm from an inbox with zero emails in it at the end of each day, or maybe what makes your email feel under control is an organization system that allows you to easily find whatever emails you’ve saved.

However you describe a minimalist inbox, you start by assessing what emails you have, deciding which you don’t need and deleting them, and then organizing what remains so you can easily find what you’re looking for when searching.

Taking stock and deleting what you don’t need

The first step to getting control of your inbox is to get familiar with what’s there. This may be quick if you have only one or two screens full of email. If you have dozens of screens worth, then quickly review three or four. What you’re doing is getting a sense of what’s there so you can begin to take action. Do you have a lot of subscriptions? Do you have a lot of junk mail? How about messages that a group of friends shares regularly, perhaps filled with jokes, trivia, or news of the day? Do you have emails you want to keep? Do you have a lot of duplicates from long strings of replies back and forth?

After you’ve done an initial assessment, start deleting what you don’t want to keep, and don’t think twice. You may want to just delete all 15,000 emails and start fresh. We certainly think that’s a viable strategy for some people, and we’re not saying you shouldn’t do it. If you want to go through them all, then you need to be fast and brutal.

Here are a few tips:

  • Sort by batch. Identify emails you know you want to get rid of, such as promotions from stores, newsletters that are months out of date, reminders for appointments, group emails that contain jokes or other non-essential content, and any senders whose emails you don’t want to keep. Note the sender or subject of each email, search by sender or subject, and delete all related emails. Repeat until you’ve deleted all such emails. This method allows you to quickly delete emails in batches.
  • Sort by date. Delete emails older than a certain number of days or months. This is less selective than sorting by batch, but particularly if your inbox is full of old mail, this could be a quick way to make strides in tidying it. And if anyone really wants to contact you, they’ll likely email you again.
  • Skim-and-delete. Select a screen’s worth of emails (often there’s a box at top of the page that, when clicked, selects all emails on the screen), quickly scan and deselect the ones you want to keep, and then click the delete or trash button to delete those that are still selected.

Leaving behind worries about junk mail

Most people get a lot of junk mail and spam, and it’s only getting worse. The great news is that today you have lots of ways to handle it. Most email programs automatically filter out email they think is spam, putting this in a Spam or Junk folder without it ever touching your inbox. You can also set up functionality where only emails from your contacts are funneled to your inbox, and everything else is placed in your Junk or Spam folder.

If someone’s legitimate email lands in your junk mail, who cares? If it’s important enough, that person will ask you if you got the email and you’ll say no and check your junk mail. It’s really as simple as that, so don’t waste your time overcomplicating the process.

What about the Unsubscribe link at the bottom of junk or spam mail? Most spam email is phishing — fraudulent messages designed to trick you into clicking links, sharing data, or downloading malware. Don’t click Unsubscribe in suspicious emails. That click confirms your address is real, telling scammers to send you even more junk. Instead, ignore these emails and move on.

Unsubscribing to lists of all kinds

You may have a bunch of email subscriptions that you actually signed up for — you wanted an informational PDF you read about on LinkedIn, you signed up to get the latest from your favorite influencers, or you joined Substack and went crazy with sign-ups. Maybe you can’t keep up with the frequency of particular subscriptions, maybe you get too many total subscriptions in your email each day, maybe both. Free up your inbox to get only the emails you really need.

It’s okay to use the Unsubscribe link for newsletters and other emails you voluntarily requested — these messages aren’t phishing (see the preceding section). Find this link at the bottom of each email. Your email program may also have some Unsubscribe functionality you can use — Gmail, for example, has an Unsubscribe button at the top of each subscription email, and Outlook has a Subscriptions tab that can make unsubscribing quick and painless.

Think carefully before giving out your email addresses. If you’re required to as part of an online purchase, unsubscribe as soon you as you get the first newsletter if you’re not interested in hearing from them.

Filing away what you want to keep

After you’ve deleted the emails you don’t want to keep, you need to organize the ones you do. Use the same approach as for your digital files and create a folder system, and then move emails into the appropriate folder so you can easily find them later. See “ Organizing Your Digital Files ” for pointers on creating a folder system that’s easy to navigate. Many email systems offer similar folder-nesting functionality.

TAKING ADVANTAGE OF YOUR EMAIL SYSTEM’S EFFICIENCY

Have you ever taken the time to become familiar with your email platform’s functionality? Discovering everything your email can do is usually eye-opening. From offering massive amounts of archive space, to starring emails for quick finds, to automatically color-coding emails based on contact names and then sorting them accordingly, many email providers offer quite a bit of intuitive functionality that can help you keep your inbox well-organized and easy to handle.

It may seem daunting to consider spending time learning about your email system’s functionality, but even the busiest among us will realize that doing so will save you time in the long run.

Keeping your inbox lean

Once you finish getting your inbox to zero (or whatever achievement meets your standard of minimalist email), take some time to reflect on how good it feels to have a tidy inbox and well-managed email. Then, set a target for a quantity of inbox emails you don’t want to exceed. Many people pick 50 and perform a minimalist decluttering review when their inbox gets to that number. Others want even fewer inbox emails before doing a quick declutter.

You can only maintain your decluttered inbox and keep it manageable if you deal with it regularly.

Addressing Apps Across All Devices

Another opportunity to accumulate too much, and feel disorganized or slightly overwhelmed, is via apps you download. Many of them can serve a real purpose, helping you be more productive, providing a more efficient shopping experience, guiding you in meditating or working out, giving you a creative break, and so on. But as with many things, it’s easy to have so many apps that you can’t remember all that you’ve downloaded, which means you aren’t using everything you’ve selected and getting the benefits each offers. A minimalist review and pare-down will help you decide which are important to keep and then delete the rest.

On all your devices — your mobile phone, tablet, computer, and so on — review the apps you’ve downloaded, identify those you haven’t used in at least three months, and delete them. You can redownload them instantly if you ever need them again, and it’s likely that anything with a log-in is keeping data in the cloud, so you probably won’t lose important data by deleting the app.

A former colleague said he doesn’t like to have more apps than what fits on the first screen of his iPhone because it helps him feel clutter-free. That’s a lean approach! We aren’t quite that strict with the quantity of apps we keep, but we group apps into folders so we can easily find them, and this helps keep the quantity from feeling overwhelming. For example, we have folders for Travel, Social, Work, Music, Shopping, Accounting, and so on, and all the related apps fit within each.

Just as we review our closet regularly, every month we go through our phones and delete apps we haven’t used and don’t need. We recommend you do the same.

In order to take full advantage of the apps you keep, make sure you regularly update your devices and your apps so you’re always able to take advantage of the newest features. You can check for updates every few days, or even better, turn on automatic updates so that when you’re sleeping, apps will update in the background.

Managing Your Digital Photos and Videos

One of the questions that we get asked the most is how we store our photos and videos, and what organizing strategies we use. Not only do we have a lot of each (smart phones have made it nearly impossible to not accumulate thousands of photos and hundreds of videos; we’re guessing you’re in a similar situation), but we also need to access many of them from multiple devices, depending on how we intend to use them.

Creating a photo or video strategy and then moving your items so they’re all where you want them does take some time, but since you’ll continue to accumulate both, it’s time well spent and will bring you relief from any low-level anxiety you may feel about yours.

The first thing to do is get your existing photos and videos organized. This means figuring out what you have, deciding what to delete, and organizing what remains so each is easy to find and you can enjoy them all.

Locating all of your pics and vids

To figure out what you have, you need to identify where you’re keeping all of your digital photos and videos. Are they just on your phone, or do you have some on an old digital camera or video recorder? Do you have some stored on your personal computer (and if so, are they in the same location — a Photos folder, for example — or are they spread around your hard drive), and are any hiding on your work computer? And what about social media — are you using Facebook or Instagram or others to store images you want to make sure to keep? Finally, you may have some stored in online albums such as Shutterfly — this solution may be sufficient for you, but we know someone who used a since-acquired online photo service for hundreds of vacation photos, and due to a communication snafu, she lost access to all of them. Businesses close, habits shift, and priorities change, so we think the best way to store your photos and videos is all together in one location.

Once you find them all, you’ll probably notice that you have a lot of duplicate photos (wait, the phone wasn’t level; Chris, stop with the bunny ears; that guy just photo-bombed us, so let me take one more; now one in portrait mode). You may also have a bunch of videos that at the time were important to take, but now you can’t imagine watching. And in both cases, you likely have photos or videos with dubious quality or featuring people you aren’t friends with anymore and don’t want to keep. Which leads us to a review strategy, which we cover in the next section.

Creating a strategy to review your photos or videos

We recommend you make and then implement a strategy to deal with all your current photos and videos. If you have hundreds or thousands of photos and videos, this could take a while (but trust us, it’s worth it, and your nervous system will thank you).

Save your photos and videos to the cloud. Whether you choose Google Photos, iCloud, OneDrive, or another cloud service, saving them to the cloud versus a single device (your phone or computer, for example) allows you to access them from anywhere and via any device. iPhones and Androids have functions that allow you to automatically save your photos and videos to the cloud, which also frees up storage space for other uses.

When you create a strategy for minimalizing and organizing your photo and videos, make it manageable. Reviewing, sorting, and organizing thousands of images and videos is a job, but if you break it into pieces, you can make steady progress without feeling overwhelmed. For example, you can review and sort two years’ worth of items every month. Figure 15-2 is an example of a review schedule that tackles 16 years of photos, which one of this book’s authors used to keep on track during her minimalist journey.

You may find you’re faster at addressing photos from the earlier years if you didn’t take as many photos then. The selfie age and the ever-increasing storage capacity of smart phones has definitely contributed to more photos in the later years, so you may find you need to adjust your review schedule as you get closer to the present. (Instagram started in 2010, which helps explain why many people take so many more photos and videos now than they did back in the early days of digital cameras.)

Once you’ve got your photo and video house in order, we want you to follow through with this strategy forever. Once you’ve dealt with your existing photos and you move into maintenance mode, where you’re staying on top of new photos and videos (see next section), it’s important that you never miss a month of tidying and minimalizing. Staying consistent is the key to staying decluttered and keeping the calm you’ve worked hard to achieve.

Taking photos like a minimalist

Getting your existing photos lean and well-organized is a little more complicated because we often have so many that are nearly or exactly the same, due to photo bursts, use of Live mode, and simply seeking that one photo where everyone is smiling and nobody has their eyes closed.

ALRIGHT, IT’S TIME TO DELETE PHOTOS AND VIDEOS WITH YOUR EX

When you’re going through your photos or videos, you’re sure to find some of your ex (that is, if you have an ex; if you don’t, you’re really lucky).

Love should be celebrated, but if or when that love ends, get rid of the photo or video. If you really want to keep it because it was a great vacation or you happen to look phenomenal, keep it digitally. There’s no need for your new someone to find physical photos of you and your ex.

Get rid of the photos or videos with these helpful tips:

  • View and remember the good times. Know that many more good times are coming in the future.
  • Appreciate the special occasion. Maybe you took a once-in-a-lifetime vacation and want to keep those memories. Check back to see if you have photos or video with only you in them, and discard the ones with both of you.
  • Let go of the past. Often, we have trouble letting go of things because we’re holding onto the past. We’re not relationship experts, but we think focusing on the past prevents you from being open to the future, so it’s best to move on.

To avoid this challenge once you’ve organized everything, adopt these habits to help your digital photo albums stay more minimal:

  • After you take multiples, pick your favorite photo immediately. If you don’t do this right away, you probably won’t until you get the reminder that your phone’s storage is full.
  • Turn off “live” functionality. Turn off live forever to avoid taking up so much space on your devices. Live photos can be cool as you see a movement and can pick the best photo from a few seconds of shooting, but seriously, when have you ever done that? Once you’ve turned off the live setting, check it every few months because sometimes it turns back on.
  • Be mindful of how many photos you take. It takes time to review and organize them later. When you’re snap-happy and take 20 photos of the same latte, you’ll regret it when you’re reviewing and organizing your photos later.
  • Review your photos and organize them every month. Staying on top of this is much easier than dealing with months of images at once.

Dealing with Other Digital Devices

Maximalism isn’t isolated to photos or files, so we want to mention some other digital tools that often could use a minimalist approach and a little paring-back — or maybe a lot.

Minimalizing your phone

In addition to keeping only the apps you use regularly (see the section “ Addressing Apps Across All Devices ” earlier in this chapter), you can take some additional steps to bring a less-is-more approach to your smart phone:

  • Music/videos: Delete what you don’t listen to or watch anymore.
  • Notifications: We turn off all notifications because we don’t want to be constantly bombarded with distractions, and we only want to check emails twice daily. If you’re scared to eliminate all notifications, let your customers know via an auto response when they can expect to hear from you (for example, you can turn on an email autoreply that informs recipients that you answer all non-emergency emails each day from 8–9 a.m. and 4–5 p.m.), or let your friends know the times you check your phone and ask that they avoid calling you outside of these times unless it’s an emergency.
  • Screen time: Do you know how much time you spend on your phone? It’s important to know for your time management. Most smart phones can tell you about your weekly screen use; there are also many apps that can help you be mindful about this.

Clearing out your digital bookshelf

Purging and organizing your e-reader is a bit different than decluttering your physical books, because you can’t donate ebooks or give them to libraries or friends when you’re done with them. To bring organization to the chaos that e-readers can become, we recommend you delete what you’ve read, or you organize all your ebooks so you can easily find them.

Here are a few ways you can organize your e-reader:

  • Create collections or folders (much like you do with your digital files; see “ Organizing Your Digital Files ” earlier in this chapter). Stick with 10 or fewer collections/folders, lest you keep the chaos going.
  • Tag your ebooks by purpose. For example, Deep Read, Quick Reference, Inspiration, Personal Growth, and so on.
  • Keep samples in a separate collection. This way you can more easily find and review them, and delete the ones you’re not interested in.
  • Rename cryptic filenames so they can be used for search. For example, if your ebook filename is a bunch of numbers (the ISBN), rename the file with the genre and author last name, or the topic and specific angle.

If you’re worried about deleting ebooks you’ve already read (and those you downloaded a year or more ago and haven’t even started), most e-readers allow you to go back and redownload the book from where you originally bought it, so it’s not irreplaceably gone. Plus, the beauty of an e-reader is that you can highlight and take notes on passages you want to remember or come back to, which helps make your search easier.

Remembering your tablet, too

Whether you use your tablet every day or once a month, chances are it could be a more minimalist device for you. Follow the same rules as you use for to phone or computer minimalizing. Delete what you don’t need or use, and organize the rest. And be realistic. Tablets are often cluttered with apps, so go through these with diligence and be brutal. Remember, you can download any app again in 5 seconds, so don’t waste any time pondering which lesser-used apps to remove (remove them all!).
