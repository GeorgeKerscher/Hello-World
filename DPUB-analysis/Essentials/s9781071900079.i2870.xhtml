Have Leaders Lost Their Followers’ Trust?

156 Each year, Richard Edelman conducts a global survey on which people and institutions we trust and how much we trust them. 1 He is the president and CEO of the world’s largest public relations company, Edelman. In 2023, they surveyed almost 7,000 employees in seven countries about their trust at work, publishing their findings in the Edelman Trust Barometer Special Report: Trust at Work . Overall, 2023 saw a shift in employees reconsidering the workplace—meaning that employers must rethink what work means to people. Employees want a work–life reset, with 67% saying that because of everything that has happened in the world over the past few years, they are reevaluating how they spend their time, and 72% want employers to rethink what work means to employees. Edelman looked at deskless workers (remote employees) and found that they are less trusting than executives. In addition, as shown in Figure 6.1 , deskless employees reported that, if managers don’t trust them, they don’t give their trust in return. Remote workers want to be trusted to do their work well when not in the office, and there is a “trust gap” when they feel they are not trusted. Given these findings, it is no surprise that trust has emerged as a major concern for research in organizational behavior (OB) and is even more important for remote workers. The key role of trust and how to repair it when it is broken is covered later in this chapter.

Differentiating Management and Leadership

  • In his classic book On Becoming a Leader , Warren Bennis composed a list of the differences between being a manager and being a leader: 4
  • The manager administers; the leader innovates.
  • The manager is a copy; the leader is an original.
  • The manager maintains; the leader develops.
  • The manager focuses on systems and structure; the leader focuses on people.
  • The manager relies on control; the leader inspires trust.
  • The manager has a short-range view; the leader has a long-range perspective.
  • The manager asks how and when; the leader asks what and why.
  • The manager has their eye always on the bottom line; the leader’s eye is on the horizon.
  • The manager imitates; the leader originates.
  • The manager accepts the status quo; the leader challenges it.
  • The manager is the classic good soldier; the leader is their own person.
  • The manager does things right; the leader does the right thing.

Managerial Leadership

158 Zalesnick also posed the question, “Leaders and managers: Are they different?” 5 He makes an important point that both managers and leaders are needed for an organization to function optimally. The manager is a day-to-day problem-solver, and the leader is focused on developing new approaches and options for the future. To some extent, leaders must engage in some problem-solving activities, and the two roles overlap (see Figure 6.2 ). As shown in the figure, leadership is about inspiring others to follow the leader’s vision for the organization. Managers, on the other hand, are concerned with controlling the operations of the organization so things run efficiently. Both are needed, and some managers have the adaptability to do both and switch between the roles of leader and manager as the situation demands. These are managerial leaders, as shown in the figure where the two roles overlap. Leadership during COVID-19 was a reminder that this flexibility is important—at times leaders must be inspirational motivators, but at other times, they must take charge in a more command-and-control manner. 6 While articulating a positive vision that we would come through the pandemic, it was also necessary to impose some strict guidelines for organizations and people to comply with.

The Trait Approach

In the trait approach , it is believed that leaders are born with the talent and abilities for leadership. This is in contrast with most other leadership theories, which propose that leadership can be learned. The focus on traits suggests that the best way to ensure effective leadership is to select the right people for leadership positions rather than to train them. Leaders are believed to be different from followers because they hold special attributes that make them great leaders. Early research identified traits such as drive, vigor, and originality in people that held leadership positions. 7

159 The trait-based perspective of leadership has a long history, and dozens of traits have been studied. Trait approaches were largely dismissed by OB researchers because they didn’t show reliable differences between leaders and followers. Interest in traits lessened when the behavioral approaches began (described in the next section). However, interest in traits resurfaced when a review from the early 1990s urged scholars to reconsider traits and suggested that the following traits do matter for leadership: drive (achievement, ambition, energy, tenacity, initiative), leadership motivation, honesty, integrity, self-confidence (including emotional stability), cognitive ability (IQ), and knowledge of the business. 8 Recently, OB researchers found that the personality trait of extraversion helps us to understand leadership emergence and effectiveness. 9 A review of decades of research on traits suggests that it may be the combination of traits rather than a single one that best explains how traits may influence leadership. 10 Over the past 10 years, interest in leader traits has been undergoing a renaissance. Basic personality traits and capacities contribute to explanations of leader emergence and effectiveness. Traits interact with the situation in that certain traits may predispose a person to assume leadership roles in organizations. 11

160 The trait approach helps organizations identify employees that best fit specific jobs. One study found that conscientiousness, extraversion, and openness to experience predict who will be effective in field sales. 12 Once hired, personality traits can be used to screen employees who will benefit most from leadership training. For example, one study found that extraversion, agreeableness, intellectual curiosity, and emotional stability were positively related to both self- and director ratings of leader development in a training program. 13 Thus, traits offer a way to predict who will succeed in certain positions and who is best suited to leadership development.

Implicit Leadership Theory

Research has shown that followers have implicit ideas about the traits of an effective leader. Implicit leadership theory (ILT) initially identified 59 attributes describing leaders. 14 The more a leader shows prototypic traits, the more they will be perceived as leader-like by direct reports. Further refinement of ILT resulted in six strong traits, with two being anti-prototype: sensitivity, intelligence, dedication, dynamism, tyranny, and masculinity (the last two traits are anti-leadership prototypes). 15 Understanding what traits followers expect leaders to exhibit can be helpful. Research has shown that employee ILT attributes influence the quality of the relationship between leaders and followers. When a leader matches direct reports’ leadership prototypes, it can have positive outcomes for the leader, such as positive evaluations and higher chances of promotion. However, people might tend to overemphasize the leader’s traits and to deemphasize the role of the situation when explaining success or failure. Training for followers can help to prevent perceptual biases and unrealistic expectations of leaders. Research has also shown that traits seen as effective in one culture might not be perceived as being effective in other cultures. 16

What’s #Trending in OB?
Is Narcissism Good or Bad for Business?

Given the popularity of television series such as Billions and Succession , many people think of successful CEOs as having a take-charge personality that is self-centered and ruthless. Narcissism is generally defined as “an exaggerated, yet fragile self-concept of one’s importance and influence.” A narcissistic leader has the following personality traits:

  • A sense of entitlement and superiority
  • A strong need for attention
  • A single-minded focus on themselves
  • A lack of empathy for others
  • Constant criticism of others
  • High levels of aggression
  • An unwillingness to hear feedback

161 There are pros and cons to narcissistic leadership. Such leaders drive high performance and achieve bottom-line results during times of rapid change. Leaders that are high on narcissism are impressed by followers who promote themselves because they see them as being like themselves. These grandiose, self-confident leaders often motivate followers by providing a vision of a promising future. But narcissistic leaders might be bad for business. First, they may fail to serve customers because they focus too much on themselves. CEOs receive high compensation and may become greedy. They may inhibit innovation because they prefer their own ideas and don’t listen to others. They may even avoid learning new things. This is supported by a study of 467 MBA students in a leader development program, which found that narcissism was negatively related to director ratings of reflection on development and engagement with peers.

Narcissistic transformational leaders may have a dark side, mobilizing followers to pursue goals that are dangerous. For example, Elizabeth Holmes promised to revolutionize health care. At one point, her company, Theranos, had 800 employees, was valued at $9 billion, and had attracted sophisticated investors. She was subsequently found to have engaged in a massive deception, resulting in the loss of the $700 million invested in the firm. She has been criminally convicted of fraud.

Narcissistic behavior, in the long term, may have more cons than pros, and followers must be aware of the potential negative aspects of this behavior. Grandiose narcissists are difficult to detect and have some of the same traits as visionary leaders. Identifying the dangerous ones is difficult but important. All top leaders need a certain amount of self-confidence and ability to make change and take risks. But some leaders in top positions may have unhealthy amounts of these traits.

Limitations of the Trait Approach

162 The trait approach appears to provide a partial explanation of leadership. When the trait approach did not fully explain leadership, researchers turned their focus on what leaders do. It may be difficult to change employees’ perceptions of leader traits. However, perceptions matter because followers rely on them to define what an effective leader is. The behavioral approaches followed and are covered in the next section. These approaches represent those that are focused on understanding the behavior of leaders rather than their inborn traits and talents. In contrast to trait theories, the leader behavior approach assumes that leaders are made and not born. In other words, anyone can learn the behaviors needed to be an effective leader.

Leader Behaviors

One of the best-known research studies on leader behaviors was conducted at The Ohio State University in the late 1950s. 17 Researchers asked followers to describe what their leaders did and created a list of over a thousand leader behaviors but were able to combine them into two broad categories using statistical analyses. These two categories were labeled Initiating Structure and Consideration . 18 Initiating structure refers to defining tasks for employees and focusing on goals. Consideration is the degree to which the leader shows trust, respect, and sensitivity to employees’ feelings. These two leader behaviors have been studied for decades, and a meta-analytic review of over 150 studies found that both consideration and initiating structure have moderately strong relationships with outcomes. Consideration was more strongly related to follower satisfaction (with the leader and job), motivation, and leader effectiveness. Initiating structure was slightly more strongly related to leader job and group performance. 19 A review expanded the behaviors of leaders to include three task-oriented behaviors (enhancing understanding, strengthening motivation, and facilitating implementation) and three relation-oriented behaviors (fostering coordination, promoting cooperation, and activating resources). Task-oriented behaviors are directed toward the accomplishment of shared objectives; however, relation-oriented behaviors support the coordinated engagement of team members. 20

Which leader behavior is most effective? It appears that what matters is behavioral flexibility and knowing when to engage in the right behavior at the right time. The ability to switch leadership behaviors when needed is highlighted in the contingency approaches to leadership. Behavioral approaches were criticized because they did not consider the influence of the followers or the situation on the emergence of leadership. The next phase in the development of leadership theory was the contingency or situational approach. For example, the situational leadership theory considers the “readiness” of followers in terms of their ability, motivation, and confidence to perform a task. 21 The leader changes their behavior based upon how able and willing a follower is to perform a specific task. One of the most researched situational theories of leadership is the path–goal theory (PGT), which is discussed next.

Path–Goal Theory

Leaders motivate followers to accomplish goals by establishing the paths to the goals . 22 Specifically, leaders increase the quality and number of payoffs from reaching goals and then make the path to the goals clear by removing obstacles. 23

Motivational Leadership Behaviors

163 Path–goal theory (PGT) specifies four leadership behaviors that motivate employees:

  1. Directive leadership —giving followers specific instructions about their tasks, providing deadlines, setting standards for performance, and explaining rules
  2. Supportive leadership —showing consideration, being friendly and approachable, and paying attention to the well-being of followers
  3. Participative leadership —allowing followers to have a voice in decisions that affect them, sharing information, inviting followers’ ideas and opinions
  4. Achievement-oriented leadership —challenging followers to perform at high levels, setting standards for excellence, showing confidence in followers’ ability to reach goals

Adapting to the Situation

164 The leader should be flexible and adapt leadership behavior to employees and the situation. PGT incorporates several considerations, but it is useful because this model reflects key aspects of employees and the situation that leaders consider when increasing motivation. As shown in Figure 6.4 , employees work toward goals but may encounter obstacles. The leader learns of obstacles that the follower faces and helps by removing them. For example, a follower may need market data from the research department in the organization to complete a report. The leader can help by calling the department and asking them to expedite the requested report. The removal of barriers strengthens employee motivation and results in employee satisfaction, effort, and performance.

A key aspect of the situation that the leader needs to consider is follower ability—the leader must adjust expectations in relation to a person’s ability to complete a task. As we learned in Chapter 2, individual differences matter, including personality. For example, some followers have a higher need to socialize with others at work. Other followers may have a higher need for control or a preference for more structure in their work. The PGT framework also considers aspects of the situation, including the task itself; if the task is not clear, the leader must explain what needs to be done. In highly repetitive tasks, leaders can show concern for followers’ well-being (by providing support). The formal authority system is another situation characteristic to consider. In other words, if the formal authority system is strong, the leader can enforce rules. Finally, the norms of the work group may influence individual motivation, and the leader can build cohesion to support the employee’s motivation.

Research on PGT has shown support for propositions of the model. 24 , 25 However, its strength lies in the application of motivation to leadership. The framework informs leaders about what aspects of followers and the situation to consider when setting up pathways to their goals. The model is practical in that it helps leaders to clarify the motivational aspects of goals and performance. It is also important that PGT stresses the removal of barriers to effective performance.

Path–goal theory and other theories focusing on leader behavior were criticized because they assumed that leaders treated all followers the same. Research found that this was not the case. In fact, leaders develop unique relationships with each of their followers. A theory emerged that focuses on these unique working relationships that develop between the leader and each follower called leader–member exchange (LMX) , and this approach is discussed next.

Leader–Member Exchange

165 Should a boss treat everyone alike? Leaders treat subordinates differently based upon their unique abilities and contributions to the work group and organization. Leader–member exchange is defined as the quality of the working relationship developed with each follower and is characterized by more delegation of authority to those with high-quality LMX. 26 In a relatively short period of time, leaders decide on their in-group members and out-group members . Out-group members perform to the specifications in their job descriptions, but they don’t go above and beyond and don’t take on extra work. In-group members do. A diagram of what in-groups and out-groups might look like in a seven-person work group is shown in Figure 6.5 .

Note that there is a poor performer in the work group (labeled P7). This is a different type of relationship where the supervisor is monitoring performance and attempting to get the minimally accepted level of performance. The first goal would be to move this person to the out-group where they are performing to the basic expectations of the job. If performance does not improve, in time, they may be transferred to another work group (where they get a second chance) or dismissed from the organization altogether.

166 The other important thing the diagram shows is that norms of fairness must pervade this entire process. 27 , 28 Fairness must be established to avoid possible negative effects on the performance of the entire work team. A review study found that follower answers to these questions strongly relate to their performance on the job. 29 Another review of 3,327 studies with over 930,000 observations demonstrated that LMX consistently predicts performance compared to other theories of leadership. This study underscores the importance of LMX relationships for understanding what contributes to individual performance at work. 30 Given the strength of LMX predictions on these outcomes, a review study looked at what predicts LMX and found that LMX may be caused by supervisor support, organizational support, work–family balance, and job autonomy. 31 A comprehensive review of the research on LMX offers the following practical implications for managers: 32

  • The process of bringing new employees into an organization is critical for relationship formation.
  • Good leaders take responsibility for their team’s culture and actively manage emotional conflicts. Good leaders do not allow themselves to be drawn into unhealthy follower dynamics.
  • A leader and a follower should agree on the status of the relationship.
  • Leaders communicate constantly, even when unintended, so vigilance against accidental communication of unwanted messages is wise. Pay special attention to the most crucial conversations that involve communicating critical or corrective action.
  • In-groups and out-groups are inevitable and complex. Base high-quality relationships on performance, not bias.
  • Leaders must be more conscious and deliberate when maintaining virtual relationships.
  • Followers likely differ in their desire and capacity for a high-quality relationship with a leader.

Inclusive Leadership

167 Inclusive leadership suggests that leaders help all team members feel part of the group (belongingness) while retaining their sense of individuality (uniqueness) so they contribute to the team atmosphere. 35 Inclusive leadership most benefits diverse teams through its attention to the uniqueness of women and minorities while valuing all members for their attributes, perspectives, and contributions. Leveraging team member uniqueness leads to higher performance. Facilitating belongingness includes valuing each member, ensuring fairness, and sharing decisions with followers. Encouraging uniqueness is valuing individual contributions and helping all members contribute to team performance. 36 Inclusive leaders are effective for facilitating an inclusive work environment that welcomes employees from diverse social identities. The evidence shows that inclusive leadership improves psychological safety, work group identification, and psychological empowerment, along with increasing positive employee attitudes and enhanced performance. 37

Research in Action
Being Proactive With Your Boss: Speak Up!

Reviews of research on proactive behavior at work show that speaking up is positively related to such favorable outcomes as quality LMX and superior job performance. Leaders trust followers who are more proactive, and they are more likely to empower them. They like them and see them as more competent. Organizations may look at proactivity in selecting employees and create a culture that supports proactivity.

How can you be more proactive at work? Here are some evidence-based recommendations:

  • Seek information. Regularly ask your boss for information regarding the formal and informal organizational system, policies and procedures, politics, and organizational culture: How do things really get done in the organization?
  • Seek feedback. Ask for honest feedback about your performance during and after assignments from your boss and others. Don’t wait for your annual performance appraisal to learn how you are doing on the job.
  • Manage your boss. Learn as much as you can about them. Ask them to lunch to discuss your plans (i.e., develop an effective working relationship).
  • Negotiate more job challenge. Ask for changes to your job and develop it so you can be most effective, playing to your strengths but at the same time meeting organizational goals.
  • Be positive. View challenges as opportunities and see teammates and the organization in positive ways. Be optimistic unless someone proves you wrong.

Leaders trust followers who are more proactive, and they are more likely to empower them. They like them and see them as more competent. Proactivity has other benefits. Research conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic in China found that doctors and nurses who were more proactive reported higher levels of perceived strengths use, a job-related motivational resource. Being proactive was also associated with performance and two indicators of well-being (resilience and thriving) through perceived strengths use.

168 Despite the benefits of being proactive, there is a potential downside. Research shows that proactive people tend to outperform coworkers. However, proactive people may be envied by their coworkers because of their relatively high status in work groups, which may then prompt coworkers to withdraw help or even undermine them. Proactive employees need to pay attention to managing relationships with coworkers and provide them with help and advice.

Leader–Member Exchange Development

So how do effective LMX relationships develop? There are three steps in the process: role-taking, role-making, and role routinization. 38 In role-taking, the boss tests the commitment of the follower by offering extra work. Through this testing and response, the boss forms an overall assessment of whether the follower is in the in-group or out-group. It is important to pay attention to the boss’s signals when a person takes a new job or gets a new boss. If a person wants the benefits of more challenging work, promotion potential, or higher salary, they need to respond in a way that the boss views positively. During the process of role-making, mutual expectations of the working relationship are established, and the follower’s role is made clearer. The final step is role routinization. Once roles are made, they become stable since the leader and follower both know what to expect. For example, relationships develop best when the leader can delegate tasks to the employee. 39 Therefore, everyone who reports to a boss (and that’s most of us) should be concerned with managing the relationship and developing a relationship by being dependable. The next sections focus on how to develop a high-quality relationship through followership.

Followership

169 It is often said that “without followers, there can be no leaders.” 40 While understanding what leaders think and do is critical to understand leadership, it is important to note that leaders are not the only ones that play a significant role in the leadership process; it is also important to consider the follower role. Leaders have implicit theories of what makes a good follower, just as followers have their own theories 41 The implicit followership theory (IFT) research has shown that leaders who believe that followers are hardworking and productive are more likely to have higher expectations for those followers and provide them with more autonomy. 42

Interest in followership is not new. 43 From a historical perspective, followership evolved to address coordination problems in social groups (e.g., hunting and gathering). Some individuals did not have the physical or psychological strength to be the leaders and thus tended to become followers. Research has shown that followership styles, behaviors, and engagement can be explained by examining the payoffs that accrue to followers when they follow their leader. This may be affected by the adaptive strategies of followers to meet the leader’s goals and the leader’s style (e.g., dominant leadership). Followership is a voluntary deference process; however, it is different from submissiveness to authority. 44

Interest in followership has been steadily increasing in the past 20 years. Followership has been defined as the behaviors a person engages in while interacting with leaders to meet organizational objectives. 45 In this perspective, followers are viewed as partners with leadership, which is consistent with the LMX model of leadership. Thus, the role of the follower is a “coproduction orientation,” which is the belief that followers should actively engage with leaders to work toward shared goals. Coproduction has been shown to benefit leaders by having more follower support, more motivation, and more contributions from followers, which is explained by followers having a voice in decisions. 46 This is contrasted with “passive orientation,” which is the belief that followers should show deference and follow directions from the leader. A study of followership of remote workers during the COVID-19 pandemic showed that leaders who increased virtual interaction and engagement with followers had mixed results. Some followers had high levels of effort while others declined. This was explained by followers who saw their role as coproduction and giving more collaborative reporting as more effort. Followers with a passive orientation reported less effort. When managing remote workers, leaders should consider follower interest in collaboration rather than treating all followers alike. 47

170 Followership is based on the idea that leadership occurs in the interactions between leaders and followers. In developing a working relationship with each follower, leaders make judgments regarding the reasons for their behaviors, particularly in the case of poor performance. These judgments may or may not be accurate, but they have a strong influence on whether a follower will become a member of the leader’s in-group. Thus, it is important to understand what causes these judgments (known as attributions ) to guard against inaccuracies. Leadership research has examined the attributions that leaders make about the causes of follower poor performance, and this is addressed next.

Attributions and Leader–Member Relationships

There is a great deal of OB research that shows that the attributions of what causes behavior in organizations matter. Attributions represent a person’s attempt to assign a cause to a behavior or event they observe. Attribution theory proposes that the attributions people make about events and behavior can be either internal or external. 48 In organizational settings, attributions are particularly significant when events are important, novel, unexpected, and negative. Attributions also are important when a follower fails to meet performance expectations of the leader. 49 In the case of an internal attribution , people infer that an event or a person’s behavior is due to their own character traits or abilities. If a person makes an external attribution , they believe that a person’s behavior is due to situational factors. For example, Damon is late to class. If the professor believes the tardiness happened because of Damon’s lack of motivation to attend class, she is making an internal attribution. On the other hand, if the professor believes Damon is late because he could not find a parking space in overcrowded university lots, she is making an external attribution. Thus, internal attributions will lead the professor to infer that Damon is a poor student.

Critical Thinking Question: Why are people harsher when making internal attributions? Give an example of when you heard an internal attribution (e.g., when something was blamed on a person’s character).

How can a leader avoid attribution bias? When we ascribe a cause to behavior, we should gather additional information. By paying attention to overall patterns of behavior, we make more accurate conclusions by considering the following:

  1. Consensus information —information about how other people would behave if they were in the same situation. High consensus means others would behave the same way. Low consensus means other people would behave differently.
  2. Distinctiveness information —information about the extent to which the individual behaves the same way in different situations. Low distinctiveness means the individual behaves the same way in different situations. High distinctiveness means the individual behaves a particular way toward a particular situation only.
  3. Consistency information —information about how the individual behaves toward a certain stimulus across time and circumstances. High consistency means the individual behaves the same way almost every time they are in a particular situation.

171 To avoid attribution bias, they should consider how well other people would do in the same situation. For example, do all employees make the same mistake when filling out forms for customers? If so, maybe the form needs to be revised. Second, how distinctive is the behavior? For example, does the employee behave rudely to all coworkers or just one in particular? Finally, how consistent is the behavior? For example, over time the leader observes that an employee does not pay close attention to their work every Friday afternoon, and this is a consistent pattern. Attending to these three possibilities alerts the leader to investigate and gather additional information before making a definitive conclusion about the cause of behavior. This is important because attributions play a role in how leader–member relationships develop.

Attributions play a clear role in the development of work relationships between leaders and followers. 50 , 51 A dyadic (two-party) relational approach to attribution theory is shown in Figure 6.6 . As shown in the figure, an internal or an external attribution might be made, either that the person was not putting in enough effort or that another person was more deserving. A relational attribution would be that the boss does not like the person. The actions a person might take to remedy the situation will vary depending upon the attribution. In the case of an external attribution (the most likely according to the fundamental attribution error), the person may feel helpless to change it except for finding another job. However, if a relational attribution is made, the person might try to improve the working relationship with the boss. Thus, relational attribution theory offers another way a person might improve their work situation by changing how they attribute the causes of an event. If it is the working relationship (and not internal or external factors), actions can be taken to improve it.

When the right attributions are made, the working relationship between a leader and follower will develop into a high-quality one. Next, we turn to how you can increase investment in the LMX relationship with your boss so that it develops into a mentoring relationship, which has also been shown to relate to performance and positive career outcomes for the junior person, or mentee.

The Mentor Connection

172 Mentoring is defined as follows: 52

One of the best things that can happen is when the boss becomes a mentor. Career mentoring from a boss contributes to performance, promotions, and salary increases above and beyond a high-quality LMX relationship. 53 Research on mentoring in organizations shows that having a mentor can have a powerful impact on your career, and the boss is an important place to begin. You want to show them that you want to be a trusted member of the in-group and that you are willing to take on more responsibility. Hopefully, they will then view you as a mentee—someone worth the investment of time and energy to develop. There are many cases where a boss gets promoted and a trusted in-group member is promoted along with them. So you want to be sure to do an inventory of what you have to offer your boss in exchange for career investment. For example, you might offer to take on a challenging problem that the boss has been working on (particularly if it fits your strengths). Or you might lead the team meeting while your boss is out of town (and be sure to keep them informed with a summary of how the meeting went).

Mentors provide two general types of support to protégés: (1) career support and (2) social support. 54 In addition, mentors may serve as role models to mentees. Cultivating a mentoring relationship enhances your job satisfaction and performance. 55 Mentoring is now seen as a network of developmental relationships, 56 including peers, managers other than your boss, and people outside of the organization. However, keep in mind that research has demonstrated that the boss is a key node of your mentoring network since supervisory mentoring has been associated with higher potential, productivity, and organizational commitment. 57

A review of mentoring research points out some ethical concerns to be aware of with respect to workplace mentoring. Mentors have a responsibility to dedicate sufficient time and attention to meet protégé needs and expectations. Mentors must be willing to assume these responsibilities and be professional. Protégés should be supported regardless of gender, ethnicity, or disability. Organizations should assure fairness in access to mentors and the associated benefits. Every employee must have equal access to an effective mentor to ensure fairness. Mentors should be selected to participate in mentoring relationships based on their commitment to devoting time to the development of protégés. 58 Otherwise, negative mentoring experiences may occur that reduce mentoring effectiveness. Examples include neglect, exploitation, sabotage, bad attitudes, and mentor–protégé conflicts, as well as mismatch within the dyad. 59 Organizations should take steps to provide training and support for both mentors and protégés to reduce the risks of negative mentoring. In addition to training, research shows that attention to the compatibility of mentor–protégé pairs—taking into consideration protégé self-esteem and the ability to cope with stress—may alleviate negative mentoring experiences. 60

173 When mentoring is effective, mentoring relationships can be transformative and “change the way we view our careers, our environment, and ourselves.” 61 Mentoring relationships are characterized by a willingness to be vulnerable and trust one another. For a high-quality relationship, mentees expect mentors to be trustworthy and dependable. A study of 315 health care workers found that health care managers’ mentoring behaviors influenced worker perceptions of their trustworthiness in terms of ability, integrity, and benevolence. 62 Trust is at the heart of all effective working relationships—with bosses, mentors, and peers. Next, we discuss research on trust, which is essential for a leader to understand to be effective and address the “trust gap” highlighted at the beginning of this chapter.

The Importance of Trust

The following definition of trust is often cited: “the willingness to be vulnerable.” 63 Trust is related to important outcomes, including risk-taking and job performance. 64 A review of the various definitions of trust offers the following summary: 65 Trust is a psychological state comprising the intention to accept vulnerability based upon positive expectations of the intentions or behavior of another. A review study found that trust is related to LMX as well as job satisfaction and performance. 66 Several other theories of leadership mention trust (e.g., servant leadership, which is discussed later in this chapter). Trust is therefore fundamental to the development of effective working relationships with bosses (and others). A review study concluded that employee trust in the leader explains the influence of leadership styles on job performance. 67 Leader trustworthiness was found to be one of the key factors that made a difference to employees during the COVID-19 pandemic when everyone was working remotely. Remote employees were more dependent on the leader to define and sustain the organization and team culture at that time. Leaders needed to spend extra time and effort building trustworthiness. Employees needed to trust in the leader as truthteller, source of information, and advocate for the team. Since many employees continue to work remotely (at least part-time), building and sustaining trust remains important for leaders to manage the risk of team members feeling isolated, negatively impacting productivity. 68

There are some helpful frameworks to organize your thinking about how trust operates in organizations. A three-part view of trust is a useful way to think about trust development with another person: calculus-based, knowledge-based, and identification-based. 69 Each of these forms is explained in detail in the sections that follow.

Calculus-Based Trust

174 Calculus-based trust (CBT) is a form of trust based upon keeping records of what another person does for you and what you do for them. It is an “arm’s-length” form of trust in which neither party really becomes that vulnerable to the behavior of the other person. The expectations are like contracts, and the consequences of violating trust are punishment or the severing of the relationship. An example of CBT (sometimes called deterrence-based trust) in an organization is a leader telling a follower to perform a task because it is in their job description and reminding them that they are paid to do it (in other words, “Do it or you are fired!”). Many relationships in organizations (and in our daily lives) operate on this level. For example, this is the type of relationship most people have with the person they hire to mow their lawn. It is a straightforward transaction. If the person shows up, cuts the grass, and it looks good, you pay them. If not, you don’t.

Knowledge-Based Trust

In this framework, the second level of trust is called knowledge-based trust (KBT) . This level of trust is grounded in how predictable the other person is. Over time, through interactions where benefits are exchanged between two parties, each person comes to expect the other to come through for them. It is based upon information gathered about the other person in a variety of circumstances. An example of this form of trust in an organization would be a follower becoming the go-to person for the boss in terms of creating his PowerPoint decks for important presentations. The boss can jot down a list of bullet points in a document and send them to the follower. Within a day, the boss receives a professional-looking presentation deck to review and edit. The boss does not have to remind the person that it is in their job description (and it may or may not be). They just make the request, and it happens because the follower’s behavior is predictable.

Identification-Based Trust

Finally, the highest degree of trust in this model is called identification-based trust (IBT) . This form of trust is characterized by the leader and follower sharing the same goals and objectives. In other words, the follower identifies with the leader’s vision. There is no need for recordkeeping, and the predictability of the follower’s behavior is assumed. The follower sees the work group and organization in the same way that the leader does and will perform the right tasks without being asked. For example, a trusted follower would step in and resolve a conflict with another work group member for the leader. IBT becomes a highly efficient form for the leader since followers take care of details while the leader focuses on the strategic vision or negotiating resources for the group with their boss (i.e., working the “linking pin,” as suggested by the LMX model).

Trust develops over time. 70 As shown in Figure 6.7 , trust may transform over time and develop different “faces” as it matures. In other words, the types of trust build upon one another over time based on the nature of the working relationship. CBT develops to a point, and the trust level becomes KBT as the behavior of the parties becomes predictable. As shown in the figure, most relationships in organizations fall into the KBT range of trust. Once stable, a few relationships will reach the next point and will become stable IBT relationships. At this point, both parties fully understand what the other party cares about, and there is a level of empathy that emerges (i.e., the ability to put oneself in the boss’s place and see the organization the way they do). As noted previously, at this level the follower becomes able to act effectively for the boss. There is a sense of harmony in thoughts, emotions, and behavior. Elements that strengthen IBT are the creation of joint projects, shared goals, and shared work values.

175 The development of trust is viewed as “tactical climbing” in which there are increasing levels of risk and vulnerability over time. A longitudinal study of new hires found that the cues that are perceived early in employment predict the emergence of trust. In fact, information attended to in the first hours on a job is crucial. 71 Trust becomes stable, but it is important to remember that trust is vulnerable. Even in high-quality leader–follower relationships, IBT can revert to the CBT stage, so followers and bosses must be careful about maintaining relationships. 72 According to this model, movements between the stages of trust can be smooth and incremental, or they can be dramatic and transformational. The curved arrows on the left of the figure show that trust may increase or decrease over time. Trust may move up dramatically, or it may be a slower, climbing process. If trust is broken, it may move to a lower level. Remember that the development of trust takes time and that trust does require testing to see how durable it is.

From the examples provided, you can see the critical role that trust plays in the development of working relationships with bosses (and others, including peers) in the organization. It’s hard to imagine an organization that could function without trust. So it’s important to understand what you need to do should you damage trust with your boss (of course, the best strategy is to never damage trust, but it can happen). Research on trust repair has examined what strategies work to get the relationship back on track.

Repairing Broken Trust

176 There are three important questions to ask after a trust violation has occurred (the trustee is the person who is the target of the trustor’s trust): 73

  1. Is the trustee innocent or guilty of committing the transgression?
  2. If the trustee is guilty of the transgression, should this be attributed to the situation or to the person?
  3. If the transgression is attributed at least in part to the person, is the personal shortcoming fixable, or is it an enduring characteristic of the trustee?

These questions are important because they offer guidance for repairing trust.

Regarding Question 1, if a trustee is actually innocent, they should emphasize a lack of guilt through denial of the transgression and offer any available exonerating information. 74 Not addressing the issue—remaining reticent (e.g., saying “no comment”)—can be risky because people usually assume the worst. 75

Regarding Question 2, if the trustee is guilty, an apology may be an effective way to repair trust because the expressions of remorse and repentance, which are part of an apology, can alleviate some of the trustor’s concerns. However, the way the apology is given also matters. Research shows that if extenuating factors also played a role, there is benefit to mentioning these circumstances when the apology is offered. 76 Keep in mind that though explanations and excuses can restore trust, they have to be seen as adequate; incomplete explanations don’t work. 77

Excuses might be effective, depending on the characteristics of the person violating the trust and the relationship quality, but adding reparations (i.e., attempts to “make it right”) increases the effectiveness of explanations. 78 , 79 However, further research suggests that it depends on other contingencies, and these are described next.

Finally, Question 3 represents one of the most important—but least obvious—contingencies for trust repair: Will the trustor see the cause of the transgression as stemming from an enduring flaw in the trustee or something the trustee can, in fact, address? If the transgression stems from an honest mistake or lack of knowledge (i.e., a competence-related issue), people are more likely to give the benefit of the doubt and trust again. However, if the act is seen as demonstrating a lack of integrity, trust is much more difficult (if not impossible) to repair. For example, promises may restore trust but not if the trust is broken because the person has initially lied. Lying is one of the most damaging behaviors. 80 If someone feels deceived, trust may not be restored, even if apologies, promises, and repeated trustworthy actions follow the deception. Even more “substantive” responses, such as offering reparation or paying a personal cost, are limited in their effectiveness when questions of integrity are at the center of the issue. 81

A review of research on trust repair offers the following summary for repairing trust in leader–follower relationships. 82

  1. 177 After leaders breach trust, when a long time has passed, followers express the most need for an apology, but an apology is less effective at enhancing trustworthiness than when one is offered right away.
  2. Leaders must first openly discuss the violation(s), apologize, and demonstrate support for followers. That said, some trust violations are so serious that the relationship cannot be repaired, and followers withdraw from the relationship.
  3. The effectiveness of apologies depends on the leaders’ intentions and the severity of the consequences of the trust violation. Moderate severity and intentionality are more likely to result in forgiveness compared to mild or intense ones.
  4. When violations are personal, apologies and empathy repair trust more effectively than if there is no empathy. When combined with denial of guilt, the perception of the leader’s integrity is affected.
  5. Follower emotional intelligence (EQ) has a positive effect on their trust in the leader, and setting direct goals compensates for low levels of follower EQ.
  6. Apologies with explanations significantly reduce the erosion of trust compared to efforts that do not include these. The loss of trust was minimized when the leader engaged in more trust repair.

If you are interested in becoming a leader at the top levels of an organization or starting your own business, you will tend to be interested in research on transformational and visionary leadership. You might view yourself as transformational. However, for those of you who view yourselves as managers who sometimes take the leadership role, you will also benefit from knowledge about leadership. Leadership involves a “full range” of behaviors, and the next section discusses both management (i.e., transactional behaviors) and leadership (i.e., transformational behaviors).

Full-Range Leadership Development

The full-range leadership development model is based upon over 25 years of research on transformational leadership . 83 , 84 , 85 People are more engaged when their leaders behave in certain ways at the highest end of the full-range model. The full-range model starts at the lower end of leadership, which is termed transactional leadership . Leadership is a continuum, with transactional leadership being the foundation upon which transformational leadership is built. These behaviors range from passive to active and ineffective to effective as depicted in Figure 6.8 . The specific elements of transactional and transformational leadership are described in the following sections.

Transactional Leadership

178 Transactional leadership is defined as behaviors that motivate followers through rewards and corrective actions. The transactional leader behaviors are as follows (from worst to best):

  • Nonleadership/laissez-faire leadership. This is the “near-avoidance of leadership,” 86 the least active and least effective of all the leadership styles in the full-range model.
  • Management by exception. This has two forms: active and passive. In management by exception–active (MBE-A), the leader looks for the follower to make errors and then corrects them. In management by exception–passive (MBE-P), the leader does not actively look for errors or deviations from work standards, but when noticed, they take corrective action.
  • Contingent reward. This is promising or delivering rewards to followers contingent on their performance.

There are times when a manager must use the transactional approach. For example, if they have a low-performing employee, a leader may need to employ the management-by-exception approaches.

At the next level in the full-range model are the transformational leadership behaviors.

Transformational Leadership

179 Transformational leadership is defined as behaviors that mobilize extra effort from followers through emphasis on change through articulating a new vision for the organization. As noted earlier, it is this set of behaviors that is most related to positive attitudes, commitment, and performance of followers. Leadership is active, and this leads to effectiveness, as shown in Figure 6.8 . These behaviors include the following (known as the Four I’s):

  • Idealized influence. Being admired and respected by followers is the core of this leadership component. These leaders are seen as change agents in the organization.
  • Inspirational motivation. Leaders inspire others to work hard toward organizational goals by providing challenge. They are positive and upbeat and get others to feel optimistic.
  • Intellectual stimulation. Transformational leaders encourage innovation and new ideas. They listen to followers openly and don’t criticize novel solutions to problems.
  • Individualized consideration. Transformational leaders treat each follower as a unique person. They get to know people one-on-one and mentor them.

Transformational leaders increase intrinsic motivation by aligning followers’ tasks with their own interests and what they value most. Meta-analyses have confirmed that transformational leadership behaviors are positively and significantly related to both productivity and performance ratings by supervisors. 87 Transformational leadership also predicts employee creativity, especially when leaders communicate high expectations for creative behaviors. 88

Ethical, Servant, and Authentic Leadership

There has been an increase in attention to ethics in the study of leadership in the past 15 years. Following the scandals of Enron, Wells Fargo, and others, researchers in OB responded by working on new theories that incorporate ethics and an emphasis on followers. These theories consider ethics and how leaders today must be authentic and serve followers rather than their own goals exclusively. The study of ethics in leadership will continue to be of interest to OB researchers as they continue to demonstrate relationships of ethical leadership with employee well-being and performance. Development of these approaches now appears in textbooks for students and in corporate training programs to sensitize the next generation of managers to ethical aspects of leadership and improve leadership practice.

These emerging theories are a good example of how OB research responds to current challenges organizations face and how researchers generate new knowledge to guide leaders.

Ethical Leadership

180 Ethical decision-making is important to the practice of leadership, and contemporary theories of leadership address this. Ethical leadership is defined as “the demonstration of normatively appropriate conduct through personal actions and interpersonal relationships, and the promotion of such conduct to followers through two-way communication, reinforcement, and decision-making.” 89 Research on ethical leadership has found four components:

  • Moral sensitivity involves recognizing that our behavior impacts others.
  • Moral judgment involves determining the right decision.
  • Moral motivation is having the need to do the right thing.
  • Moral action is doing the right thing. 90

Ethical leadership has been found to be positively related to work group–level ethical behavior and negatively related to relationship conflict among coworkers. 91 A review of the research on ethical leadership concludes the following:

Another review found that when supervisors are perceived as more ethical, employees not only engage in fewer deviant behaviors, they also have higher job performance and help their coworkers more. The study concluded that it is thus valuable for organizations to select and develop leaders who hold and practice high ethical standards. 93

The ethical behavior of top managers matters as well. If leaders at the top of the organization are viewed as ethical by their followers, then ethics have a cascading effect throughout the organization; lower-level employees also view their manager as ethical. 94 Thus, ethical leadership at the top of an organization has a trickle-down effect to lower organizational levels. For example, Jack Ma, the founder and executive chairman of Alibaba, has fought piracy (counterfeit goods) for years. Alibaba investigators have sent 400 people to jail, and they have deleted 370 million listings for fake products. 95 To prevent further occurrences, Ma explained that they could trace the origins of the fake products from their database and deliver the information to the police for crackdowns. He recognized that fighting counterfeit products is a war against greed. It is not an easy fight, but it is a necessary one.

Ethics are emerging as a key aspect of leadership theories. Next, we discuss two other recent approaches to leadership that focus on leaders doing the right things by employees and the organization: servant and authentic leadership.

Servant Leadership

181 In addition to ethical leadership, two other theories have emerged: servant leadership 96 and authentic leadership . 97 , 98 Findings indicate that followers respond positively to these leader behaviors. Servant leadership dates to the 1970s when Robert Greenleaf was inspired about leadership while reading Hermann Hesse’s Journey to the East . In this novel, a group of men undertake a long journey. A servant named Leo sings to them and inspires them while doing his tasks. Leo disappears along the way, and the group falls into chaos and cannot complete their journey. 99 The basic idea is that followers are first rather than leaders. Researchers have shown that servant leadership relates to positive attitudes and performance of followers. 101 A review found that followers feel more empowered, committed, and engage in better service to customers when they have servant leaders. 102 Servant leadership predicts such outcomes over other leadership styles so organizations would benefit by developing current leaders into servant leaders. 103 Seven servant leadership dimensions have been identified: emotional healing, creating value for the community, conceptual skills, empowering, helping subordinates grow and succeed, putting subordinates first, and behaving ethically. 104 Servant leaders facilitate team confidence, affirming the strengths and potential of the team and providing developmental support. 105 , 106 This developmental support is also characteristic of humble leadership , where a leader’s humility allows them to show followers how to grow as a result of work. This leads followers to believe that their own developmental journeys are legitimate in the workplace. Leader humility creates shared leadership in teams by encouraging proactive team members to take responsibility. 107 Shared leadership is most strongly related to team performance when team members had high levels of task-related competence. 108 However, humility may be a double-edged sword for leaders. Five separate studies 109 found that while humility increases cooperation, it may also reduce follower perceptions of skill, influence, and autonomy. This results in lower leader effectiveness due to undermining—followers are more likely to check the leader’s directives and less likely to recommend rewards. Despite this caveat, a review found that humble leadership can positively impact outcomes such as innovation, learning, job satisfaction, and performance. 110

Authentic Leadership

Authentic leadership involves knowing oneself and behaving in a way that is consistent with what is intuitively right. Authentic leaders are most effective when they develop an effective vision that relates to the shared interests of their team. Dan Vesella (CEO of the pharmaceutical company Novartis) is an example of such a leader because he not only is successful but also demonstrates compassion by assisting people suffering from life-threatening diseases. 111 Authentic leadership has four dimensions: 112

  • Self-awareness —for example, seeks feedback to improve interactions with others
  • Relational transparency —for example, says exactly what they mean
  • 182 Internalized moral perspective —for example, demonstrates beliefs that are consistent with actions
  • Balanced processing —for example, solicits views that challenge deeply held positions

When considering authentic leadership, a question arises regarding how much to reveal about oneself. A research study examined this and found that employees who would like to present themselves effectively at work should authentically and transparently present themselves while avoiding exaggerating or falsifying strengths. Presenting their genuine self (including both merits and flaws) can make them more trusted by others and enhance their job performance. Leaders can also help employees make their self-presentation more effective by adopting authentic leadership, especially with people who want to present themselves authentically. 113

There are some similarities among these approaches to leadership, but there are also some key differences. A recent review compared these approaches and found that authentic and ethical leadership may not offer unique contributions to understanding the relationship of leadership to employee performance, organizational citizenship behaviors, and attitudes. However, servant leadership did appear to be significantly different from transformational leadership. 114

Leadership Implications: Leading Through Paradox

A key takeaway from this chapter is that leaders need to stay flexible and adapt to both followers and the situation they are in. This chapter covers the essential core leadership theories that are best supported by research. Leadership represents a full range of behaviors, and this is suggested by the full-range leadership model. Leaders engage in both transactions and transformation, and the importance of behavioral flexibility is a theme in the chapter. This adaptability has been supported by research on the paradox in management.

Paradoxical leadership addresses the need for leaders to adapt to contradictory demands due to increasing technological change, global competition, and workforce diversity. For example, leaders must increase efficiency and foster innovation, support both individuals and teams, and think globally while acting locally. Leaders’ responses to paradoxical tensions is a key of organizational effectiveness. 115 Paradoxical leadership refers to “seemingly competing, yet interrelated, behaviors to meet structural and followers’ demands simultaneously and over time.” 116 When it comes to people, research has identified leader paradoxes of (1) self-centeredness with other-centeredness; (2) maintaining both distance and closeness; (3) treating subordinates uniformly while allowing individualization; (4) enforcing work requirements while allowing flexibility; and (5) maintaining decision control while allowing autonomy. 117 Paradoxical leadership predicts employee innovation, and this is explained by the leader’s vision. A review of research on paradoxical leadership found that it is consistently positively associated with follower in-role performance, organizational citizenship behavior, creativity, voice, and innovation. 118

183 Research was conducted with data from five leading firms that effectively manage paradox to achieve strategic agility: Astro Studios, Digital Divide Data, IBM Global Services Canada, Lego, and Unilever. The leaders in these firms share practices that enable them to seek creative solutions to paradox. The cases revealed five paradoxical leadership practices that enable strategic agility: 119

  • Value paradoxes as a vital ingredient of high performance.
  • Proactively identify and raise tensions.
  • Avoid traps of anxiety and defensiveness.
  • Consistently communicate a both/and vision.
  • Separate efforts to focus on both sides of a paradox.

In sum, paradoxical leadership replaces “either/or” thinking with “both/and” thinking. For example, a leader may be faced with decisions that must maximize profit and protect the environment simultaneously. The most effective leaders reject choices and realize that paradoxes underlie the most important issues that organizations face. Rather than choose between options, they work to reconcile competing demands at the same time. 120

Now what we have covered leadership, is important to note that it is integral to the topics covered in all the chapters in this textbook. Leaders need to understand individual differences such as personality (Chapter 2) and manage emotions and moods (Chapter 3). Leaders can change follower attitudes and improve job satisfaction (Chapter 4). An important leadership role is the decision-making role, and leaders are problem-solvers (Chapter 5). To motivate effectively, leaders need to build trust and establish high-quality relationships with their followers (Chapters 8 and 9). You will learn that leadership matters in teams (Chapter 10) as well. One of the most important things a leader must do is resolve conflict and negotiate with others effectively (Chapter 11). Effective leaders know how to communicate one-on-one as well as in large groups (Chapter 12). 121 , 122 Leaders today must understand diversity and cultural differences that may affect how employees interact with one another at work (Chapter 13). Organizational culture is, in part, shaped by the leader (Chapter 14). Leaders play an important role in the implementation of organizational change through transformational leadership and helping employees cope with change (Chapter 15). Leadership is a two-way influence process, and the next chapter discusses power as well as influence and politics (Chapter 7).

Case Study 6.1 Cynt Marshall: Game-Changing Leadership

Cynt Marshall became CEO of the Dallas Mavericks in 2018. She had retired after a remarkable tenure as president of AT&T. But when she received a request to come out of retirement and become CEO of the Dallas Mavericks, she embraced the opportunity to make a positive change. When Mark Cuban, one of the owners of the team, called her, she didn’t know who he was. After he introduced himself to her, he told her, “I need a leader over here.” At that time, the Mavs were plagued by headlines of sexual misconduct, domestic violence, and a toxic locker-room environment that had lasted over 2 decades. Sports Illustrated referred to the Maverick organization as “the animal house.” A few months later, he introduced her to the media as his “fixer.” She gave herself 100 days to effect change with a no-tolerance policy for inappropriate behavior. Her vision was that the Mavs would become the global standard for diversity, equity, and inclusion.

Cynt turned the culture around with her leadership style. She believes in strong values. “I’m a values-based leader, and I rely on my values wherever I’m called to lead. What I have to do in this job and what I’ll have to do in the future is learn the subject matter, and that’s the only really big difference.” She communicated values with her New Dot Com (every word starts with “com”) mantra:

  • Compassion: People needed it. Give them the space to process the way they needed to process. Allow them to heal the past injustices and move forward.
  • Communication: Have courageous conversations and not just once. People were invited to people to come in and talk to others any time.
  • Community: Community service is in great demand and not just outside the organization. Their work group is the community as well.
  • Compromise: None of them would compromise their health, and they wouldn’t allow each other to compromise theirs.
  • Compliance: They ensured compliance with COVID-19 protocols and adhered to the advice of the local government.

188 She is a servant leader, stating, “When I took the job at the Dallas Mavericks, I decided I would walk in every day to serve our fans and our leadership. I walked in with the mindset of a servant leader, and I continue to lead with that mindset today.” In addition, she listens to followers: “A long time ago I learned that there are only a few things that I need to do as a leader. I need to listen to the people. Not just hearing them, but actively listening. I need to learn from them because they are the experts. They’re in touch with what’s happening out there. If I can’t listen to them, if I can’t learn from them, if I don’t love them, I absolutely cannot lead them.” By listening to them and considering their viewpoints, she empowers them.

Cynt also believes in authentic leadership. She recalled another top executive job offer where they asked her to change her name to “Cynthia” or “Cindy” instead of “Cynt.” But she had always gone by Cynt. They also said that her laugh was too loud and that she needed to cut her hair. She declined, knowing that she needed to lead as her authentic self. On authenticity, she says: “Authenticity matters.” It’s okay to show up to the workforce as who you are. You might have to temper it, she said, “but I brought value with my background and who I am, and that contributes to the workplace.” Her self-awareness of her background contributes to her leadership style.

When Cynt started, there were no women or people of color on the leadership team. By leading with servant and authentic leadership, she created a winning team, on and off the court, with a 14-person executive team composed of half women and half people of color. The Mavs are no longer the animal house. They have a set of shared values of character, respect, authenticity, fairness, teamwork, and safety (CRAFTS). During this time, business metrics greatly improved with ticket revenue increasing 64%, sponsorships up 111%, and grants through the Mavs’ foundation rising 162%.

Cynt took the job because she believed that by doing the right thing, she would transform the Mavs organization. As the first and only Black woman to become CEO of an NBA team, Cynt led the Mavericks through a cultural transformation, setting the tone for DEI efforts across the entire league. She has developed and mentored others as a positive role model.

Explain which of the behaviors Cynt Marshall demonstrates: behaving ethically, creating value for the community, putting others first, helping others grow and succeed, emotional healing, empowering others, and conceptual skills.
  2. Consider the dimensions of authentic leadership from this chapter. Explain how Cynt Marshall demonstrates self-awareness, relational transparency, moral perspective, and balanced processing.
  3. Explain why listening to employees is an important part of a leader’s job—and more specifically, why listening is important when implementing major organizational transformation such as what the Mavs went through.
