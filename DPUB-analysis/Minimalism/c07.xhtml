How often have you visited a minimalist friend’s kitchen and wondered how on earth they keep it looking so perfect and decluttered? This may be extra confusing if this person has a large family or hosts weekly dinner parties.

We think there are two keys to a minimalist kitchen. The first is that you have what you need to cook and bake as you like, and you keep nothing you don’t use regularly. In the kitchen, your definition of “regularly” could mean a few times per year if you have special tools that are uniquely functional, such as baking tins for Norwegian sandbakkel cookies, or sentimental pieces that bring you joy, such as your grandmother’s divided relish tray.

One person’s minimalist kitchen may contain more or fewer items than someone else’s, which leads us to our second key to a minimalist kitchen — it is well-organized and uncluttered. You can quickly find what you need because you don’t have to wade through a sea of items you rarely (if ever) use, and the items you retain are stored where you’d expect to find them.

You can lessen the feeling of a chaotic kitchen in two ways: owning fewer items or adding more storage. For a minimalist kitchen, we think it’s both — you pare back what you own so you have fewer items to store, and you improve your kitchen’s organization and its storage solutions. This chapter covers both.

Taking the Scary Out of Starting

The kitchen may be the most popular room in the house — from family meals and neighbor coffee catch-ups, to laundry folding, arts and crafts, and Lego assembly, kitchens host a wide array of activities. So they usually contain a lot of stuff, including food and appliances, space for cookbooks, at least one catch-all junk drawer, and possibly a bar, coffee center, craft cart, and even a built-in desk (hello ‘90s design aesthetic).

Feeling daunted before you’ve even begun? You’ve got this. Remember that every minimalist kitchen starts with one shelf or one drawer. So start with something easy and let the momentum help you keep going. And if you don’t have time to dedicate an entire afternoon to assessing and paring back your kitchen stuff, don’t worry — the trick isn’t doing it all at once. Even spending ten minutes each day assessing and purging items adds up to five hours of decluttering every month, which will steadily help you create the calm, minimalist space you’re striving for.

Discovering the Basics of Kitchen Decluttering

First and foremost, we want you to think about what you do in the kitchen the most. Your kitchen should work for you, and if you have items you never use in the middle of your counter and your most-used gadget tucked away, you need to reevaluate their placement.

As you prepare to review your kitchen’s contents, start by thinking about what you do in the kitchen and what items you use every day. Cooking dinner, making tea, whipping up a smoothie, and baking your own bread all require different equipment, but if you do any of these things often, you want to make sure the needed equipment is within easy reach.

The amount of time you spend in your kitchen doesn’t have to impact how much stuff you have! Avid cooks don’t need to have three times the gadgets of someone who eats most meals out and uses the oven to store jeans and sweaters (sometimes storage is storage, however it looks). In fact, the opposite is true — spending more time in the kitchen means you have to consistently spend more time evaluating what you actually use so you can maintain a minimalist approach; it’s too easy to start accumulating stuff again.

Remember, your kitchen has to fit your lifestyle, and what works for you today may not work for you as your lifestyle changes (including your cooking habits, dietary needs, and family size). So be prepared to assess your kitchen’s contents regularly.

Determining your needs

Getting clear on what you need is the basis of your initial kitchen pare-down. (After you know what you need, you can decide what to keep and what to get rid of.) Every kitchen is as unique as the person, family, or tribe it is supporting. This means that every kitchen has unique uses.

To figure out what your needs are, ask yourself these questions:

  • What’s the current size of your household?
  • How often do you or someone in your household cook?
  • How often do you entertain guests?
  • What area of your kitchen is the most inefficient?
  • Which kitchen storage area is the most stuffed or overflowing with items?
  • Is your kitchen efficiently organized? Do you have to walk far to get what you need?
  • Which cabinets and drawers are used most?
  • What other stuff is in your kitchen that’s is not related to cooking? For example, do you have paperwork, computers, laundry detergent, or mudroom supplies stored inefficiently in your kitchen?

Pay close attention to how your kitchen is currently organized and make a simple list of what you know could be improved. Use Figure 7-1 to note these improvements.

Taking stock of your stock

After you’ve got a good idea of what your kitchen needs are (see the preceding section), take inventory of your entire kitchen. This “before” assessment will help you appreciate the efficiency, reduced frustration, and increased joy and calm you’ll feel “after” you minimalize your kitchen items and organize what remains. To perform this inventory, go through everything thoroughly. Make a checklist if you need to, and take note of all the items you currently have. Use the following categories to help you group your list:

  • Food storage
  • Pots and pans
  • Preparatory items (cutting boards, cooking knives, mixing bowls, and so on)
  • Cooking utensils
  • Bakeware
  • Small appliances
  • Glasses, cups, and drinking items
  • Dishes
  • Flatware
  • Serving ware
  • Storage containers
  • Cleaning supplies and tools
  • Bulk storage
  • Linens
  • Management/home office
  • Miscellaneous

Don’t worry, this inventory doesn’t have to be exact and can be done in under 15 minutes. It’s more of an exercise to get you started on the decluttering process, because as you go through our list and add your own items, it quickly becomes clear where you have excess.

After you inventory your kitchen as a whole, take stock of the food in your pantry, fridge, and freezer. Use the checklists in Figure 7-2 to help you with your food inventory. (We encourage you to do this quarterly to make sure you’re using all you have and aren’t inadvertently stockpiling items you don’t really need.)

Scaling back your kitchen stuff, item by item

When you’re ready to get rid of your kitchen extras, have several boxes or bins nearby labeled Sell, Donate, Trash, so that as you identify the items you won’t keep, you can immediately sort them based on how you plan to get rid of them. Items in good shape can be sold at online or in-person garage sales. You can also donate unneeded items to charities, churches, Buy Nothing groups on social media, and nearby shelters. (We like grouping items into a Get Started bundle for people furnishing a kitchen for the first time.) Any items that are broken, missing pieces, or expired should go into the trash.

SIZING YOUR STORAGE TO FIT YOUR NEW SPACE

Many people have asked us how much cupboard space they should have. If you’re starting from scratch by building a new home or renovating your much-loved kitchen, seek help from a kitchen designer, who’ll be familiar with many space-saving kitchen layouts that can take best advantage of your space.

The National Kitchen & Bath Association (NKBA, https://nkba.org ) offers a few best practices too.

In the kitchen, the standard measure for storage space is shelf/drawer frontage, which can be calculated by cabinet width multiplied by cabinet depth multiplied by the number of shelves and drawers.

The NKBA recommends a total shelf/drawer frontage of

  • 1,400 inches for small kitchens (less than 150 square feet)
  • 1,700 inches for a medium kitchen (151 to 350 square feet)
  • 2,000 inches for a large kitchen (more than 350 square feet)

We find that having fewer kitchen items is especially important, not only because it helps with kitchen hygiene and cleanliness, but it can also bring increased energy, creativity, and motivation for staying healthy and cooking healthy meals.

As you begin to sort through everything in your kitchen, keep in mind the following general principles:

  • Don’t have endless multiples. We know you have more plates, mugs, and cups than you need. You may not have enough of one pattern or way too many of another. Decide how many of each item you need for the household you live in (six coffee drinkers in one home will need more than four coffee cups), and then sell, toss, donate, or repurpose the extras.
  • Be logical. Are large, clunky, barely used gadgets taking up a lot of counter or other space? When evaluating appliances especially, you have to be ruthless about what you keep and what you don’t. Yes, you might use that juicer one day, but have you used it in the last year? In the last six months? We often hang onto larger gadgets because they were a gift, or we have good intentions to use them one day, but you’ll reclaim valuable space if you’re honest about what you actually use. The good news is that if appliances and gadgets still work, you can likely sell them!
  • Evaluate your stockpiles and bulk. Do you have so much food that you don’t have space to store it all? It’s important to have dried and canned food on hand for emergencies, and who can forget the paper goods shortage of 2020? But as you adopt a minimalist approach to food and pantry items, start buying only what is really necessary. Trips to big box stores such as Costco and Sam’s Club can leave any kitchen stuffed to the brim with too many of one item, as well as items so large they don’t even fit on your shelves. Instead, start buying items only because you need them.
  • Assess the worth of each item carefully. Get rid of anything when the answer isn’t a quick and certain Yes to the following:
    • Do I use this item?
    • Is this the only one of its kind in my kitchen?
    • Would I buy this again?
    • Does this help make my kitchen life more efficient and calmer?

Once you’ve examined your kitchen and gotten rid of what you don’t need, keeping this space minimal is key. Try not to accumulate lots of kitchen gadgets or devices linked to food fads and trends. If your friends are constantly gifting you items because you’re a foodie, perhaps mention you’d like more experiences as gifts rather than “stuff.” You can let everyone know that you’re on a decluttering mission to avoid accumulating more items.

SHOPPING LIKE THE EUROPEANS

We don’t buy in bulk anymore. When one of this book’s authors first moved to Europe, they wondered where to find Costco because they felt they needed to always stock up, even for a one-person household. They quickly discovered that shopping in bulk isn’t the European way; instead, they grocery shop more often, sometimes daily, and only get what they need for that day or the next few days.

Europeans have less space and typically smaller dwellings than North Americans, so this could be part of the reason for less bulk. However, they also have a different mindset of “buy what you need and use what you buy.” The University of Arizona published an article that found that buying in bulk often leads to more food waste, and that the idea that bulk purchases save you money is actually a total myth. So next time you feel bad for forgetting your reusable grocery bag twice in one week, think about how much worse you feel when you can’t use the food you bought and it goes to waste.

Eliminating excess kitchen tools

What you need in your kitchen is based on the habits of your household, the space you have, and your definition of kitchen minimalism. But no matter your answer to each, here are some specific guidelines to help you assess everyday tools and weed out the excess:

  • Dish towels: As soon as you read this, you’re probably either thinking you need to purchase more dish towels or that you already have too many. Whichever thought crossed your mind, go through all your dish towels and discard ones that are past their due date; if they’re torn or ragged, get rid of them. Then, evaluate what you have left and whether you actually use them. See if the ones you’re discarding can be repurposed for cleaning rags, donated to thrift stores, or given to animal shelters.

    Good quality dish towels truly can last for years, even with an intense amount of wear and tear. We’ve found that European dish towels, especially those from Switzerland, are the best quality and surpass any we’ve found in North America. If you’re purchasing dish towels elsewhere, make sure they’re 100 percent Turkish cotton, which are softer and more durable than other styles. We like to keep two dish towels in our kitchen at all times; one for drying hands and one for drying dishes.

  • Mixing bowls: Go through these and eliminate what you don’t use or what you have in excess.
  • Bakeware: We get that you used to love to make muffins in the shape of hearts for your kid’s Valentine’s Day bake sales, but do you use them anymore? Anything not used should be either tossed, sold, repurposed, or donated.
  • Cutlery: We’re sure you have a few stragglers that you don’t use, or sets that aren’t complete or are missing all the little spoons or a few forks. We suggest donating the cutlery that doesn’t match your set so that everything has a streamlined and organized look.
  • Coffee mugs and glassware: Only keep what you need. Do you use the mugs with your kids’ pictures on them from 2008? Maybe these are sentimental items, and if they are, then you should store them with other sentimental items instead of in the kitchen. (Of course if you use them every other day, then by all means, keep them in the kitchen.) Most people have a lot of mugs or glasses, but you only need one or two per person in your house.
  • Storage containers: We’re not sure why there’s a lid monster in every kitchen, but that’s just the way it is. Go through all your storage containers and discard any without a lid (and discard any lids without their containers). The better the quality of your containers, the less often, surprisingly, the lids go missing. Maybe it’s because you take better care of quality items, or you’re more careful overall, but investing in good storage containers will help you be more mindful of their uses.
  • Oven mitts: Chances are, the oven mitt you use every day is one you’ve had since you graduated from school, no matter if it’s your only mitt or one of a half dozen. If either is true for you, consider upgrading your oven mitt or minimizing the number you have.
  • Aprons: We believe in using aprons because they help keep your clothes clean and happy. Go through your aprons and only keep the ones you use. We only keep ones that make us look good (some aprons have a magical way of making you look slimmer, taller, and more stylish when cooking, while others make you look like your great-grandmother). We swear you’ll look differently at your aprons after reading this.
  • Spices: Going through your spices can be a messy job, but you have to do it. You likely have at least a few you’ve never used or maybe are even expired; toss these (or donate the unused ones if they’re still good). Keep on-hand only the spices you actually use regularly or know you’ll use in the next few months. You can always rebuy a spice if you need it down the road, or you can purchase a small amount in your grocery store’s bulk section if you need it for something specific.
  • Cookbooks: Go through these and keep only what you use. This can be tough, as you may think you’ll need one as a reference someday. But be realistic about what you actually use versus what you aspire to use, and remember that the Internet has a recipe for everything.
  • Medicine, vitamins, and supplements: Even if you keep most of your medicine in your bathroom, some types have a way of trickling into the kitchen for as-needed use. And we know many people who keep their vitamins and supplements in the kitchen to eat with meals. If either describes your kitchen, go through all medication, vitamins, and supplements and discard any that are expired, and certainly toss any prescriptions you no longer need. If you do store medication in your kitchen, make sure you keep them away from steam from ovens or kettles, and either stored up high or locked away from anyone who shouldn’t be accessing it.
  • Cleaning supplies: No matter where you store the bulk of your cleaning supplies, chances are a few (or more than a few) supplies are always in your kitchen. Don’t keep a huge stash of items, or multiples. Make sure that each item is labeled properly, and store only what you actually need in the kitchen. Also, move the bathroom cleaners to your bathrooms, and any other specific supplies to their respective locations.
  • Gadgets: Don’t save those impulse-buy kitchen items if you don’t already use them. Get over their sunk cost; donate, sell, toss, or repurpose them and move on. Assess every gadget, even things like your microwave — if you’ve only used it once in the last year for popcorn, is it really necessary in your kitchen?

Figuring Out How Many Dishes, Cutlery, and Other Items You Need

You may be wondering how many of each item you should have in your kitchen. Although this differs for each person and household, we think you need enough dishes and cutlery for a daily meal and a few extras for spares and guests.

This may not seem like a lot, and you may like hanging onto extras for when some are dirty or waiting to be cleaned. The solution is simple: Clean your dishes or empty the dishwasher. The fewer dishes you have, the less you’ll need to store; you’ll certainly be using all you have; and the better you and your household will be about staying on top of kitchen chores.

Many people hang onto excess items in case they have a dinner party. Ask yourself this: When was the last time you had a party? Last week, last year, or never? If the answer is either of the latter two, you likely don’t need to have a lot of spare dishes. If you do decide to host a gathering, you can always borrow dishes and cutlery from friends or rent them.

We dig a little deeper into volumes of items in the following sections.

Dishes

We recommend having one place setting per household member, where a place setting is one larger (dinner) plate, one smaller (salad) plate, and one bowl (possibly two bowls, one with higher sides for items like cereal and one with lower sides for items like pasta). Clean your dishes after each use so that you don’t need excess settings while your dishes are dirty.

You may need a few additional dishes based on your household’s needs — for example, if your house often hosts neighborhood kids, you may need more smaller plates for the time being. Think about your lifestyle and then be ruthless with the number of settings you actually need.

Dishes often come in sets of four or eight place settings. If you buy a set with more place settings than you need — for example, a set of eight but your household has only three members — you don’t necessarily have to store the excess settings in your kitchen. You may opt for an alternate storage space and then retrieve more when you have guests or to replace broken items.

Silverware, flatware, or cutlery

Your silverware drawer is likely the most-opened drawer in your home. We call it silverware out of habit, but it may not be sterling silver at all; it’s also called flatware or cutlery. These eating utensils can be plastic, gold, stainless steel, or whatever material you happen to have chosen. Since we use silverware so frequently, it may not make sense to take an extremely minimalist approach to these items (let’s be honest — we need more than one fork in our lives). To begin assessing and scaling back this drawer, take everything out and do a deep clean at least quarterly; this high-traffic drawer accumulates the most dirt and usually has the greatest number of different people’s hands rummaging through it.

Here is a calculation to help you determine how much silverware you need: Multiply the number of people in your house by the number of meals you eat per day, and multiply that by the number of times you run your dishwasher. This means that even if you only run your dishwasher at night, you’ll have enough clean silverware for the next day’s meals. (And if you don’t have a dishwasher, we suspect you’re doing dishes at least twice per day, so make sure you have enough silverware to last your family between washups.)

Coffee mugs

Figuring out the “right” number of coffee mugs to keep can be tough; many of us have shelves and shelves of mugs. So we give you a calculation rather than a number.

If you never have guests, you should have as many mugs as the following calculation indicates: Multiply the number of people in your household (who use mugs) by the number of mugs they use per day by the number of times you run your dishwasher (skip this step if you don’t have a dishwasher).

Here are a couple examples:

  • There are two people in your house, you both use two mugs a day, and you run the dishwasher once a day. You should have four mugs.
  • If you run your dishwasher every three days because there are only two of you, then you likely need 2 x 2 x 3 = 12 mugs (or, better yet, hand wash your mugs after you’ve used a couple).

If you often have guests, add a few mugs and think about how many guests you normally have. Whether you have one couple or several couples staying with you makes a difference in how many you need.

If you have collector or sentimental mugs, really think about whether they need to be stored in the kitchen. Broken mugs can be repurposed into vases, makeup brush holders, and so on. The bottom line is you don’t need 50 mugs for two people unless you consistently entertain a lot of coffee or tea drinkers!

Kids’ stuff

Although many of us grew up using the same utensils and plates as our parents, there has since been a huge marketing boom for kids’ accessories. These days, kid-friendly dishes abound, made from unbreakable plastic and with grippy bottoms to prevent tipping, and featuring every cartoon or animated character imaginable. The key is to declutter and get rid of these items as soon as your kids outgrow them. These are great to pass along to parent groups if they’re still in good condition. Resist the urge to keep them as sentimental items or for your future grandkids. Just don’t go there.

Fine china

Some of you may have special-occasion china. Perhaps it was gifted to you, it was passed down from grandparents, or you like to collect it. If you use it, then keep it. (And if you’re not using it, consider putting it in regular rotation — even if you chip or break a piece, isn’t it better to use the items as they were intended?) Don’t hold onto fine china only because it was an expensive gift and you feel bad about getting rid of it.

If you keep your china only to display it, then it’s likely not in your kitchen. Display china must be regularly dusted, but if it’s decoration you love, keep it! Just remember that decorations and trinkets can become clutter over time and require constant dusting and reevaluating.

Kitchen knives

The essential knives you need for cooking are usually a chef’s knife, a paring knife, and a bread knife. You may have others as well, but these three are all you really need for nearly any type of cooking. (If you’re a more advanced cook and use specific knives for deboning or cleaving, for example, then those are obviously essential for your kitchen.) If you have more than these three types, or if you have multiples of any of them, consider donating them. If they’re poor quality or broken, discard them. Quality knives are important for avid kitchen cooks, so prioritize quality over quantity.

Cooking utensils

Utensil decluttering depends on what items you use and how often, so we can’t really give you guidance on specific quantities. As you assess what you have, keep in mind the guidance we’ve provided so far — minimize duplicates, get rid of what’s in poor condition, and keep only what you’ve used in the last three to six months. When organizing utensils, make sure they’re within easy reach, as shown in Figure 7-4 .

Plastic bags

Yes, it’s great to have a few plastic bags on hand, but you don’t need to keep every plastic bag that’s ever crossed your path. Keep only a handful of the types you know you’ll use in the next six months. And try to bring your own plastic or reusable bags when you shop, which minimizes your accumulation of them and helps save the environment.

Holiday items

Many people like to celebrate the holidays with themed kitchen items. But just because something fits the criteria for “kitchen” doesn’t mean you need to store the items in the kitchen year-round. Do you have special Christmas tree plates that you use every December? If so, store them with your Christmas decorations instead of your kitchen cupboard. Have special mugs for Halloween apple cider? Store them with your Halloween décor. Be mindful of how many holiday accessories you buy, make sure you consistently use whatever you keep, and get rid of anything you don’t.

Freezer items

The freezer is often where we put food that we’ll use “later.” Freezers can be the biggest clutter traps in any kitchen, especially in North America (in Europe, they don’t have large freezers, so they don’t have this problem of excess). Go through your freezer regularly to identify and toss anything with the dreaded “freezer burn,” and when meal-planning, look into your freezer to identify any items that you can plan meals around so you actually use food as you’ve intended.

At minimum, go through your freezer once per year to remove anything old, expired, or no longer appetizing; we recommend doing it quarterly, in addition to your regular meal-planning forays.

Getting rid of junk drawers forever

Many of you may be thinking that ridding your kitchen of its junk drawer is an impossible feat. We’re not saying you need to get rid of the kitchen drawer catch-all filled with essential items that you grab regularly, such as scissors, tape, and the odd marker to make labels.

On the contrary, we suggest having a drawer like this! Yes, you heard us right! Let’s go back to the words “junk drawer” for a sec, because this space has a bad rap, and rightfully so. Many of you probably remember being a kid and reaching into your kitchen’s junk drawer and finding buried treasures — Pokémon cards you thought you’d lost, that old McDonald’s Happy Meal toy you loved years ago, or five pencils that all needed sharpening. You probably also remember not being able to close those junk drawers, getting sticky fingers from rifling around in them, and worst of all, not actually finding what you needed!

That’s why we say get rid of junk drawers forever, and do not call your kitchen drawer with essentials a junk drawer. If you do, your kids, partners, friends, dog walkers, or even guests will put junk in it. Tame the beast once and for all by renaming this drawer the “essentials drawer.”

To turn your junk drawer into an essentials drawer and keep it that way, do the following:

  • Throw stuff away. As with any other place in your home, review this drawer and toss whatever’s broken, incomplete, or worn out.
  • Separate and group what’s left. As you’re reviewing this drawer’s contents, make piles of like items. After you’re done purging and categorizing, if you have similar piles, decide on the best way to keep them separated. Get dividers, small bins, or plastic containers to help keep everything organized. We find that napkin rings work well for really small items, and dividers for the rest. The goal here is not to move items to another spot in your home. If an item doesn’t go back in the essentials drawer, donate or toss it.
  • Evaluate the drawer regularly. Your household will be inclined to toss all their “junk” into this drawer, especially if they’re used to it being a junk drawer. It’s estimated that 80 percent of stuff in typical junk drawers is junk; hence, the name. Make sure once you change this drawer’s purpose that you don’t let junk back in. Keep this new drawer close to the trash so you can easily purge items.
  • Make it visible. If junk starts to build up again, dump all the drawer’s contents into a see-through container and leave it on your counter. Let your household know it will be in the trash shortly to encourage them to grab any items they want or need. Discard unclaimed items immediately.
  • Get rid of the physical drawer. If all else fails, and you’ve tried everything but failed to tame the junk drawer, consider getting rid of it. Keep a see-through caddie or box on your counter or desk for your essential supplies — this way you’ll constantly be staring at it as a reminder to stay lean and minimal.

Optimizing Your Kitchen for Efficiency

One of the most important ways to make your kitchen a minimalist haven is to make sure that you have only what you need and that your efficiency and accessibility are dialed in. You want items used every day to be conveniently located, and you want everything to go back to its rightful spot after you use it.

Organizing the kitchen can seem like a big job, so don’t try to tackle the task all at once. Choose a single section, such as straightening up the pantry or arranging pots and pans, to get started. Here are some tips that can make organizing your kitchen a little easier:

  • Start by getting rid of what you don’t need: Toss out broken or worn items, from appliances you haven’t fixed to dull kitchen knives that you don’t plan to sharpen. The same goes for outgrown kids’ dishes and cleaning supplies you tried but didn’t like. In the pantry, fridge, and freezer, anything old or unidentifiable goes in the garbage. Say goodbye to expired coupons and untried or unsuccessful recipes. Donate anything that’s still got life left in it.
  • Organize similar items together: Group like-items together, including dishes, utensils, pots and pans, appliances, and cleaning supplies. Arrange pantry, refrigerator, and freezer shelves like supermarket sections.
  • Locate items where you’ll use them: Place appliances, dishes, pots and pans, and utensils closest to their most frequent use, creating one-stop centers to make coffee, cook at the stove, serve meals, package leftovers, and wash dishes. Heavier items can go on lower shelves, while lighter things can be kept in cupboards above the countertop. Move seldom-used items to out-of-the-way cabinets or their deepest corners.
  • Tuck away items you don’t use every day: Move non-everyday items (appliances, cutting boards, knives) off countertops and into cabinets and drawers. Add dividers to drawers to contain their contents by type.
