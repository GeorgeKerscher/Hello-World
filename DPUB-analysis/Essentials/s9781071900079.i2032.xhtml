Job Satisfaction: Trends and Causes

The Conference Board conducts a survey each year of employee job satisfaction. In 2022, they surveyed U.S. employees to track their job satisfaction and factors that contribute to their satisfaction at work. Trends for job satisfaction show an interesting pattern. As shown in Figure 4.1 , overall job satisfaction experienced a negative trend from 1987 to 2010. Job satisfaction recovered from a slump in 2011 and has been rising ever since then. The survey also asked employees how satisfied they were with their current role and their work environment. Job satisfaction is at the highest level since our survey began more than 3 decades ago, largely due to a tight labor market and more flexible work arrangements.

92 The survey also looked at the reasons why people are satisfied with their jobs. Across the majority of 26 aspects of work surveyed, employees with hybrid work arrangements reported the greatest job satisfaction compared to fully remote or fully on-premises workers. Where possible, leaders should continue their support of hybrid work arrangements to keep job satisfaction high. Job satisfaction is the most often-studied work attitude. In this chapter, the concept of attitude is defined, followed by a discussion of why attitudes matter for both individuals and the organizations they work for.

What Is an Attitude?

Captain Jack Sparrow in the movie Pirates of the Caribbean utters, “The problem is not the problem; the problem is your attitude about the problem. Do you understand?” Attitudes have been researched in psychology for many years and are one of the earliest concepts studied. From the outset, researchers maintained that it is possible to measure attitudes but also recognized that attitudes are complex. 1 It has been noted that the concept of attitude is indispensable to the study of social psychology. 2 An attitude is defined as “a psychological tendency that is expressed by evaluating a particular entity with some degree of favor or disfavor.” 3 Attitudes are, thus, a person’s evaluation of something.

Components of an Attitude

93 Attitudes have three components—cognitive, affective, and behavioral 4 —which are supported by research. 5 , 6 The cognitive component of an attitude is a statement of belief about something—for example, “My boss is a mean person” reflects a person’s statement that they believe to be factual. The affective component of an attitude is the emotional part. For example, an affective statement related to the previously stated cognitive component might be “I am angry because my boss is mean.” The behavioral component of an attitude refers to an intention to act based upon cognitions and affect—for example, “I am going to go to the human resources department and report my mean boss.” Figure 4.2 provides another example of these components.

This three-part conceptualization of an attitude helps us understand that attitudes are complicated; it isn’t just that we think something and believe it to be true. We also experience feelings related to our beliefs, and we contemplate taking actions based on them.

94 While this three-part conceptualization is useful in explaining the complexity of attitudes, the three components are closely related and converge. 7 This is useful for a leader to recognize since research shows that behavior does not always follow an attitude. In fact, thoughts and feelings can be changed by changing behaviors first. For example, having followers state their perceptions of the mission of the organization during a team meeting may change their belief about the mission’s importance. Another example is a professor reducing cheating (behavior) by having students read the honor code statement to change their thoughts (“cheating is not okay”) and feelings (“I would be embarrassed if I were caught cheating”). When behaviors don’t line up with thoughts and feelings, cognitive dissonance may occur.

Cognitive Dissonance

Cognitive dissonance is the incompatibility between two or more attitudes or between attitudes and behavior. 8 This creates stress for an individual, and the person will be motivated to resolve the stress by making a change in one (or both) of the other components. Thoughts, feelings, and behaviors need to be aligned. Very few people can completely avoid dissonance in their lives. For example, you may be reminded of this when a child corrects you for swearing because you have told them not to do this in the past. Festinger proposed that the degree to which people are motivated to resolve dissonance is related to the importance of what creates it and how much influence the person has over it. 9 The final motivating element is reward. So, one of the best ways to learn to stop swearing is to have your children remind you because it is important to you and you have influence over what you say. Rewards from swearing are not much, so you are willing to change it to be a positive role model for your child, which is more rewarding.

Here’s a workplace example of cognitive dissonance. Suppose you work for a company that prides itself on promoting sustainability and protecting the environment. During a meeting, you learn that the company’s manufacturing process produces a significant amount of harmful emissions and waste, contradicting corporate values. Since you care about environmental conservation, you experience cognitive dissonance. You value the company’s sustainability mission. But you learned that the company’s actions don’t align with those values. To resolve this dissonance, you try to cope. You could rationalize that the emissions are unavoidable. Or you might convince yourself that the company is actively working on solutions to mitigate its environmental impact. Another coping strategy might be to confront the issue by advocating for changes in the company’s practices to reduce their environmental footprint. However, this could potentially lead to conflict with management or colleagues who are less concerned about sustainability, creating further discomfort. The discrepancy between the company’s stated values and its actual practices creates stress that must be resolved.

Do Attitudes Matter?

95 Work-related attitudes are key outcome variables in organizational behavior (OB) research. In some cases, these same attitudes are employed as predictors. As in social psychology, attitudes have become indispensable to the understanding of people’s reactions to their work and leaders. Thus, attitudes are important in and of themselves. Knowing how satisfied people are with their work or how engaged they are is important because this contributes to their employee well-being. Evidence indicates that job satisfaction is strongly and consistently related to subjective well-being and life satisfaction. 10 A dissatisfied employee may express their negative perceptions to coworkers. When this happens, others may become dissatisfied. Dissatisfied employees may post anonymously on career websites, and this could reduce the number of excellent applicants for jobs. Leaders should be aware of these possible negative outcomes, address dissatisfaction, and convert negative attitudes to positive ones. 11

OB research shows that attitudes are related to behaviors that organizations care about. A review study concluded that job satisfaction and organizational commitment are significantly related to job performance and turnover. 12 Attitudes are one of the most important things for a leader to know about their followers.

Specific attitudes predict specific behaviors. 13 For example, it is better to ask an employee how much they trust the boss rather than how much they trust all the leaders in the organization. The importance of an attitude and the correspondence between the attitude and the behavior increase the prediction of behavior. 14 A review of research examining the link between attitudes and behavior found that how accessible an attitude is makes a difference, as well as how stable the attitude is over time. 15 For example, having direct experience with an attitude such as having a job you love increases the relationship to performance. Also, being asked frequently about an attitude increases the link to behavior (stability). Organizations that implement yearly employee attitude surveys may be increasing the awareness of favorable aspects of the work, and this might decrease turnover.

OB research has identified and researched dozens of work-related attitudes. The following sections discuss the most researched attitudes and how they significantly and positively affect outcomes for organizations.

Job Satisfaction

As suggested in the research conducted by The Conference Board at the beginning of this chapter, most people are satisfied with their jobs, and this is showing an upward trend. Job satisfaction is defined as “a pleasurable or positive emotional state resulting from the appraisal of one’s job or job experience.” 16 Job satisfaction can be measured as an overall (or global) concept. For example, the Hoppock Scale of Job Satisfaction is one of the oldest global measures from OB research dating back to the 1930s. 17

96 Job satisfaction is one part of a person’s reactions to their life resulting in happiness and life satisfaction. 18 Current perspectives on positive OB suggest that it is important for organizations to care about the well-being and health of their employees. 19 Thus, job satisfaction is important because it shows how positive an employee is regarding their work. This often translates into better performance. For example, one study found that frontline employee job satisfaction is significantly related to customer satisfaction and engagement. 20 Another study found that family-supportive organizational cultures were related to job satisfaction and higher performance. Both men and women in the study were more satisfied when their organization supported work–life balance. 21

Research in Action

More Than Job Satisfaction: What’s Your Calling?

The notion of responding to a “calling” means that we find work that fulfills our sense of purpose in life. Have you ever noticed that “What do you do?” is often one of the first questions people ask when they meet someone? Work is a major part of a person’s identity. After all, we spend most of our hours awake doing our work. A calling has been defined as a “consuming, meaningful, passion people experience toward a domain.” 22 One’s calling is related to work engagement and career satisfaction. But what’s the difference between a job, a career, and a calling?

  • Job —A job provides you with pay, benefits, and possibly some social contacts. It’s primarily about earning a paycheck. People with this view of work are typically more invested in their lives outside of the office.
  • Career —You do your job for other people (to provide for your family); a career is what you do for yourself. Career-oriented people are working for money but seek opportunities for promotion.
  • Calling —Those who experience a calling feel a deep alignment between their work and their identity as a person. They are personally and emotionally connected to their work. They are passionate and derive meaning from work.

Employees who feel a higher calling to their work experience attitudes beyond job satisfaction. For example, one study looked at zookeepers. Most have a college degree, but they make less than $25,000 a year. So, it’s not about the money. Also, the work isn’t always pretty. They scrub animal enclosures, clean up waste, and work outside in bad weather. There are few opportunities for promotion. But those who love this work are passionate about it. Most worked as volunteers for years before a zookeeper position became available. The mission of conservation and protecting endangered species motivates them—it’s their calling. Many zookeepers feel the work is their destiny. It isn’t just a job.

The COVID-19 pandemic created changes in the long-term demand for some occupations (e.g., health care and technology), and this affects the social recognition and individuals’ perception of these occupations positively. This is important not just because occupational status affects attraction into some occupations but also because occupational status is related to the meaningful work and calling that individuals perceive different occupations to have. Changes in the status of these occupations can alter individuals’ perceptions regarding the positive meaning of work, meaning making through work, and the greater good motivations associated with work.

97 Having a calling has many positive benefits, including high performance and a sense of fulfillment. However, researchers also warn that it may lead to workaholism and burnout. A study of 232 employees in Germany found that living a calling is not just about finding work that fits the call. People with a calling are more likely to have jobs with more resources, such as decision-making autonomy, task significance, and social support at work. So, it’s important to be sure that you have the resources in the job you choose to live your calling.

Job satisfaction may change over time. A study of 132 newcomers with data collected at four periods showed a curvilinear pattern for job satisfaction, such that satisfaction increased after they started their jobs and then decreased as they settled into their jobs at the 1-year point. 23 A research study of new employees earning a college or graduate degree in a recession or an economic boom found that the recession had lasting effects on job satisfaction. Across three studies, well-educated graduates who entered the workforce during economic downturns were more satisfied with their current jobs than those who entered during more prosperous economic times. 24 Another study conducted for an even longer time—40 years—examined survey data from 21,670 participants in nationally representative samples. Results found interesting patterns for age and organization tenure. People appeared to become less satisfied as their tenure within a given organization increased. However, as people became older and moved to different organizations, their satisfaction increased. This finding is, in part, explained by pay increases over time. 25 However, research has found that changing to a completely different occupation may result in decreases in job satisfaction that last up to 6 years. 26 What appears to be important is the degree to which a person’s career interests fit the job they have chosen. A review study found that this interest fit predicts job satisfaction, and interest fit is related to performance satisfaction with one’s overall career path. 27

98 Job satisfaction has been examined across cultures. Typically, studies seek to understand whether U.S.-based models of job satisfaction hold in other cultures. For example, a study of over 70,000 employees in four cultural regions (Asia, Europe, North America, and Latin America) found employees’ sense of achievement from work was related to job satisfaction in all regions. 28 A review of cross-cultural research on job satisfaction concluded that the relationship of job satisfaction to performance in a number of other cultures was similar to that in the United States (the review included studies from India, Poland, Australia, Canada, Israel, and South Africa). 29

Other demographic differences in job satisfaction have been explored. For example, research on differences between generations (older workers compared to younger workers) has not found any consistent differences in job satisfaction. 30 Research has also found that women are more satisfied with their jobs than are men, despite income disparities and fewer opportunities for promotion in some cases—which has been called the “gender paradox.” 31 A groundbreaking study examining employees who were transitioning collected data before and after sex reassignment surgery. This study revealed that trans employees were more satisfied with their work during and after transitioning. 32

Global job satisfaction measures assess a worker’s overall attitude toward their job by asking employees to rate statements like “All in all, I am satisfied with my job.” 33 Facet satisfaction measures, on the other hand, assess a worker’s attitude toward specific attributes of their job. 34

Job Satisfaction Facets

It is recognized that it is possible for a person to be satisfied with one aspect of their work but dissatisfied with others. In other words, an employee might love the work they do but dislike their gossiping coworkers, for example. Thus, measures of facet job satisfaction have been developed. One of the most widely known measures of facet satisfaction is the Job Descriptive Index (JDI). 35 This measure includes different scales that measure attitudes about various aspects of the work experience: pay, promotions, supervision, coworkers, and the work itself. Examples of items from the JDI are shown in Table 4.1 . Research has suggested the strongest relationship of these facets to overall job satisfaction is the work itself, followed by supervision and coworker satisfaction. While it may be surprising, satisfaction with pay has the lowest relationship to overall job satisfaction. 36 , 37 This topic is discussed further later, but you will learn more about the impact of extrinsic rewards such as pay on motivation in Chapter 9. 99 If work is the most important component of job satisfaction, then you may be wondering what the relationship is between pay and job satisfaction. In a review study including over 90 samples, researchers found that pay was only weakly related to job satisfaction. In fact, employees who were highly paid were just as satisfied as those who made less. Once a person reaches an income level where they can live comfortably, the relationship between income and job satisfaction goes away. This is known as income satiation , which means that there is a point at which higher income does not result in higher satisfaction. A recent study examined pay over a wider range and found that the relationship between pay and job satisfaction may be more complex. Researchers obtained data from 25,465 working adults in the United States using the company rating website www.careerbliss.com. They found that income and job satisfaction had a significant curvilinear relationship such that people began reporting decreased pay satisfaction above income levels of $260,000. 38 These findings suggest that income is not always related to job satisfaction once we achieve a comfortable standard of living but that when we attain very high salaries, we become more dissatisfied. Another study found that, as income rises, the work gets more difficult, and this offsets the satisfaction employees experience. 39 Another explanation for this may be that as employees are promoted and earn higher salaries they compare themselves to others more and pay more attention to their pay relative to others rather than the amount they make. 40

100 The positive effects of job satisfaction are clear. A review including over 300 samples showed that job satisfaction is significantly and positively related to performance. 41 But what happens when employees are dissatisfied?

Responses to Job Dissatisfaction

Dissatisfaction with work produces four possible responses that are summarized in Figure 4.3 . As shown in this figure, these responses can either be active or passive . 42 Thus, the employee can do something about it or choose not to respond in an active way. The second dimension is whether the response is constructive or destructive . The employee who is dissatisfied can respond by trying to do something positive or negative about the situation. There are thus four reactions shown in the figure, and their definitions follow:

  • 101 Exit. The employee can search for another job and leave. This response is active and destructive. Exiting the organization is a way that employes remove themselves from the situation.
  • Voice. The employee can discuss their dissatisfaction with their supervisor, making suggestions for improvement. This is an active and constructive response to being dissatisfied. There are a number of ways that employees can express their voice, such as meetings with management, suggestion boxes, or employee feedback surveys.
  • Loyalty. The employee can wait for the situation to improve, showing loyalty and trust in the management to address it in time. This is a passive response, but it is constructive. Employees remain loyal despite their dissatisfaction because they may believe in the organization’s mission, value relationships with colleagues, or trust their boss.
  • Neglect. The employee allows the situation to get worse and may be late or absent from work and put in less effort on the job. This is a passive response that is destructive. An employee might arrive late to work, miss deadlines, or show a lack of interest during meetings.

This framework is also known as the EVLN (Exit–Voice–Loyalty–Neglect) approach to job dissatisfaction. Research has shown that voice leads to turnover—when employees speak out about their concerns, they are more likely to quit. However, this research also found that leaders who allowed participation and provided support were able to prevent turnover from occurring. 43 This effect is even more pronounced when leaders and followers agree on the extent to which followers voice their concerns. By leaders having open discussions with followers regarding what makes their input valuable, followers can get their concerns addressed. This may also avoid potential negative consequences for being out of sync with follower perceptions and the destructive responses of exit or neglect. 44 When faced with a dissatisfying situation, employees with positive relationships with their boss are less likely to engage in exit and neglect and are more likely to be loyal. However, the use of voice may depend on how many other avenues employees have for expressing their concerns, such as sharing with coworkers, the human resources department, and others outside of the organization. They may even skip a level and speak to a manager higher up in the organization. For followers, it isn’t just a matter of speaking up (voice); it is also a matter of being heard by the boss. 45

The previous section reviews research that shows that attitudes make a difference on the job. But what happens when a person is looking for a job? The next section discusses how having a positive attitude about oneself and the job search process can lead to a higher degree of success in finding a great job.

Job Search Attitudes

102 OB research has shown that the job search is like a “roller coaster,” with ups and downs in attitudes during the process. 46 Perceived progress, experienced affect, and the belief that a job will be found may vary on a day-to-day basis during the job search process. In one study, individuals were asked about how confident they were that they would find another job. The researchers asked questions such as the following:

  1. Will you find a job if you look?
  2. Will you get a good-paying job?
  3. Will you find a job that you like?
  4. Will you land a job as good as or better than the one you left?

It is important to keep a positive attitude and maintain a positive self-image during a job search. A longitudinal study of 709 graduating Chinese university students found that their perceptions of their own employability (for example, “I achieve high grades in relation to my studies”) is related to positive job search attitudes and intensity. 47 Another study found that career adaptability was related to university graduates’ beliefs that they would have a great career and their success in finding a job. Career adaptability has been defined as “a psychosocial construct that denotes an individual’s resources for coping with current and anticipated tasks, transitions, [and] traumas in their occupational roles.” 48 It is composed of the adaptive resources of concern, control, curiosity, and confidence (known as the Four Cs). 49 Additional research has shown that organizations should focus on motivational (e.g., career involvement) or environmental (e.g., career opportunities) career resources because they relate most strongly to career success and career satisfaction. To enhance career success, promoting knowledge and skills as resources is critical, for example, by encouraging employees to take training courses. 50

A review of 90 studies found that career adaptability is significantly associated with optimism, career planning, and improved attitudes such as organizational commitment and life satisfaction. 51 Keep in mind, however, that some forms of flexibility may have a downside with respect to finding a good career match. Being too flexible may result in poor person–job fit, so it’s important to balance flexibility with finding the best match. 52

You are looking for the best fit for you, so don’t obsess over the interviews and job offers that your classmates are talking about. Some people develop job search envy , which is defined as “a situational form of envy specific to the job search domain.” 53 Being envious of the success of others, combined with time pressure, resulted in job applicants committing résumé fraud (e.g., making false claims, overstating information, and even inventing degrees and work experience). The frustration of a job search is understandable, but a word of caution is to not compare yourself to others during the process. What’s #Trending in OB?

Your Attitude May Derail Your Job Search

Looking for a job can be frustrating. You spend hours completing applications and waiting for responses. You get some interviews and then wait again to be called back, and you may get rejection letters. The process can make you feel negative at a time when staying positive is essential. Employers want to see enthusiastic applicants.

103 John Liptak has developed a measure of job search attitudes called the Job Search Attitude Inventory (JSAI). These attitudes include the following:

  • Luck vs. Planning: How much do you rely on luck or careful planning to find a job?
  • Uninvolved vs. Involved: How involved are you with your search, such as learning new job search techniques and working hard at it?
  • Help From Others vs. Self-Help: How much do you depend on outside agencies for help in finding a job (such as your university’s career placement center)?
  • Passive vs. Active: How much control do you think you have in your job search? Will you find a job through your own efforts?
  • Pessimistic vs. Optimistic: How positive are you about being able to find a job?

A review of the research on job search attitudes found that attitudes toward the job search process, such being positive, being committed, believing that you will get a job, and lower anxiety about the search, are related to the ability of individuals to landing a quality job. Job seeking demands effort and resources until employment is found, so these attitudes make a difference.

Your attitudes may manifest themselves in how you conduct yourself during the job search. Here is some advice for keeping your attitude during your job search on track:

  1. Be polite. People don’t appreciate arrogant behavior. Remember to say “please” and “thank you.”
  2. “Dress for success.” Make sure you are presentable when you might be in contact with a potential employer or someone who could help you get a job.
  3. Be aware of your nonverbal behavior matters. Try not to look down at the floor or frown.
  4. Be outgoing. Don’t view networking as only formal, arranged events. Seize every opportunity you can to make connections with people who might provide job leads.
  5. Follow up. Be sure to end with, “It was great meeting you.” After an interview, be sure to send thank-you notes to everyone you spoke with.
  6. Don’t appear desperate. People will want to help you, but don’t try to get sympathy. Don’t say, “I really need this job.”
  7. Keep your composure. If you are angry about how you were treated unfairly in your last job or graded unfairly by professors, don’t talk about it. Focus on the positive things.

It may be important to also keep an open mind about using social networking during job search (e.g., LinkedIn). In recent years, the number of job seekers who find jobs through this method has increased. Job seekers can benefit from connecting with both strong (i.e., friends and family members) and weak (i.e., acquaintances and referrals) social network relationships. Social media networking may result in more leads and job offers.

Be ready to experience rejection and frustration during a job search. Develop strategies for maintaining a positive attitude during the process.

After you are hired, work-related attitudes are important because they reflect an employee’s reactions to work and serve as an important barometer of how well the organization is attending to employee needs. Organizational Commitment

105 Organizational commitment is another work-related attitude that has proven to be important in OB. Reviews (including several meta-analyses) have shown that organizational commitment relates to turnover. 54 , 55 , 56 OB research has also shown that people who are not committed to their jobs are absent more often, are less motivated, and perform at lower levels. 57 Organizational commitment is a psychological state that describes an employee’s relationship with their organization and a propensity to continue the relationship with the organization. 58 It links an individual to the organization because of their identification with the organization’s values and goals. 59 A three-component model of organizational commitment captures different aspects of this work attitude. 60 First, affective commitment refers to an employee’s emotional attachment to an organization (they stay because they care about the organization and are loyal to it). Second, continuance commitment is the degree to which an employee is aware of the costs of leaving the organization (they stay because they are not able to leave). Third, normative commitment is the moral obligation to stay with the organization (they stay because it is the right thing to do). A review of research in the tourism industry where turnover is very high found that organizational commitment was negatively related turnover. 61 Employees that are more committed to the organization are less likely to engage in organizational deviance (e.g., taking overly long breaks, intentionally poor work quality) and interpersonal deviance (e.g., gossiping about peers, making fun of others). 62 Organizational commitment may be affected by the economy. One study found that after the Great Recession of 2008, employees experienced higher job insecurity, which led to lower organizational commitment. 63 Leaders need to pay attention to the potential negative effects of economic downturns on those who remain employed. Employees who are stressed and coping with personal and work problems may become less loyal to the organization if they feel they are not supported.

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered a severe shock, economic crisis, and a potential future crisis. Many organizations had to downsize, which created stress among employees. Many employees worried about their job security during the crisis. Research during the pandemic showed that employee perceptions of job security were significantly related to organizational commitment when leaders were benevolent and communicated that jobs were safe. 64

Job Involvement

Job involvement is how much an employee identifies with their job and views their performance at work as an essential part of their self-esteem. Job involvement has been related to employee turnover, 65 organizational citizenship, and job performance. 66 A study of volunteers found that those with higher social responsibility (following correct ideas, complying with justice, and dedication and sacrifice) are more likely to experience high job involvement. 67

The COVID-19 pandemic presented challenges for organizations to keep employees involved in their work, as many had to work remotely from home. A research study found that when employees work remotely they disconnect from the workplace. Organizations can generate higher job involvement by supportive supervisors who convey a higher perception of hope. 68 And the challenges didn’t end when workers returned to work after the lockdowns. An interesting study was conducted in Wuhan, China, which was the epicenter of the COVID-19 pandemic, and where the workers struggled when they returned to work in May 2020. Researchers learned that job reattachment (e.g., mental preparation for work) significantly predicted job engagement after workers returned to work. 69

106 By combining organizational commitment and job involvement, we can better understand the relationship of these variables to employee withdrawal behaviors (absenteeism and turnover). 70 As Figure 4.4 shows, when organizational commitment and job involvement are both high, employees can be viewed as institutionalized “Stars” because their efforts are focused on both the task and the group to which they belong. When both organizational commitment and job involvement are low, employees are “Apathetics” because they don’t put forth much effort and are not concerned about the team. The other two quadrants represent interesting scenarios in which “Lone Wolves” are involved with their jobs to a high degree and have a task focus, but they are not concerned about the team. They prefer to “go it alone” and are more likely to leave the organization than the final group, “Corporate Citizens.” Corporate Citizens are not focused on the task, but they do attend to the maintenance of the team. They may not be Star performers, but they are loyal. The figure also indicates suggestions for what aspects of satisfaction are important for each type of employee. For example, the Corporate Citizen is most concerned with coworker satisfaction to maintain their organizational commitment. In contrast, if the leader has a Lone Wolf on their team, they should focus more on the satisfaction with the work itself, working conditions, and pay to avoid absenteeism and turnover.

Employee engagement has emerged as an important concept due to research conducted by both OB professors and consultants. Engagement appears to be distinct from job involvement and adds to our understanding of the relationships of attitudes such as job satisfaction.

Employee Engagement

Employee engagement is related to job involvement and enthusiasm for the work performed. 71 Engagement has been defined as “the investments of an individual’s complete self into a role.”

A study of 245 firefighters and their supervisors found that engagement plays a key role in the relationship between perceived organizational support (POS ; discussed in the next section) and job performance. This study included job involvement, but engagement explained additional variance in performance. A large-scale study of 7,939 business units in 36 companies found that engagement was related to customer satisfaction, productivity, profit, employee turnover, and safety (fewer accidents). 72 Improving employee engagement may increase business-unit outcomes including profit since disengaged employees cost organizations due to low motivation, poor customer service, and higher turnover.

107 Gallup now estimates that these actively disengaged employees cost the global economy $8.8 trillion, which is 9% of the global GDP, and they note that this is enough to make the difference between success and failure of humanity. 73 Gallup conducted a review of research using their engagement measure (the Q12), where they looked at 263 studies, including 49,928 business and work units with almost 1.4 million employees. Gallup researchers statistically analyzed business- and work-unit-level relationships between employee engagement and performance outcomes. Four years later, this review was repeated, and the results were consistent with the prior analysis. Median differences between top-quartile and bottom-quartile units were 10% in customer loyalty ratings, 21% in profitability, 20% in sales production, −24% in turnover (high-turnover organizations), −59% in turnover (low-turnover organizations), −70% in safety incidents, −28% in shrinkage, −41% in absenteeism, −58% in patient safety incidents, and −40% in quality (defects). 74 An updated review of research on employee engagement concluded that research interest has increased in recent years and confirmed the prior findings linking engagement to employee performance. 75

In 2022, Gallup conducted surveys of the world’s adult population covering more than 160 countries and areas (112,416 employed respondents). In 2022, engagement surged to the highest level since Gallup began the survey (23% of the world’s employees were engaged at work). The global trend is shown in Figure 4.5 . Engagement declined in 2020 (likely due to the pandemic), but it has now returned to its positive trend. In the United States, engagement was 34% of the employed respondents in the survey. 76

Given these positive findings, many organizations are implementing formal engagement programs. The Aberdeen Group, a Boston-based research organization that identifies best-in-class practices by working with industry practitioners, conducted a study that compared companies that have engagement programs with those that don’t. Their analysis found that companies that have a formal engagement program reduce the loss of customers due to better responsiveness. In fact, customer referrals increased. Additional outcomes of engagement were higher revenues, sales teams meeting their quotas more often, and improved cost savings. 77 A study conducted by the HR.com Research Institute also asked employers about the benefits of having engaged employees. The results are shown in Figure 4.6 . As the figure shows, engagement improves customer service, well-being, company brand, retention, productivity, quality, and financial performance. One of the most striking findings is that 26% of the HR professionals surveyed said that engagement has a very high degree of impact on retention. In other words, engaged employees are less likely to quit. 78 These findings mirror those of previous research on engagement supporting the importance of employee engagement at work.

109 Engaged employees feel valued by their organization. A longitudinal panel study found employee perceptions of how much they were valued by the organization were related to changes in affective commitment. 79 Also, the resources employees feel that they have at their job positively relate to engagement. A research study found that three job resources in particular relate to engagement: performance feedback, social support from colleagues, and supervisory coaching. 80 Another study found that resources of supervisor support, innovativeness, appreciation, and organizational climate mattered even more when the demands of a job were high. 81 Thus, employees respond positively to the work environment when they feel their supervisors and organization support them. This attitude—POS—is discussed next.

Perceived Organizational Support

An emerging line of research suggests employees pay attention to whether the organization values their contributions and cares about their well-being—otherwise known as perceived organizational support (POS). 82 Organizational justice and fair rewards are important to the perception of organizational support. 83 A review of over 70 studies of POS indicated that there are three major categories of beneficial treatment: fairness, supervisor support, and organizational rewards along with favorable job conditions. 84 A more recent review concluded that recent findings indicate that the average level of POS has modestly increased over the past 3 decades in the United States. Furthermore, cross-cultural differences have emerged: POS appears to have stronger positive outcomes in Eastern cultures than Western cultures. 85

The supervisor also plays an important role in POS, and it is important for employees to feel that they have a voice in decisions. 86 Employees with higher POS engage in more helping behavior and are less likely to show up late for work. 87 A review of studies found strong positive effects of POS on job satisfaction and organizational commitment, a moderate positive effect on employee performance, and a strong negative effect on intention to leave. 88 An updated review reached the same conclusions: POS is an important link between various types of favorable treatment by the organization and employees’ positive feelings about the organization, psychological well-being, and performance. 89 The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted the way that people could do their work. A U.K. study conducted during the pandemic found that POS and supervisor support was associated with more positive changes in employees’ job-related well-being. This effect was further explained by the degree of employees’ affective organizational commitment. 90

More than 25 years of research suggests that POS appears to be distinct from other attitudes and adds to understanding of why some employees perform at higher levels than others. Here are eight evidence-based tactics for increasing POS in organizations to enhance employee engagement: 91

  1. Implement supportive workforce services that are discretionary—“Don’t just do the things you are required to do.”
  2. Be fair and equitable in the making, monitoring, and enforcement of all management practices.
  3. Set achievable goals and reward proportionately.
  4. 110 Offer individualized benefits—“Learn and provide the type of support your workers and workforce need.”
  5. Support supervisors so they will foster POS in their subordinates.
  6. Train subordinates to be supportive.
  7. Promote strong social networks.
  8. Begin organizational support prior to the start of employment.

Research on POS indicates that employees respond positively when they feel the organization values them. Such employees want to have a voice in decisions that affect them. Feeling a sense of power to have an impact at work is another work attitude that has been researched in recent years, with results indicating strong relationships to other work attitudes and organizational effectiveness. The next section discusses the research findings for psychological empowerment.

Psychological Empowerment

Psychological empowerment refers to “intrinsic task motivation manifested in a set of four cognitions reflecting an individual’s orientation to their work role: competence, impact, meaning, and self-determination.” 92 These four cognitions are defined as follows:

  • Meaning —How much work goals align with your personal standards (i.e., how well the work “fits” your values)
  • Competence (or self-efficacy)—Your belief in your capabilities to show mastery in your work role
  • Self-Determination —The degree to which you feel that you have a choice in your work and autonomy to carry it out according to your own preferences
  • Impact —Refers to how much you believe that you can influence important work outcomes (e.g., administrative policies at work)

111 Research has shown that psychological empowerment is positively related to managerial effectiveness, innovation, 93 and organizational commitment. 94 , 95 A review study found that psychological empowerment is positive, strong, and significant. 96 Empowerment is related to lower stress as well. 97 Meaning is the driver of psychological empowerment; however, all four components make unique contributions to outcomes. For example, competence is most related to ratings of managerial effectiveness. Today, more than 70% of organizations have adopted empowerment initiatives. 98 It is important for leaders to allow their followers to experience meaning in their work but also feel that they have an impact. Also, leaders should coach followers to develop their sense of competence and allow them discretion in how they do their work. A study of leaders who coached their teams found that team members felt more empowered, and this translated into higher team performance. 99 A review of leadership style found that leaders who are empowering and transformational impacted the psychological empowerment of their employees. Further, servant leaders (those that express a strong desire to serve the needs of their followers) also had more empowered followers. 100

As noted previously, leaders can develop their followers’ feelings of empowerment. They may also create positive attitudes through developing a sense of meaning with respect to the work performed. By creating a sense of meaning, leaders may be able to activate other positive attitudes about work and improve employee motivation.

Leadership Implications: Creating Meaning at Work

Positive attitudes give people a sense that their work has meaning. Over time, individuals who feel committed to their career derive more meaning from their work and are more satisfied with their jobs. As you learned in the Research in Action box, these individuals believed that they were living a “calling” rather than going to work for money every day. 101
