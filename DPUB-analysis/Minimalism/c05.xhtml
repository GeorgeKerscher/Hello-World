Restoring Simplicity to Getting Dressed

When you think about your closet and your clothes, the word “minimalism” may conjure images of a monochromatic wardrobe containing few tops, even fewer bottoms, and two pairs of shoes. And that type of spare closet stock works for some people — it certainly makes it easier to assemble an outfit for a casual dinner with friends, an afternoon cheering from the sidelines of the soccer pitch, or a day in the office.

Our take on clothing-related minimalism is a bit more expansive — we don’t feel a minimalist wardrobe is exclusively about having less. We think it’s about being intentional — figuring out what you like, getting rid of what doesn’t serve your needs well, and wearing everything you own. A curated closet means you have just what you need (and love!) and not a closet full of clothes that never see the light of day.

Making sure to wear what you own and avoid buying what you don’t need is possible when your closet is well-organized. Being able to see all that you have makes it quick and easy to assemble outfits creatively so your look feels fresh regardless of the amount of contents in your closet.

Understanding How an Overstuffed Closet Wastes Your Time

One reason to take a minimalist approach to your closet contents is to help you be as efficient as possible when deciding what to wear. How many hours have you wasted looking in your closet for something to wear, struggling to find the top you have in mind, failing to find the pants you know you own, or digging through piles of clean clothes you haven’t put away yet because you’ve run out of shelf space or hangers? Whether your closet is smaller or larger, a lot of stuff makes it harder to find what you’re looking for.

So it’s useful to be aware of how the volume of the clothes you own and how they’re stored are affecting your time. The best way to do this is to track this time for a week. You don’t have to track everything you do in the closet, but simply track your daily closet habits. Here are some tips to get started:

  1. Track how much time in the morning you spend picking an outfit or finding the desired clothes in your closet, and record it on your phone’s Notes app, a piece of paper you’ve stashed nearby, or your kid’s Etch-a-Sketch. Make sure to count time spent rifling through drawers, cubbies, or baskets trying to find an item you can’t locate.
  2. Record the time you spend on the following activities: deciding what to wear, looking for a specific item, re-ironing or re-washing, changing outfits, and packing for travel. This step identifies which actions are the biggest time-wasters, which will help you figure out what to focus on first.
  3. Analyze which areas of the closet take the most time or cause you the most stress. You’ll likely find you’re spending the most time on the areas that have the most items and/or are most cluttered.

If you find you’re spending the most time wondering what to wear, you may not be able to easily see the clothes you own. If you’re spending a lot of time looking for an item you have in mind, then your clothes may not be organized very well. If your hanging clothes are often wrinkled, you may have too many in too small a space, which can cause wrinkles. A pared-back wardrobe and a well-organized closet will help with each of these time-wasters.

Creating Your Definition of a Minimalist Wardrobe

In the past, you may have found yourself shopping unnecessarily because you had no clue what was hiding in a messy closet (no judgment — this happens to all of us!). Or maybe you bought unnecessary items because you didn’t really know what types of clothes worked (and didn’t work) for you, and you didn’t know your personal style. A thoughtful, minimalist wardrobe is one based on pieces that look good on you, that you feel good wearing, and that allow you to dress appropriately for your life’s activities.

To help you pare down your closet and keep a minimalist wardrobe, start by assessing what types of clothes you need so you pare back the right amount and in the right places.

Figuring out what clothes you really need

Finding the right balance of clothes — the right amount and type that you use daily for your lifestyle so you have the variety you want and can dress for the various occasions in your life — can be trickier than it looks, and you might only get there through some trial and error. Finding a balance between what you should or want to wear and what you actually do wear is difficult.

Start by reviewing your lifestyle and determining your clothing needs. Accounting for both your work and personal life, what do you need to wear the most? If you’re a businessperson, you may need a lot of business casual outfits. If you’re in a creative field, you may need lots of casual tops and bottoms with some funky pieces for interest. If you’re a stay-at-home parent or student, you may primarily need activewear and clothing built for comfort.

Finding the right clothing balance

The idea of an uncluttered, airy closet — where hangers don’t touch, stacks stay perfectly aligned, and nothing threatens to topple — is a glorious thing. Or terrifying, if you’re used to a wardrobe that covers every occasion and keeps you ready for the occasional obscure but intriguing social invitation. So we’re not prescribing what “minimalist” means — instead, we want to help you figure that out.

As you define what “minimalist wardrobe” means for you, think about the following:

  • Having fewer pieces means fewer decisions to make each morning. This can lower your decision fatigue overall.
  • The less you have, the less you need to organize. And if you’re worried about having a small closet, you may realize that it gives you all the storage you need for your pared-back wardrobe.
  • Fewer pieces means you’re more able to hang and shelve everything within sight. You’re less likely to overlook pieces if nothing’s tucked under a stack or behind seasonal items, and you’re more likely to wear all the clothes you own.

  • Having too few pieces may mean you don’t always have the right clothes for the occasion. This is where trial and error comes in as you find the right balance of too little and too much.

    We once knew someone who decided to only wear black pants and white button-down shirts every day, and they had seven of each. Along with some workout clothes and lounge wear, that’s all they owned. They looked appropriate no matter the event — and they said the relief from making daily clothing decisions was significant. So “too few pieces” to some is “just right” to others. What works for you is what’s important.

  • Having fewer clothes means you’ll probably do laundry more often. But smaller loads and less to fold!

If you’re thinking of trying a very lean approach, consider examining your discard pile and holding back some clothes that you still like. Tuck them into a box or even a trash bag and move them out of your closet for a few weeks — and see if the clothes you’ve retained are just the right amount. If not, go “shopping” in your held-back discards and pick some more items to retain.

Auditing Your Closet and Sorting the Keepers from the Clingers

After sussing out your biggest get-ready time-wasters (see “Understanding How an Overstuffed Closet Wastes Your Time”), and thinking about how minimal you want your wardrobe to be (see “ Finding the right clothing balance ”), you’re ready to plan your purge so you can achieve minimalist nirvana.

Auditing your closet allows you to know what you have. Closets are places where you keep stuff, but they can also be places where you lose track of your stuff. As you start your minimalist closet journey, auditing your closet helps you remember what you have, and identify what has or hasn’t worked for you. Once you know what you have and what’s worked, you can more easily identify where to start paring down and can target some obvious items to discard.

A closet audit as the first step in adopting a minimalist wardrobe isn’t a purging exercise, it’s a way to discover your style and to enable you to make future fashion choices in the context of your minimalist commitment. (Don’t get us wrong. You’ll still need to purge, but that happens after you get a solid idea of what you have and what you need). Instead, use the audit to really understand what kind of clothes work for you and discover your personal style.

To get started, take all your clothes out of the closet and dump them in a pile on your bed. If this sounds overwhelming and messy, don’t worry; you’ll love your closet much more after you’ve gone through the process.

As British novelist John Galsworthy commented, “Beginnings are always messy,” but you’ve got this.

The following steps guide you through the process and, hopefully, make it less daunting.

  1. Set aside time on a day that you’re not too busy.

    You’ll need a few hours, maybe three or four.

  2. Get a notebook or use the notes app on your smartphone to take notes.

    You can use them to document your style discoveries and findings during the process.

  3. Set a relaxing atmosphere.

    For example, put on some good music or stream a favorite TV show in the background.

  4. Take all your clothes out of your closet and put them in a pile on your bed. If this sounds overwhelming and messy, you can start small. See the sidebar “ When getting started feels overwhelming ” for pointers.
  5. Sort your clothes into three piles: No’s; Yes’s; and Maybes:
    • The No pile: Think about why you don’t like or wear these clothes, especially if they’re still in good condition; you want to understand why they didn’t work for you. Are you bothered by the cut of the pants (maybe you favor low-rise over high-rise pants, for example), or is the quality of a specific piece (or even an entire brand) not as high as you prefer? (Lower-quality items may not wash as well as items of higher-quality, so they may not last as long.) Whatever the reason, take a physical or a mental note to not buy these types of clothes again. The No pile contains clothes you’re probably going to get rid of. But don’t throw your “no-go” clothes in the trash! The section “ Getting Rid of Your Discards and Keeping Your Closet Minimalist ” tells you how to donate and dispose of clothes responsibly.

    • The Yes pile: The clothes in this pile are probably the ones you wear the most, and they give you an indication of your personal style. Note your specific preferences: for example, the length of the skirts, the cut of the pants, the colors of the clothes, and so on.

      You may be tempted to put some pieces in the Yes pile that don’t really belong there. These are pieces that you have some kind of emotional attachment to. Maybe they were expensive, or you hope to wear them in the future. When contemplating these “are they or aren’t they” potential Yes’s, try them on, see how you feel in them, consider where you’ll wear the pieces, think about when you last wore them, and ask yourself whether they fit with your lifestyle.

    • The Maybe pile: These are clothes that may have potential. Try styling them or, if they’re in season, try wearing them that week to see how you feel about them.

Phew! Now that you’re done auditing your closet, you can begin to sort the mess. Put the clothes in the Yes and Maybe piles back in your closet. (The section titled “ Storing Smart So You Can Grab and Go ” helps you do that properly.) The clothes in the No pile should be donated, given to friends and family, or sold (more on this in “ Getting Rid of Your Discards and Keeping Your Closet Minimalist ” later in this chapter). Put them in a box or container. Work on getting them a new home as soon as you can.

Have a system in place to recognize clothes you aren’t wearing. This helps you continue to be intentional with what you own, and will make another closet audit less stressful next time. A good system is the reverse hanger method. Reverse your hangers to face out instead of in (they’ll feel backwards), and when you wear a piece, turn the hanger to face in (the usual way you’d hang it). Regularly review your hangers to identify if any items aren’t being worn and should be removed from your wardrobe.

WHEN GETTING STARTED FEELS OVERWHELMING

The idea of reviewing every item of clothing you own and deciding what to do with each may feel overwhelming, especially if you have a full closet. Our advice to get started is simple: Push your resistance to the side, dive in, and build your minimalist momentum as you go.

Here are a few strategies to make it less intimidating to get started:

  • Start small — categorize your closet and begin with one section rather than attacking your entire closet at once. For example, start by assessing and paring back your shoes, then your handbags or belts, and then your work attire. This process helps you avoid that panicky feeling of getting rid of much of your closet and then feeling like you have nothing left to wear. Tackling categories can also help give you a sense of accomplishment when you complete each category.
  • Envision the rewards of not only how much time you’ll save in your closet but how much better you’ll feel with the carefully selected pieces that remain. Having a mindfully curated closet helps you feel appreciative of the items you own. It helps reduce decision fatigue because you know every piece looks good on you and fits. Knowing exactly what you have will also encourage you to take better care of the clothes you decide to keep.
  • Avoid lapsing into old behaviors and immediately replacing items due to “empty closet syndrome.” Paring back and decluttering your closet can make you feel lighter and more organized, but it won’t immediately change your purchasing habits. Make sure you stay mindful of your new minimalist habits to avoid impulsive purchases post–closet overhaul.
  • Commit to regularly evaluating your closet and eliminating items that don’t fit your minimalist wardrobe vision. Your commitment to constant assessment (versus annual clean-out binges) will help you keep track of new purchases, frequently identify what isn’t getting worn (and can be eliminated), and avoid unneeded purchases where you think you have gaps to fill but really just forgot what all you owned.
  • Determine a strategy for how to make minimalist assessments a habit. For example, schedule quarterly wardrobe review sessions with yourself to stay on track.

Organizing Closets of Different Sizes

You’re probably thinking that a small closet is easier to audit and maintain, but this isn’t necessarily the case. Larger spaces let you see more of the items inside, which helps you be intentional about wearing what you own and keeping your wardrobe minimal. On the flip side, larger closets can also be why you accumulate so much stuff and feel the need to fill the space (decidedly not minimal). The moral of this paragraph is that closet space alone is not the reason you accumulate too much stuff.

If you have a small closet, don’t get discouraged, as there are plenty of ways to streamline and organize the space to make it seem bigger. The first step when maximizing your closet space is to audit what you have and pare back to just what you need. Then with a few products, installations, and decorating tips, every closet can be the perfect size.

Evaluating your space

While you may dream of 500-square-foot closet spaces, adding closet space isn’t the best approach to minimizing. Less space naturally forces you to be more conscious of what you’re putting into that space. Evaluate your closet to make sure it fits the clothes you want to keep and that your existing clothes have a well-designed home. You want your space to reflect your needs and optimize your efficiency. Evaluate whether you need to add or change the space to make it easier to grab items, or add another section if there is not enough room.

As you evaluate your closet, regardless of its size, consider if you have enough hanging space for the items you keep on hangers. Do you have enough space for all of your shirts and skirts? Do you have enough long space to hang pants or dresses? Do you need more shelving for the items you prefer to fold, such as sweaters, shorts, and workout gear? If not, research how you could add more room to hang or fold your items. Closet organization is a big business with do-it-yourself packages available at home improvement stores or Ikea, and you can find many professional consultants and custom builders if you’ve got a bigger closet budget.

Organizing a walk-in closet

Walk-in closets can vary in shape and size depending on your home’s layout. If you do have the chance to design your closet, it’s a great time to work in your specific lifestyle clothing choices and build around them. If you need to work with the existing size and layout (yep, that’s most of us), then finding the right balance of rods, shelving, and storage options like baskets and drawers is important. For example, if you hang most of your clothes, make sure to have enough rods and not too much shelving (which could prompt you to buy more if the shelves look empty, thus sabotaging your minimalist goal). Typical walk-in closet shapes are square, rectangular, and L-shaped. Figure 5-1 shows your basic rectangular walk-in closet space.

The key is to design a solution around your newly lean wardrobe that now fits your current needs and lifestyle.

Organizing a reach-in closet

Reach-in closets (see Figure 5-2 ) are typically found in smaller bedrooms and apartments. They’re usually rectangular and approximately 8 feet long. They’re perfect for single users, and with the right decluttering strategies, we’ve seen these closets fit most people’s needs. The key to organizing a reach-in closet is to be sure to plan for seasonality, if it applies. You may need to make modifications to the closet (using all vertical space on a wall, even if you need a ladder to get to the top shelf) or find extra storage space elsewhere for seasonal items (such as under beds, in a guest room dresser, or on a shelf in the linen or entryway closet).

DETERMINING WHAT TO HANG AND FOLD

Don’t underestimate the importance of deciding what to hang and what to fold! If you have four shelves full of folded pants, it may be time to reevaluate whether you need them all. If you have no room to hang blouses because your pants are taking up all the hanging space, this is another opportunity to reevaluate what to hang and fold. Keep in mind the type of clothes you own and what takes up the most space. Do most of your clothes hang or need to be folded? If you mostly wear sweaters and t-shirts, you need a lot of shelving. If you mostly wear collared blouses and dresses, you need plenty of hanging space.

Paring back your clothes and creating a minimalist wardrobe is your first step; once you’ve done that, make sure you’re properly hanging and folding what you’ve retained. Following are our guidelines to help you get started.

What to hang:

  • Pants with a crease (pants without can be folded)
  • Blouses made of any material (iron and button top, middle, and bottom buttons)
  • Jackets
  • Blazers
  • Slippery silks and satins
  • Clothes made of delicate fabric
  • Most dresses
  • Camisoles

What to fold:

  • Sweaters
  • Knitwear
  • T-shirts
  • Jeans
  • Sweats
  • Lingerie
  • Ornate or unusual dresses (for example, items that are heavily beaded, or dresses with unique silhouettes that don’t hang easily)

Storing Smart So You Can Grab and Go

An organized closet means that when you open it, you’re inspired to wear the clothes you have, and you have a feeling of calm, not chaos, overwhelm, or frustration.

There is no one standard way to organize a closet, but the goal should be to make your clothes easily identifiable, tidy, and visually inspiring.

Here are some ways to create that ideal closet setup:

  • Organize by color. If you have a colorful closet, organizing by color is a logical way to easily identify your clothes.
  • Organize by category. Keep clothes of the same category in one place. If you have a colorful closet, organize it by color and then by category: for example, yellow dresses, yellow pants, then green dresses, green pants, and so forth.
  • Organize and rotate by season. Have the clothes you need for the current season most accessible. One of the authors of this book lives in New York City and has limited closet space, so she has to store some off-season items and swap them out when seasons change. You can also put off-season items on higher shelves and put in-season items on more accessible shelves.
  • Create space for movement. Although your definition of “minimal” may be more clothes than your neighbor has, your closet shouldn’t be so jam-packed that you can’t easily get to or see your clothes. If it is jam-packed, wear your items for a month, using the hanger technique we describe in the section “ Auditing your closet and sorting the keepers from the clingers ,” and at the end of the month, evaluate if any pieces were unworn. If so, consider if you need to get rid of these items, or if they aren’t seasonally appropriate and should be put away to free up space.
  • File-fold your foldable clothes. File-folding lets you easily see and sort through the items you prefer to fold. For example, we recommend not stacking your T-shirts on top of each other — when you pull one out, they all tend to fall over, and stacking makes it harder to reach clothes at bottom of the stack. Instead, fold your clothes into small rectangles and store them vertically in your drawer, like files in a filing cabinet. This way, you can see all the pieces in your drawer at once, and you’re more likely to wear everything.
  • Keep your closet visually appealing. Use matching hangers, fold your clothes neatly, and vacuum or dust your closet regularly.

ORGANIZING FOR THE SEASONS

Planning your closet for each season ensures that you’re ready and excited when each season arrives.

Our motto is quite simple: If you haven’t worn something this season, will you wear it the next? Many factors likely come into consideration: emotional, practical, financial, and fashion. If you haven’t worn a piece after two seasons, and your goal is to maintain a minimal, thoughtful wardrobe where every item gets to shine, then maybe you need to assess if the item needs a new home in someone else’s closet. We’re not saying to get rid of your cashmere coat that you didn’t wear for two seasons — maybe it was a warm couple of autumns, and you plan to wear that classic again next year when temperatures hopefully cool down earlier. But that pink puffy jacket you haven’t worn for two winters may be a different case.

Seasonal items fall into the following categories, each of which has its own assessment considerations:

  • Day-to-day wear: Which of your seasonal clothes are your staple items? This clothing should be assessed quarterly to determine if it’s still something you plan to wear with intention. And if you move to a different seasonal climate, make sure you assess your everyday wear for that new season. (You may still need a few hoodies if you move to the Florida Panhandle, but maybe not a dozen.)
  • Sporting attire: Some sports are seasonal, and related clothing, such as ski attire, should be stored when you won’t need to use it. If an item’s season went by without you wearing it, evaluate first if you should get rid of the item, and only store what you truly intend to wear the next year.
  • Vacation staples: These items may be part of your seasonal routine if you vacation at the same time and place annually, or if you have certain staples you always take on vacation. However, when you return from trips make sure to note what you didn’t use or wear, and take this opportunity to decide if your wardrobe can be a little more minimal.

Getting Minimalist with Accessories

Accessories can often be the clutter that causes the most stress, so don’t skip this important step toward minimalism! The small things can cause more clutter and feeling of chaos than larger items, and having perfectly organized boxes for all your accessories is useless if you never wear their contents or, worse, forget what you own and end up buying more.

The following sections walk you through the ins and outs as you take a minimal approach to different types of accessories.

Sorting your seasonal shoes and boots

We believe in creating systems that work for your lifestyle, and shoe and boot storage is no exception. We always suggest sorting through your shoes as the starting point for minimizing accessories.

We recommend the following approach as you get started:

  • Sort by type, color, or occasion. As with your clothing, sort shoes and boots by similar types, colors, and usage. Shoes and boots can easily be sorted by need or occasion. For example, you can organize by exercise shoes, daily work shoes, and special occasion shoes. Boots can also be further broken down into height, warmth, and function.
  • Assess based on usage. Seeing all your boots and shoes grouped by how much you use them makes you more aware of which shoes and boots you already wear intentionally and which ones may need to go. Sorting by usage also helps you see which shoes are overworn and helps you keep a rotational system going to evenly wear your footwear. As shown in Figure 5-3 , we encourage you to look inside every shoe box to really figure out what shoes you currently have.

Once you have your boots and shoes sorted, you’re more easily able to decide which go into a Yes, No, and Maybe pile (see the section, “ Auditing your closet and sorting the keepers from the clingers ,” for more on sorting your items). And to keep a minimal footwear inventory, take a quarterly or seasonal look at what you have and how much you’re wearing each, and determine if you need to pare back your footwear further.

Scaling back your handbags, totes, and backpacks

Handbags and their carryall cousins are a true passion of ours. (Full disclosure: We sometimes cringe when we have to pare back handbags.) Some minimalists will choose to have one bag for all occasions, and others choose to have several bags in order to match their style and needs — perhaps a bag for the gym, a backpack for carryon travel, and a laptop sleeve for everyday journeys from here to there. Our point is to have what you need and use what you have. Make sure to regularly evaluate your handbags and other bags to make sure they’re efficient, fit your current lifestyle, and add ease and purpose to outfits.

Decluttering handbags (the term we’ll now use to refer to any bag you use to transport personal items) should involve thorough analysis of what you need and what you currently have, and an honest assessment of whether you keep handbags strictly for purpose or if you collect them for investment and fashion. The differing approaches to handbags will eventually determine your approach to minimizing.

Which of the following handbag user are you?

  • Purposeful handbag user: Someone who solely has handbags for purpose versus style will probably already have a streamlined collection, and getting too minimal is usually a simpler process. Keep anything you consider functional and that you use 80 percent of the time. You still may have a few staples versus just one, when your primary concern is function and whether the style and color of each bag fits with most of your outfits.
  • Fashion handbag user: If you collect a variety of different handbags based on fashion trends, you may have a sizeable collection. Assessing and paring back to just what you need (even if that’s quite a few more bags than the Purposeful handbag user, keeping in mind the goal of intentionally using all that you have) is very important in order to meet your minimalist goals. Without this deliberate assessment, you could still end up with a handbag wasteland.
  • Investment handbag user: Classic handbags can become not only wardrobe staples but also investment pieces if cared for properly. If your minimalist definition includes handbags as investments, keeping them stored properly preserves them and ensures you’re using them enough. Figure 5-4 shows an ideal handbag storage option; the plexiglass dividers allow you to see all the bags you own, access each easily, and store each properly to maintain its investment status.

Examining inside your bag

We’re not going to list what you should or shouldn’t carry in your purse or backpack; all of us have different needs and meet those needs uniquely (although when celebrities are asked what’s in their bag, it’s fascinating to find out what they cart around!).

What’s also fascinating is the amount of clutter that can end up in your purse or pack and stay there for years. We think taking a minimalist approach to bag contents is important for a variety of reasons, including your health, because carrying a bag that’s too heavy can cause pains in your back, neck, and shoulders and can lead to worse problems.

If you’re the Fashion handbag user (see the preceding section) and switch your handbags for occasions, your bag contents are probably fairly lean, since you move them from one bag to the next with some regularity. However, when moving items between bags, don’t just move them. Check to make sure you’re moving things you actually use, and anything that you’re not using intentionally (hello empty mint tin or spare pair of socks) needs to go!

If you carry only one bag consistently, you need to make an even more conscious effort to do a thorough edit of what’s in your handbag.

Here are some suggestions to help lighten your bags of all types:

  • Go through your handbags quarterly. This is especially important if you don’t switch handbags, because you could easily accumulate a lot of unneeded items. A quarterly review session should be the minimum frequency you evaluate your handbag contents. The more often you review with a minimalist eye, the less stuff you accumulate!
  • Go digital. As much as possible, avoid carrying too many coins or cards. Make use of your phone’s digital payment function, such as Apple Wallet, Google Wallet, or Samsung Wallet. Keep membership numbers from your favorite shops in your phone as well. Avoid carrying too much cash.
  • Use see-through pouches. Use a see through-pouch to carry essentials while making them easily viewable. This way, you’ll be reminded of what’s in the pouch, making it easier to intentionally use or deliberately eliminate items.
  • Consider a handbag organizer. If you switch your bags often, consider investing in an organizer that you can transfer from one to the next. However, make sure you’re mindful about the organizer’s contents, and get rid of anything you don’t consistently use.

Storing your scarves

Another accessory that’s often daunting on the minimalist front is scarves. From winter to summer, scarves are understated accessories that have challenged organizing gurus for years! We suggest keeping larger scarves on scarf hangers and folding smaller silk scarves (see Figure 5-6 ).

Here are our top tips for creating a minimalist scarf collection:

  • Take inventory. As you take inventory of your scarves, decide which ones you want to keep and which you want to tailor, repurpose, donate, sell, or toss.
  • Be brutal. The number of scarves you own can sneak up on you. Try them on and ask yourself if you wear them (for some maybe it’s been years!) and how often. Any you haven’t worn in a year may need to go into the No pile. As you decide what to keep, remember your goal of wearing all that you own, so get rid of any scarves that don’t fit your style anymore or are worn and can’t be repaired.
  • Determine what’s sentimental. If you have sentimental scarves passed down from friends or family members but don’t plan to wear them much, make a special box for them and store them in a safe place. We recommend putting a see-through lid on the box or a photo on the outside so that you can keep track of which scarves are inside. For more on containers, see Chapter 4 .
  • Consider what’s real-estate worthy. If you have expensive scarves that you never wear, consider selling them or putting them on display by framing them and making them part of your home décor.

Choosing the jewelry you’ll keep

Jewelry is an interesting and often overlooked part of the wardrobe review process. It’s easy to forget about because jewelry often doesn’t take up much physical space (at least compared to dozens of shirts or bulky coats). Jewelry can be sentimental, valuable, or have hidden meanings.

When minimizing your jewelry collection, you need to be fully committed, because we often store items hidden in boxes or drawers, and this makes it nearly impossible to wear all that you own or even remember what you have.

First, figure out your style based on how much you accessorize. Some people love to change accessories daily and have pounds of jewelry for every occasion. Others stick with two or three items they wear every time they go out.

Whether you’re constantly changing your jewelry to match your outfits or you stick to your staples, a minimalist review is necessary. Consider the following factors:

  • Frequency of use: Sort your jewelry by frequency of use, which automatically helps you pare down your jewelry. Determine what your staples are and what you wear once in a while, and eliminate accordingly.
  • Repurposing potential: If you don’t wear a certain piece, make it into something else, give it to a friend, or sell it.
  • Storage possibilities: How you store your jewelry helps you maintain a minimalist collection. Don’t store items in covered boxes tucked away at the back of your closet — you won’t remember what you have, and instead of wearing all you own, these items will likely collect dust. Have your jewelry visible and easy to access, which makes selection and use a breeze.

Assessing belts and everything else

We’re always amazed by how this accessory is often forgotten. Many of us seem to have more belts than we need, and often we store belts that don’t even fit. Review each of your belts to determine what to remove from your wardrobe, and resist the urge to keep the ones that “will hopefully fit” someday or that you “might” wear with certain pants that you have yet to purchase.

Beyond belts and what we’ve already discussed, we have lots of other items that make their way into our closets. When minimizing other stuff you find there, stick to the tips we’ve given you so far, the biggest being that if you don’t use it or wear it, it’s likely time to get rid of it and move on.

Getting Rid of Your Discards and Keeping Your Closet Minimalist

During your closet audit and purge, we recommend labeling a few boxes “Donate,” “Repurpose,” “Tailor,” “Sell,” and “Toss.” As you take inventory of your closet, place your items in the appropriate box:

  • Donate: Donating is a great way to support your minimalist mindset of wearing what you have — because what you don’t need may be someone else’s must-have. Donating doesn’t just mean giving away items via your local Buy Nothing social media page or a nearby thrift shop; it can also mean giving items to neighbors, friends, and family. Schedule friends’ dates where you swap clothing — over champagne, of course! As long as your items are in wearable condition, someone should be wearing them.
  • Repurpose: Many closet items can be repurposed. Clothing can be cropped, cut, changed, or added to, and once it’s no longer fit for wear, some can be used for cleaning rags. Jewelry can be used in creative ways (put a broach on a cowboy hat, or turn some funky earrings into a necklace), scarves can be used for decorations, and leather boots can be used for repair patches or made into smaller leather goods.
  • Tailor: Tailoring can mean so much more than getting your jeans or dress pants hemmed. Even if you love that special blazer or sport coat, you won’t wear it if it hangs weirdly or looks shapeless. Find a good tailor and bring in items that you’re not ready to part with but never wear. A good tailor can work wonders and turn many of your Maybe items into signature Yes’s.
  • Sell: Besides donating items, you can also resell valuable, lightly worn pieces. There are many apps, online consignment stores, and retail stores that sell lightly used items — and many people looking for a good deal on a pre-owned item they may not otherwise have been able to afford. This is a great option if you like to infuse your minimalist wardrobe with one or two trending items each season while keeping your wardrobe lean and intentional.
  • Toss: At some point, closet items are no longer usable. They may be too stained, ripped beyond repair, or just plain overworn such that you cannot sell, donate, tailor, or even repurpose them. At this point, you can toss these items.

Once you’ve achieved a minimalist wardrobe and a closet organized well enough to see and easily select every piece, you want to keep things lean and well-organized.

Here are a few ideas to hold yourself accountable:

  • Schedule weekly calendar pop-ups. A regular reminder, even if only five minutes, titled “Minimalist Closet” will help keep your minimalist approach top of mind and help you avoid accumulating more stuff.
  • Create quarterly friend dinners and closet review sessions. Sharing your task with friends adds a fun, team aspect to maintaining minimalism.
  • Have pre-scheduled pickups for your donation items. This ensures that your items actually get donated instead of sitting in the garage for weeks.
  • Get your family or school involved by arranging donation drives. Having a support system makes the minimizing process much easier. Schedule in a few donation drives so that you’re held accountable to your goals.
