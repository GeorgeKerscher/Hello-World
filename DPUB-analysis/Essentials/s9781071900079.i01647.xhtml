Appendix 2: Organizational Structure

Most people go about their daily work without paying much attention to organizational structure. They do their job, and its relationship to other jobs is invisible. However, when an organization goes through a significant change such as a restructuring or merger, organizational structure becomes especially important, as it is the way the work is organized, and often redundancies are identified and layoffs occur. Organizational structure refers to the rules, relationships, communication channels, and duties that allow members to work toward shared goals. 1 Organizational structure is the result of strategic decision-making by the top management team. 2 An employee’s job description summarizes the expectations that the organization has for their performance. There are also written documents such as policy manuals, employee handbooks, and training guides. These documents provide guidelines to reduce uncertainty for employees with respect to what is required to perform their job. 3 Jobs are the building blocks of organizational structure.

A job is defined as “a set of task elements grouped together under one job title and designed to be performed by a single individual.” 4 For every job performed by a human being, the social aspects and technical requirements must align to produce products or services. When both are optimized, the most positive outcomes are obtained. This contrasts with traditional scientific management methods that first designed jobs technically and then tried to fit people to them. Socio-technical system design emerged in the 1960s to optimize relationships between the social and technical aspects of jobs in organizations. 5 Consistent with socio-technical systems theory, jobs can be designed so that people are more motivated and satisfied, as well as perform better. They are also less likely to quit their jobs when their work is designed properly. 6 Some key questions that a manager should ask regarding the organizational structure from the socio-technical systems perspective are as follows: 7

  • Are the existing structures useful for the attainment of the goals and tasks?
  • How do people adapt to or cope with these structures?
  • Are the structures supportive of the needs and capabilities of the leadership (management) and the workforce?
  • Do the structures encourage cooperation and collaboration?
  • Do the structures facilitate a rational use of resources and technologies?
  • What major strengths/weaknesses result from the structures interacting with other elements in the system?

526 Once jobs are specified, employees also need to understand reporting relationships and proper communication channels within a structure, which is accomplished with an organizational chart . The following sections describe the most common organizational charts seen today.

Organizational Charts

Functional Organization

The functional organization chart , also known as the departmentalized structure, is the most common. In fact, when asked to draw an organization, this type of chart is what most people draw. It is an implicit view of what an organization looks like. This structure organizes work into different functional areas or departments based upon specialized expertise ( Figure A2.1 ). As shown in the figure (as an example), a functional organizational structure organizes employees into human resources, finance, accounting, marketing, and information systems. If the organization is in manufacturing, they might also have a vice president (VP) of manufacturing. Most large organizations have a chief executive officer (CEO) (the highest-ranking person in a company) as well as a chief operating officer (COO) (a senior executive tasked with overseeing the day-to-day operations). VPs of the functional areas report to the CEO and COO. There may also be an office of legal counsel that reports to the CEO. An advantage of functional organization is that work can be assigned to the units staffed by experts in the relevant field. For example, the marketing department would have market research analysts and creative individuals that develop advertisements based on the market research analysis. Another advantage is the communication within departments is easier because everyone has similar training and speaks the same “language.” For example, the accounting department would have people that were accounting majors who understand the meaning of accounting terms and are up on the latest tax regulations.

There are disadvantages to specialization. It may create a silo mentality , which makes integration across the functional areas difficult. The silo mentality is a reluctance to share information or resources with other divisions of the organization. The managers of each unit may be more interested in furthering the goals of their area rather than the entire organization. For example, the research and development (R&D) division may be slow to complete product testing, resulting in the sales team not being able to beat the competition to the market. Another disadvantage is that specialists use jargon and terminology from their profession, and decision-making may be slower due to misunderstandings. For example, an accountant may understand the term “FIFO” (first-in-last-out) inventory, but others from different disciplines may not.

527 An example of a company organized by functions is Apple Computers. There’s a chief design officer and a senior vice president of software engineering, a senior vice president of hardware technologies, and a senior vice president of hardware engineering. There are also more traditional senior functional executives like a general counsel and a chief financial officer. The functional structure allows for collaboration across product lines. For example, Apple is able to leverage technology across devices such as using a chip developed for the Apple Watch in the TouchBar product. This works because top executives are responsible for “software engineering” and “hardware technologies” (i.e., chip development) rather than for specific products. 8 But Apple is evolving—for example, its retail business is not organized by function but rather is a separate division, run more as a product line. Next, we turn to the product organization structure. Another example of a company with a functional organizational structure is Macy’s department stores.

Product Organization

A product organization (also referred to as service or brand organization) organizes the work into units that are responsible for producing specific products or services ( Figure A2.2 ). In some instances, the product organization is broken down even further into brands. This type of structure is often seen in large companies with very different products known as conglomerates . General Electric, for example, has structured product-specific divisions: (1) Power, (2) Renewable Energy, (3) Oil and Gas, (4) Aviation, (5) Healthcare, (6) Transportation, (7) Energy Connections and Lighting, and (8) Capital. 9 This works for GE because the product lines are highly technical and require specialized knowledge. These product divisions are supported by centralized services, which include public relations, business development, legal, global research, human resources, and finance. This type of structure is ideal for organizations like GE with multiple products and can help shorten product development cycles. Other organizations that have product structures include Boeing, Hewlett-Packard, and Sony.

528 The product organizations has several advantages. Within each product area, there are managers and employees in the functional areas (human resources, finance, accounting, marketing and production) that focus on the particular needs of the product. Managers develop specialized expertise related to the product. Performance evaluation in the product organization is more straightforward because the top management team can track the performance (e.g., sales) for each product line. Another advantage is that decision-making is faster due to the dedication of the managers to a particular product, rather than being loyal to a department of specialists. The biggest disadvantage of the product organization is that there is typically a lot of duplication of effort. As shown in Figure A2.2 , each product line has its own human resources, finance, accounting, marketing, and production, for example. It may be difficult for the administration and finance division to implement standardized policies across all of the product lines.

Geographical Organization

529 An example of a geographical organization chart is shown in Figure A2.3 . Organizations that use this type of organizational structure need to be close to their customers, and the delivery of products is important. For example, Coca-Cola has a geographical structure organized by regions. It’s important to recognize that the company has nearly 400 brands serving consumers in over 200 countries. The company is organized for the areas of Southeast Asia and the Pacific Rim, North America, Africa, North Asia and the Middle East, the European Union, and Latin America. The company relies on its ability to provide fresh products within the regions and connect with local customers. Tastes and lifestyles vary from region to region as well as incomes and consumption patterns. 10 You may have noticed that a Coke has a different taste if you are visiting another country. Another example is that their tropical Lilt beverage is only sold in the United Kingdom and nearby countries. This is no accident. The formula is adjusted for the region, and the organizational structure allows the regional manager to make appropriate decisions that are right for the market. Another example of a company with a geographical organizational structure is WeWork, which leases office space in the United States and Canada, Latin America, and the Middle East. There is also a division for Asia joint ventures. 11

The geographical structure has some disadvantages. As with the product organization, a major challenge of the geographical structure is the duplication of effort. Also, coordination across all business units and direction from the headquarters may be problematic. A research study of geographically distributed R&D projects 12 found that the sites were “out of sight, out of mind.” Study respondents felt that those at other sites were much less responsive to coworkers who reside at a different site. For example, they were much less likely to provide information quickly, to follow up on requests, and, generally, to consider concerns of the other site. Meeting face-to-face seemed to help; several respondents noticed a significant change for the better after they had actually met the person at the other site. Nevertheless, respondents at the geographically dispersed sites believed that they still did not get the same level of attention they received from colocated coworkers.

Matrix Organization

530 Figure A2.4 shows what a matrix organization chart might look like. In the matrix organization, functions are combined with projects. 13 This organizational structure is well-suited for organizations that are engaged in R&D, advertising, or management consulting. 14 For example, Starbucks has a matrix organizational structure that is a hybrid mixture of different features from the basic types of organizational structure. The structural design involves intersections among various components of the business. For example, the company’s product-based divisions intersect with functional groups and geographic divisions, which in turn intersect with other parts of the organization. Employees work in teams that reflect functions, geographic regions, and products. 15

The main advantage of a matrix organization structure is that there is less duplication of effort compared to the functional and geographical structures. Resources are shared between the units, allowing more open communication, which helps in knowledge sharing. When a matrix organization functions well, the organization is both efficient and effective. 16 Another advantage is that employees are exposed to both projects and functions, and this expands their skills and knowledge of the business. Employees develop broad-based skills, and this enhances the work done on project teams. This also enhances employees’ career development and promotion potential.

531 The matrix organization is not without disadvantages, however. The main challenges of the matrix structure are conflict between the horizontal and vertical departments. Managers experience information overload and may complain that there are too many meetings, and decision-making is slow. 17 Employees have to report to two managers, which may cause conflict. 18 For example, a product manager may want to spend a lot to develop a new drug, but the finance (functional) manager may want to contain costs and review return on investment. Employees may be confused about priorities. In other words, employees working in matrix organizations may experience more stress because they must report to two bosses. The matrix organization is complex, can be difficult to manage, and can be costly. 19 A research study of 86 R&D teams 20 in a matrix organization found that performance reached the highest level when organizational influence was centered on the project manager, and influence over the technical details of the work was centered on the technical manager. Thus, balancing the power within the matrix organization is necessary.

Virtual Organization

The dot-com era gave rise to a new form of organization known as the virtual organization . A virtual organization is defined as “a temporary collection of enterprises that cooperate and share resources, knowledge, and competencies to better respond to business opportunities.” 21 Yet, some virtual organizations are not temporary, such as Wikipedia. Often, the virtual organization is not tangible in terms of offices, facilities, and other types of infrastructure—it exists in people’s minds. The sense of purpose and resources that are dedicated to achieving certain goals give meaning to the virtual organization. Virtual organizations are common in high-technology environments where there is intense global competition. For example, Symbian Ltd. was a virtual organization that developed mobile phones and was a consortium of competing companies, including Psion, Nokia AB, Sony Ericsson, Samsung Electronics, Panasonic, and Siemens AG. It’s goal was to combine efforts to develop mobile phones and PDA devices (handheld devices with email and Internet access) to prevent Microsoft from extending its monopoly into the mobile phone market. 22 They didn’t want to be tied to Microsoft’s high licensing fees and coding. Often, virtual organizations are temporary and set up to take advantage of a threat or market opportunity. The organization dissolves or is merged into a larger company after the opportunity is exploited. In the case of Symbian noted earlier, it was acquired by Nokia in 2008.

The advantage of this type of organization is quick response and flexibility, with no long-term commitment to the project. Also, the strengths of different organizations that combine can be leveraged. The disadvantages are communication difficulties since they typically operate in a virtual environment, lack of trust among partners, and no real commitment to an organization and its mission. Another example of a virtual organization is Airbnb. Their business model is to link people with rental properties with visitors. They provide access to the rental market for hosts and visitors through their online search platform. They charge on the front end and the back end of the process: Guests pay a service fee, and hosts pay a renting fee. The organization was founded by Joe Gebbia and Brian Chesky in 2007 who were struggling to pay their rent. Because they were familiar with such challenges, they dreamed up a business that linked renters with available housing. Due to their unique characteristics, virtual organizations don’t have traditional organizational charts. The business model structure for the virtual organization Airbnb is shown in Figure A2.5 .

532 Organizational structures evolve over time. In a new start-up company, the organizational structure may be simple, with a few employees having a single leader. 23 As companies grow and evolve, however, so do their structures. There are some common patterns that tend to emerge, as we discuss in the next section, but every organization must grow its structure in accordance with the unique challenges it faces. 24 This process of constructing and adjusting an organization’s structure to achieve its goals is called organizational design . 25

Dimensions of Organizational Design

533 Organizational design (and redesign) responds to an organization’s business environment . This environment consists of various stakeholders such as shareholders, customers, suppliers, distributors, the government, and the employees. As you learned in Chapter 15, changing organizational environments drive change, and this often results in changes in strategy , which necessitate organizational design. An organization’s strategy is its overarching goals and objectives aimed at generating revenue. Organizations must change their structure to be more effective in changing environments. In other words, “structure follows strategy.” Alfred Chandler coined this phrase in 1962, 26 emphasizing that an organization’s design for managing and performing work must adapt to the business environment and its stakeholders. For example, Cirque du Soleil was a model of innovation in the Canadian context. Since the 1980s, it grew into a global corporation, sourcing talent and staging productions around the world. The company owed its success to its creative process, and a balance between art and commerce. However, the Cirque struggled to maintain this balance as it became larger and they began standardizing processes, which harmed the creative aspects of the work. To meet the demands of shareholder stakeholders, they shifted their structure to be more top-down and bureaucratic. Whether this shift will be effective long term is a question, since the organizational design led to deskilling of the workforce and limited creativity. As a consequence of these structure changes, an increasing number of workers left the company to pursue their artistic ambitions elsewhere. 27

When organizations encounter radical change, they often need to undergo major changes to their organizational structure. Restructuring can encompass a broad range of changes, including selling lines of business or making significant acquisitions, and changing the internal organization. 28 In some cases, organizations go through downsizing , which makes the organization more efficient by reducing the staff through layoffs. Reducing the workforce has an immediate effect of lowering compensation and benefit costs. However, downsizing reduces employee morale and organizational commitment. Those who remain have job insecurity. 29 A study found that downsizing had negative effects on remaining employees’ well-being including negative work rumination, physical, mental and emotional work fatigue, depression, anxiety, and anger. Employees also had lower job satisfaction and organization commitment and more thoughts of quitting. 30

There are five dimensions of organizational design that can be used to describe most organization’s structural elements. 31 These include specialization, formalization, centralization, standardization, and complexity. We discuss each of these dimensions and provide examples in the following section. Span of control and dotted-line relationships are also important organizational design considerations, and they are discussed as well.

Dimensions of Organizational Design

Specialization (sometimes referred to as division of labor ) occurs when jobs are narrowly defined and depend on unique expertise. Employees become proficient and effective in performing these specific tasks. Early in the 20th century, Henry Ford developed the assembly line with specialized workers to produce automobiles. This type of production still exists today in many factories.

Formalization refers to the amount of rules, regulations, and procedures an organization requires its employees to follow. A very formalized job expects the workers to follow directions and not innovate. A less formalized job allows for creativity in how the work is performed. For example, an artist creates a painting based upon their inspiration, and they have total discretion in how the final artwork looks.

Centralization occurs when most or all key decisions are made by an organization’s top leadership rather than by the managers or employees of individual work units. In some organizations, decisions are highly centralized and made by the top executive team. Decentralization occurs when decisions are delegated to lower levels in the organizational hierarchy. For example, a manager at the Gap may have discretion over how they display merchandise in mall store windows.

534 Standardization occurs when work is performed in a routine manner, following the same steps each time. Standardization relates to the degree to which employees can recall and apply organizational knowledge. A study found that standardization related to employees’ ability to recall information, personalize their job routines, and communicate this with other departments. 32

Complexity describes the range of activities conducted within an organization. In the 1960s, Joan Woodward studied 100 manufacturing firms in the United Kingdom 33 and categorized the technical complexity of their operations from simple to more complex: unit production (small batches such as artwork), mass production (assembly lines), and continuous processing (intensive production without human involvement such as oil processing). She determined that the type of operating process an industry used determined the best structure for an organization. A highly complex organization might create hundreds of unique products and provide a range of services as well. Such an organization is likely to require a complex structure to support this wide range of activities, whereas a company producing only one or two products can often utilize a more basic structure. Table A2.1 provides examples of each of these dimensions at low and high levels of the dimension.

Table A2.1 Dimensions of Organizational Design
  Examples
  High Levels Low Levels
Specialization An automobile assembly plant: Each employee performs a specific task repeatedly. A craft furniture workshop: Employees are involved in multiple phases of the production process.
Formalization A bureaucratic government agency in which employees are bound by many rules and procedures. Many universities: Professors are often given significant latitude in how they structure classes and deliver course material.
Centralization Chain restaurants such as McDonald’s, Burger King: Although franchise owners have some discretion, menu content, pricing, and appearance are largely dictated by central management. 535 In addition to the five elements of organizational design, additional considerations for designing organizational structures are the chain-of-command and span of control. These are discussed next.

Chain-of-Command

The chain-of-command in organizations refers to the flow of authority in the organization. In his classic 1949 book General and Industrial Management , 34 Henry Fayol emphasized the importance of unity of command (each employee receives orders from only one boss to avoid confusion). As the organization grows and becomes more complex, unity of command becomes more difficult and the leader must rely on intermediaries to transmit directives. The leader’s authority must flow down the organization through levels in the organizational hierarchy to maintain order, control, and organizational performance. 35 Another important concept in organizational design is span of control, discussed next.

Span of Control

536 Individuals who report to another person higher in the organization are often called direct reports . The number of direct reports each person has is referred to as their span of control , which generally increases as one moves down the chain-of-command in organizations. 36 Of course, those higher in the organization have authority over more people in total since their span of control flows down the hierarchy. Classic organizational theory suggested that the optimal span of control was five to seven direct reports. 37 But this is changing as organizations change. A comparison of managers with a small and larger span of control is shown in Figure A2.6 . Some organizations are flat and have large spans of control. For example, in a call center, a manager may have 100 direct reports. However, leaders with large spans of control may not be able to develop high-quality working relationships with all direct reports. 38 Other organizations have smaller spans of control. For example, an R&D team may have only four research scientists reporting to a project team leader. As this example illustrates, one determinant of the optimal span of control is the complexity of the job. Smaller spans of control enable managers to supervise their followers more closely. Some employees may resent being “micromanaged,” and research has shown that a moderate span of control is optimal for job performance. 39 What’s the right span of control? According to the consulting firm McKinsey, it depends on the complexity of the work. Managers can assess this by asking these questions:

  • Time allocation . How much actual time is the manager spending on their own work versus time spent managing others?
  • Process standardization . How standard and formally structured is the work process?
  • Work variety . How similar or different is the work of individual direct reports?
  • Team skills required . How much experience and training do team members’ jobs require? How independent are the direct reports? 40

Spans of control are sometimes augmented by dotted-line connections between two positions. These indicate communication channels that exist without a direct power relationship. Dotted-line relationships often occur when an employee can provide useful information to a manager in a different part of an organization’s structure. 41 For example, the marketing research analyst for a consumer products company might not have any direct reporting relationship to a product manager. Thus, if providing feedback on a sales campaign for the manager’s product is a formal part of the research analysts’ job, this would be represented as a dotted-line relationship.

Forms of Organizational Structure

537 Organizations can be described in terms of the various functions of different organizational units as shown in Figure A2.7 . 42 As shown in the figure, the top management team is at the strategic apex of the organization. They are supported in their decision-making by the technostructure (operations research, for example) and the support staff (legal counsel, for example). The technostructure consists of analysts whose job is to standardize the work of others in the organization. The support staff provides advice and guidance (public relations and legal counsel, as examples) and other support for the organization’s employees (the cafeteria workers, for example). The strategic apex (top management team) meets organizational goals by directing the operating core (i.e., the workers that create products or serve customers) through the middle line management. The technostructure and support staff may also provide guidance to middle managers (i.e., the middle line) in the execution of their job duties.

The top management team creates the strategic mission, sets goals for the organization (the strategic apex), and delegates the implementation and control to the middle line managers. Thus, senior managers establish long-term organizational goals and directives. Middle managers (the middle line) are responsible for translating the general strategic plans into detailed action plans, specifying managerial responsibilities for particular tasks and how resources are distributed. 43 This model results in five general types of organizational structures that may emerge. 44 , 45 These types are summarized in Table A2.2 and described in the following sections.

Simple Structure

538 This type of organization has a simple and flat structure. It is also known as the entrepreneurial organization. It consists of one large unit with one or a few top managers. The organization is relatively unstructured and informal compared with other types of organization, and the lack of standardized systems allows the organization to be flexible. A family business that is carefully controlled by the owner is an example of this type of organization. A strong leader may be able to sustain an entrepreneurial organization as it grows. The entrepreneurial organization is fast, flexible, and lean, and it is a model that many companies want to copy. However, as organizations grow, this structure can be challenging because decision-makers become so overwhelmed that they can start making bad decisions. Delegation is needed to share power and decision-making authority. Also, when a company’s success depends on one or two individuals, there is significant risk if they sell the company, move on to new entrepreneurial ventures, or retire. The entrepreneurial organization, while it may be effective in the founding and early years of the growth of an organization, is vulnerable. As the organization grows, it typically evolves into another of the organizational types.

Machine Bureaucracy

539 The machine bureaucracy is defined by its standardization. Work is very formalized, there are many routines and procedures, decision-making is centralized, and tasks are grouped by functional departments. Jobs are clearly defined; there is a formal planning process with budgets and audits, and procedures are regularly analyzed by the technostructure employees for efficiency. This is also known as a mechanistic structure . 46 When organizations face hostile conditions, they may shift to this type of structure to maintain control. 47

These characteristics contribute to high levels of bureaucracy in most machine organizations. Although this term has a negative meaning for many people today, bureaucracy can exist without the long lines, confusing forms, and general inefficiency we often associate with it. Believe it or not, bureaucracy was once viewed as the optimal form for organizing work efficiently. 48 It has fallen out of favor, partly due to poor implementation in highly visible organizations (in the United States, the Departments of Motor Vehicles and the Postal Service often come to mind). Changes in technology, societal norms, and the insights gained from management research have also led many organizations to shift away from the tight vertical structure of machine organizations toward flatter, more decentralized formations. The high degree of formalization and specialization can make work feel monotonous. When employees feel like “cogs in a machine,” intrinsic motivation is very hard to come by. 49 Functional units can also form conflicting goals that can be inconsistent with overall corporate objectives. This silo mentality may emerge as organizational divisions begin to hoard information due to these divergent goals. 50

Machine organizations can still be extremely efficient, relying heavily on economies of scale for their success. Economies of scale occur when more units of a good or a service are produced on a larger scale, yet with (on average) fewer input costs. This means that as a company grows and production volume increases, a company can decrease the cost per unit. Large manufacturers are often machine organizations, as are government agencies and service firms that perform highly routine tasks. Despite the criticisms, there has been recent interest in reviving the positive aspects of bureaucracy because of its effects on operational efficiency and organizational performance. 51 If following procedures and meeting precise specifications are important, then the machine structure works well.

Professional Bureaucracy

540 The professional bureaucracy is highly structured and has many levels like the machine bureaucracy. The key difference between these and machine organizations is that professional organizations rely on highly trained professionals who demand control of their own work. So, while there’s a high degree of specialization, decision-making is decentralized. This structure is typical when the organization contains a large number of knowledge workers, and this is why it is common in organizations, such as schools, and in accounting and law firms. Another good example of a professional organization is a hospital with its highly trained staff of surgeons and nurses with a wide range of responsibilities for patient care and research. 52 The professional organization is complex, and there are lots of rules and procedures. This allows it to enjoy the efficiency benefits of a machine structure. The difference is that the output is generated by highly trained professionals who have autonomy and considerable power. However, such professionals may resist being micromanaged or supervised too closely. 53 Universities are examples of professional organizations. Your professors are highly trained with advanced degrees. You may have noticed that they have autonomy over what happens in the classroom, give lectures without supervision, and design projects for students to work on. They have the responsibility of grading papers and exams and assigning final grades.

Divisional Form

If an organization has many different product lines and business units, you’ll typically see a divisional structure in place. A central headquarters supports a number of autonomous divisions that make their own decisions and have their own unique structures. You’ll often find this type of structure in large and mature organizations that have a variety of brands, produce a wide range of products, or operate in different geographical regions. The divisional structure is the most common configuration found in large organizations. Commonly referred to as the M-form 54 (multidivisional form), organizations such as General Motors and Ford operate under a divisional structure. 55 A divisional structure is organized according to specific products, product groups, services, markets, customers, or major programs. 56 Within this type of structure, management has direct responsibility for their own department’s operation and performance. The key benefit of a divisional structure is that it allows line managers to maintain more control and accountability than in a machine bureaucracy structure. Also, with day-to-day decision-making decentralized, the top management team can focus on “big picture” strategic plans. This allows them to ensure that the necessary support structures are in place for success. A significant weakness is the duplication of resources and activities that go with a divisional structure. Also, divisions can tend to be in conflict, because they each need to compete for limited resources from headquarters. Moreover, these organizations can be inflexible, so this structure works best in industries that are stable and not too complex.

Innovative Adhocracy

541 The structures discussed so far are best suited to traditional organizations. In new industries, companies need to innovate and function on an “ad hoc” basis to survive. The innovative adhocracy allows for flexibility through decentralization and loose rules. Entrepreneurial organizations (also known as an organic structure ) 57 work well with small businesses. This is because when innovation is needed, the complexity of a bureaucracy and centralized decision-making are too limiting. For example, filmmaking, consulting, and pharmaceuticals are project-based industries that often use the innovative structure. Such organizations may hire experts from a variety of areas to form a creative team. They may also make use of consultants to bring in new ideas to the organization. Decisions are decentralized, and power is delegated to where it is needed. The clear advantage of adhocracies is that they maintain a central pool of talent from which people can be drawn at any time to solve problems and work in a highly flexible way. Workers typically move from team to team as projects are completed and as new projects develop. Because of this, adhocracies respond rapidly to changes in the environment by bringing together skilled experts that can meet new challenges. But innovative organizations have some limitations. There can be lots of conflict when authority and power are ambiguous, and constantly dealing with rapid change is stressful for employees, making it difficult to find and keep talent. Thus, employee turnover may be high. Adhocracy is a common structural choice for new business start-ups that need flexibility.

Legal Structures of Organizations

Many students are interested in starting their own business, so it’s important to understand the different ways to structure a small business. The type of business entity chosen will depend on concerns regarding liability, taxation, and record keeping. The most common forms of small business entities are as follows: 58

  • A sole proprietorship is the most common form of business organization. It’s easy to form and offers complete managerial control to the owner. However, the owner is also personally liable for all financial obligations of the business.
  • A partnership involves two or more people who agree to share in the profits or losses of a business. A primary advantage is that the partnership does not bear the tax burden of profits or the benefit of losses. Profits or losses are “passed through” to partners to report on their individual income tax returns. A primary disadvantage is liability—each partner is personally liable for the financial obligations of the business.
  • A corporation is a legal entity that is created to conduct business. The corporation becomes an entity, separate from those who founded it, that handles the responsibilities of the organization. Like a person, the corporation can be taxed and can be held legally liable for its actions. The corporation can also make a profit. The key benefit of corporate status is the avoidance of personal liability. The primary disadvantage is the cost to form a corporation and the extensive record keeping required.
  • A hybrid form of partnership, the limited liability company (LLC) , is gaining in popularity because it allows owners to take advantage of the benefits of both the corporation and partnership forms of business. The advantages of this business format are that profits and losses can be passed through to owners without taxation of the business itself while owners are shielded from personal liability.

Regardless of organization size (small business or large corporation), there’s no right or wrong organizational structure, but it’s important to understand how structure relates to the variety of attributes in a company. Different structures are appropriate in different circumstances. In fact, it’s common for a company to have a combination of elements of each structural type. Organizations must adapt their structures to changing organizational environments, so what the “right” structure is may change over time. In a review of the evolution of organization designs, Anand and Daft conclude, “With increasing global competition, managers have to be astute and realistic about the organization design that provides them with competitive advantage and their customers with greatest value.” 59 There are varying levels of managers within organizational structures, and these are discussed next.

Management Hierarchies

The top management (or C-Suite ) refers to the CEO and other top management team members such as the chief executive officer (CEO) who reports to the organization’s board of directors, which is led by the chairman of the board. In some cases, the CEO and chairman are the same person; however, in most cases, these roles are separated to best represent the interests of shareholders. Examples of other members of the top management team include the chief operating officer (COO), the chief financial officer (CFO), the chief marketing officer (CMO), and the chief diversity officer (CDO). C-suite executives are responsible for making key strategic decisions regarding the future directions of the organization. They take a long-term perspective on the goals and how the organization can best meet them. The CEO’s symbolic actions have a positive impact on organizational performance. They provide a vision and develop the organizational culture. Middle managers play an important role as “linking pins,” 60 who then demonstrate similar leadership behaviors to their followers. Thus, leadership cascades through the organization through the middle managers to the operating level. 61

Operating-level managers are often responsible for managing employees that interact directly with customers. Operating managers generate and allocate resources and identify new areas of growth for the organization. They focus on managing the performance of their followers and continuous improvement in operations. Middle managers play the role of coaches and developers of people. They must be boundary spanners across the divisions of the organization to get things done. Boundary spanners are “individuals who are especially sensitive to and skilled in bridging interests, professions and organizations.” 62 For example, middle managers may serve on cross-functional task forces to solve organizational problems. They are literally the people in the middle—they implement the vision of the top management team but temper it with what can be realistically achieved. Summary

This appendix provided an overview of organizational structure. The key takeaway points are as follows:

  • The structure of an organization is composed of jobs and the relationships among them. This appendix defined jobs and different types of organizational structure, highlighting their importance for organizational efficiency and effectiveness.
  • Organizational charts provide important information about the power relationships and lines of communication built into an organization’s structure. There are different types of organizational charts, and this chapter provided examples of functional, product, geographical, and matrix charts. An emerging form is the virtual organization, which is an “organization without walls.”
  • Most organizational designs contain five key elements, including the operating core, middle line, technostructure, support staff, and strategic apex. These areas have specific functions, with the strategic apex directing the organization’s overall mission through the middle line managers.
  • Organization design elements result in different organizational forms, which include the simple structure, machine bureaucracy, professional bureaucracy, divisional form (M-Form), and the innovative adhocracy. The features of these different forms were discussed in this appendix. It’s important to recognize that structure evolves over time. A small business may start as an innovative adhocracy, but as it grows and hires more employees, it evolves into one of the other forms.
  • Small businesses have particular structures that have implications for how they are managed: A sole proprietorship is the most common form of business organization. It’s easy to form and offers complete managerial control to the owner. However, the owner is also personally liable for all financial obligations of the business. A partnership involves two or more people who agree to share in the profits or losses of a business. A corporation is a legal entity that is created to conduct business, and the limited liability company (LLC), a hybrid form, is gaining in popularity because it allows owners to take advantage of the benefits of both the corporation and partnership forms of business.
  • 545 Just as fit between an employee and an organization is important, so is fit between organizational structure and the business environment. Different organizational designs, including machine bureaucracies and divisional structures, can be used to improve this fit. Thus, there is no “right” organizational structure, and organizations may need to adapt their structure to respond to competition. Organizational structures evolve over time in response to demands from organizational stakeholders.

This appendix reviewed the types of organizational charts and organizational design principles that guide their creation. It is important for a manager to understand how the structure relates to how employees interact with one another in the organization. Managers are also responsible for the design of work, considering both the social and technical requirements (socio-technical systems). They not only design jobs that get the work done but also motivate employees to higher levels of job performance and satisfaction.
