Although you’ve pared back (or are in the process) and have adopted a minimalist mindset, you’ll still shop from time to time. Goods wear out, clothing fits differently, and your priorities change as your life does (particularly if you have kids, whose needs change frequently during the first 12-15 years as they grow and develop). When you do make purchases, you can buy less if you’re intentional about your shopping habits.

Shifting your shopping habits to incorporate a minimalist mindset requires knowledge and a little willpower. Yes, a little willpower because so many consumer goods, including household items and clothing, are made to reflect the latest trends and to fit a range of budgets — both of which encourage you to make purchases even if you don’t need to. But you’ve got this! This chapter helps you build some minimalist shopping habits, as well as helping you take good care of what you own so you have less need to replace things.

Staying Minimalist When Shopping

Shopping as a minimalist in a quick-and-cheap world of goods certainly can be challenging. It’s normal to feel like you’re missing out on the latest and greatest, making your shopping even more complicated for you. Everywhere you turn — the malls, your inbox, social media feeds, and so forth — you see the latest trends in fashion, furniture, and home goods that make your home oh-so-welcoming, so when shopping you need to filter out the maximalist noise and actively seek out items that support your minimalist commitment. In our experience, shopping as a minimalist has been liberating; we’re not swayed by trends and the need to always own the latest and greatest of anything. In general, we’ve spent less on clothes and home items. Moreover, we love the items we purchase more because we bought them more intentionally.

As a minimalist consumer, you should focus on the following as general guiding principles:

  • Shop intentionally . This means buying furniture, kitchen gear, décor, and clothes and shoes that you need versus items that you may want . It also means buying these items to fit your personal style, and choosing items you can use or wear long term.
  • Don’t shop trends unless you really, truly feel like something brings joy to your life. It’s okay to let some trends slip by. (It’s also okay to buy something that fits today’s trends if it also fits your style and you plan to use it for years. Mid-century modern is back in style, but it’s been the aesthetic for some of us for a couple of decades.)
  • Shop from sustainable sources, sustainable brands, and secondhand whenever you can. This ensures your money is being well-spent.

Avoiding Impulse Buys

You might have some clothes in your closet that you’ve never worn, or some coasters you’ve never used, or some kitchen gadgets that haven’t seen the light of day since you brought them home. These are likely items that were impulse buys. Through your minimalist decluttering, you’ve hopefully gotten rid of these items (see the other chapters in this Part), but without shopping intentionally, you could fall into old habits and buy unnecessary items.

Most of the time, your shopping probably isn’t urgent. You may have a specific event you’re preparing for, or something important breaks and needs to be replaced quickly. But we’re guessing that like us, many times you can get sucked into today’s “grab stuff before it’s gone” shopping mentality, which creates an artificial urgency to purchase items.

How do you figure out whether you really need a particular item? To determine whether the item you’ve got your eye on makes the cut, ask yourself these questions:

  • Do I really need the item I’m about to buy?
  • Am I wanting this item just because it’s on sale and too good to pass up?
  • Do I have something similar? (How many throw blankets does one household need, anyway?)
  • Do I have something else that can fill this need? (For example, a special strainer you attach to a saucepan might strain pasta water successfully, but so can a carefully placed lid.)
  • If it’s clothing, where will I wear this outfit? Does it fit my lifestyle, or will it collect dust in my closet because I have nowhere to wear it?
  • Do I have shoes to match it? Do I need any particular undergarments or accessories? Think practically.
  • Does it feel comfortable? Consider that every item of clothing you buy should have the potential to be worn 30 times. And you will only wear clothes multiple times if you’re comfortable wearing them.

Making Some Lists, Checking Them Twice

A great way for you to stay minimalist while shopping is to make shopping lists and do your best to stick to them. Buy only what you need. Making your list is easy, but you need to put some time into it so the items on the list are worth being there.

Avoiding repeat purchases

Before making a list of items you need to buy, first make sure you know what you already have. If you’ve already pared down your clothing and your household items (see the other chapters in this Part for guidance), you may have a good sense of what you currently own. If you haven’t recently minimalized your possessions, or if it’s been months or even years since you first did so, take a look around your home and your closet to refresh yourself on what you own and what clothing or aesthetic works for you. (You don’t want to buy stuff that you already have or buy things that haven’t worked for you. That’s how clutter and other unneeded items build up.)

Getting inspiration

If you’ve identified a need but you’re not sure how to fill it — your corner lamp just broke, but you don’t know what style you want to replace it with — you may need some inspiration. The Pinterest app is a great place to get visual inspiration, and you can create mood boards if you save the images or websites (therefore pinning them) you like. These mood boards serve as digital collages of images for your ideas that you can refer back to when needed.

When it comes to clothing, we get a lot of inspiration from casually watching people and noting how they put outfits together. We also get some inspiration from social media and from mannequins in stores. If you’re considering a capsule wardrobe (wardrobes with selections of interchangeable clothes that complement each other), do an Internet, Pinterest, or social media search for “capsule wardrobe essentials” for ideas.

Check your closet to see if you have anything similar to what you’ve saved to your clothing mood board. You may be lucky and find that you can shop your closet to create the look you’re going for.

Separating your needs from your wants

We organize our lists into two categories: our “needs” and our “nice-to-haves.” The needs are essential items that are the core of a space (think seating, lighting, storage opportunities, for example) or are foundational clothing pieces that work well for many outfits (including black pants, button-up shirts, layering pieces, or a winter coat). These may be essential items that, if absent for any reason, make life more cumbersome or awkward.

The nice-to-haves have less priority. Be very thoughtful about these items as you keep your minimalist vision in mind. Ask yourself the questions in the section “ Avoiding Impulse Buys ” to see if they meet your requirements for owning. If a nice-to-have item fits the bill and is something that fits within your minimalist approach, then get it — these are great items to thrift.

Researching your purchases before buying

Now that you’ve made a shopping list, it’s time to find out where to get the stuff on the list, focusing on quality (and of course your budget). Higher quality lasts longer and often looks better, which minimizes the need to replace them as often. You shouldn’t be rushing to shop. Look at reputable businesses, customer reviews, and guidance from friends or online experts. Then for costly purchases and clothing, go to the store, if a brick-and-mortar one is nearby, and check out the items in person. Sit on furniture, test out lighting, pick up storage containers or cooking vessels, try on clothes to see how they feel. You want to make sure if you buy it, you know it fits your needs and you’ll use it regularly.

Keep your list handy, preferably on a notes app on your smartphone (as you’ll usually have your phone with you), so if you’re out and about and are tempted to buy something, you can check your list to determine whether you really need that item.

Becoming a Slow Shopper

Practice being a slow shopper; take time to think about items you’re considering buying. If you’re shopping online, pin them to a Pinterest board or save your searches. Then, come back to them later.

If you’re shopping in person, take the time to test out or try on the item. If possible, go when stores aren’t too busy so you can shop more leisurely. Ask the store to hold your purchases, go out for a coffee, and think about whether you want to go through with the purchase.

Get an accountability partner — phone a (practical!) friend and ask for their opinion. Our friend Brian texts his sister whenever he goes shopping and sends a picture to her of what he’s thinking about buying. Brian’s sister asks him for information on the price, the brand, the make and model, the fabric, and other important details, and how Brian’s planning to use it or where he’ll wear it. These questions help his sister advise Brian whether to buy the item and help him stay accountable to his minimalist commitment.

Here’s a-not-so-fun fact: A lot of that stuff that people are rushing for ends up on resale or returned. According to Forbes ( www.forbes.com ) , Black Friday sales returns are estimated at about 30 percent or even up to 50 percent for some brands.

HITTING PAUSE ON YOUR PURCHASING

Sometimes you just need a clean break to help yourself reset. When it comes to shopping while sticking to our minimalist principles, we find that we can reduce temptations by doing shopping fasts. A shopping fast entails committing to no-buy months, meaning you won’t buy any household items, clothes, or shoes for a specified period of time. It can be one month or three months, or whatever duration feels right to you.

Of course, shopping fasts can be challenging, especially for those who used to spend a lot of time shopping and are trying to establish new habits. Here are some tips to help make shopping fasts work:

  • Schedule time for other fun activities to keep you busy.
  • Announce your fast to your shopping buddies and family. Tell them not to invite you to shop or send you shopping links.
  • (Re)organize your closet. You may appreciate what you have or be reunited with forgotten gems. (See Chapter 5 for pointers.)
  • If you’re active on social media, join a fun styling challenge that requires you to style a few clothes in multiple ways. You’ll see the potential in what you already own.

On the flip side, avoid shopping with that friend or family member who encourages impulse purchases. You may love these special people, but they’ll probably only get you into shopping trouble!

Scheduling Your Shopping Trips

Some of us love to shop; some of us hate it. Being the latter doesn’t mean that you buy less. Maybe you buy more to avoid shopping again. Whatever love/hate relationship you have with shopping, the key is to make it intentional, whether you’re shopping for food, clothes, cars, or vacations.

Have in mind what you need, what you already have, and of course your budget and purpose. (See “ Making Some Lists, Checking Them Twice ” for pointers.) If you’re shopping aimlessly, browsing racks or showrooms and particularly if you can’t remember what you already have, you’re likely to buy something you don’t need.

Schedule your trips based on your lists and the research you’ve done (see “ Researching your purchases before buying ”). And as simply as that, you’ll be a lot less likely to buy on impulse and overbuy what you already have. Avoiding shopping as an activity, and knowing what you need and where to get it, will help you be intentional with all your purchases from this day forward.

Detoxing Your Social Media and Inbox

You’re probably bombarded with ads from small businesses and large ones alike, as well as shopping hauls from social media influencers on TikTok, YouTube, and Instagram — we certainly are. Clever marketing and good photography can tempt you to buy things that you don’t need.

Reduce your digital temptations by focusing on your inbox and your social media feeds. Unfollow the accounts that promote frivolous spending, especially for trendy, lower-quality (but oh-so-budget-friendly) items.

Another place to detox is your inbox. Unsubscribe from marketing emails from shops and influencers, all of which can be tempting. Some may even send you special discount codes, saying that they miss you and inviting you to come back and shop. They don’t miss you. They miss your money, and it’s a trap. Don’t give in! When you need something, you’ll find your way back to the right shops, and there may be discounts available at the time.

Diversify your social media feed. Reduce the content you see daily related to buying stuff and give thought to other things that bring you joy. For us, that’s comedy, photography, and travel. You can also delete social media apps if that’s a more effective way for you to avoid temptations.

Train your social media feed to show you less about buying goods or clothes. When you stop liking and following such content, the algorithms will send less of this content to you and more content related to what you are liking or commenting on (we’ve gotten some great ideas for travel destinations this way).

You can also follow minimalist groups on social media. This gives you a chance to tap into additional ideas and pointers from people who’re on the same journey as you are. See the nearby sidebar for inspiration.

FINDING A MINIMALIST COMMUNITY

Because of the way our society consumes goods (including fashion), scaling back your shopping can be a pretty big lifestyle change. It’s completely normal if you need some community support to keep you motivated on your minimalist path. Social media is a good place to find some like-minded minimalists. We’ve built a community with people halfway around the world, whom we continue to learn from and have become very friendly with. You can find engaged communities on Instagram and Facebook. You can follow minimalist bloggers and thrifted style bloggers, as well — find them using relatable hashtags such as #minimalismblogger, #thriftedstyle, and #slowshoppingbloggers. You can also join relevant local Facebook and other social media groups.

Scheduling your online shopping

Just as we advise scheduling your physical shopping trips, we suggest you schedule your online shopping as well.

Scheduling allows you to think ahead, plan what you actually need, and avoid making random purchases. Since we schedule everything else in life, why don’t we do the same with online shopping as well?

The best way we’ve found to schedule online shopping is to physically put it on your calendar. This helps you time-block your shopping so you don’t spend endless hours scrolling and purchasing. Give yourself a block of time — for example, 30 minutes — after which you’ll stop browsing. If you’re using a digital calendar already, that’s a bonus: You can enter the websites you plan to buy from in the notes, so when a window pops up for your 30 minutes of online shopping, you already know where to go.

As with in-person shopping, we recommend you do some research before you buy, but make sure to set time limits for your research. You can easily get lost in analysis paralysis and end up wasting time and being more confused than when you started. Before you research, know your needs and budget, and then set a timeline to decide.

Be aware that your research behavior online may influence your social media feeds, so that tooth-whitening solution you’ve been checking out may suddenly result in social media posts selling various smile-brightening products.

Staying disciplined about sticking to our minimalist principles can be a challenge sometimes. Which is why scheduling your shopping is so important: If you encounter an ad while looking at your niece’s softball playoffs but it’s not time to shop, it’s easier to scroll past the digital interloper.

Unlinking your digital payment methods

When shopping digitally, having all your credit cards and PayPal info saved to your smart phone or favorite websites can be a great time-saver, but it can also be extremely costly to your wallet and to your desire to stay minimalist and clutter-free.

To help you be thoughtful and intentional about your purchases, unlink your payment methods to any site you frequent. If you want something and have carefully assessed your need for it, you can input your credit card information at the time of purchase. If you’re tempted to make an impulse purchase, and the prompt asks you for a credit card but you’re not willing to get up and get your wallet (or you change your mind on the way from your computer to your credit card), you likely didn’t need the item in the first place.

Caring for Your Clothes to Help Them Last

Once you buy something, the most minimalist thing you can do is take care of it so it lasts. It saves you money and means that less stuff needlessly ends up in landfills. Knowing that you’re doing your best to keep your possessions in good shape is a good feeling. Wear and tear happens, but excessive wear and tear can be prevented with good care.

We’ve picked up a lot of tips over the years on how to look after our clothes, and we share them with you in this section. How you do your laundry and how you store your clothes really matters. Treat the clothes in your closet with care and devotion, and you’ll be doing your wallet and your nervous system a favor.

Doing laundry with longevity in mind

When you think about wear and tear, you probably think of the damage that happens as a result of wearing your clothes, such as rips and tears, but your clothes take quite a beating in the washer and dryer. Clothes are not supposed to last forever, but they shouldn’t be ruined after a few washes either. Granted, there are too many poor-quality clothes out there that won’t last in any case, but laundry habits and machine-washings contribute to cumulative damage of even good quality clothes. Anyone with a shrunken cashmere sweater from machine-washing knows what I mean.

Your clothes suffer laundry-related damage from machine agitation (in the washer and dryer), over-washing, and heat (from hot water and hot dryers). When your clothes spin in a washer, they get some damage, and they get even more when they spin in your dryer. Don’t believe us? Check your lint tray and you’ll find all the proof you need. All that dust in the dryer lint tray is accumulated fibers from micro tears as clothes tumble dry. Hot water fades clothes, and the heat from the dryer shrinks clothes. Add to that a tendency to wash clothes more frequently than they need to be, and it becomes evident how laundering clothes can be a source of damage.

The point is that laundry habits need to be designed to minimize damage to garments so that they can last longer.

Making sense of laundry labels

Before you wash your clothes, you need to check the label for instructions. Labels let you know whether the clothes are machine-washable or must be hand-washed or dry-cleaned and whether they can be tumble dried or must be line dried.
