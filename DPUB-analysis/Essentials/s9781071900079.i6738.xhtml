Leading Change in a VUCA World

478 Deloitte’s “2024 Global Human Capital Trends” survey of over 14,000 respondents from 95 countries found that technological change due to the emergence of AI, virtual realities, and even virtual avatars of ourselves have become an everyday reality. This is a time of uncertainty shaped by global events, advances in technology, changing workplace culture, and increased employee mental health and well-being concerns. The shift to hybrid or totally remote work has transformed the workplace, but 85% of leaders say it is challenging to have confidence in employee productivity. And only 3% of the respondents said their workplace was well equipped to capture the value they create. This has created an environment of tremendous change for organizations and their leaders. 1 The VUCA framework is a way for leaders to formulate a response to organizational change. The term characterizes change as presenting volatility, uncertainty, complexity, or ambiguity. VUCA is an acronym first used in 1987 by the U.S. Army War College in anticipation of changes in the world’s political landscape. The end of the Cold War brought a need to revise how the military approached strategic leadership during change. The army used it to describe the level of chaos that could be expected on the future battlefield. 2 VUCA stands for the following:

  • Volatility—unexpected or unstable and of unknown duration; the nature and speed of change
  • Uncertainty—insufficient information for decision-making; lack of predictability and the element of surprise
  • Complexity—a large number of interconnected parts that each introduce a number of variables to the situation; confounding of issues, no cause-and-effect chain
  • Ambiguity—situations require experimentation to determine the best of many possible paths forward; reality is unclear, and potential for misreading situations. 3

Planned Organizational Change

The forces for organizational change have resulted in the need for organizations to be proactive rather than reactive in reading the environment they operate in. Planned organizational change can have a number of targets, including structure, technology, processes, teams, and people. 12 The idea of being proactive when it comes to change is not new and dates to the classic 1990 management book Overcoming Organizational Defenses . 13 Yet most organizations still are in reactive mode (i.e., “putting out fires”) when it comes to change. Change may also be incremental (e.g., adding upgraded telephoto cameras to mobile phones) or radical (e.g., a major restructuring). 14 , 15 Incremental change is evolutionary, and radical change is revolutionary. Revolutionary change and evolutionary change are different in terms of the scope and rate of upheaval. Revolutionary change occurs quickly and affects virtually all parts of the organization at the same time, whereas evolutionary change occurs slowly and gradually and may involve only one area of the organization at a time. 16 An example of a revolutionary change is Ford Motor Company’s shift to producing affordable electric cars. The company invested heavily in the growing electric vehicle business to create more affordable choices for customers that generate lower CO 2 emissions. The planned change required adjusting the company’s North America production to offer a range of options with lower prices and longer ranges. In its fully electric portfolio, Ford will prioritize a new digitally advanced commercial van in 2026, followed by two new advanced pickup trucks in 2027. To accomplish this, Ford also realigned its U.S. battery sourcing plan to reduce costs, maximize capacity utilization, and support current and future electric vehicle production. 17 This change affected nearly every aspect of the organization. Putting these concepts together (reactive/proactive and incremental/radical) results in a useful framework for classifying the types of organizational change (see Figure 15.2 ). As shown in the figure, the most intense form of change is proactive and radical, and it is often when an organization engages in planned organizational change that affects all organizational subsystems, which is discussed next.

Organizational Subsystems Involved in Planned Change

Planned organizational change involves four organizational subsystems:

  1. Formal organization. This provides the coordination and control necessary for organized activity; examples are formal structures and reward systems.
  2. Social factors. These factors include individual differences, team interactions, and the organizational culture.
  3. 482 Technology. This is how raw materials and inputs transform into outputs, such as work flow design and job design.
  4. Physical setting. These are the characteristics of the physical space and how it is arranged. 18

An example of planned change affecting subsystems is Starbucks’s goal to eliminate single-use cups by 2030. Starbucks aims to reduce waste, encourage customers to use reusable cups, and contribute to a more sustainable economy. Here are the steps in their planned change: 19

  1. Assessment and Goal Setting:
    • Starbucks has recognized the environmental impact of its single-use cups, which are lined with plastic and difficult to recycle.
    • The company set a long-term goal to phase out single-use cups globally by 2030, with an intermediate goal of cutting waste by 50% by 2030.
  2. Reusable Cup Trials:
    • In select markets, Starbucks introduces the Borrow A Cup program, where customers can borrow reusable cups and return them for cleaning and reuse.
    • Tests include implementing cup return stations at stores, offering discounts to customers who use reusable cups and partnering with companies that specialize in cleaning and redistributing cups.
    • Customers are charged a small deposit for each reusable cup, which they can retrieve upon returning it.
  3. In-Store Adjustments:
    • Starbucks piloted operational changes by offering ceramic mugs and glassware for in-store dining and encouraging more customers to bring their own cups.
    • In select stores, disposable cup use is limited or eliminated entirely.
    • 483 The company invests in upgrading store infrastructure, including dishwashing capabilities and additional storage for reusable cups.
  4. Expansion:
    • After successful trials, Starbucks will expand the reusable cup program to more stores in North America, Europe, and Asia.
    • Starbucks will collaborate with local waste management and sustainability organizations to ensure the program fits within local recycling and reuse systems.
  5. Customer Education and Incentives:
    • Starbucks will continue to increase customer education through in-store signage, digital platforms, and marketing campaigns explaining the environmental benefits of reusable cups.
    • They will offer financial incentives, such as discounts for customers who bring their own cup or use the Borrow A Cup service.
    • Starbucks introduces a loyalty program that rewards sustainable behavior, like using reusable cups multiple times.
  6. Monitoring:
    • Starbucks will monitor the effectiveness of the reusable cup program through customer feedback, waste audits, and analysis of overall waste reduction metrics.
    • The company will refine the program based on results from different regions, scaling it globally while adapting to local waste management infrastructures.

This change demonstrates the Starbucks commitment to sustainability through a structured, long-term strategy and is a major change for their organization. The Starbucks commitment to planned change is supported by research. A study found that outcomes of change efforts are different under unplanned and planned change. A study of employees in two different organizations, one undergoing an unplanned change and one undergoing a planned change, revealed that trust related to less resistance to change. However, in the unplanned change situation, trust had no effect on the proactive component of agility. In contrast, in a planned change situation, trust had a positive effect on the proactive component of agility. These results were explained by the presence (or lack of) resistance to change respectively. 20

Organizational Development

484 Organizational development (OD) is a collection of social psychology methods employed to improve organizational effectiveness and employee well-being. OD reflects the insight that “there is nothing so practical as a good theory.” 21 Organizational behavior (OB) is an applied science, and theories of OB are applied in organizational settings through OD interventions. Also, the researchers of the Hawthorne studies (described in Chapter 1 of this book) found paying attention to workers increased productivity. Thus, OB can be considered the theory and research that underlie the practical application of OD interventions in the workplace. There is a difference between theories of change and theories of changing . 22 Theories of change try to answer the question of how and why change occurs (e.g., the impact of the Affordable Care Act on employee benefit plans). Theories of changing focus on how to implement successful organizational change (e.g., enhancing the innovationl climate). Academics tend to write about theories of change, and management practitioners tend to write about theories of changing. 23 This chapter focuses on theories of changing with an emphasis on the leader’s role in creating and sustaining organizational change. A review of research on OD concluded that effective leadership is essential for the development of a clear value base for the change effort. It’s about the future direction and values, as well as the “why” regarding the need for change. 24 Organizational leaders have employed a variety of organizational change interventions. Specific examples of OD interventions follow.

Examples of Organizational Development Interventions

Survey feedback is one of the most commonly employed OD techniques. Refer to Table 4.1 in Chapter 4 for an example of a measure of job satisfaction often used in survey feedback. Data are collected from employees regarding their attitudes toward work, and their confidentiality is assured. These data are then analyzed and reported back to the organization as group averages. For example, a survey report might state that 55% of the employees surveyed indicate that they don’t trust the top management team of the organization. This report becomes a starting point for further OD efforts, which may result in task forces to address the concerns identified in the survey. Most large organizations today conduct attitude surveys of their employees—typically on an annual basis. Participation in surveys should be organization-wide to ensure that the survey results accurately reflect employee attitudes. 25 Surveys are a key tool for understanding important organizational issues.

  2. The manager leaves, and the employees work together for about 2 days on the problem.
  3. The manager returns, and the employees report their proposals to solve the problem.
  4. On the spot, the manager must accept the proposals, decline them, or ask for more information.

If more information is requested, a process to make a final decision must be articulated. Resistance to Change

When faced with an organizational change, employee reactions range from resistance to compliance to commitment to change . Resistance means that the employees fight the change and may try to undermine it. 52 In compliance, they simply go along with the change but secretly hope that it is a program that will come to an end soon. Commitment to change is the most desirable reaction in which the employees support change and help the organization implement it. 53 Since resistance is the most difficult reaction for the leader to deal with, this section focuses on understanding and overcoming resistance to change.

Research on resistance to change dates back to a classic 1948 study of participative decision-making conducted with a strong research design. 54 Employees were transferred to new jobs in which they encountered significant changes to their work. Interviews revealed employees resisted changes in their work methods due to resentment, frustration, and a loss of hope of regaining their former levels of proficiency. A second experiment was then conducted in which the control group was transferred to new jobs, but they were allowed to participate in the changes through chosen representatives; this group recovered rapidly and reached a higher level of productivity compared to the group that was not allowed to participate in the change. 55 This study shows that OD can impact resistance to change through an intervention that increased employee participation in the process. A recent review of 70 years of research on resistance to change concluded that the findings of this classic study have held up over time and through subsequent research studies. 56 Lack of participation and input into the change is one reason employees resist change. There are a number of additional reasons why people resist change that include both personal reasons and organizational reasons. 57 , 58 , 59 Personal reasons include habit, security, economic, and fear of the unknown. Organizational reasons for resistance are structural inertia (the structure is too rigid to support the change), group inertia, threats to expertise, and threats to established power relationships.

Resistance to change has a negative impact on employee health. It has been related to insomnia and lower employee well-being. 60 Using a longitudinal research design, a study 61 of 709 participants in 30 work units revealed that resistance to change predicted emotional exhaustion a year later. Organizations should offer coaching and training to cope with organizational change for employees who are highly resistant. The next section discusses additional methods for overcoming resistance to change.

How to Overcome Resistance to Change

488 A Harvard Business Review article offers the following guidelines to help overcome resistance to change: 62

  1. Educate followers. Communicating reduces misinformation about the change and helps convince employees that change is needed.
  2. Participation matters, as demonstrated by the participative decision-making study described in the previous section. People are more likely to accept changes that they help design.
  3. Building support and commitment reduces resistance because employees have the support through counseling or sabbaticals to ease the strain. Commitment to the organization also increases commitment to change.
  4. Develop positive relationships through trust in management increases commitment to organizational change. Research has shown that high-quality leader–member exchange (LMX) relationships reduce resistance. 63 , 64 Leaders provide explanations to followers that help them cope with change and commit to it. 65
  5. Implementing changes fairly improves the chances that employees will accept change. As reviewed in Chapter 8, organizational justice is a major concern of most employees, and it becomes even more important during change.
  6. Select people who are open to change since research shows that people have personality traits that enable them to be more flexible when it comes to coping with change . 66

The article also includes the following tactics, but they may backfire and should only be used as last resorts:

  1. Manipulation and co-optation tactics are sometimes used in organizational transitions because they are relatively less expensive than the tactics listed previously. Manipulation occurs when change agents use underhanded techniques such as the selective sharing of information and careful staging of events. Co-optation is basically “buying” the support of those who are needed. For example, members of a task force for change would be paid a bonus for serving on it.
  2. Coercion should be used rarely, if at all, as discussed in the Power and Politics chapter of this book (Chapter 7). Direct threats of loss of status, pay, or other things that employees care about may gain short-term compliance, but these tactics will rarely gain commitment to the change.

489 Research has shown the the resistance to change of leaders and resistance at the organization level (i.e., the collective) influence individual resistance to change. 67 At the organization level, readiness for change refers to organizational members’ shared resolve to implement a change (change commitment) and shared belief in their collective capability to do so (change efficacy). 68 Leadership is necessary to create organizational readiness for change. 69 Leaders can address resistance early and improve the chances of a positive and productive organizational change outcome. Offering employees an inclusive, empathetic, and considerate environment can lead to early organizational buy-in and change acceptance. 70 Transformational leadership reduces follower resistance to change and increases their extra effort. 490 A survey of 199 employees and their supervisors found ethical leadership matters in the context of organizational change. 74 Technology changes were the change that was most frequently listed as having the most impact, followed by downsizing, and then restructuring. Other changes reported were relocations, mergers, process changes, and people-centered changes. These changes have a significant impact on employees’ regular work routines and cause stress. If an employee senses that their leader is behaving unethically, this may exacerbate stress and result in lower performance. Followers need to be able to trust the integrity of their leaders during change. In addition, the degree to which employees felt that they were involved in the organization’s change process made a difference as well. For example, having leaders discuss changes with employees results in higher job satisfaction, performance, and organizational citizenship behavior during organizational change. The relationship of ethical leadership and resistance to change is explained by followers having a high degree of moral disengagement and being sensitive to unfairness. For example, after a mass layoff, an employee who was directly involved may try to reframe their involvement by (a) portraying it as the ultimate best for the company (moral justification), deliberately not calling it a layoff but an “outplacement”; (b) minimizing their role in the execution of the layoff by claiming they had to follow orders; or (c) devaluating the individuals affected by their involvement—for example, through deliberately avoiding thinking of the layoff candidates as human beings whose lives will be severely impacted. However, when ethical leadership is higher, employees are less likely to engage in such rationalizations. Ethical leaders offer positive communication in times of change and thereby encourage employees’ change commitment, which subsequently reduces their dysfunctional resistance. The organization plays a role as well: Research shows that human resource practices that focused on commitment reduced resistance to change, but this depended on high levels of ethical leadership.

Leading Change

After the issue of resistance to change is anticipated, analyzed, and addressed, the next step in changing organizations is to implement the change process through effective leadership. Next, the models of leading others through change are discussed.

Lewin’s Three-Step Model

491 Kurt Lewin’s three-step model, developed in the 1940s, is the starting point for understanding the fundamental process of leading change. As shown in Figure 15.3 , there are three steps in the change process: unfreezing , changing , and refreezing . First, unfreezing challenges the status quo by shaking up assumptions; next, changing represents movement toward a new desired state. Finally, refreezing the changes by reinforcing and restructuring is the third phase to make the changes permanent. Think about an ice pack that you use when you have a sports injury. When you take it out of the freezer, it is hard and can’t be changed much. As you use it, it becomes soft and malleable (this is the changing phase). After 20 minutes, you put it back in the freezer and it becomes solid again. Change is like this: When people and systems are in the frozen stage, you can’t change them. You have to literally “heat things up” to soften the attitudes and assumptions about change so that the system is malleable like a defrosted ice pack. Once you have the changes you want in place, you reinforce the new behaviors with rewards or change the structure to support the change (refreezing).

Employees will tend toward the status quo or equilibrium, so there needs to be constant attention to refreezing the new system and behaviors after a change is implemented. For example, an organization that wants to implement teams must first challenge the old assumptions regarding working alone and getting rewarded for individual effort. A new organizational chart is presented to employees showing teams the new way that work will be organized. Some employees may resist the team concept and even leave the organization as the change is implemented. There may be storms and team conflict (things are heating up). Once there are successful teams in place, the new approach can be refrozen by offering team rewards and reinforcing the new organizational chart.

492 Leadership makes a difference: A study of a planned organizational change in a hospital system found that authentic leadership influenced the processes of unfreezing, change, and refreezing. 75 An interview study of 15 top executives who had been involved in significant organizational change found that it is necessary to balance the use of power with the autonomy of employees during change. One executive commented, “Leading change involves building social relationships to mobilize resources to create the power I need to make changes.” 76 Change leaders should concentrate their resources on a few positively or negatively influential individuals and take advantage of communication networks to persuade and inform others to help with their change adoption. By partnering with opinion leaders in the organization, leaders can facilitate each player’s proper role in the change effort. 77

As this example illustrates, the Lewin model is still relevant today. A review of research evidence on organizational change over the past several decades concluded that “rather than being outdated or redundant, Lewin’s approach is still relevant to the modern world.” 78 A review of this model concluded that this approach emphasizes mitigating employee resistance to change by indicating two significant factors in an organization: The driving forces that inspire workers to adapt to the change and the restraining forces that make the employees hesitant to welcome any change. 79 Lewin’s approach is based upon field theory and the analysis of forces for and against organizational change. This approach is discussed next.

Force Field Analysis

To implement organizational change using the three-step model, a force field analysis of the forces for and against an organizational change should be conducted. 80 The steps in force field analysis follow:

  1. Define the problem (current state) and the target situation (target state).
  2. List forces working for and against the desired changes.
  3. Rate the strength of each force.
  4. Draw a diagram (the length of line denotes strength of the force).
  5. Indicate how important each force is.
  6. List how to strengthen each important supporting force.
  7. List how to weaken each important resisting force.
  8. Identify resources needed to support forces for change and reduce forces against change.
  9. Make an action plan: timing, milestones, and responsibilities.

This model elaborates on the Lewin model of change and provides specific guidelines for changing organizations. 81 As the figure indicates, each step builds on the previous one, and descriptions of these steps follow. 82

  1. 493 Establish a sense of urgency. Change typically begins with leaders noticing challenges the organization faces. The threat of losing ground in some way sparks these people into action, and they, in turn, try to communicate that sense of urgency to others. Leaders begin a frank discussion of potentially unpleasant facts, such as increased competition, lower earnings, or decreasing market share. Most leaders are then convinced that business as usual is no longer acceptable.
  2. Form a powerful guiding coalition. Change efforts may start with just one or two people who begin to convince others that change is needed. In this step, an initial core of believers is assembled (this group should be powerful in terms of their roles, reputations, and skills). This coalition for change needs to have three to five people.
  3. Create a vision. A compelling “picture” of the future must be created. This vision motivates people and keeps the change processes aligned. This vision needs to be communicated to employees clearly and effectively in 5 minutes or less.
  4. Communicate the vision. Regardless of how much communication leaders think is needed, they should multiply that by 10. Every possible communication channel should be utilized including the CEO’s speech, meetings, newsletters, email, and face-to-face conversation as examples. Employees will be looking to see that the leaders in the organization are behaving consistently with the vision.
  5. Empower others to act on the vision. Employees should be allowed to participate in making changes in their areas. This may require restructuring work and allowing key people the time to focus on the change effort. Also, this involves providing the money and support needed to bring about change.
  6. Plan for and create short-term wins. Change takes time, and change efforts sometimes lose momentum as frustrations set in with employees. Plan and execute celebrations of short-term successes to reinforce people’s efforts and maintain their commitment and motivation. This provides proof to employees that their efforts are working.
  7. Consolidate improvements and sustain the momentum for change. A change process can take 5 to 10 years—as force field analysis shows, the forces against change such as resistance may result in regression to the prior ways of doing things. Leaders should resist the temptation to declare the change a success too soon. Leaders must enter the process believing that their efforts will take years.
  8. Institutionalize the new approaches. A leader will know that the change is frozen in place when followers believe that it is “the way we do things around here.” Employees understand that their efforts have led to a different outcome for the organization. The organization’s leaders serve as role models for the new ways.

Despite some criticisms that the model has not kept pace with the current rate of organizational change, the Kotter model has been shown to be a useful road map for guiding organizational change. 83 Research has also shown that there are other elements that help ensure successful organizational change. In the next section, the research evidence on change implementation is reviewed, and it generally supports what Kotter envisioned in the 8-step model.

Stress in the Context of Organizational Change

Stress has been rising at the workplace due to organizational change. Three research studies 91 conducted using different research methods found that change is more likely to lead to stress when the change has consequences that threaten employees’ sense of identity. This effect is explained by employees’ feelings of uncertainty. Change increases uncertainty and disrupts employees’ regular work routines. 92 A qualitative interview study 93 conducted in a district health board undergoing change found that staff unhappiness with some organizational processes occurred when they were not consulted or informed, and where they suffered from uncertainty, heavy workloads, and inadequate support. The new organizational reality is one of competition, with mergers, acquisitions, downsizings, and restructurings. These forces have increased psychological strain from work. 94 Uncertainty during organizational change may be related to layoffs, pay cuts, fewer promotion opportunities, and changes to the culture of the organization (the importance of organizational culture was discussed in Chapter 14). 95 Change uncertainty is defined as uncertain negative cognitive outcomes of a high level of perceived change excessiveness among employees. 96 It is created by strategic, structural, and job-related factors. 97 The uncertainty about outcomes and the perceived importance of these outcomes combine to produce this type of stress. 98 For example, a hospital was studied 99 as it underwent a major divisional consolidation. Employees were surveyed before and after the change was implemented. Results showed stress was related to job dissatisfaction, intent to quit, and work-related irritation after the consolidation. However, organizational commitment reduced the stress effect.

Organizations are stressed due to environmental pressures to compete or even stay out of bankruptcy in some cases. This translates into stress experienced by individuals as organizations try to adapt to the demands of their environment. Organizational change affects all aspects of work design due to increases in shift work, night and weekend work, and overtime work, as well as demands of employees being asked to work faster and not having enough time to finish their work. 100 The intensification of work (fast pace and constant change) over the past 30 years has also resulted in strained relationships with customers, coworkers, and supervisors. 101 It has been noted that “change is difficult for an institution and for its employees. There is uncertainty about the future, about what the organization will ‘look like,’ and how the employees feel they will fit into the new structure.” 102

496 Change creates job insecurity, which leads to emotional exhaustion during change. 103 Thus, employees may resist the change efforts. Such resistance is one of the most common sources of work stress. 104 A study followed the level of perceived stress over time and found that stress increased during the 2008–2009 economic recession, particularly for college-educated white men aged 45 to 64 years who were employed full-time. 105 This may be due to threat of job loss, actual job loss, or the loss of retirement funds during the recession. A study of an organization going through layoffs found that active listening by leaders predicted a decrease in job insecurity due to an increase in employees’ perceived control. 106

Both organizations and individuals can take actions to alleviate the stress due to change. 107 One study of a consolidation of human resource functions across divisions of an Australian public service organization found organizations can manage the frequency of change and the planning involved in change. 108 An important finding of this study was that supportive leadership made a difference in the reports of stress during the change. Managers need to understand how to deal with the acceleration of change to overcome the negative consequences for individuals. 109 During the COVID-19 pandemic, employee stress increased due to job insecurity and the rapid acceleration of the digital transformation. A research study during the pandemic found that leaders’ transparent communication reduced the organizational change uncertainty, and this was explained by employee coping. 110 Employees need such support to deal with the stress of organizational change. We all think we know what stress is and how to cope with it. Take the following stress fact-or-fiction quiz to see how much you know about stress:

  2. You always know when you are under stress.

If you answered true to even one, you’re a victim of a stress myth. 112 Psychological job strain is defined as the combination of greater psychological job demands and lower job control . 113 This results in organizational stress , which has been shown to have serious consequences to employees in terms of their well-being and health. Over half (55%) of all workers suffer from moderate to severe stress while on the job, according to a survey reported by the American Institute of Stress, and about 83% of employees report that they feel emotionally drained from work. 114 Mental health costs the U.S. economy more than $280 billion annually, stymying investment, productivity, and wealth accumulation, among other measures of progress. 115 A survey of employees from different industries in North America found that the major cause of stress in the workplace is workload (41% of respondents). This was followed by people issues (32%), balancing work and personal life (18%), and lack of job security (9%). 116 Other statistics include the following:

  • According to 41% of employees, stress leads to a decrease in productivity.
  • Stress makes 33% of employees feel less engaged.
  • 498 Stress has made 15% of workers look for a new job.
  • More than half of the workers frequently skip lunch because of work, increasing their stress. 117

The estimates for the cost of stress also include lower productivity due to job burnout as well as workplace accidents. Job burnout “is a prolonged response to chronic stressors on the job.” 118 Symptoms of burnout are exhaustion, cynicism, detachment from work, and feelings of ineffectiveness or failure. 119 Burnout is a serious problem for many employees and affects their attitudes, motivation, and job performance. The COVID-19 pandemic significantly impacted stress, especially among those aged 18 to 34, increasing from 26% in 2019 to 34% in 2023. This age group also saw the highest increase in mental health diagnoses, from 31% in 2019 to 45% in 2023. 120 Figure 15.5 shows stress levels pre- and post-COVID for different age groups.

499 The COVID-19 pandemic increased workplace stress. A survey 121 of 3,023 adults found that the economy and work were significant stressors for more Americans during the COVID-19 pandemic. The economy was reported as a significant source of stress for 70% of the respondents. Research also found that during uncertain situations, such as the COVID-19 partial lockdown, maintaining satisfactory levels of employee performance was a concern for many organizations. A sample of 213 bank employees was collected during COVID, and study results revealed that COVID-19 stress had a negative impact on employee performance. 122

Stress Episode

The stress response “is the generalized, patterned, unconscious mobilization of the body’s natural energy resources when confronted with a demand or stressor.” 123 The nervous and hormonal systems of an individual are activated, producing adrenaline, and this may result in an elevated heart rate, perspiration, and tightening of the muscles. This is known as the fight-or-flight response , which is the result of human evolution in that the response was originally needed to defend oneself from predators or run away from them.

Strains resulting from the stressors reviewed in the previous section trigger a stress episode as depicted in Figure 15.6 . Responses may be physiological, psychological, and/or behavioral. 124 These strains are listed next with examples found in research on stress at the workplace (however, one stressful event may trigger one, two, or all three of the following strains for an employee).

  1. Physiological —high blood pressure, coronary heart disease, high cholesterol, stomach ulcers, compromised immune system
  2. Psychological —anxiety, burnout, emotional exhaustion, fatigue, hostility, irritation, tension, lower self-confidence and self-esteem
  3. Behavioral —accidents and errors, alcohol use, caffeine intake, drug use, smoking, workplace deviance (e.g., doing inferior work on purpose, stealing, damaging property)

A study 125 examined how the stress due to the pressure to perform relates to performance by looking at daily fluctuations in pressure at work. These fluctuations explain why performance pressure can be a double-edged sword, producing both bright and dark side effects for organizations. On a daily basis, performance pressure may be appraised as a threat but might also be seen as a challenge, which elicits engagement with the work. So can stress have a positive side? Next, research on the negative and positive impacts of stress is discussed.

Is There an Optimal Level of Stress?

500 Two studies revealed that people achieve higher performance on creative tasks when they have a moderate rather than low or high level of job stress. When they reported they were thriving, employees maintained a high level of creativity through knowledge acquisition and learning, regardless of how stressed they felt at work. 126 Some peoples thrive on stress, and there may be an optimum level of stress for performance for them, known as the Yerkes–Dodson law. 127 , 128 This law states that performance goes up with increasing stress to an optimum point, but then the stress becomes too much, and performance declines. Increased arousal can help improve performance but only to a certain point. At the point when arousal becomes excessive, performance diminishes. Psychologists Robert Yerkes and John Dillingham Dodson discovered that mild electrical shocks could be used to motivate rats to complete a maze, but when the electrical shocks became too strong, the rats would scurry around in random directions to escape. Increasing stress and arousal helps focus attention on tasks but only up to a certain point. For example, when you are studying for an exam, an optimal level of stress can help you concentrate and remember the correct answers while taking the exam. However, too much stress (“test anxiety”) impairs your ability to focus, and you start missing answers. Thus, there are two forms of stress: challenge-related and hindrance-related. 129 Challenge-related stress is when work demands lead a person to be energized and work harder, resulting in higher performance. Hindrance-related stress constrains or interferes with work performance.

Both forms of stress relate to psychological strain, but one study found that hindrance-related stress is more related to lessened loyalty and intentions to quit. 130 Hindrance-related stress also negatively affects learning. However, a challenging learning environment increases learning. 131 A review of 183 independent samples found support for the negative relationship of hindrance-related stress to job satisfaction and organizational commitment, and positive relationships with turnover intentions, turnover, and withdrawal behaviors (absenteeism and turnover). 132 Challenge stressors had the opposite effect on these outcomes.

501 Stress negatively impacts job performance and attitudes. Stress relates to lower job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and job involvement as well as higher frustration (these are psychological outcomes). Second, stress is related to more use of health care, sick days, and workers’ compensation claims (physical outcomes). Third, stress is related to lower job performance, more counterproductive behavior, and accidents (behavioral outcomes). 133

Stressors are situations and events that result in an employee experiencing strain. 134 The stressors found at work are physical, task-related, role-related, social, work schedule–related, career-related, traumatic events, and stressful change processes. 135 For example, some work has physical demands, such as the repetitive nature of factory work or transcription for a court reporter. Work schedule stress may be due to having to work the night shift in a hospital. Career-related stress may emanate from anxiety over whether a promotion will be given. Some of these stressors come from roles that employees must play in the organization.

Role Stress

Roles are the behaviors that are expected of a person in a particular organizational context. People learn their roles in organizations and “act” them out daily. Roles can be a source of stress in a number of ways because they place demands on a person to fulfill expectations. Role ambiguity occurs where there is a lack of specificity or predictability about what a person’s role is. 136 In short, the person does not know what is expected, which may be due to not understanding what a boss requires, for example. Role conflict occurs when there are incompatible demands regarding a person’s role. 137 For example, the boss may want an employee to work late, but their coworkers want them to leave when they do so they don’t look bad (known as intersender role conflict). Role conflict may also happen when organizational requirements conflict with personal values (person–role conflict). Interrole conflict occurs when a person holds multiple roles at the same time and feels torn by the demands of people who have varying expectations (role senders). For example, a student wants to please the professor by studying for and doing well on a midterm exam, but friends want to go out to dinner with the student the night before the exam.

502 Role overload is a third form of role-related stress, and it is caused by too much work, time pressure, and deadlines that a person feels unable to meet. 138 Role overload may be quantitative (the number of demands) or qualitative, which refers to an employee not having the qualifications to perform their work role. 139 A study 140 of 210 managers found that reducing all three forms of role stress (role ambiguity, role conflict, and role overload) and having social support play a crucial role in increasing job satisfaction. Role stress may also cause employees to act out at work. For example, a study of sales and service representatives 141 found that role conflict had a positive relationship with both interpersonal (e.g., making a racial joke at work) and organizational deviance (e.g., taking property from work). Role ambiguity was positively related, while role overload was negatively related, to organizational deviance. Role stress also affects job performance. A study of 227 hotel employees in Turkey 142 found that role ambiguity and role conflict are negatively associated with job performance; however, role overload is positively associated with job performance. Another study of 583 hotel managers found that role stress was related to burnout, which led to thoughts of quitting. Managers with high role stress did not consider resigning immediately unless they experienced a high degree of burnout. 143 The effects of role stress can “add up” to produce higher stress. In other words, role conflict, ambiguity, and overload produce higher levels of stress when they are combined. 144

Reconciling conflicts between work and family roles (known as work–life balance ) is a specific form of interrole conflict that has received a great deal of research. Work–life balance is when work is compatible with participation in family life. This conflict can have an important effect on the quality of both work and family life. 145 Research has indicated that conflicts between work and family roles negatively affect employee well-being. 146 , 147 It is not uncommon for employees to carry stress home with them from the workplace (known as the crossover stress effect ), and this affects family interactions. A study of 142 married police officers found that those who were experiencing stress were more likely to display anger, spend time away from the family, be uninvolved in family matters, and have unsatisfactory marriages. 148 Other research on working couples found burnout in one partner affects the other. 149 There is also family-to-work conflict , another form of the crossover effect, which occurs when family problems crossover to work, and research has found this affects job performance, increases psychological distress and alcohol abuse, and negatively impacts physical health. 150

Some students must work while they are in school, and work–school conflict may occur, which is defined as “the extent to which work interferes with school-related demands/responsibilities and has been shown to be negatively related to school performance and satisfaction.” 151 Of course, school and family conflict can combine to impact work outcomes. A study of 100 working married students 152 found that school-to-work and family-to-work conflict resulted in burnout and lower job satisfaction. This finding was more prevalent for women than men. However, the crossover from school to work may be beneficial. A study of 170 undergraduates 153 who were surveyed at several points in time during a semester found that having a good day at school was related to having a good day at work. Thus, employers may benefit from the performance gains and positive attitudinal shifts that stem from experiences of facilitation between roles.

A study of graduates in the early years of their career concluded that although graduates seek work–life balance, their concern for career success draws them into a situation where they work increasingly long hours and experience an increasingly unsatisfactory relationship between home and work. 154 This underscores the importance of organizational policy and practice to help employees manage the relationship between work and family. There are a number of sources of stress that come from outside the workplace, and Toolkit Activity 15.3 is an assessment where you can determine how much stress comes from events in your life.

Stress Is a Global Concern

503 The prevalence of work stress has been documented in numerous countries and regions, including the United Kingdom 155 Germany, 156 Sicily, 157 Saudi Arabia, 158 and China, 159 to name a few. Stress has been related to outcomes of lower job satisfaction and increased turnover intentions in other cultures as well. For example, a study of public-sector employees working in Botswana found stress was related to turnover. 160 This has raised the question of whether the stressor-strain model reported in much of the literature is universal, prompting cross-cultural comparisons.

Relationships among role stressors, self-efficacy, and burnout were studied in nine countries and regions (i.e., the United States, Germany, France, Brazil, Israel, Japan, China, Hong Kong, and Fiji). Findings indicated self-efficacy had a universally negative association with burnout across all regions. Furthermore, self-efficacy reduced the impact of role conflict or role ambiguity on burnout in eight of the nine cultures (the relationship did not hold for role ambiguity in the United States). 161 The relationship of job stress to burnout and turnover intentions among employees in Canada, China, Malaysia, and Pakistan was studied, examining four job stressors (i.e., work overload, conflict, ambiguity, and resource inadequacy) as well as burnout and turnover motivation. Overall, job stress and the four job stressors were significantly related to burnout and turnover motivation in all four countries. 162

Two large-scale studies of cross-cultural stressors and cultural values found role conflict and role ambiguity to be related to power distance and individualism. 163 , 164 A review of cross-cultural research on stress suggests there are different sources of stress for people working in different countries. 165 For example, time pressure, deadlines, long working hours, and interpersonal problems are a source of stress for executives from Latin America and South Africa. In China and the United States, however, the most frequently reported stressors were poor working conditions, equipment failure, lack of training, difficulties with teamwork, and lack of structure. In the United States, the lack of adequate control was reported more frequently than in other countries.

While cross-cultural research on stress is relatively new, evidence suggests that stress occurs worldwide. A review of studies from 47 countries involving 193,337 participants concluded that negative emotions at work increased during the pandemic, and depression had the largest elevation. Being younger was associated with more stress globally, and being older was associated with higher anxiety in Asia. During the pandemic, females reported higher levels of depression, anxiety and stress compared to males, and this was most pronounced in Europe. 166

There may be differences in how people cope with stress that depend on culture, however. For example, Indian and U.S. employees in similar jobs tend to use different coping strategies to deal with work-related strain. Indians preferred to talk with family members and close friends about their frustrations, whereas U.S. employees engage in more action-oriented coping mechanisms to deal with the source of the stress. 167 This may be the result of having more job “decision latitude,” which is “the working individual’s potential control over job-related decision making.” 168 For example, problem-focused coping and job latitude reduced stress in a study of the United States, New Zealand, Germany, Spain, South Africa, and Japan. 169 The next section reviews coping mechanisms for job stress in more detail.

Coping

504 Coping is defined as “constantly changing cognitive and behavioral efforts to manage specific external and/or internal demands that are appraised as taxing or exceeding the resources of the person.” 170 There are two types of coping: behavioral methods (problem-solving behaviors) and cognitive methods (managing thoughts and emotions). 171 Examples of behavioral (or active) coping strategies are keeping a positive outlook, working harder, and seeking advice and help. 172 Examples of cognitive methods include reordering life priorities and convincing oneself that work isn’t all that important. During COVID-19, university students experienced a high degree of stress due to heavy academic workloads and adjusting to online education while social distancing. Research showed that they coped by time management, avoiding procrastination, problem-solving, and exercise. In addition, students reported that they managed their personal expenses to prevent stress from financial constraints. 173

Men and women may cope differently. A review found that women are more likely than men to engage in most forms of coping strategies. In particular, the strongest effects showed that women were more likely to use strategies that involved verbal expressions by seeking emotional support. For example, they were more likely than men to ruminate about problems and use positive self-talk. 174

Social Support

505 Social support refers to the help that people receive when they experience stressful job demands. Support during a stress episode is beneficial. This is called the buffering effect because help from others serves as a buffer from stress and strain. 177 Social support is a resource for coping with stress that comes from others—family, friends, coworkers, and supervisors. It may be emotional or instrumental support . 178 Instrumental support refers to tangible help that a person may provide to another person (e.g., loaning them money if they have been laid off from work). Based on a longitudinal study of blue-collar workers, the buffering effect of social support on work hours, employee health, and well-being is a function of the pattern of exchange relations between an employee and their close support providers (friends and family). 179 Social support aids stress management in the following ways: 180

  • By building up the person’s sense of identity and belonging
  • By improving self-image
  • By enhancing the sense of control and mastery over the stressful situation

A review found social support is negatively related to both stressors and strains. 181 Social support may help prevent a stress episode as well as alleviate the stress when it occurs. The COVID-19 crisis drastically affected employees and reduced their psychological well-being. A study investigated coworkers’ instrumental support in predicting employees’ emotional exhaustion due to the perceived uncertainties experienced due to the COVID-19 pandemic. The findings revealed a negative association between coworker support and an employee’s emotional exhaustion. Also, family support mitigated the negative effect of uncertainty. 182 Research has demonstrated that leaders play a key role in providing social support for employees experiencing stress, 183 yet there may be gender differences in social support. A study of 402 employees found that men experience relatively high levels of support regardless of supervisor gender, whereas women experience more support with a female supervisor. 184

Given the high costs of stress, organizations have developed interventions and policies to help their employees cope with stress. In many of these interventions, the emphasis is on prevention, and these strategies are reviewed next.

Preventive Stress Management in Organizations

506 Given the rising costs of workplace stress, organizations have been addressing stress through a variety of policy changes and interventions. Preventive stress management is a set of methods that promote health at the workplace and avoid distress. 185 While organizations assume that employees will take some individual responsibility for preventing stress, it is a clear trend that organizations are being more proactive regarding stress management. Many organizations have wellness programs that offer workshops on time management, weight loss, alcohol or drug abuse, smoking cessation, and exercise. Reviews of the research on these organizational interventions 20 years ago showed that they minimally impact employee well-being and performance. 186 Yet a more recent review study of 36 wellness programs found they are effective in helping employees cope with workplace stress. 187

What’s #Trending in OB?
The “Sunday Scaries”

Surveys continue to show alarming levels of workplace stress. Employees are burned out on the job, losing sleep, developing illnesses, having relationship difficulties, performing at lower levels, and have lower well-being. A survey conducted by Robert Half found that more than half (52%) of the respondents said they were overwhelmed by work on a daily basis. Another survey, commissioned by LinkedIn, found that 80% of working adults in the United States worry about the upcoming workweek on Sunday. It’s been labeled “the Sunday Scaries”—high levels of anxiety the night before the start of the workweek. It’s not a clinical diagnosis, but Jonathan Abramowitz, a psychologist and professor at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, states, “This feeling, whether we call it anxiety, worry, stress, fear, whatever, it’s all really the same thing. Psychologically, it’s a response to the perception of some sort of threat.”

The “Sunday Scaries” start on Sunday afternoon, after 3:00 p.m. The weekend is waning away, and a sense of low-grade dread begins to set in as employees begin to worry about the upcoming workweek. There is even a brand of CBD gummies named Sunday Scaries. The use of the phrase has grown rapidly on Twitter since 2016. A new found that many employees experience the “Sunday Scaries.” This often takes the form of mental preoccupation with the week ahead, as well as feelings of dread, nervousness, and difficulty sleeping. “It’s as if your mind starts to turn from what is generally either relaxation or enjoyment at the weekend, into worries about everything you’ve got to do in the working week ahead,” according to a respondent in the study. The research also found that these Sunday Scaries were particularly pronounced among people who frequently checked their emails on the weekend, had undone tasks from the previous week, and had unreasonably high expectations of themselves. This got worse after the pandemic when the rise of remote work blurred the boundaries between work and home life.

Our Expectations Predict the “Sunday Scaries”

507 It’s important to keep in mind that our expectations play a role in the “Sunday Scaries” experience. Harvard psychologist Daniel Gilbert states, “We overestimate how happy we will be on our birthdays, we underestimate how happy we will be on Monday mornings, and we make these mundane but erroneous predictions again and again, despite their regular disconfirmation.” Research has shown that day-of-the-week predictions (T.G.I.F. and the Sunday blues) were most powerful when people predicted their moods for each day of the upcoming week, but the actual moods on those days differed from expectations.

What’s the Cure?

Kerry McClure, a wellness practitioner with 25 years of corporate experience, says employees need to learn to “stress well.” McClure has a three-step mindfulness process she calls “the three Ps” that helps employees work with the underlying feeling that they’re always “on” and have to do it all:

  • Pause —Push your chair away from your desk and give yourself a moment or two to check your breathing, posture, and thoughts. Stand up and move around, making time to be present.
  • Perspective —Ask yourself, “Am I busy, or is my day full?” “Am I fine, or am I well?” “Do I have to do something, or do I get to do it?” This shifts your energy so you can gain a positive perspective.
  • Prioritize —Decide what you need to do today and what you can do later. Create to-do lists, clear your desk, and cross off what you finish.

She says this process “helps you manage your stress so you can change your focus, create healthy boundaries, and move through your day and through the world with mindfulness.”

Employee Assistance Programs

508 When an employee exhibits a clear stress reaction that is affecting their relationships with coworkers and performance, a referral may be made for counseling through an employee assistance program (EAP) . EAPs first started in the 1930s in the United States as employer-sponsored internal support programs to restore work function for alcoholic employees in manufacturing industries. In the 1980s and early 1990s, only a third of employers in the United States offered an EAP. Today in the United States, all federal and state government workers and a vast majority of employees in large and medium-sized businesses in the private sector have access to an EAP. 188 Although these counseling programs may be in-house, they are usually delivered through an outside agency. The employer typically offers the services of the EAP as an employee benefit. EAPs may provide counseling, information, and referrals appropriate for treatment and support services. An interview study of organizational leaders in Australia found that EAPs were an important source of support that can be offered by organizations to their staff for the management of both personal and professional concerns. 189 A longitudinal, matched-sample study 190 comparing 156 employees receiving EAP services with 188 non-EAP employees found that over time, EAP employees had significantly lower absenteeism and “presenteeism” (being at work but not working).

EAPs were a resource that was in place for many organizations when the COVID-19 pandemic occurred. These programs offered vital resources that addressed the emotional, psychological, and physical well-being of employees. For example, some organizations increased counseling resources to help employees establish new routines. Some expanded their EAP to accommodate specific pandemic-related needs of their employees, such as those who have children at home because of virtual schooling, or in finding ways to help those who did not have adequate technology at home they needed to work successfully. Several organizations offered payments ranging from $400 to $1000 to allow employees to enhance their home workspace. While the pandemic forced many employees to find ways to balance their professional and parental responsibilities, feeling supported by the organization by EAPs helped them cope with stress. 191

509 Most organizations today are undergoing changes that create stress for employees. Case Study 15.1 Organizational Change at Amazon

Amazon has reinvented itself again and again and thrived on organizational change. Since the company went online in 1995, the e-commerce giant has evolved under the leadership of one person—Jeff Bezos. When Amazon Web Services (AWS) started out, most investors didn’t take it seriously. However, 10 years later, it was the largest cloud retailer in the world. It now has more capacity than its nearest 14 competitors combined, but this shouldn’t come as a surprise.

When the company first launched its website, all it sold was books. Amazon was viewed as being like eBay or Craigslist—a website you went to for a specific purpose. It wasn’t a daily habit like using Google or Instagram. Gradually, they expanded Amazon’s offerings to include CDs and DVDs. Kindle was one of Amazon’s biggest innovations. This revolutionized the book publishing industry and changed how books are read, sold, and distributed. Some estimates have placed Kindle e-book revenue at over half a billion dollars per year. But Amazon never really stopped changing.

The products offered on the website expanded and new products emerged, and the company now sells over 353 million products to customers all over the world. Amazon has a market valuation of more than $1.981 trillion. It has become the second-largest employer in the United States with more than 1.1 million full-time employees, mostly in its fulfillment centers and warehouses, up from less than 34,000 at the end of 2010.

Amazon drastically changed product delivery and automation in their warehouses. For instance, customers with Amazon Prime memberships can enjoy free 2-day delivery. Changes continue as Amazon explores delivering packages by drones in 30 minutes or less. Amazon’s logistics and shipping operations have become almost impenetrable moats that few other companies will ever have the resources or ability to match—the company said in April 2019 it was spending $800 million in a single quarter to offer free 1-day delivery for all Prime customers in the United States.

515 Regarding hardware, Amazon learned from a failed phone launch (the Fire Phone) in 2014. They pivoted and released an entirely new gadget: the home assistant. Alexa was fully developed, turning Apple’s Siri into a legacy technology and leaving Google—the self-proclaimed artificial intelligence experts—playing catch-up.

Amazon now produces its own video, television series, and films, winning Academy and Emmy Awards. After acquiring Whole Foods, Amazon began making changes to the grocery store chain, including price cuts, adding Amazon products, integrating Whole Foods and Amazon Prime, and restructuring. Other programs include food delivery from Whole Foods, rewards for customers using Amazon credit cards, and discounts for Prime members. They seek to further disrupt and dominate retail grocery stores with Amazon Go, which is a physical grocery store with no checkout lines, no hassles, and high convenience. One of Amazon’s largest initiatives is Project Kuiper, which will create a global satellite broadband network. Estimates of the cost of getting the project off the ground are $16.5 to $20 billion. Project Kuiper aims to put 3,232 satellites into low Earth orbit to support a high-speed internet network that could serve tens of millions of users around the world—and, not accidentally, provide infrastructure for AWS and for other current or future offerings from Amazon.

Amazon’s defining trait has been its willingness to change. What started as an online bookstore has become a multi-industry giant. Amazon dominates non-innovative competitors and changed several industries. If there is one guiding principle at Amazon, it is that the customer is always first.

CEO Bezos uses symbolic acts to reinforce customer obsession, such as the famous “empty chair.” Bezos leaves one seat open at a conference table to inform all attendees that they should consider that seat occupied by their customer, “the most important person in the room.” Ultimately, these symbolic gestures are highly calculated and often repeated in messages created to reinforce Amazon’s leadership principles.

Lessons from Amazon’s successful change management include the following:

  • Innovation fuels success, especially in today’s digital economy.
  • Speed is the ultimate weapon.
  • Those who resist organizational change can easily get left behind.

Case Study 15.2 Stress Impacts on Small Businesses During COVID-19

The COVID-19 pandemic drastically altered the business landscape, especially for small businesses. Unlike large corporations, small businesses often lack the resources and infrastructure to adapt quickly to unforeseen crises. Many businesses faced prolonged shutdowns, disrupted supply chains, a sudden shift to remote work, and a drop in consumer demand. These factors contributed to heightened stress levels among business owners, employees, and even customers.

“Pizza Planet,” a local restaurant that had been in operation for 5 years prior to the pandemic, is a small, family-owned business in a medium-sized urban area, offering pizza, pasta, and beverages. The café had built a loyal customer base due to its special sauce, fresh ingredients, and community engagement. Customers loved the space-themed décor of the restaurant and adjacent arcade. Prior to the pandemic, the café had 10 employees and was generating steady profits.

Business Impact of COVID-19

  1. Closure due to lockdowns: In March 2020, the government-mandated lockdown forced Pizza Planet to close its doors for in-person dining and the arcade. This sudden halt in posed an immediate threat to the business’s financial stability. Although the restaurant quickly pivoted itself to offering only takeout and delivery services, revenue plummeted by 50%, triggering severe cash flow problems.
  2. Health and safety regulations: To reopen later in the year, Pizza Planet had to implement strict health and safety measures, including reduced seating, physical distancing, and constant sanitation. The additional costs of these measures, such as purchasing protective equipment and training, increased the financial burden on the business.
  3. Supply chain disruptions: Another stressor was the disruption in the supply chain. Many of Pizza Planet’s local suppliers were facing their own challenges, causing delays in deliveries and shortages of key ingredients. This impacted on the restaurant’s ability to maintain a consistent menu, leading to customer dissatisfaction.

517 The owner, Andy, experienced significant stress due to uncertainty about the business’s future. Studies show that small-business owners faced higher levels of stress during COVID-19 than larger businesses, given their limited access to financial reserves or credit. A survey by the National Bureau of Economic Research reported that 43% of small businesses had temporarily closed due to COVID-19, leading to anxiety among owners regarding the fear of permanent closure.

  • Financial worries: Andy’s primary concern was managing cash flow. With rent, utilities, and wages continuing to accumulate, he applied for loans under government assistance programs, but the lengthy approval process created uncertainty and added to the stress.
  • Employee well-being: Another stressor was employee safety. Andy felt responsible for the staff, some of whom were hesitant to return to work due to health concerns. The emotional toll of balancing his employees’ well-being with the need to keep the business operational created additional stress.

Employees at Pizza Planet were also affected by the stress of the pandemic. The restaurant industry, which relies heavily on face-to-face interaction, saw a significant mental health decline among workers. According to a report by the American Psychological Association, workers in high-contact jobs like food service experienced higher levels of anxiety about contracting the virus.

  • Job insecurity: Employees at Pizza Planet were unsure about their job stability, especially when the café was closed for months. Some of them had to take on secondary jobs to cover lost wages, increasing their stress levels.
  • Health concerns: Returning to work after the lockdowns, many employees were anxious about potential exposure to COVID-19, leading to heightened stress.
Coping Strategies

To alleviate financial stress, Pizza Planet adopted several coping mechanisms:

  • Digital transformation: Andy invested in an online ordering platform and partnered with food delivery services like UberEats. This pivot helped increase delivery sales, although not enough to cover the losses from in-person dining.
  • Government assistance: Pizza Planet applied for the Paycheck Protection Program (PPP), a government loan designed to help small businesses retain their workforce. The funds were instrumental in keeping employees on the payroll during the most challenging months. However, applying for these loans was itself a stressful process due to the complexity and changing regulations.

Andy introduced initiatives to reduce stress levels among his employees by providing social support:

  • Flexible scheduling: Employees were offered flexible working hours, allowing them to balance their work and home lives, which had been affected by the pandemic.
  • 518 Access to resources: Andy provided employees with information on mental health resources, including counseling and stress management. He had a psychologist from a local university make a presentation to his employees followed by questions and answers.
Long-Term Impact and Recovery

By late 2021, Pizza Planet saw a gradual return to pre-pandemic levels of business, but the long-term effects of stress remained. Andy experienced continued financial anxiety, especially as inflation increased food and supply costs. Moreover, the restaurant experienced turnover, as some employees, having reconsidered their career paths during the pandemic, decided to leave the restaurant business.

Research suggests that small businesses continued to face financial and psychological challenges even after restrictions were lifted. A McKinsey & Company report noted that recovery for small businesses post-pandemic would likely be slower compared to larger enterprises.

The COVID-19 pandemic significantly heightened stress for small business owners and their employees, particularly in industries like food service that rely on in-person interactions. The key stressors included financial instability, health and safety concerns, and supply chain disruptions. While the businesses managed to adapt and survive through innovation, government support, and social support, the long-term effects of pandemic-induced stress on the workforce and the owner remain visible.
