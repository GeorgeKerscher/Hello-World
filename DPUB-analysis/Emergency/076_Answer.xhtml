You Make the Call

1. In this situation, you will want to use one of the following techniques to remove this patient from the hazardous environment:

Two-person walking assist:

1. Help the patient stand.

2. Have the patient place one arm around your neck and hold the patient’s wrist, which should be draped over your shoulder. Put your free arm around the patient’s waist and help the patient walk.

3. Have the patient do the same with the other rescuer.

4. Two rescuers can support the patient as they escort him from his home.

Two-person chair carry:

1. Rescuer 1 stands behind the seated patient, reaches down, and grasps the back of the chair close to the seat.

2. Rescuer 1 then tilts the chair slightly backward on its rear legs so that Rescuer 2 can step back in between the legs of the chair and grasp the chair’s front legs.

3. The patient’s legs should be between the legs of the chair.

4. When both rescuers are correctly positioned, Rescuer 1 gives the command to lift and walk away.

Two-person seat carry:

1. The rescuers kneel on opposite sides of the patient near the patient’s hips.

2. The rescuers then raise the patient to a sitting position and link arms behind the patient’s back.

3. The rescuers then place their other arm under the patient’s knees and link with each other.

4. If possible, the patient should put his arms around the necks and shoulders of the rescuers for additional support. (pages 42–48; Emergency Movement of Patients)

2. Your answer should include the following steps and information:

  • Because this patient presents with no potential life-threatening conditions, a short backboard device can be used to stabilize the patient.
  • One of the clinicians should go behind the patient to stabilize the patient’s head while the other responder places a cervical collar on the patient.
  • While maintaining neutral, in-line motion restriction, lean the patient forward while a responder slides the device behind the patient, head ﬁrst.
  • Carefully lean the patient against the board.
  • Fasten the middle strap of the device, and then fasten the rest of the straps.
  • Place the wings of the device around the patient’s head and strap the patient’s head to the device.
  • Carefully move the patient from the vehicle and place her on a long backboard (if available) while waiting for EMS to arrive. (page 53; Treatment of Patients With Suspected Head or Spine Injuries)

Skill Drill 8-4: Performing Automated External Defibrillation

1. If CPR is in progress when you arrive, verify that the patient is unresponsive and has no pulse . If no CPR is being performed and the patient is unresponsive, has no pulse, and is not breathing, begin chest compressions .

2. Once an AED arrives, turn it on and follow the prompts. Remove clothing from patient’s chest area. Apply pads to the chest: one just to the right of the breastbone (sternum), just below the collarbone (clavicle), the other on the left lower chest area with the top of the pad 2 to 3 inches (5 to 8 cm) below the armpit. Be sure that the pads are attached to the patient cables (and they are attached to the AED in some models). Plug the pads’ connectors into the AED .

3. Stop CPR . State aloud, “Clear patient,” and ensure that no one is touching the patient. Push the analyze button, if there is one, and wait for the AED to determine if a shockable rhythm is present. If your AED has voice prompts, follow the verbal instructions. Allow the AED to analyze the rhythm. If a shock is not advised , perform five cycles (about 2 minutes) of CPR, beginning with chest compressions. Then, reassess the patient’s pulse and reanalyze the cardiac rhythm. If a shock is advised, reconfirm that no one is touching the patient and push the shock button. After the shock is delivered , immediately resume CPR, beginning with chest compressions.

4. After five cycles (approximately 2 minutes) of CPR, reanalyze the patient’s cardiac rhythm. Do not interrupt chest compressions for more than 10 seconds. Repeat the cycle of 2 minutes of CPR, one shock (if indicated), and 2 minutes of CPR. Arrange for transport and contact medical control as needed. (page 169; Early Defibrillation by EMRs)
