Chapter 20

Ten Tips to Make Minimizing Easier

IN THIS CHAPTER

Building momentum with easy decisions

Breaking a monumental feat into manageable tasks

Digitizing papers and photos to keep what’s important

Ultimately, a minimalist home relieves stress. Living in a space with less clutter and organized things lightens your load. However, the process of minimizing can subject you to some short-term stress. Any major life change is accompanied by both positive and negative emotions — fear, excitement, regret, hope, sadness, happiness, feeling overwhelmed, and so on. And reducing your possessions to create a leaner living space is no different.

If getting your home in minimalist shape is giving you the jitters, you’ve come to the right place. In this chapter, we present ten ways to make minimizing your things a little less intimidating.

Start with the Easy Wins

Starting your minimalist journey with easy wins is smart way to build real momentum. Instead of diving straight into sentimental items or big decision categories, you begin with the low-emotion, low-stakes stuff that doesn’t require soul-searching. You don’t even have to focus on one entire room at a time. Start with expired pantry items or medications, toiletries you don’t use, random cords, duplicate kitchen tools, or clothes you haven’t worn in ages. These items don’t hold much meaning; they’re just taking up space. So clearing them out gives you immediate progress without the emotional exhaustion.

The beauty of easy wins is that they build confidence. Every drawer you tidy, every bag you donate, every surface you clear reminds you that, yes, you can do this. Small victories add up, and they make the harder categories feel more manageable later. This approach also helps you establish your decision-making muscles: “Do I need this?” “Do I use this?” “Would I buy this again today?” Practicing on simple items strengthens your clarity for the tougher ones ahead.

Starting small also prevents overwhelm. You don’t need a whole weekend or a dramatic, TV-show-style purge. Ten minutes here or one drawer there is enough. Easy wins create a steady rhythm of progress, and that rhythm is what ultimately helps you reduce your possessions and meet your minimalist goals.

Focus on What to Keep, Not What to Get Rid Of

One of the biggest challenges and most stressful aspects of downsizing is deciding what to get rid of. If you’re like most people, you’ve developed strong emotional attachments to much of what you own, even if that attachment can be attributed to something as basic as wondering whether you’ll eventually need a particular item someday. It’s easy to think, if I get rid of it now and I need it later, I’ll have to buy it again. And nobody wants to do that .

Instead of mulling over what you’re going to get rid of, focus on what you need or what you’re going to keep. Depending on how minimalist you decide to go, here are some examples of what you’ll probably keep:

  • Two-to-three weeks of clothing plus a couple outfits for special occasions
  • Toiletries and beauty supplies
  • A bed, a couch or recliner, and a table for eating
  • A medium and a large pot, a large frying pan, and a small set of cooking utensils
  • A good set of dishes, mugs, glasses, and silverware
  • Tools you use regularly, such as screwdrivers, pliers, and a wrench
  • Essential documents
  • A small collection of photos
  • A few of your more cherished memorabilia

If you’re reconfiguring any of your spaces, sketch out a floor plan and include a list of everything you need and want in each room, so you’ll know what’ll fit and what won’t.

Follow the One-Year Rule

Another way to simplify the decision-making process for what to keep and what to get rid of is to follow the one-year rule — if you haven’t used something in the past year, you’re very unlikely to use it in the next year. This rule is simple and effective, and it applies to a broad range of items — everything from clothing, to furniture, to pots, pans, and plates.

You may encounter a few exceptions, but even in those cases, carefully consider your options. Is the item you’re thinking of keeping something you can borrow or rent for the short time you’ll need it? If it is, then you’re probably better off getting rid of it.

Tackle One Room at a Time

If the mere thought of minimalizing your entire home makes you break out in hives, think smaller — one room at a time. If that’s too much, break down the task even further — one area of one room or one cabinet or a single drawer at a time. What’s important is that you get started and build on your success. Making a little progress is always better than freezing up and doing nothing, and helps build momentum.

Consider taking out the trash first. For example, if you’re sorting bathroom items, discard old meds and products first, so you’ll have less to sort.

Set a Manageable Deadline and Milestones

Some experts advise giving yourself plenty of time to pare back all that you own, but that approach can be counterproductive. If you have too much time, you may not feel any sense of urgency to complete the task. Procrastination is far too easy when you have no firm deadline.

Choose a reasonable deadline and mark it on your calendar. Then set reasonable milestones leading up to that deadline. You can set milestones by room, by percentage (for example, 25, 50, 75, and 100 percent dates), or by some other metric, but be sure to set milestones — they help ensure that you stay on track to meet your deadline. They also alleviate stress by providing a structured schedule to follow.

Follow the OHIO Rule

You can waste a lot of time, energy, and thought by being indecisive about which items you want to keep and which ones you want to get rid of. The purpose of the only handle it once (OHIO) rule is to discourage such indecisiveness. As you sort items, you make a final decision of what to do with each item: Keep it, donate it, or throw it away. You don’t change your mind. You place the item in its designated box, and that’s the last time you touch it.

Make the OHIO rule a part of your daily routines. For example, when you open the mail, pay your bills as they arrive instead of setting the bill aside and then paying it later. By dealing with issues as they arise, you move past them immediately so they’re not cluttering your mind.

Host a Sorting/Packing Party

Flying solo when minimalizing isn’t fun, nor is it efficient, and even if you and a partner are laser-focused on minimalizing together, you may still want to get help from others to get to calm faster. As you’re sorting through items, having family members and other loved ones available to consult about what to keep and what to get rid of can simplify that decision-making process and alleviate some stress, particularly if they’re interested in taking some of your gotta-go items off your hands.

Host two or more pare-back parties. You may want to schedule your first party for immediate family members who may want some of the items you’re getting rid of (especially if you have years of items stored, including things your kids may want) — this gives them the pick of the litter. Then, schedule additional parties for friends, neighbors, former coworkers, and so on. Have food and beverages available and play music to make the experience more fun.

Have Your Adult Children Clear Out Their Belongings

Children often move out of their parents’ house but continue to use it as their informal self-storage unit. Going minimal with your stuff provides the perfect opportunity to put an end to that practice. Give your children reasonable notice that you’re substantially paring back your possessions and they need to come and get their stuff by a specified date or it will end up in the dumpster or shredder. If they don’t retrieve their belongings by that date, follow through with your plan.

Don’t let your adult children make their problems your problem. And at the same time, if the sentimental value lies more with you than with them, and they don’t want the college notebooks, kiln creations of childhood, and mountains of medals and certificates from various competitive activities, that’s okay. Toss these items if the kids don’t want them, even if you’re surprised by what they decline to retrieve.

Create a Yes and No Pile (But No Maybe Pile)

In Part 2 , we recommend that you sort items into various categories, including keep, store, sell, give away, donate, and toss, but it really boils down to two categories: what to keep and what to get rid of, a yes and a no pile.

Don’t make the common mistake of creating a third category for items that you can’t decide about. That pile will only get bigger and bigger, and the likelihood that you’ll end up deciding to keep it all will increase substantially. At the same time, your stress over having to make the decision of whether to keep or get rid of an item in the maybe pile will continue to build.

Digitize What You Can

Digitizing the right things can free up an enormous amount of physical and mental space. Paper piles, old notebooks, and files you’ve retained for years can live more efficiently on your devices after you’ve first decided you need to keep them. When you scan documents (or download and save the documents via your web-based accounts), save receipts digitally (you can get them via many store apps), or move your recipes into a simple online folder, you’re not just reducing clutter — you’re making your life easier to navigate. No more digging through boxes or binders wondering where that one form went. It’s all searchable, organized, and accessible in seconds.

Photos are another big win. Most of us have albums full of prints we never actually look at. Digitizing your photos gives you the chance to curate the best ones, toss the blurry duplicates, and finally enjoy the memories instead of letting them sit in storage. Bonus: Digital albums are far easier to share with family than a stack of fading prints.

The magic here is that digitizing doesn’t erase the emotional value of any items — it preserves it while reclaiming physical space. And once you feel how much lighter life becomes without paper mountains or overstuffed filing cabinets, you’ll start thinking twice before letting new clutter in. Digitizing isn’t just about going “paperless”; it’s about making room for clarity, calm, and ease.
