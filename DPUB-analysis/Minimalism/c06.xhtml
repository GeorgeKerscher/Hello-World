The first thing you see in the morning — and the very last thing you see before you drift off into a hopefully peaceful sleep — is your bedroom. Your bedroom may be a standalone room, or it may be a corner of a studio apartment, or even a repurposed closet (big-city real estate prices are no joke, and you get creative where you can!). Your bedroom may also serve as a home office or an exercise space. No matter what else you use it for, this chapter covers bedroom basics.

Your clothes closet is probably located in your bedroom, and it’s a key space to declutter and minimalize so your bedroom feels peaceful and well-organized. We suggest you address your closet before setting your sights on your bedroom — see Chapter 5 for information on how.

Your bedroom may also have an attached bathroom, so we’re covering bathrooms here too. Although most are modest spaces with just a few drawers and maybe a medicine cabinet, they somehow get full of stuff! Your countertops may be littered with items, your drawers may be full to the brim, and everything may have a thin layer of dust because cleaning lots of small and tippable items can be a chore.

In this chapter, we help you tame bedroom and bathroom excess so both are spaces that work for you and allow you to find what you need and feel peaceful in the process.

Restoring Order to Your Sleep Space

Your bedroom should be a sanctuary or peaceful retreat. You know that your bedroom is for sleep, relaxation, and maybe a few other adult activities, all of which are meant to take place in a peaceful setting.

Often, our bedrooms become grounds for laundry, unpacked bags, paperwork, and other miscellaneous items. One of the reasons this happens is that the bedroom is usually tucked away behind a closed door, making it easier for clutter to accumulate. When guests come over unexpectedly, our bedrooms may be an easy place to stash clutter and close the door for later. Also, these days we’re accustomed to taking work home with us or working from home, which causes work stuff to seep into our bedrooms, too.

Whatever the reason your bedroom feels like “too much stuff” has accumulated, you need to quickly deal with it, as your bedroom is one of the most important rooms in your home. Your future sleep and health depend on it!

A bedroom doesn’t need to include only one item — a bed — but the less bedroom furniture and stuff you have, the more relaxed you’ll feel and the better you’ll sleep.

Clearing chaos from the nightstand

Anything that you keep in your bedroom can affect your sleep. Too much stuff can actually prevent a good night’s sleep; if your bedroom is littered with items, you may have a difficult time shutting off your brain. The items that are directly in your sight on your bedside table are especially important to assess and pare back.

What is sitting on your bedside table can affect not only your sleep but also how you wake up. Waking up to reminders of uncompleted tasks causes immediate stress and leaves you feeling anxious for the rest of the day.

Bedside tables can be excellent places to store essential items you use every day before bed, or they can turn into bedside junk drawers. Tables with no drawers can prevent most stuff from accumulating, so one option is to replace what you have with drawer-free pedestals. Because chances are, what you use every day, such as an alarm clock, lamp, book, glasses, and a bottle of water, will be on top of the table, so why do you need drawers, anyway? However, you may like your bedside table with its drawers, and if you keep only essential items within them, drawers can be a great way to tuck away items and visually create a calming space.

Stick to these tips to keep your bedside table tidy and organized with only your must-have items:

  • Reading material: A book or magazine is a great way to end or start the day without the glare of screens! But it’s easy to accumulate a large stack of items you intend to read one day. Have only the current book you’re reading on your bedside table, tuck one or two into your table’s drawer if you expect to read them in the next few weeks, and store the rest of your reading materials on a bookshelf in your bedroom or elsewhere in your home.
  • Tech gadgets and electronics: Don’t store digital devices close to your bed, because the light they emit can affect the quality of your sleep, and it’s too easy to lose track of time while you scroll through videos or social media entries. If you use your phone as an alarm clock, or if you don’t have a land line and want to make sure you hear any late-night phone calls, then move your phone off your bedside table and onto a chest or bookshelf on the other side of your room.
  • Other accessories and stuff: Your bedside table may include other items such as a box of tissues, lip balm or hand lotion, frequently used medications, a notebook and pen (great for recording dreams or clearing your mental load before bed each night), and a hodge-podge of other stuff. Take everything out of the drawers or shelves, review each item, and ask if you use it at least once a week while you’re in the bedroom. Anything you answer Yes to stays; then organize these items neatly in your bedside drawers or shelves. Anything you don’t use at all, get rid of. Anything you use less often than weekly but you use regularly is worth keeping, but store each where it belongs elsewhere in your home.
  • Preventive measure: After you’ve pared back what you keep on your bedside table, prevent the drawers from filling up again by storing a few books or a similarly large item in them to remove the temptation to fill the space with stuff.

Figure 6-1 shows an example of essentials on a bedside table. Without drawers, it’s easy to maintain a minimalist approach and avoid accumulating items you don’t need.

Considering hidden storage areas

We love unique space-saving storage solutions, especially for those who live in smaller quarters and don’t have many traditional storage areas. For a calm, organized environment, we think it’s worth investing in quality storage systems or built-ins so you can avoid having boxes or bins sitting along the perimeter of your bedroom. This unconventional storage can be sneaky spaces for surplus stuff, though, so don’t forget to bring your minimalist eye to these hidden zones.

When looking at the hidden storage areas you currently use, first assess what you have in each and get rid of what you don’t need (use the methods we talk about in Chapter 5 ). Then, ensure that everything you’re keeping is placed away neatly, ideally in such a way that you can see everything you’re storing. When you know what you have, you’ll more consistently use what you own, you’ll be less inclined to accumulate unnecessary items, and you’ll more successfully avoid building collections of things that have little or no use.

Here are some ideas for storage spaces you may find in a bedroom:

  • Under the bed: Always check under your bed for monsters and clutter; that’s our motto. Today’s modern platform beds don’t have an “under the bed” at all, which helps prevent dust and clutter buildup! We recommend this type of bed to help you maintain a minimalist bedroom. If you don’t have a platform or need to store things under your bed (whether via a bedframe that hides space under the mattress via built-in drawers or a moveable mattress on a hinge, or through use of a bed skirt or a long bedspread), shoes and off-season clothes are perfect items to tuck away in this unseen space. But out of sight can quickly mean out of mind, so check under your bed quarterly if you use the space for storage.
  • Headboard storage: Some headboards offer built-in or add-in storage solutions. Be wary of the clutter that can accumulate here and only keep what you regularly reach for while in bed.
  • Storage benches: We love a good bench for sitting down and pulling on boots or tying shoes. Today, many benches offer hidden storage solutions, which can help keep your room looking chaos-free while allowing you to organize needed items. As with under-bed storage, regularly review the contents of your storage bench to make sure you’re retaining only what you need and you can easily find what you’ve kept.
  • Behind doors: Over-the-door organizers are great for storing shoes, jewelry, or other accessories out of sight. You want your bedroom to feel calm and peaceful even when the door is shut, so if you store items behind it, make sure that when you close your door, what you see is what you use regularly and is organized neatly.
  • Baskets, boxes, and bins: These are great items that can provide extra storage if needed — but only if needed! When making a minimalist bedroom, select baskets, boxes, or bins that are just large enough for your stuff, and make sure you’re keeping only what fits into a regular rotation of use.

Cutting clutter from corners

For some reason, bedroom corners seem to accumulate excess stuff like no other. It would be unusual to toss your laundry in the corner of your kitchen, but in your bedroom, it could be normal practice. Examine the corners of your bedroom to see if you’re storing stuff there; if so, evaluate if the items you keep there support your minimalist mindset. If you don’t need or don’t regularly use the items, get rid of them and create empty space where light and air can freely move.

Furnishing for the Win

A decluttered bedroom is a good start to bringing minimalism to your sleeping space, and you want to keep the restful bedroom setting going. Which brings us to furniture. The more functional your furniture is, and the better organized it is for your lifestyle, the less likely you are to accumulate items you don’t need or make duplicate purchases because you don’t know what you own. We’re not suggesting you need to buy all new furniture now, but when you’re ready to upgrade or replace what’s no longer working for you, the information in the following sections can help you make thoughtful purchases that fit your space, needs, and minimalist ideals.

Figuring out your bedroom essentials

Determine what the essentials are in your bedroom. Minimalism, by our definition, means keeping just what you need and use in most spaces, especially the bedroom. As you consider what your “bedroom essentials” are, ask yourself what you truly need for sound sleep, and assess whether those must-have items are conducive to ensuring sufficient shut-eye. Some people consider a TV to be essential for the bedroom, for example, because it enables the bedroom to be a quiet oasis in an otherwise busy household. Other people would never consider having a noisy TV in a space dedicated to sleep. “Essential” items are what’s important to you .

For a sleep-focused environment, here are what we consider the bedroom essentials:

  • A bed
  • Sheets and one pillow per sleeper
  • A few surfaces for lamps

You probably have many more items of furniture than this in your bedroom — bookshelves, a desk, a dresser, and so on. We encourage you to review each piece of furniture that’s taking up valuable real estate in your sleeping haven. Consider that furniture can also be clutter, and too much can make for a crowded space that feels busy and not peaceful. Many of us may hang onto bedroom furniture because it came as part of a set, or because we’re conditioned to think we need to have two bedside tables and a dresser. However, if you don’t use a piece of furniture, it’s interfering with your minimalist goals.

Simple is key for the bedroom. Figure 6-2 showcases the simple bedroom furnishings of one of this book’s authors. This room is influenced by European design and furnishings, which tend to be much simpler when it comes to bedroom linens and furniture. Lower beds create an illusion of a larger space, and so do duvets with classic sheet sets. Fewer pillows, and more functional versus decorative ones, are best.

Focusing on your bedroom’s star: Your bed

Your bed should take up the most space in your bedroom. It should be the piece of furniture that you’ve paid the most attention to when selecting it, and it should be surrounded with lots of space (as much as your size bedroom allows), not clutter underneath, on top of, or generally around the bed. When getting a new bed, spend time finding the right bed and mattress for your bedroom’s size and design, your preference, and your storage needs. A quality bed will likely last decades, so it’s an investment worth making. And to promote a minimalist aesthetic, the simpler the better!

STARTING SERENITY WITH A QUICK TUCK

Here’s a simple tip that creates quick calm in your space. Make your bed every morning! Making your bed every morning sets the tone for your entire day. You start with an immediate accomplishment. It makes your room look neat and tidy. And you eliminate the obvious distraction that an unmade bed is. We’ve been surprised at how good we feel just by the small task of making our bed every morning.

Also, your made bed makes the best, largest surface for folding and organizing other things, so you can keep the calm going.

What goes on your bed is just as important. Use quality linens that feel good to sleep on. Use as many pillows as you enjoy sleeping with, but avoid an excessive amount — a bed with 20 pillows that you have to remove before crawling in diminishes the calm experience a minimalist bedroom strives for. A pile of pillows also creates the opportunity for dust build-up, which can trigger allergies and make sleep less restful.

Encouraging your sleeping partner

After reading this chapter, you may have some ideas that you want to put into action right away regarding how to make your bedroom a minimalist, sleeping sanctuary. You may be sharing your bedroom and your home with other people, or even pets. We think it’s important to involve them in your decluttering process (at least the people! Fido may not have a strong opinion either way) and share your new ideas so that you can collaborate and enjoy the sleeping benefits as a household.

Remember the following as you share your new ideas and minimalist bedroom goals with your household:

  • Be respectful. With shared spaces, it’s important to discuss and share ideas, as one person’s clutter may be someone else’s important paperwork or creative project.
  • Explain the discomfort too much stuff is causing. Be specific about the detrimental effects of having an overstuffed bedroom, and the potential benefits of having a minimalist environment.
  • Negotiate. It can take time for mindsets to change, and you may have to change your own first. Be patient and stick to what you believe will help make your bedroom a more restful sleep area for you. It’ll take time to settle into your new habits, especially to adopt the technology-free bedroom ideals. We’ll be frank — we sometimes still keep a phone or laptop next to the bed. It takes time to change habits, and patience is key!
  • Keep clutter styles in mind. You may have a different clutter style than your partner. Maybe you keep stuff because you’re sentimental and your partner keeps stuff because they have a hard time making decisions. Or you can’t get rid of any tech cables, cords, or gadgets (one day you know you’ll need one of them), and your partner won’t part with decades of old comics. No matter the reason for the clutter, parting with a lot of items may be difficult for your partner, so be gentle and kind when discussing your goals and how you’d like to achieve them.
  • Designate the bedroom as your home’s minimalist zone. For a fully minimalist experience, the best-case scenario would be to make your entire home, finances, digital experiences, and mind minimalist zones! However, that may not happen overnight (or ever). The bedroom can be one of those places you and your partner designate as a minimalist zone and stick to for all the reasons mentioned in this chapter.
  • Make it easy. Keep “declutter boxes” in your home. If you get aesthetically pleasing baskets or boxes that can remain permanently in your home, decluttering will stay top of mind, and minimalism will be easier to implement and sustain. Figure 6-3 is an example of these boxes.

SETTING THE STAGE FOR SUPERIOR SLEEP

Having a minimalist bedroom promotes better sleep. When considering health and wellness, sleep is one of the biggest and most important factors involved. Where we sleep has a significant impact on this.

Here are some tips on creating a bedroom that’s conducive to a good night’s sleep:

  • Create an environment that is dark, quiet, and cool.
  • Turn off electronic devices at least 30 minutes before bedtime.
  • Don’t charge your phone next to your bed. Try to keep all devices out of your bedroom.
  • Use your bed for sleep and sex only, not for work.
  • Keep pets out of beds.
  • Choose a real book over an e-reader, and pick something not work-related for pre-bedtime reading.
  • Make a list of what you’re grateful for before dropping off to sleep. We think this is a sure way to happier dreams.

Taking a Minimalist Approach to Your Bathroom

Bathroom clutter is often forgotten behind closed cabinets or drawers, and products you’re keeping are often expired or no longer needed. Show of hands – who’s reading this right now and thinking about their overflowing bathroom cabinets? Too much stuff, not all of it usable, probably some duplicates, and much of it hard to easily get to — this sounds exhausting! If you can’t see what you have, you can’t use all that you own — minimalism to the rescue. This section can help you get a handle on your bathroom excess.

Minimizing unwanted products

We love decluttering bathrooms and bringing them into the minimalist space. Bathrooms usually are the smallest rooms in most homes, and they can be the messiest, so reducing junk and keeping just what you need brings us intense joy.

Your bathroom shopping habits have a lot to do with how cluttered your bathroom is. It’s easy to get pulled in by the latest skincare discovery from your favorite influencer or nearby beauty shop. You may not think of skincare and makeup as a hobby, but tell that to the countless makeup YouTubers making seven figures on their monthly favorites and product reviews.

However, we’re here to help you work through this. Don’t get discouraged — you’re on your minimalist journey, and that means taking one step at a time. We’re confident that your overflowing bathroom cabinets will soon emit a clutter-free, zenlike serenity that sets the stage for your entire bathroom, as seen in Figure 6-4 .

Here are a few concepts to keep in mind as you begin making your bathroom minimal:

  • Avoid storing multiples. We think being prepared for anything is important, from earthquakes to having spare toothpaste so you don’t run out at midnight. However, we don’t agree with storing duplicates of every bathroom product and certainly not more than one extra of each. If you’re at Costco and you see your favorite moisturizer on sale in a set of 20, think twice. Will the bottles expire before you’re even ready to use them? Will you switch moisturizers next month? Will your skin type change? Are you willing to give up your precious real estate in the bathroom for these to sit for possibly years?
  • Let your bathroom real estate guide you. Often, we caution against space — just because you have a 1,000-square-foot attic doesn’t mean you have to fill it! But we think the bathroom is the only room where available space should help you determine how much stuff you can physically keep. If you’re lucky enough to have a newer modern bathroom, you actually have less storage space than older bathrooms with their large vanities, which makes it easier to part with whatever you don’t regularly use. So, let an overflowing countertop be the first sign to declutter.
  • Your bathroom is not Sephora. This was a hard one for us to grasp, because we understand the love for every lip color, collagen treatment, and newest face-massaging gadget. But as you take a minimalist view of your bathroom contents, remember that multiple different products that essentially do the same job simply take up space. You can have a spare of your essentials, but duplicates beyond that aren’t necessary. Do an inventory of how many bathroom products you have and think about how much each cost you, and you’ll soon realize how much money you’re wasting filling up this room! The bathroom can be a serious money pit, which makes it all the more satisfying after you’ve scaled back to just what you deem essential.
  • Let go of the past. You don’t need to keep the prescription throat spray you got for strep throat five years ago; if you get sick again, you’ll want an unexpired medication. That special shade of eyeshadow you wore at your wedding ten years ago and haven’t worn since? You likely won’t wear it again. Ditto the overpriced beard oil you bought two years ago but always forget to use. Bathroom decluttering applies to everyone. Even dogs somehow have more shampoo selections than we did when we were kids (and we’re certain one shampoo and maybe one conditioner is all Fido needs to look their best).

Assessing what you have and deciding what to keep

When faced with drawers, baskets, and cabinets full of bathroom products, you take the same approach as auditing and purging your closet (see Chapter 5 for more information): Take everything out so you get a sense of what you have. And then assess each item, asking yourself these four questions to help you decide what to keep and what to get rid of:

  • Do I use this? This should be a quick yes or no answer. We didn’t ask you if you’ll possibly, maybe, potentially use it one day. Do you use it now?
  • Is this a duplicate or extra? You can have a spare for your most-needed supplies (see the next section). But how many extra lotions do you need? Do you really need to keep that sparkling lip gloss? Beauty products expire (see the section “ Setting Strict Timelines for Product Lifetimes ”), so think hard about whether you need to keep that extra. Perhaps you can donate it or pass it along to a friend who would love it!
  • Would I buy this right now? You may be keeping some things because you feel bad about the money you spent on them — toiletries and beauty supplies can be very expensive. But this question can help you analyze without considering cost. As you contemplate if you’d buy the item now, think about the product’s color, the way it works or doesn’t work for your skin type, and whether it’s as effective as you thought it would be. If you wouldn’t buy it again right now, then it’s excess that’s ready to be given away or tossed.
  • Is the space worth it? Is the product taking up precious space? Can you transfer it to a smaller container or make it smaller by removing the packaging?

Bathroom items are easy to find and replace — after all, the cosmetic and pharmaceutical industries are two of the largest industries in North America, and both continue to innovate and put new products in front of you. So deciding what to get rid of versus what to keep shouldn’t stress you out; if you toss something and later realize it was critical to your skincare well-being, you can easily buy a replacement if needed. We’re not advocating throwing out items that you then spend good money to replace; we just don’t want you to feel compelled to keep “someday I might use this” items when you’re trying to pare back to just what you regularly need and use.

Examining your bathroom goodies, item by item

We’ve covered some general tips for tackling your bathroom stash, but some stuff needs a little more judgment before it earns its spot. Take a look at the following sections for item-specific pointers.

Your toothbrush and other must-have items

We think it’s important that you fill your bathroom only with products you use daily. This allows your space to be serene and uncluttered, you’re able to find everything easily, and you’re likely to use all that you own, because nothing is tucked away in a hidden drawer or shelf. So divide the items you use regularly into two piles — the stuff that you use each and every day (toothbrush, toothpaste, shampoo, and so on) and the stuff you use often but not daily, such as face masks, hair accessories, a trimmer, and so on). Move the “used regularly but not daily” items out of the bathroom, perhaps into a nearby linen closet.

Consider keeping your toothbrushes away from anything that splashes or sprays. Instead, put them in a see-through holder that can be easily cleaned (as pictured in Figure 6-5 ) and set them in a safe place to avoid dirt and buildup.

Cosmetics

Go through all of your cosmetics to first assess any that may be expired (see “ Setting Strict Timelines for Product Lifetimes ” for our guidelines on product lifespans). Then go through the remaining non-expired items and determine whether you actually use them. We just know that you have some beautiful colors that look better in the case than they do on you (doesn’t everyone?!). Keep only what you use and give the rest away to someone it might look great on!

Makeup brushes

When purchasing a complete set, we end up using only one or two brushes. If this is the case for you, get rid of the remaining brushes you don’t use. Reviewing your makeup brush collection is also a great time to clean them, which many people don’t do regularly (see the sidebar “ How to clean your makeup brushes ” for instructions). Remember, the fewer brushes you have, the easier it will be to keep to a consistent cleaning schedule.

HOW TO CLEAN YOUR MAKEUP BRUSHES

Makeup brushes should be cleaned regularly — at least monthly, and more often if you use the brushes frequently.

To clean them, do the following to each brush:

  1. Start by dunking the brush into a cup of warm (not hot) water.
  2. Squirt some shampoo into the palm of your hand and rub the brush back and forth on your palm, saturating the brush with shampoo. With your fingers, rub the suds throughout the bristles.
  3. Once the brush seems clean, dunk it back into the warm water and swish it around to get the soap out.
  4. Repeat Steps 1–3 (using clean water each time you rinse) until the soap in your palm isn’t discolored from brush residue and the rinse water is clear.
  5. Pat your brush dry with a towel until the bristles are just damp, and then set the brush along the edge of your sink or upright in a glass so it completely air dries. (For really fluffy brushes, we like to let them air dry for at least half a day, or better yet, clean them in the morning and let them air dry until you use them the next morning.)

Skin care

Be brutal with skin-care products. Many of you will pick up a product and think, “Oh I wish I used that more” or “If only I used this every day, I wouldn’t have these fine lines.” If you haven’t used a product consistently, chances are you won’t start. Start by checking expiry dates (see the section “ Setting Strict Timelines for Product Lifetimes ” for details), and test the products you want to keep to make sure they still smell right.

Trial sizes

We fully admit that back in our makeup junkie days we were addicted to trial sizes, especially all the free ones that the makeup counters would give away. We thought we needed to have a stash for our travels, but alas, we never used them. Be honest about what you use. Often you can donate these if they’re unopened, or give them to friends who have frequent house guests, as they love to have travel sizes for their visitors!

Hair brushes/combs

We have a pet peeve about hair and dirt buildup in combs and brushes. The fewer of these grooming items you have, the easier it is to keep them clean. Pick out the brushes and combs you use most days and toss the rest, or see if an animal shelter would accept them as donations.

Hair accessories

Go through your hair accessories with a fine-toothed comb (don’t mind the pun). What’s old may be new again someday — hello scrunchies — but the possibility of a trend resurgence isn’t reason enough to keep accessories you no longer wear. The elastics or mechanics may wear out over time, anyway! Toss hair accessories you don’t use, as giving these away can be tough sometimes (many people can be fussy about hair germs).

Nail polish

If stored properly, nail polish can last quite a while, but do you use it? Or do you get your nails professionally done? Check for clumps and proper consistency of the colors you still wear regularly.

Perfume/cologne

Perfumes and colognes are better stored outside the bathroom due to temperature fluctuations. Perfumes can last from one to five years, depending on how they’re stored and their formulations. Review all your bottles and figure out what you actually apply. If you have more scents than you can use, this is a great item to donate to a friend and share the scent love!

Appliances/gadgets

Sort through your hairdryers, curling irons, straighteners (do you have two? more?), shavers, face massagers, nose clippers, or whatever other bathroom gadgets you may have. These are items you may hold onto for “someday,” but if you haven’t used something in the last year, what are the chances that you will?

Bath accessories/toys

Often large bathtubs can be real estate for bath mittens, loofahs, scented bath bombs, and fancy bubbles we rarely, if ever, use. Plus, if you have kids, how many bath toys do they have that line the bathtub walls? Get rid of items with mold, which easily grows on damp surfaces, and then of course discard whatever you don’t use.

First-aid supplies

We don’t recommend storing first aid supplies in the bathroom unless you’re accident-prone and use them daily. Go through your supplies and see what you use frequently and need to restock and figure out what you never use. Chances are you have no Band-Aids but enough alcohol swabs to swab the neighborhood clean. When an emergency does happen, you want a well-stocked first-aid kit. And one you can find, not lingering behind other clutter!

We don’t recommend storing any medications in the bathroom, due to the temperature and humidity of the environment. See the next section for pointers on minimalizing and relocating your medications.

Getting Minimal with Your Medicine Cabinet

Chances are you have lots of items — not simply medicine — in your medicine cabinet. These cabinets can be a great catch-all for frequently used items, products you share with your partner or kids, and anything you need to quickly tuck away when guests pop by (although many guests take a peek to see what you’ve stashed!) Don’t get distracted by the junk — you’ll get to it in time. When tackling your medicine cabinet, start by examining the medications and keep your focus on them, because they have unique considerations.

After you’ve reviewed all of your medications and decided what to keep and where to put it, then take a look at the remaining cabinet contents, using the guidance in the section “ Assessing what you have and deciding what to keep .”

Deciding what to keep

Start your medicine cabinet assessment by collecting all your cold remedies, prescriptions, pain relievers, and whatever else you have stored, and check their expiry dates. Get rid of anything that’s expired. Next, toss what you haven’t used in the last year. Yes, even the NyQuil. (You may say, “But I could get a cold this year.” Yes, you could, but you also may not. And chances are your local pharmacy will have an updated cold medication if you need it.)

When it comes to medications, we even get a bit scientific. According to a study at the University of California, many staples that you have in your medicine cabinet, such as nasal sprays, eye drops, and even some lotions, have ingredients that can inhibit mitochondria (which power cellular function), and we don’t want anything interfering with them! Interference can lead to many diseases and even affect fertility. Therefore, you really don’t want to keep more products than you need. It’s hard to determine which products are harmful from ingredient lists, but any ingredient that ends in “ammonium chloride” is best to stay away from if possible.

When medication is prescribed, it’s usually for 7–14 days, and you’re usually prescribed an amount you’ll use up by the end of that timeframe. In theory you shouldn’t have any leftover medications to purge. But sometimes you do, for whatever reason, and you think you can use the remnants the next time you get sick. Using medication without talking to your doctor first, even if you’ve taken it before, can lead to misuse, development of antibiotic resistance, and susceptibility to other diseases, plus you may not be getting the right treatment for whatever ails you. So get rid of old prescriptions during your cabinet review.

Deciding where to put your keepers

After you’ve decluttered your medications, decide if your medicine cabinet is the best place to store the medications that you’re keeping. Wait, isn’t that the purpose of a medicine cabinet? It’s in the name!

Contrary to what many people understand, most medicine should be stored at room temperature — 68 to 77 degrees — and kept away from moisture. Most bathrooms are prone to higher temperatures and humidity, due to the bathing and face-washing taking place regularly. So despite what the name suggests, medicine cabinets are poor places to keep drugs. After you audit and purge your expired or unneeded medications, consider moving everything you want to keep out of the medicine cabinet and out of your bathroom in general. (We like to keep our medications in a nearby linen closet, so everyone can access them regardless of who’s using the loo. And a high shelf means we can safely stash this stuff out of our young children’s reach.) Whatever you do, keep the packaging or at least the label. You want to avoid the cluttered mess of unidentifiable products shown in Figure 6-6 .

Figuring out what to do with the remains

So what’s the best way to quickly get rid of medications after you’ve assessed and purged any on-hand? Take them back to the pharmacy, because pharmacies can professionally dispose of them.

Don’t flush unused medications down the toilet, as this can contaminate our waterways.

Setting Strict Timelines for Product Lifetimes

Once your bathroom is nearly at its minimalist best — purged of what you don’t use, organized so what you use regularly is easy to find and access, and medications examined for currency and placement — we recommend you set timelines for all your products so you never store expired or old products again. In this section are the guidelines we try to follow, based on what’s worked for us in our own minimalist journey.

Some expiration dates are set by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA, www.fda.gov ), after which point the product isn’t as effective or can become dangerous as the formulation ages. Seeing these expiry dates will hopefully also make you think twice before you buy and store too many multiples. Keep in mind that any products that have been compromised by heat or light (both of which abound in a well-used bathroom!) can expire even faster.

The more you focus on only essentials, the less you need to worry about extensive expiry lists.

Makeup

Here are our recommendations on how long to keep makeup:

  • Mascara: Three months — yes, that’s right. Such a short duration for the expense of some brands! Past three months, you can be at risk of an eye infection, and we have one friend to whom this actually happened!
  • Liquid eye liner: One year; again, due to the risk of eye infection.
  • Eyeshadow: Powder forms can last up to three years depending on the quality. Liquid eyeshadow has a shelf life of one year, after which it starts to separate.
  • Lipstick: Up to three years. Lip gloss may start to separate after one year. Lip balms can last up to five years.
  • Blush: Powders can last up to three years; creams usually one year.
  • Bronzer: Up to three years.
  • Foundation: This is a tough one, as it depends on the type. Oil-free foundations usually last only a year, while foundations with oil can last up to two years. Follow the supplier’s recommendations, found on the product box or their website.
  • Concealer: Liquid forms last up to one year; powder up to two years.
  • Other powders: Up to two years.

Everything else

We often forget how long we’ve had miscellaneous bathroom products. Create a system to help you keep track of when your items expire, and if the packaging doesn’t list an expiration date, write the purchase date on the package using permanent marker, and then keep track of each item per the following guidelines:

  • Moisturizers: Up to two years; however, check the expiration dates because sometimes products in jars are pretty but not properly packaged, so they expire faster than they should. How manufacturers package moisturizers can make a huge difference in how long they can last. You now see many lotions in darker glass bottles for this very reason.
  • Sunscreen: Three years if no specific expiration date is provided (this is actually federally regulated in the U.S.). Exposure to heat and sunlight can make sunscreen expire more quickly, so cover it with a towel or tuck into a bag when you’re using it outdoors.
  • Acne products: Any products containing benzoyl peroxide will only work for three months once opened.
  • Anti-aging products: It depends on the ingredients, but anything with vitamin C, retinol, or glycolic acid tends to break down pretty quickly. It’s best to stick with six months.
  • Shampoo: Unopened shampoo should last for three years (but don’t use this as a reason to stock up on a bunch of extras). Once opened, shampoo is good for two years. The same rules apply to conditioners.
  • Deodorant/anti-perspirant: Up to three years, but check expiry dates.
  • Toothpaste: Check the expiry date, since most toothpastes contain fluoride, and this ingredient expires.
  • Soap: Usually up to three years; the same applies to body washes.
  • Shaving products: Up to two years. Depilatory and bleaching creams usually only last for up to six months because of their harsh ingredients.
  • Nail polish and remover: Some say that nail polish remover never expires, which may be a good thing, as it’s one of those products we don’t usually use daily; however, nail polish usually goes bad in under two years.

We tried to be comprehensive with this list; however, we also want to mention that organic or all-natural products, which we recommend if you have the option, actually expire much sooner. Their ingredients expire very quickly due to having less chemicals in their recipes. Even more reason to keep a clutter-free bathroom if you’re into organic products!

Keeping Dry the Minimalist Way

Many people ask us, “How many towels do I need to keep?” We like to keep a limited number of towels for us and guests because we don’t want to waste water washing them all the time.

As a general rule, each person in the household should have two full sets of towels. That can include a large bath towel, hand towel, and face towel or washcloth. (You may want to get a couple more face towels for each person, because faces are likely washed more frequently.) This quantity means one set is available while the other one is being washed. Plus, we keep two additional sets on hand for guests. And when you have more than two guests, you can borrow from the household towel stash. We also have two hand towels per bathroom sink, which means one’s in use while the other is in the wash.

To find the right number for you, track how many fresh towels you and your family use in a week, and challenge yourself to use fewer. You’ll eventually find the quantity that means you have a fresh towel when you need one without needing to do laundry more often than you think is reasonable — meeting your definition of minimalist.

Make sure your towels are in good shape. Examine each, and those you don’t need and are in good condition can be donated to a charity. If any are ripped or stained beyond repair, can you use them as rags or use them for your furry friends?

Our biggest keep-it-simple tip is to find a color/brand you like and stick with it. This way, towels are easier to replace, and you don’t need to buy 50 of them at once. We prefer all white; they’re easy to bleach, replace, and find.
