Raise your hand if you know where your money went last month. According to a survey published by Mint.com on December 11, 2020, 65 percent of Americans have no clue where they spent their money in the previous month. Basically, millions of people are saying that their money disappeared without their knowing its whereabouts. No wonder money is considered the number one stressor in today’s society! (It’s also one of the leading reasons people cite when filing for a divorce.)

We’ve been in the personal finance industry for years, and we’ve seen and heard many people talk about money. But one thing we don’t hear enough of is how accepting financial responsibility has helped them reach their personal goals — including their goals driven by intentionality and minimalism.

Having a successful budget based on your values (see Chapters 10 and 11 ) helps you accomplish whatever minimalist goals you may have, whether that’s saving money for a big purchase or a rainy day, paying off debt, or something else. Knowing where your money goes and how much you have coming in is key to removing the feeling of chaos that often surrounds spending money.

Knowing What a Budget Is and Why You Should Use One

A budget is a plan that tracks where you’ll spend your money. Think of your budget as a monthly money to-do list. A budget helps you make sure your finances are in order so you can save for a rainy day (an emergency fund), spend your money intentionally on things that matter to you, and make sure all your bills are taken care of. Budgeting helps you take charge of your finances to ensure your current and future financial needs are met, and a minimalist budget is one that’s driven by your values and priorities, not a bunch of unneeded expenses gobbling up your paycheck.

Understanding the benefits of budgeting

Budgeting can be challenging, but it can also impact your minimalist life more than you realize. Here are several reasons why you should be budgeting:

  • You can control your spending. When you have more cash going out than coming in, you find yourself in a deficit. A deficit means you end up short on money for your bills. You can have a deficit if you don’t have enough income to cover your general expenses. This scenario is one of the main ways people find themselves in credit card debt. If you know where your money is going, you can manage your spending and keep your account with a positive balance.
  • You’re less likely to waste time and money. It’s okay to be disorganized sometimes (we’re all busy; it happens), but disorganized finances can hurt you in the long run. You lose time looking for bills. You lose money by paying an occasional (or frequent) late fee because you forget when your bills are due. You buy compelling but unneeded items from your social media feed. All of these common scenarios are the antithesis of what you’re going for — a life that’s more intentional, focused on what you value, and a financial approach that supports both. Utilizing a budget helps ensure you’re buying stuff based on your priorities, and your bills are in order so you’re making payments on time.

  • It helps you make progress on your short- and long-term goals. A budget helps free up money for accomplishing the goals you’ve identified (see Chapter 11 for tips). Budgets can also help you track your progress to see whether you’re moving that needle closer to the finish line.

    When you practice budgeting, you become more aware of when your money is coming in and where it’s going, making you think about your finances differently. Even just having this awareness may prompt you to put money toward savings goals you may have, or allow you to pay off your debt faster. (The faster you pay off your debt, the less money you pay toward interest and finance charges. Chapter 12 talks about debt.)

  • It helps you flex your “be resourceful” muscle. When people first start budgeting, they may realize that they don’t have enough money to cover all their wants after first covering their needs and stashing some away for a rainy day. When trying to make your money stretch further, you may find yourself being more resourceful than usual. This dovetails with becoming more minimalist — you’re choosing to own less and, sometimes, be creative with what you own.
  • It can save your physical, emotional, and mental health. Stress comes in all shapes and forms, and money problems are definitely one cause. Budgeting can keep you from wondering where your money is and how you’ll pay your bills — you’ve created a plan (your budget) based on your values and your needs, so you can sleep well at night, knowing your money is being managed capably and in support of your priorities.
  • Gaining financial stability is a game-changer. Utilizing a budget that’s based on your values and priorities is key to financial stability, which is a huge part of financial wellness. Besides feeling a sense of pride, you have the comfort of knowing your life is financially covered no matter what. When you’re financially stable, the chaos surrounding your finances decreases dramatically. You can leave toxic employer situations and unsafe relationships, or cover unexpected expenses, or prepare for big future expenses, all on your own.
  • It helps you think about retirement before it’s too late. Setting aside money for a rainy day isn’t the only reason you should be budgeting. Your retirement is also essential and can be an overlooked category. Many employers match retirement contributions, but when money doesn’t flow like wine, taking them up on the offer can be hard. To start saving as early as possible to take advantage of compound interest (interest earned on your money and on the interest it already made) is important, but it can also be more complicated when your cash flow is strapped.

Figuring out why budgets sometimes fail

Budgets get a bad rap, and many people hate them. They can feel too restrictive; isn’t life supposed to be enjoyed? Maybe you’ve tried a budget and it just didn’t work, or tracking down all your bills was too stressful.

We may lose our personal finance street cred, but we’ve been known to agree with these statements. A budget can be time-consuming, frustrating, and exhausting for a few reasons:

  • You forget to adjust it. You’ve assigned values to all of your expense categories and you’re working your budget as planned. But after a period of time, you find you’re overspending consistently. Your life and needs may have changed, and those original assignments may not work anymore, but you didn’t tweak your budget to reflect how your life has changed.
  • You don’t have an emergency fund. An emergency fund is money you set aside for unexpected expenses or emergencies: car repairs, medical bills, vet expenses, a last-minute flight to see a sick family member, and so on. When unexpected calamities happen, you want a stack of cash sitting nice and neat for you, whether in a high-yield savings account or a cookie tin that’s usually reserved for the sewing supplies. If you’re ill-equipped for an emergency, you’ll most likely take on debt to cover it.
  • Your budget exceeds your income. Maybe your discretionary expenses are too high (for example, you frequently get food delivery, or you often make additional purchase to hit a total that qualifies for free shipping). Or your living expenses become greater than you’ve budgeted (weather-related utilities bumps are one example; so are increased costs due to inflation). Regardless of the reason, you’re spending more than you’re making.

We give you some pointers throughout this chapter to help you avoid these common budget-busters.

Budgeting Basics: Breaking Down a Budget’s Parts

A budget isn’t a punishment, a spreadsheet of shame, or a documentary of your past financial crimes. It’s simply a plan — a tool that helps you decide, in advance, what your money will do for you. At its core, a budget is a map: it shows where your money needs to go, where you want it to go, and where you may be able to stop wasting time and cash.

Most people think budgets are restrictive. In reality, a good budget is freeing. It creates clarity. It cuts noise. It keeps impulse buys and “oops” expenses from hijacking your goals. Instead of reacting to every sale, subscription, and social-pressure purchase, you’re choosing how to use your money based on your values, priorities, and long-term well-being. That’s financial minimalism in action — spend on purpose and skip the rest.

A budget also gives you the data you need to make smart decisions: how much you can safely save, whether you can afford a vacation without wrecking next month, how close you are to paying off debt, or whether a raise should go toward lifestyle upgrades or your emergency fund. You don’t need a complicated system to get these answers; you just need a process that helps you see your money clearly.

Every budget has three components:

  • Expenses (how much money you’re spending)
  • Income (how much money you have coming in)
  • Financial goals (things you want to accomplish, such as paying down debt or saving for something in the future)

There are different types of budget approaches you can use. We think zero - based budgeting is most aligned with minimalism — every dollar is assigned to a priority you’ve identified, whether that’s an expense or an investment (including savings). Before we get to specifics about zero-based budgeting, we want you to determine how you’re spending your money. Then you can use that information to inform the zero-based budget you create (and any other type of budget, as well).

Starting with a Spending Check

The first part of living a minimalist financial life is taking inventory of your finances. You’re going to want to know where you’re coming from so you can create a budget, identify opportunities for minimalist spending, track your progress, and reap the rewards of spending that’s in line with your minimalist mindset.

Listing what you own and what you owe

To figure out your assets (what you own) and your liabilities (what you owe), start by listing each. Just jot down all the types you have — you can get specific information about each one after you figure out which of these two buckets the stuff in your financial life belongs to.

Spoiler alert: This step may take a bit longer if you don’t already have all your financial accounts in one place. In the following sections, we give you an overview of assets and liabilities and your options for corralling your financial info in one place.

Assets

Financial assets are anything with a positive financial value. Lots of things can qualify, as shown in Figure 11-1 :

  • Checking and savings accounts, the cash you have under the mattress, and any checks you’ve been meaning to cash.
  • Money market accounts, your retirement fund at work, pensions, brokerage accounts, company stock shares, treasury bills, and bonds.
  • Some life insurance policies.
  • Physical items you can liquidate (convert into cash), such as your home; your car or other vehicles like ATVs, motorcycles, or boats; jewelry; collectibles; and anything else that you can sell for a profit. (That fancy purse you keep in a dust jacket that you can sell tomorrow for a few hundred dollars? Put that on the list, too.)

Liabilities

Your financial liabilities are anything you owe money on. Your home is considered a positive financial asset (see the preceding section), but the mortgage you owe isn’t. Other types of housing debt can include a second mortgage, a rental agreement, or property taxes.

Other financial liabilities include the following:

  • Loans on other items you’ve financed, such as your car or education
  • Personal loans from a financial institution or family friend
  • Credit cards that currently have a balance
  • Other outstanding debt like medical bills

Even unpaid taxes, fines you owe a court, or child support can be considered financial liabilities.

Reviewing your spending for the past three months

After you’ve identified your assets and liabilities, you need to review the past three months of your spending habits. Where do you even start? Easy: your bank statements.

Check whether your bank has a spending analysis tool. Some banks have a “plan and learn” section that offers a spending report. It automatically breaks your spending into categories, shows you what you spend where, and then gives you an overall percentage of where your money went for the month. This kind of resource can be a great place to start to see your spending in action.

Open a new spreadsheet on your computer or grab a pen and a piece of paper. Start to look over every expense you have. First, note any expenses that are the same amount every month, such as your rent, Internet bill, car payment and insurance, streaming services, and any other expenses that rarely change unless you make a specific change to your service, such as upgrading a tier.

Next, look over your spending that can vary from month to month. Items under this type of spending can be groceries, dining out, gas for your car, and any entertainment expenses while having fun with your friends or family. You can list them individually by category. If this approach seems too overwhelming, or you buy different types of items in one general place, group the transactions by store (see Figure 11-2 ).

When looking over your spending, don’t forget to look at where you’re using your credit cards and any apps you use to transfer money, like Venmo or PayPal. You can easily see these transactions while reviewing your bank statements, but they often don’t list the retailer or item. For example, if you use your PayPal account when you buy items through your favorite food delivery service, your checking account will have a lot of PayPal transactions but doesn’t categorize them automatically into “dining out.” You’ll have to review the transaction in your actual PayPal account to know what business got paid for each amount.

After you’ve gone over the past three months of your spending habits, you should notice trends. You may see that you’ve spent more money than you imagined on dining out or mindlessly shopping online. You see how those $5 transactions here and there add up over time, and probably to an amount you didn’t realize. You may also be pleasantly surprised at how little you’ve spent and be able to congratulate yourself on keeping your expenses down. Either way, you’ve done some important background work that’ll help you create your budget, which is your next step.

Moving from Awareness to Action with Zero-Based Budgeting

A zero-based budgeting system is when you assign a job to every single dollar that comes your way. The overall goal of a zero-based budget is to leave no dollar unaccounted for. With this budgeting method, you should have a zero balance after you allocate funds to each category of your fixed and variable expenses.

A zero balance doesn’t mean that you should spend all your money on goods or services. Some of the funds you allocate will probably go to debt repayment, savings, an emergency fund, or a sinking fund (see the sidebar “ Accounting for periodic or unexpected expenses: A sinking fund ” for more info). A zero balance means that, in general, your income aligns with all your spending and doesn’t leave you in a deficit.

Zero-based budgeting is the natural next step after your spending check because it forces you to decide, on purpose, where every dollar should go. That’s exactly why it’s a good approach for minimalists. Instead of letting habits, autopayments, or impulses run the show, you’re aligning each dollar with something you value.

Setting up your zero-based budget

Your zero-based budget should include your fixed income and expenses, your variable income and expenses, and your minimalist financial goals. When you budget this way, you’re proactive and not reactive with your finances. This way of budgeting also helps you make sure you’re putting an adequate amount toward your priorities, whether that’s savings or charities or debt repayment. Remember, the objective is to ensure every dollar is accounted for so you can make every penny work for you. The following sections break down the steps for creating a zero-based budget.

Adding up expenses and subtracting them from your income

Go over your spending analysis for the past three months and determine your expenses — that is, where your money has been going — for the past month. Figure 11-3 shows an example of what that can look like.

List all your spending individually. Doing so helps you better categorize your spending later (in the following section, in fact). Write down every single thing you’ve spent your money on.

Along with your fixed expenses (which occur on schedule and are the same amount every time you pay them), be sure to account for your variable expenses, such as gas and groceries. Debt repayment also counts as an expense, so be sure to include any payments you may have, such as student and/or auto loans and credit card payments.

After you’ve listed your fixed and variable expenses, subtract your total expenses from your income, including both monthly and variable income. Monthly income is regular income you can count on to be there every month, come rain or come shine. Examples include a job through an employer where you receive a regular paycheck, retirement benefits such as pensions or Social Security, and child support. Variable income is income you receive irregularly, such as income earned from side gigs, freelancing, or simply selling items from around your home. Figure 11-4 is an example of how total income is calculated, while Figure 11-5 shows a budget tracking income and expenses.

In a perfect world, your income minus your expenses is as close to zero as possible. In an even better world, you’d have a surplus of income, which means you’re well on the right track to growing your savings account. If your balance is negative, that means you have a deficit that you need to fix. We explain how to do that in the following section, so don’t panic. You just need to know what you’re working with here.

Assigning your total income to budget categories

After you subtract your expenses from your income (see the preceding section), you can see not only where your money goes but also how it matches up to the income you’re bringing in. The next step is to look at your spending and group similar expenses together into categories. Some examples of main expenses placed into budgeting categories include the following:

  • Living expenses: Things you can include for living expenses are rent or a mortgage, electricity, Internet, your cellphone bill, and water. You may also have fees related to your living situation, such as a homeowners association fee (HOA) that you pay regularly.
  • Children: Clothes, school lunches, education fees, before- and after-school care, summer programs, sports equipment, club fees, school supplies, allowance, and other child-related expenses fit here.
  • Food: Dining out, groceries, meals you pay for while at work, and coffee are all expenses you can list here. If dining out and alcohol are often on the same receipt, pick between entertainment or food and then list them in that specific category only.
  • Pets: Food, vet checkups, medicine, medical supplies, grooming appointments, cat litter, animal bedding, treats, and toys are all items that can go in this category.
  • Transportation: Any costs associated with how you get around town go here. Regular expenses may be gas, car insurance, a pass to ride on public transportation, or parking you regularly pay for. We’d also include any necessary travel you may have to take, such a plane ticket to see a family member who may need you. But travel for vacation goes under entertainment.
  • Medical: Put anything health-related here, including health insurance costs, prescriptions, doctor co-pays, supplements, and over-the-counter medicines.
  • Personal: This category is for anything related to you personally that doesn’t benefit anyone but you, such as clothing, makeup, a gym membership, and anything toward self-care.
  • Entertainment: You can drop subscriptions such as streaming services, gaming apps, supplies or fees related to a hobby, events such as concerts with friends, and any bar tabs in this category.
  • Debt repayment: Put any financial obligations you have in this category. Include an amount you want to pay toward any debt you want to eliminate.
  • Financial goals: List any special events or items you want to start saving for, such as a house or that beautiful European vacation that you’ve been dreaming about.

These options are simply a few categories and examples to get you started. You may not assign all your expenses to the categories listed here because it’s your budget.

After you’ve decided what categories you’ll use to group your expenses, add up your costs to see how much you’ve spent in each separate area. You now know the amount of money you need to assign to that category in your budget. Figure 11-6 shows an example of what it could look like.

If you still have funds left over after assigning money to each category (including your financial goals), start brainstorming for what you want to do with it. Should you be putting more money toward a specific category (maybe your child-related expenses will soon increase as your kid takes up travel sports) or adding more to debt repayment (always worth paring down debt as quickly as possible if all your funds are otherwise adequately accounted for)? Think about your financial goals and what makes sense for you. Remember, your overall goal when using this budgeting method should be to get to zero.

Having enough money to pay for your monthly expenses

If you don’t have enough income to assign to all of your categories — your expenses are greater than your income — go through your spending again to see where exactly your money is going. Here are a couple of particular pitfalls to look out for:

  • Online shopping: Shopping online is one of the most significant ways to overspend, especially with money you don’t have. Retailers such as Amazon have made at-home shopping so easy! They work hard to keep their ordering processes smooth to keep you wanting to go back for more.
  • Dining out (including food delivery): Another major budget killer is dining out. Lack of time is a big reason people eat out or get delivery as often as they do. Are you grabbing a coffee daily on your way to work, catching up with coworkers over a quick lunch at the nearest fast-food place, and then ordering meal delivery when you get home? Unless you have a huge number of Benjamins in the bank, take a step back.

If your spending isn’t out of control but is still more than you’re making, you need to do one of two things:

  • Cut your expenses: Consider your minimalist goals and evaluate if you can cut any expenses in support of a leaner life. A few options include evaluating whether you can make do with a lower tier of a subscription or foregoing it altogether, trimming your home-related expenses in anticipation of buying less, deciding what you want to get out of travel (is it high-brow service or making memories with family or friends), and simple changes like taking advantage of loyalty programs at places like grocery stores and gas stations (where you may be able to earn points to use as a discount for gas at a later date).
  • Earn more income: Look for additional income streams that you aren’t tapping into. For instance, you may be able to rent out a room in your home or start a side gig.

Depending on the season of your life, one of these options may be much easier to manage than the other.

ACCOUNTING FOR PERIODIC OR UNEXPECTED EXPENSES: A SINKING FUND

What about those expenses that you know exist but only remember when you receive a bill or a payment is magically taken out of our checking account? Things like car registration, estimated quarterly taxes, potential maintenance or replacement costs for cars or appliances, and holiday gifts. May we present to you the idea of a sinking fund?

A sinking fund is an account you have to help offset the cost of irregular or one-time expenses. It’s a strategic way to set aside money every month and prevent your finances from becoming overextended on bills or purchases that might not always be on your radar. Here’s how it works:

  1. Grab your calendar and make a list of periodic payments you made over the course of that year that weren’t regular occurrences.

    Also list any events you have coming up if they require you to spend any additional money that you usually wouldn’t. Some examples include graduation or wedding gifts and age-related birthday celebrations, like your best friend’s 40th birthday party.

  2. Group the items into categories like you do your other expenses and add up the total.

  3. Divide the total from Step 2 by 12.

    That’s the number you should put aside as a monthly budget category. For example, if you plan on spending $1,200 for gifts over the year, you need to be setting aside $100 every month in a sinking fund designated for gifts — a sinking fund you allocate in your monthly zero-based budget. With this newly created sinking fund, you don’t have to scramble to pay for gifts as they pop up. Instead, you use the money you’ve already allocated for this exact purpose.

Don’t keep your sinking funds in your checking account. To prevent spending money that you’re trying to put aside, consider moving the funds to an online high-yield savings account (HYSA). HYSAs are a better idea than a traditional savings account for this purpose because you collect interest on the money you were going to put aside anyway. The interest rate on these accounts varies, but it’s usually anywhere between 1 and 4 percent.

Review your existing expenses with an eye toward your minimalist intentions. Have you reallocated expenses based on your new minimalist behaviors? If you’ve decided to purchase clothing less frequently but of higher quality, does your budget reflect this? If you’ve decided that travel is more important than other entertainment like sporting events, have you updated your budget to reflect this? If you’ve adopted a minimalist financial approach more recently than the past three months, take a look at your actual expenses every month until you have at least three months’ worth to review. Then your assessment of spending versus earning will reflect your new intentionality, and you can make expense or income adjustments accordingly.

Tracking your spending for a month

After you’ve set up your categories for the month and have included the total amount of money allowed (more on that in the earlier section “ Assigning your total income to budget categories ”), you’re ready for the tracking stage of the zero-based budgeting system.

For the next 30 days, track every penny you spend. Document your spending and label it with the category it belongs to. Document your spending in any way that works for you. You just need to track your spending and keep yourself accountable. Keep track of your new budget in an online spreadsheet, an app on your phone (You Need a Budget, or YNAB, is a popular one), or paper and pen if that works best for you (see Figure 11-7 ). Just find a method that you’ll stick with.

Making sure you track as you spend

After you spend money into a category, subtract it from the budgeted amount you decided on when creating this month’s budget. For example, if you’ve budgeted $150 for takeout and then spend $24.35, you have exactly $125.65 left to spend for the rest of the month.

Accurately documenting your expenses to subtract them from their category is essential. By entering your expenses, you’re able to monitor your spending to keep yourself on track. Overspending is easier when you’re not paying attention than when you’re regularly tracking your spending.

Because the zero-based budget method can take up a lot of your time at first, and then later on as you routinely check in, figure out a way to make it stick. Like other new minimalist habits, you want to figure out a routine that works for you as you work with your new budget. Consider putting time aside each day to catch up on your spending so that you’re not scrambling to remember later exactly what you’ve spent your money on. If daily seems like too much, consider weekly check-ins, but don’t put it off any longer than that. Keeping an eye on where your money goes so you don’t overspend makes you accountable.

Evaluating your success at the end of the month

At the end of the month, sit down and check to see how well you were able to stick to the minimalist amounts you assigned to each category. First of all, congratulations on sticking with it, regardless of whether you came out in the green or the red. If you ended up at zero with your budget (or with a surplus), you’re doing it right. When you aim for zero, extra money is always a pleasant surprise. You can put those additional funds toward different financial goals or any sinking fund(s) you’ve decided to start working on (see the sidebar “ Accounting for periodic or unexpected expenses: A sinking fund ” for more).

If you ended up with a deficit, never fear; you’re still doing it right. We say that because even if you had a deficit, you tracked your spending for an entire month and stuck with it. A deficit in your first month of budgeting isn’t the end of the world. Any type of budgeting method you use is going to require you to be flexible. Like many other things in your life, it will not be perfect on the first try. It gets easier over time and with practice.

Look over the expenses in the categories in which you went over budget and see whether anything sticks out to you, for example multiple lunches at work or online shopping purchases. Now ask yourself some questions: Are these overages a result of a lack of planning? Did you go too minimalist in your planning and need to allocate a little more for expenses? Or did you fall into some old spending habits and want to recommit to a more minimalist approach?

If planning isn’t the issue, consider adjusting your budget category. You may have assigned too little as you started your minimalist journey. Maybe you forgot an expense or a purchase you regularly need to take care of. If that’s true, adjust it. Also, look at ways you can save on costs to make your budget go even further.

LIVING ON LESS THAN YOU EARN TO HAVE MONEY FOR WHAT YOU PRIORITIZE

When one of this book’s authors was finishing college and barely able to make ends meet, she thought living on less than she earned was nearly impossible. She always had more money going out than was coming in. She knew that working a job that paid close to minimum wage to cover her tuition and other expenses would never get her ahead. Fortunately, she finally found some relief once she finished college (reducing her expenses) and started making a higher salary as she started her career (increasing her income).

Your values are now a big driver to how you spend your money, and if your income isn’t always enough to cover the expenses for what you’ve prioritized, you have some flexibility in how to get to the state of living on less than you earn. The two primary levers are increasing your earnings and decreasing your expenses:

  • You can increase your earnings through your salary, a side hustle, or income-producing projects. Your main job may have opportunities for bonuses, promotions, stock options, or other financial benefits. Besides your main job, there are ways to bring in additional income that you create on your own as a self-employed person or entrepreneur. Many people find real estate to be a lucrative way to increase earnings.
  • You can decrease your expenses by spending money on what you value and cutting what you don’t. No deprivation is required here; it’s important to set up your spending in a way that you will stick with, and feeling deprived all the time is no way to encourage longevity. If an expense gives you joy and fills you with energy, that’s probably not what you want to cut out because it’s something that matters to you. Smart spending matters just as much as cutting spending, and shining a light on your expenses helps you get more confident and intentional about where your money is going. You may be able to decrease expenses through one or two big hits such as housing or vehicle costs or many smaller hits like canceling streaming TV subscriptions you forgot you were still paying for or shopping around for car insurance to get a lower rate.

Some of us are better with one of these levers than the other, but you’ll see the greatest impact if you can do both at the same time.

Ramping Up to Zero-Based Budgeting with the Envelope System

If you have little experience with budgets, or if you’ve had more failures than successes when planning and monitoring how you spend your money, then zero-based budgeting may seem difficult or intimidating as you seek to bring your financial minimalist vision to life. We get it — a zero-based budget can seem like a lot (that level of precision does indeed require a lot of attention). We recognize that any budgeting is better than no budgeting when trying to be intentional with how you spend your money. So if zero-based budgeting has you running for the hills, then consider creating your budget using the envelope budget method.

The envelope budget method (also referred to as cash envelopes or cash stuffing ) is an effective way to monitor your cash flow visually and interactively. Like the zero-based budgeting method, you assign a dollar amount to a list of categories that your expenses fall under. However, the envelope budget doesn’t require you to sort through your checking account to track expenses or sit in front of a spreadsheet to determine how much you have left to stretch until the end of the month.

After you’ve determined the amount of money for each category, you withdraw the amount of cash you’ve budgeted for from your checking account and put it into envelopes assigned to those categories. Then you’re allowed to spend the cash in your envelopes however you see fit. But that’s it — no debit or credit card for everyday expenses. When your cash is gone, it’s gone. You’re out of money until the next payday, when you can refill your envelopes again.

This method of budgeting can help you become more disciplined with your spending. You’re physically handling the money from your checking account, so your cash becomes tangible — physically handing it over for purchases rather than using your debit or credit card. Otherwise, you’d have to deal with a credit card statement that arrives later.

This budgeting system provides firm boundaries, which many of us need to make our cash flow stretch. We think this system works, and it’s easy to implement. Envelope budgeting requires you to be disciplined with your spending without even realizing it. Its simplicity means you’re more likely to stick with it, which is a huge plus for any budget.

CAN YOU USE AN APP INSTEAD OF CASH?

Don’t want to deal with cash and prefer to use an app? Research has shown that, as a society, we hate the feeling of parting with physical cash. This theory is known as the pain of paying concept, which means you experience a feeling of loss when you spend your physical money for a product or service. Conversely, other studies have shown that spending with credit cards doesn’t provide that immediate sense of consequence. Swiping a piece of plastic doesn’t hit your brain the same way, because that’s a problem for future you.

If you love the envelope budgeting method but for whatever reason don’t have easy access to cash, try the Goodbudget app for your phone or online browser. You can assign cash to virtual envelopes and track your spending through the app. Goodbudget immediately updates to let you know how much you have left in your categories, and you can set alerts for when a category gets too low. You can plan for future spending, check on your debt progress, and sync categories with your partner or other family members.

One of the biggest downsides of an app is that it’s easier to ignore limits — you don’t have the aforementioned regret when parting with money the way you do with cash, so it’s easier to spend beyond the limits you’ve established. Still, building sound financial habits is an important step in building and maintaining your minimalist budget, and what’s important is you pick the system that can work for you.
