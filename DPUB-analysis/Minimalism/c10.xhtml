How knowledgeable do you feel about managing your money? Are you successfully living within your means, or is this something you’re hoping minimalism will help you with? Do you carry debt, and on reflection think at least one contributor could be your need to keep up with friends’ or family’s spending? Minimalism is based on what you prioritize, not competing with others. And what about spending your hard-earned dough — do you feel good about your purchases, or do you look back at a month’s spend and wonder where it all went?

Financial minimalism, as we’re defining it, means being intentional with your money. That includes deciding what material things are important to you (including necessities), and then making intentional choices with how you spend what you have. We don’t consider financial minimalism to mean deprivation or austerity, where you spend as little as possible. Life is for living, so we hope you choose some enjoyments as you decide what minimalism means to you.

Seeing Minimalism as a Smarter Way to Spend

Many people have significant gaps in their personal financial knowledge. Though more folks have greater access today to more information than in prior generations, the financial world has grown more complicated, and there are more choices and pitfalls than ever before. When it comes to doing something with your money — saving, spending, investing — the world can feel maximalist and overwhelming.

People who lack knowledge make more mistakes, and the more financial errors you commit, the more money passes through your hands and out of your life. And in addition to the enormous financial costs, you experience the emotional toll of not feeling in control of your finances. Increased stress and anxiety go hand in hand with not mastering your money.

Fortunately, financial minimalism can help reduce financial stress and diminish the chaos you may feel about money. And here’s how:

  • You determine what’s important to spend your money on — what you value. Your values can then lead all financial decisions you make.
  • You create a spending plan that supports these values and priorities. Whether you want to experience more of the world through travel, or be more comfortable in your home with furniture that’s not third-hand, or help your niece pay for college, where you allocate your money is driven by intentional decisions about how you want to live your life.
  • Your intentional decisions enable you to say no to spending opportunities that pop up but aren’t your priorities. It’s easier to scroll past a social media ad for the latest gaming technology or skincare supplement once you’ve decided your minimalist priorities.

Understanding Why You Spend What You Do

Breaking old habits can be hard, especially habits that have been reinforced for years. The temptation to spend money lurks everywhere. Ads show attractive and popular people enjoying the fruits of their labors — a new car, an exotic vacation, and a lavish home.

Your emotions and temptations can get the better of you. Certainly, part of intentionally managing your finances involves coming to terms with your shortcomings and the consequences of your behaviors. If you don’t, you may end up enslaved to an unsatisfactory job so you can keep feeding your spending addiction. Or you may spend more time with your investments than you do with your family and friends. Or unexpected disasters may leave you reeling financially. So as you prepare to make some changes towards living a financially minimalist life, it’s helpful to understand the causes of some of your current behaviors so you can overcome these challenges and stick to making values-based decisions with your money.

Lacking good financial habits

A variety of personal and emotional hurdles can get in the way of making the best financial moves. A lack of financial knowledge (which stems from having little to no personal financial education) can stand in the way of making good decisions.

Some people also tend to blame their financial shortcomings on not earning more income. Such people believe that if only they earned more, their financial (and personal) problems would melt away. Achieving financial success — and more importantly, personal happiness — has much less to do with how much income a person makes but rather with what they make of what they have.

Regardless of your income, you can lead a minimalist life and make your dollars stretch further if you practice good financial habits and avoid mistakes. In fact, the lower your income, the more important it is that you make the most of your income and savings (because you don’t have the luxury of falling back on your next big paycheck to bail you out).

Overspending and living beyond your means

If you’re like most people, you must live within your means to accomplish your financial goals. Doing so requires consistently spending less than you earn and then investing your savings intelligently (unless you plan on winning the lottery or receiving a large inheritance).

Spending less isn’t as easy as it sounds; here are some of the adversaries you’re up against as you attempt to control your spending:

  • Many folks earn just enough to make ends meet. And some can’t even do that; they simply spend more than they make. The result of such spending habits is, of course, an accumulation of debt.
  • Access to credit is around every corner. Thanks to ATMs, credit cards, myriad smartphone apps, PayPal, Venmo, and so on, it’s never been easier to access your money and spend it 24/7.
  • Credit cards encourage you to live beyond your means. If you pay your bill in full every month, credit cards offer a convenient way to buy things with an interest-free, short-term loan. But if you carry your debt over from month to month at high interest rates, credit cards make it easy and tempting to spend money that you don’t have.
  • Car loans are so easy to get. It’s easy to walk onto a car lot and go home with a new car that you couldn’t afford if financing weren’t available. The dealer gets you thinking in terms of monthly payments that sound small, but not when compared to the total sticker price once interest charges are added in.
  • It can be hard to avoid outside influences. When friends suggest dinner, a sporting event, or a show, few people say, “Let’s go someplace (or do something) cheaper. I can’t afford to spend this much.” You don’t want to sound like a cheapskate. But the reality is, some of your friends have more money than you do (and the others may be spending what they shouldn’t). Your spending habits should be driven by your goals, not those of others.

Figuring Out Your Values and Goals

Before you can simplify your money, you have to decide what it’s actually for. Most of us spend on autopilot: bills, groceries, online “surprises,” repeat. Financial minimalism asks a different question: What do you truly value — and does your spending reflect that?

Figuring out your financial values isn’t about being virtuous or perfect. It’s about getting honest. Do you care more about travel or a bigger house? Convenience or craftsmanship? Paying off debt or upgrading your car? None of these are “right” or “wrong” as you consider financial minimalism; what’s right is what is right for you.

Unless you earn really big bucks or have a large family inheritance to fall back on, your personal and financial desires will probably outstrip your resources. Thus, you need to prioritize your goals.

The following are the most common financial goals we encounter when talking to people wanting to save:

  • Owning your home: Renting and dealing with landlords can be a financial and emotional drag, so most folks want to buy into the American dream and own some real estate — the most basic of which is your own home.
  • Making major purchases: Most folks need to plan ahead for major purchases such as a car, living room furniture, vacations, and so on.
  • Retiring/achieving financial independence: No, retirement doesn’t mean parking yourself in a rocking chair and watching life go by. Retiring is a catch-all term for discontinuing full-time, paid work. Some folks may like the term “financial independence” better, because it conveys reaching a level of financial security where you no longer have to work full time or perhaps at all.
  • Educating the kids: All those diaper changes, late-night feedings, and trips to the zoo aren’t enough to get Junior out of your house and into the real world as a productive, self-sufficient adult. You may want to help your children get a college degree or some other form of higher education or skill training.
  • Owning your own business: Many employees want to take on the challenges and rewards that come with being the boss. The primary reason that most people continue just to dream is that they lack the money to leave their primary job. Although many businesses don’t require a bunch of start-up cash, almost all require that you withstand a substantial reduction in your income during the early years.

Because everyone is different, you’ll have goals that are unique to your own situation. Accomplishing such goals almost always requires saving money. As one of our favorite proverbs says, “Do not wait until you are thirsty to dig a well,” so don’t wait to save money until you’re ready to accomplish a personal or financial goal! Chapter 11 can help you with budgeting and getting started with saving.

Going Minimal with F.I.R.E.

The concept of F.I.R.E. (financial independence, retire early) has some similarities to minimalism — at least, the F.I. part does (and the R.E. part may for you, too, depending on your longer-term priorities). Financial independence is living a life you enjoy without having to rely on a paycheck to support that lifestyle. F.I. happens in the mind as much as in the wallet, because the emotions and feelings around freedom are profound and intentional.

R.E. (retiring early) is decoupling from the full-time workforce much sooner than society is used to (typically in your 60s or 70s) so that you can enjoy yourself more while you’re younger. A minimalist lifestyle may enable you to retire early, but early retirement isn’t a core aspect of minimalism.

F.I.R.E. includes some common tenets to help you achieve the primary goal of buying your time back and creating freedom in your life. And doesn’t that just sound like a minimalist approach to life! Here are some of the philosophies of F.I.R.E. that dovetail with financial minimalism:

  • Living on less than you earn: That may mean earning a higher income, lowering your expenses, or both.
  • Maintaining a high savings rate: The gap you’ve created between your income and your expenses gives you room to save and invest. A F.I.R.E. savings rate can be quite high — 50 percent or more — to help you more quickly get to the “retire early” part. The principle here that maps to minimalism is having lower expenses than income so you have some money available for paying down debt or saving or investing.
  • Keeping investing simple and low cost: F.I.R.E.’s strategy is to invest in index funds that offer a diversified basket of stocks or other investments and stay the course even when the market is down. This approach can be effective for financial minimalists, too.
  • Minimizing taxes: Taxes are controllable, and there are many opportunities to reduce your tax liability. This includes big areas such as tax-advantaged retirement accounts and other investments with favorable tax treatment.
  • Enjoying the journey: How you feel and your happiness is key to those practicing F.I.R.E. and to minimalists alike. Although this isn’t a financial philosophy per se, intentionally living your life helps you focus on the present and enjoy people and places more than things.

Using Net Worth to See Where You Stand

Net worth is the amount of money you would have if you sold all your stuff and paid off all your debts. Officially, the equation goes like this:

Net worth = Assets – Liabilities

We like to think of it more like this:

Net worth = Own – Owe

A strong positive net worth indicates that you

  • Own more than you owe
  • Can afford to pay off all your loans at any time by liquidating assets
  • Probably know more about net worth than you realize

Understanding why net worth matters

Your net worth is a great gauge for measuring your financial progress. It should rise over the course of the years leading up to retirement. From that point, if it tapers off throughout your retirement, that’s okay, so long as you don’t run out of money before your ticker stops ticking.

Your net worth is like a report card reflecting your performance in the realm of personal financial management. Knowing your net worth can come in very handy as you formulate and execute your minimalist financial plans. With net worth in hand, you’re better equipped to do the following:

  • Determine how much money you have available to support your minimalist life.
  • Prove to banks that you’re creditworthy, so you can qualify for loans at competitive interest rates.
  • Facilitate estate planning by helping to determine the equitable distribution of your wealth to your heirs.
  • Budget effectively — you can quickly and easily recognize when you’re burning through your savings too fast, or whether you have extra to spend or invest. ( Chapter 11 gives pointers on budgeting, and Chapter 13 talks about investing.)

Calculating your net worth

Listing everything you own is a major piece of calculating your net worth; so is listing everything you owe . Only with this approach can you really see where you stand financially and start working toward where you want to be.

To calculate your net worth, take the following steps:

  1. List everything of value you own (whatever you can withdraw from an account or sell for cash).
  2. Write a reasonable estimate of each item’s value next to it.
  3. Total the value of all the items to determine the value of what you own.
  4. List all your loans (including mortgages and credit cards).
  5. Write the current balance next to each loan.
  6. Total the balances to determine what you owe.
  7. Subtract what you owe (Step 6) from the value of what you own (Step 3).
  8. The remaining amount is your net worth.

Use a personal finance program, such as Quicken, or a spreadsheet program to monitor your net worth and overall financial health.
