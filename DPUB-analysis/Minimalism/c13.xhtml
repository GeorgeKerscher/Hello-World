Making wise investments doesn’t need to be complicated. However, many investors get bogged down in the morass of the thousands of investment choices out there and the often-conflicting perspectives on how to invest. A minimalist view of investing can help clear the confusion by paring back to just what you need, based on your minimalist goals and values.

Like investment choices, it can be easy to be overwhelmed by the ways in which technology can help you manage your finances. It can seem like there are thousands of websites and apps that promote all of their fintech (financial technology) offerings. In this chapter, we cut through the noise and give you our top recommendations for using technology to help you manage your money.

Establishing Your Investment Goals

Before you select a specific investment, first determine your investment needs and goals. Setting clear goals matters because how you plan to use your money determines how long you need to invest it. And knowing what you’re saving for, and the timeframe by which you want to achieve those savings, helps you determine what risk level you can consider when investing. Both of these pieces of information help you determine which investments to choose.

If you haven’t already, jot down the reasons why you’re saving money — that is, what you’re planning to use it for (for example, an overseas family vacation). And note when you plan to need the money — is it in the next few years or the next couple of decades? For example, you want to take that overseas vacation before your oldest graduates from college, which is three years from now.

You don’t need to earmark every dollar you want to invest, and your timeframes don’t need to be specific dates, but you should set some major goals and have a sense of timing before making investment decisions (flip to Chapter 10 for tips).

Understanding the Primary Investment Types

Imagine a world with only two investment flavors — like an ice cream parlor offering only chocolate and vanilla. Although your investment choices may feel more like 31 Flavors, when investing, your choices ultimately boil down to just two: You can be a lender or an owner.

Looking at lending investments

You’re a lender when you let others use your capital and pay you interest for the privilege. Some great examples for minimalist investors include:

  • High-yield savings accounts and money market funds
  • Bank certificates of deposit (CDs)
  • Treasury bills
  • Bonds issued by a corporation

In each case, you lend your money to an organization — a bank, the federal government, or a private company, for example. In return, they pay you an agreed-upon rate of interest for lending your money. The organization also promises to have your original investment (the principal ) returned to you on a specific date. Lending investments have the following upsides and downsides:

  • Pros: They’re typically low-risk, so you don’t have to stress much about whether you’ll lose any of your money. You have a high likelihood of getting back the money you lent, with the promised interest. It’s very likely you’ll make money.
  • Cons: The return on this type of investment is lower than ownership investments. And since the returns are pretty low, inflation may reduce the purchasing power of your money. Also, the value of a bond may drop below what you paid for it if interest rates rise or the quality/risk of the issuing company declines.

Exploring ownership investments

You’re an owner when you invest your money in an asset, such as a company or real estate, that can generate earnings or profits. You own a slice of something, versus just lending money to it. Ownership investments can have more reward potential than lending investments, but they often also have higher levels of volatility and risk.

  • Investing in stocks: As a stockholder, you buy pieces of a company in the form of stocks, and you hope that the company grows and becomes more profitable over time. If it does, you share in the profits via annual dividends and an increase in the stock price. The downside of stocks is that if a company doesn’t do well, your stock may be worth less than when you bought it (or even worthless!).

  • Investing in real estate: Real estate can produce profits in a couple of primary ways:

    • You can rent out a property for more than the expense of owning it. For example, if you own a property with a monthly mortgage payment of $2,000 and you rent it for $2,500 per month, you make $500 profit per month on the investment.
    • You can make money when you sell a property at a price higher than what you paid for it.

    Real estate comes with risks. Property values depend on not just the property itself, but on the health of the local economy. When jobs are growing and wages are rising, real estate tends to perform well. When employers cut jobs or housing supply outpaces demand, vacancies rise, rents fall, and property values can drop.

  • Investing in small business: You can participate in small business in a variety of ways. You can start your own business, buy and operate an existing business, or simply invest in a promising small business. Small business is the engine that drives much of the country’s economic growth. These investments aren’t risk-free; small businesses may underperform or fail, and you could lose money as an owner or investor.

SHUNNING VOLATILE INVESTMENT VEHICLES

A minimalist investment strategy is one that helps you achieve your goals in a realistic timeframe, with manageable risk, and without lots of stress about your investments. Not all investments fit this bill; here are three types we discourage for financially minimalist investors:

  • When you invest in futures, you agree today on the price at which an asset will be bought or sold on a specific future date. Your gain or loss depends on how the market price moves relative to that agreed price. Futures prices depend on short-term, highly volatile price movements.
  • Options are as risky as futures. With options, you’re betting on the short-term movements of a specific security, which is gambling — not investing.
  • Day trading, which is the rapid buying and selling of securities online in order to profit from short-term price movements, is a high-stress, low-forgiveness approach to investing. We think day trading is more speculation and gambling than intentional investing.

Understanding Investment Returns

You may be wondering how investment returns work. Let’s look at what you might earn — we say “might” because past performance offers clues, not guarantees. During this past century, ownership investments such as stocks and investment real estate returned around 9 percent per year, handily beating lending investments such as bonds (around 5 percent) and savings accounts (roughly 4 percent) in the investment performance race. Sizing Investment Risks

When thinking about risk, we like to look at the whole picture and not just consider the ups and downs of the market. For example, when compared to the yo-yo motions of the stock market, a bank savings account may seem like a less risky place to put your money — it delivers a return, and although the interest rates may vary a little over time, the changes aren’t usually substantial. Over the long term, however, despite its yo-yo tendencies, the stock market usually beats the rate of inflation, while the interest rate on a savings account does not, especially when factoring in taxes. Thus, if you’re saving your money for a long-term goal such as retirement, a savings account can be a “riskier” place to put your money if you’re concerned about the future purchasing power of your investments.

In addition to determining your savings and investment goals and the related timelines (see the section “ Establishing Your Investment Goals ,” earlier in this chapter), you’ll want to consider the historical volatility of the investment you’re considering, and ask yourself if that suits your comfort level and timeline for this investment. The following sections can help you do just that.

Comparing the risks of stocks and bonds

Given the relatively higher historic returns for ownership investments, some people think they should put all their money in stocks and real estate. Your investment goals and related timelines will help you decide if stocks or real estate are right for you.

In order to earn the generous long-term returns that result from ownership investments like stocks and real estate, you must be willing to tolerate volatility. Over the last century, stock markets have fallen more than 10 percent about once every five years, and more than 20 percent about once every decade. Real estate experiences similar periodic setbacks.

This type of investment is one to consider for your longer-term goals, such as retirement decades from now and college for your toddlers, where you can ride out the ups and downs of the market.

A lending-type investment, including bonds, is good for your emergency money or money you expect to use within the next five years. You don’t have to stress about recovering from a potential market downturn, and you’re still earning a return on what you’ve invested.

Benefiting from diversity

Diversification is one of the most powerful investment concepts. It refers to saving your eggs (or investments) in different baskets. Diversification requires you to place your money in different investments with returns that aren’t completely correlated. This is a fancy way of saying you pick of mix of investments so that when some of your investments are down in value, odds are that others are up in value.

To decrease the chances of all your investments getting clobbered at the same time, put your money in different types of investments, such as a combination of bonds, stocks, real estate, and small business.

Using Technology to Make Money Management Easier

Technology can assist you with your personal finance decisions and make financial minimalism an easier goal to achieve. Computers and smartphones can help you stay on top of bills, create and maintain a budget, monitor your spending, research and make investment decisions, and plan for retirement. In this section we discuss how to use technology to get your financial life in order and keep it that way.

Making it easier to manage your finances

Although the number of personal-finance software packages, apps, and websites is large and growing, quality is lagging behind quantity, especially among the free Internet sites. The best software programs can

  • Guide you to better organization and management of your personal finances
  • Help you complete mundane tasks or complex calculations quickly and easily and provide basic advice in unfamiliar territory
  • Make you feel in control of your financial life

A good software package, like a good tax or financial advisor, helps you better manage your finances. It simply and concisely explains financial terminology, and it helps you make decisions by offering choices and recommendations, allowing you to “play” with alternatives before following a particular course of action.

One of our favorites is Quicken. Quicken ( www.quicken.com ) is a good software program that can help you with all of your personal finance needs. We like this tool because it’s been around for decades and has a solid track record. In addition to offering check paying and electronic bill-payment, Quicken is a financial organizer. The program allows you to list your investments and other assets, along with your loans and other financial liabilities. Quicken automates the process of paying your bills, and it can prepare reports that detail your spending by category so you can find the fat in your budget.

Quicken can be used via a mobile app and via your computer, and costs between $2.99-$11.99/month, depending on the option you choose.

Paying your bills, finding ways to save, and tracking your money

Websites and smartphone apps can be great to help you save money and reduce your spending. Some cost money and some are free. Well, nothing is “free” — if you’re not paying for the technology, it’s probably loaded with advertising and/or have affiliate relationships with companies, which means the sites or apps get paid if you click on a link to one of its recommended service providers and buy what it’s selling. Cost-free apps and websites aren’t necessarily bad; just know you may need to navigate around options you don’t want or need.

We recommend the following websites and apps to help with your spending:

  • Goodbudget ( https://goodbudget.com ): This app is great for its simplicity and practicality. The basic (free) version provides you with up to one year of expense-tracking history in ten main categories (envelopes). A paid or premium version ($10/month or $80 per year) that provides up to seven years of expense tracking with unlimited categories as well as email support is also available. Start with the free version and then decide if an upgrade is worth it for you.
  • GasBuddy ( www.gasbuddy.com ): GasBuddy shows you the price of gasoline at various service stations in a local area. It’s free for consumers to use.
  • Tollsmart Toll Calculator ( www.tollsmart.com ): Especially when going on lengthy car trips, car tolls can add up quickly. This free app enables you to compare toll costs for alternative routes.
  • CamelCamelCamel ( https://camelcamelcamel.com ): This app is a price tracker that scans items on Amazon, shows you their price history, and sends you alerts when a product you’re interested in drops in price. ( PriceGrabber scans items everywhere online, although its website is much easier to use than its app.)

Planning for retirement

Good retirement-planning tools can help you plan by crunching the numbers for you. They can also teach you how particular changes — such as your investment returns, the rate of inflation, or your savings rate — can affect when and in what style you can retire.

Here are some high-quality, low-cost retirement-planning tools to consider:

  • T. Rowe Price’s website ( www.troweprice.com ) has several tools, including its “Retirement Income Calculator,” ( www.troweprice.com/usis/advice/tools/retirement-income-calculator ) that can help you determine where you stand in terms of reaching a given retirement goal.
  • Vanguard’s website ( www.vanguard.com ) can help with figuring savings goals to reach retirement goals as well as with managing your budget and assets in retirement. See their tools and calculators at investor.vanguard.com/tools-calculators/overview .

Researching investments

Get investing resources and education via the following websites:

  • The SEC: The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) allows unlimited, free access to its documents at www.sec.gov . All public corporations, as well as mutual funds, file their reports with the agency.
  • Morningstar: You can access Morningstar’s individual stock and mutual-fund reports at www.morningstar.com . The basic reports are free and the unabridged fund reports are available for a fee.
  • Vanguard: The investor-friendly, thrifty Vanguard Group of mutual funds and ETFs has an app and website (see the previous section), where investors can learn the basics of fund investing.

Paying your bills online

You can avoid dealing with paper checks of all types by signing up for online bill payment through your bank, credit union, or brokerage firm. You’ll save money on checks, stamps, and envelopes. Most online bill payments take 2-5 business days for funds to be released from your institution and received by the bill payee.

Using autopay for fixed expenses

Remembering every bill’s due date can be difficult, especially when you’re making sure various bills are paid on time. This juggling act is why you should consider using your credit card or your bank’s ACH checking option to help autopay some of your fixed expenses. By signing up for autopay, you can sometimes receive discounts from your service provider. Plus, you’ll never have to worry about paying a late fee again.

In order for credit cards to work for you, pay them off in full every month. Check out Chapter 12 more on using credit cards successfully.

Saving stealthily with automatic savings account transfers

One of our favorite saving hacks is to set up automatic transfers to a savings account. With this method, every time you swipe your debit card, an amount you choose automatically transfers to your savings account. Without thinking, you can accumulate a little savings (or a lot!) every month, and surprise yourself with the unexpected savings every time you log into your account. Whether you choose $1 or $25, those small amounts add up over time.
