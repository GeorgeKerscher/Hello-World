Chapter 16

Getting Minimalist When Sharing, Watching, and Playing

IN THIS CHAPTER

Understanding how social media can sideline your minimalist vision

Disentangling yourself from TV bingeing and video game overuse

Becoming intentional with digital entertainment

Digital entertainment is woven into everyday life. Social media, streaming media like YouTube and Netflix (and many others), and video games are easy to access, easy to enjoy, and can be genuinely useful. They help us unwind, stay connected, learn new skills, and fill small pockets of time. Used deliberately, digital entertainment can support rest, curiosity, and even creativity.

The challenge is how quietly “a little” turns into “a lot.” Many digital platforms are designed for frictionless use — autoplay, endless feeds, algorithmic recommendations, and social rewards remove natural stopping points. Without clear boundaries, these tools slip from intentional choice into background habit.

A minimalist approach isn’t about rejecting digital entertainment; it’s about right-sizing it.

In this chapter, we explore why social media, streaming content, and gaming are so easy to overuse, how that overuse affects daily life, and how to pare back without deprivation. The goal isn’t less pleasure, but mindful enjoyment — choosing what truly adds value and letting go of what merely fills space.

Getting to Know Social Media

What exactly is social media? We scarcely go a day without hearing the term, and it seems everyone is using Facebook, Instagram, Snapchat, X (formerly Twitter), TikTok, Reddit, YouTube, LinkedIn, and endless other platforms. Social media has invaded almost every aspect of our lives, including shopping, politics, entertainment, marketing, news, and — in perhaps some ways — social connection.

Social media was initially created to connect people online. For many users, social media has become a way to communicate regularly with friends and family — news, photos, events, and updates are just a few clicks away. It morphed to include a wide range of topics curated especially for you (based on your scrolling behavior), and it created the opportunity to become part of a wider “friend” network based on common interests regardless of geography or direct connections. More recently, social media has morphed into a colossal media machine and e-commerce platform.

Recognizing what makes you come back for more

Imagine having a conversation with a friend; they’re leaning forward, moving their head in response to your words, showing facial gestures that are indicative of someone who’s listening and engaged in what you’re saying. You behave similarly in return, and this interaction becomes the basis of the natural ebb and flow of communication. This is an example of social connection, and we’re hardwired biologically to positively respond to such interactions. Social connections are highly rewarding, pleasurable, and health enhancing — especially when sharing is mutual. And because these experiences are pleasurable, these interactions are reflected by an increase in dopamine in your brain.

Social media operates much like this, but instead of real-time conversations to communicate, we use posts, instant messages (IMs) or direct messages (DMs), comments, photos, and videos. And instead of verbal replies that indicate and promote connection, we often respond with likes, comments, and follows . In the next sections, we explain why social media can be so compelling.

Understanding the social hamster wheel

Every time we receive a like, comment, or follow, we get a small dopamine boost — a little hit of happy — that’s tied to the brain’s natural reward for social validation. Because the responses from others are unpredictable, they’re especially powerful at reinforcing repetitive behavior, encouraging us to post and check repeatedly for more engagement and feedback. This creates a cycle — posting, receiving responses, and posting again — known as social validation looping, which keeps users coming back in search of that next rewarding response.

Struggling to resist the possible “maybe”

If you’re rewarded only sometimes — and you never know when — you tend to keep coming back, because the brain is especially drawn to “maybe” — a possible reward. This is called variable reinforcement , and it shows up everywhere, from lottery tickets and slot machines to social media, investing, and online auctions. With variable reinforcement, the chase and the possibility are what keep people engaged. This “maybe” makes habits hard to break, which is why it’s hard to slow down or stop your use of social media, even when you want to.

Exploring the downsides of social media

Social media can connect people across oceans, help you stay in touch with friends and family as lives grow and change, meet people with similar interests (so.many.niche.hobbies), and help you sell or give away all the items you clear out during your minimalist home purge (see Part 2 ). But it’s got some distinct downsides, as follows:

  • It can become easy to base your self-worth on others. Social media creates the impression that what’s rewarding isn’t the living of your life, but the recording and sharing of it. When you post about your experiences, you allow others to respond to your life and, in a sense, create a form of reflected self-esteem. You feel good about what you did, not because of your experience, but because of how others rated your experience.
  • Social media can distort your sense of reality. You’re often shown only people’s “best selves” or a narrow slice of opinion shaped by algorithms that feed you more of what you like. A feed full of holiday parties can make you feel uniquely lonely, even though many of those same people are struggling off-camera. A flood of angry posts about a few potholes can make a town seem outraged, when most people are only mildly annoyed. Your feed amplifies extremes — and in doing so, gives you a misleading picture of what’s actually true.
  • It can diminish social empathy. Social empathy scores are lower among heavy users of social media. Researchers aren’t certain, but it seems that the cues for enhancing social empathy are somehow blunted when receiving information through social media. This lack of empathy can be a driver for cyberbullying or social trolling, both of which shame or negate individuals in a socially public way.
  • It can eat away your day in a flash. Social media isn’t all bad, but it can easily suck you into a rabbit hole, stealing your time, energy, and ability to live a balanced life. Who hasn’t said, “I’ll just take 10 minutes to catch up on the news” only to surface an hour later, astonished that half the evening’s gone?

Finding relief: Life beyond social media

One of the best arguments for simplifying your social media use comes from people who stopped or significantly scaled back social media habits, and the sense of relief and increased time and energy they experienced. Most of them were reluctant to let the habit go, and all of them felt uncomfortable and ill at ease when breaking the social validation loop (see the earlier section “ Understanding the social hamster wheel ”).

However, after several weeks without social media, most, if not all, of those interviewed felt a sense of relief and renewed energy to pursue life. No one expected this, but in story after story, even though the users were expecting a temporary experiment, most decided not to return to regular social media use. Check out the upcoming section “ Mixing Minimalism into Your Digital Fun ” for tips on doing a digital detox and experiencing similar results yourself.

Binge Watching TV and Online Entertainment

Television has really changed in the last two decades. Unlike the past, now you can build your viewing around what you want to watch versus what a TV executive decided, and you can watch entire seasons of TV programs all at once, at any time, in any order.

A binge is the repeated and unconscious use of something that may have a detrimental effect on you. You often hear about binge eating, where a person eats compulsively, often to a point of hurting themselves. The same might be applied to binge TV watching. The ability to turn on your TV and stream ten episodes of an already-released season can be very appealing, but the ability to get what you want, as much as you want, and when you want it is not always a great thing.

Knowing that too much of a good thing can be bad

Streaming offers endless choices, along with ease of access and stimulating content. Streaming also lacks boundaries. Boundaries remind you of natural limits and where a story begins and ends. They serve as markers to help you set limits on your consumption, and the fewer the boundaries, the more likely you’ll just keep watching. This is the reason why the default option on YouTube, Netflix, Amazon Prime, and others is the autoplay feature that automatically takes you to the next episode in a series. Once you’ve finished all episodes and seasons, it’ll even lead you to a similar show and then start it automatically.

It’s easy to assume that more is always better, that more options mean more opportunity for satisfaction. But too many choices can produce complexity and stress. Every choice demands mental energy: comparing, predicting outcomes, and worrying about making the wrong call. The result is decision fatigue — your brain gets tired, faster. More choices also invite second-guessing (“Did I pick the best one?”) and regret (“What if I’d chosen differently?”). Instead of potentially more satisfaction, you get noise, anxiety, and a low-grade sense of pressure.

Realizing that instant gratification can make you watch more

Faster is better, and we’d be lying to you if we said we don’t appreciate faster Internet access. But faster Internet speeds mean a faster life, which means more reacting and less time for intentional decision-making.

Instant access usually means instant gratification — click it, get it. Instant gratification capitalizes on a basic addiction and pharmacology principle: the shorter the period between when you click something online and when it shows up on your screen (and arouses your nervous system through elevation of dopamine), the more addictive that behavior is. Autoplay makes that timeframe very small, and this instant gratification can contribute to a greater tendency to overuse your screen technology.

There’s a benefit in delaying your gratification through instant access: Delay gives you the ability to think through your choices and make decisions based on executive skills and reasoning, which are often inaccessible when engaging in an intoxicating and immediately gratifying experience. Delay gives you a chance to remember your goals and make intentional decisions.

Looking at other problems of watching TV all the time

The problem with television is that in some ways, it’s too good. You can find, watch, and binge TV and movies 24 hours a day (unlike the past, where network television stopped broadcasting at 1:00 or 2:00 a.m.).

Why is this a concern? Here are a few reasons:

  • Intensity is addictive. When it comes to television, bad news is compelling; scary or bad news keeps us watching. And scary sells, meaning there’s a big impetus to feature this type of programming. But a constant diet of bad news compels you to feel that the world is unsafe and dangerous, when in fact, it’s safer today than it was 100 years ago.
  • TV can easily act as your social companion. Television has become a place to hang out with your shows, movies, and content. Autoplay makes it easier than ever to keep the TV running. But if you’re constantly sitting in front of it, you’re doing so at the expense of something else — spending time with family and friends, enjoying hobbies, and interacting with others.
  • It wastes time. If you’re looking at a screen for hours on end, you’re not doing much else. You may be sacrificing sleep and productive activities that enrich your life.

Screening your streaming

Because you have limited time to spend on your screens, including TV, your choices are key. Because you can’t do everything, you need to make sure that you see your TV and Internet use as an expendable activity, not a necessary one. And because it’s hard to appreciate the passage of time when staring at a screen, you must make time-related decisions before you start watching.

Set limits on how much time you want to watch and stick to those limits. Think about the minimalist habits you’re adopting or have maintained and consider how those hours of watching will impact your sleep, health, relationships, work, or academic performance. Then make your viewing decisions accordingly. Do you have time for two episodes of your favorite show or just one? Can you watch an entire Christopher Nolan movie while sticking to your desired limit, or do you watch it over multiple sessions (more popcorn!)?

Technology and screens can be wonderful additions to your life if you apply your humanity to them.

Exploring Video Games

Video games are a part of everyday living. They’re now considered a sport, with championships, televised competitions, and professional teams and players. Video gaming has indeed gone mainstream. Estimates are that 90 to 95 percent of boys and perhaps as many as 60 percent of girls play video games; many adults of all ages play as well. Most people use video games as they were intended, as a hobby or pastime that’s fun and stimulating. But without an intentional approach, video games can suck you in and waste hours of your time just as other digital media can.

A video game is designed to be stimulating and very rewarding. The game designers are trying to create a lush and exciting gamescape that holds your attention, stimulates your senses, and challenges you, all while providing variable and intermittent rewards. These rewards must be unpredictable, because if you know what to expect in the game, you’ll quickly lose interest. Game designers and behavioral (experience) engineers use well-established behavioral and neuroscience principles to structure games in a way that not only keeps you riveted to the screen, but also wanting more.

Gamers keep playing because the expectation of possibly getting a reward within the game is very rewarding itself and is followed by a twofold increase in dopamine compared to actual hits you receive in the game. What this means is that the maybe factor that’s inherent in the game is a powerful reward in and of itself. The possibility of getting a reward is rewarding, so you keep trying over and over. All the while, your brain’s reward center is lighting up like a Christmas tree.

You don’t see or feel any of this. The process is completely automatic and unconscious. All you know is that you’re having fun and that you love the game and can’t wait to play it again.

Checking out the risks of gaming

What makes video games so compelling and, despite your best minimalist intentions, difficult to set aside once you get started? This is the million-dollar question. Video games can be addictive for a variety of reasons:

  • Your self-esteem becomes linked to your game performance. Video games give you a strong sense of identity and competence. Most gamers feel good about their gameplay and levels of mastery in a particular game or games.
  • They reduce your motivation to do anything other than play video games. Video games entice you with their complicated play. They all require attention, concentration, focus, cognitive flexibility, fine motor skills, and good problem-solving ability. Video gaming allows a user to feel an enhanced sense of mastery and accomplishment within the game world, and it’s difficult to reject that enticement.
  • They can build a sense of community but at the expense of real interaction. Video gaming offers the opportunity to join a group of people who all share the same interest, language, and passion. You get a sense of belonging when you’re a member of a gaming guild, a clan, or a team with a special purpose. Problems can arise, though, when gaming becomes the primary vehicle for your social connections.
  • They steal your time. Like other forms of screen-based activities, video games are designed to pull you in and keep your attention. Hours can feel like minutes, and you’ve lost the chance to spend time on real-world activities. Anyone who games can face this consequence.

Being an intentional gamer

As with digital streaming, you can avoid gaming overplay by making conscious choices about how long you’ll do it. Gaming can feel harmless or even creatively productive, but it deserves the same intentional boundaries you apply to other parts of your life.

Because it’s not always easy to stop playing when you’re on a roll, determine how long a gaming session will be before you start playing. Games are especially good at distorting time, making it easy to play far longer than intended. Setting clear limits and sticking to them can help gaming be a fun way to wind down or enjoy time with friends (in-person or virtual) without going overboard.

Mixing Minimalism into Your Digital Fun

With a promise of better living through technology, there are many ways in which modern life encourages you to use the Internet and screens for everything. But it’s easy to get caught up in the distraction, boredom avoidance, perceived convenience, and ease of access that these digital devices provide.

Taking a minimalist view of screen-based entertainment can help you enjoy what it offers while maintaining balance and perspective. Here are some ways to do that:

  • Resist the temptation to be on your devices all the time. Determine what your values are around digital entertainment and how they fit into your minimalist priorities, and let these values guide all of your decisions about online fun.
  • Be intentional before each sharing, watching, or playing session. Decide how long you want to engage in a digital activity and set a timer, and once the timer goes off, walk away from or set aside your device and do something else.
  • Set daily limits for how long you’ll scroll social media, play video games, or watch shows or movies. Smart phones have functionality that lets you set daily limits on specific apps or even categories of apps (such as Social or Games). Once you reach this maximum, resist the urge to override the limits. Recall your values and how you decided to prioritize digital entertainment, and recommit to them if you’re waffling about clicking the button for a few more minutes of scrolling, viewing, or play.
  • Turn off notifications. When you see a notification, you’re more likely to use the app pinging you. Notifications trigger your brain’s reward center and make you want to repeatedly engage in that behavior. Turning them off helps you intentionally choose when to engage with digital entertainment.
  • Become friends with boredom. We’ve now become a boredom-intolerant culture. No one can be bored for even a moment. The next time you brew a pot of coffee or go into a waiting room, don’t pull out your phone automatically. Just sit for a few minutes and do nothing. Relearn the value of time. The data regarding people who take a temporary or partial respite from screens unequivocally shows increased happiness scores and lower incidence of mental health issues.
  • Adopt lower-tech living. This means using technology and screens for what you need to, but cutting out the extra use that’s harmful. Numerous studies have identified that two hours per day, more or less, is the Goldilocks number of “just right.” (Although one study found one hour to be even better for children and teens due to the brain’s development.)

  • Take a digital detox. Totally eliminate, or substantially reduce, your use of digital entertainment for two to three weeks (including turning off autoplay). You’ll probably face a psychological adjustment to reduced dopamine and to the re-regulation of your brain’s reward system. During your detox you may feel anger, frustration, irritability, anxiety, depression, a feeling of loss, and overall discomfort.

    Detox allows some of the well-worn, digitally oriented neural pathways to degrade, thus allowing new behavioral patterns to be established; it also allows for the up-regulation of down-regulated dopamine receptors in the reward centers of the brain. The goal here is to break some of the biological and psychological connections with screen use. In doing so, you’re changing your behavior, which sets up your nervous system to be receptive to new, rewarding behaviors and to allow you to obtain dopamine elevations from more constructive sources.
