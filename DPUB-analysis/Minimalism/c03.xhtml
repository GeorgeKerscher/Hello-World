Defining “Enough” and Setting Goals to Get There

IN THIS CHAPTER

Clarifying what minimal means for you

Relating minimalism to your life’s goals

Discovering how to let go

When you think of minimalizing, you probably think of “stuff” that you get rid of, but we hope that after reading this chapter you can see how removing excess doesn’t just free up square footage, it frees up your life mentally, too. In order for this to happen, you need to incorporate and sometimes change your mindset around what minimalizing is.

When we talk about minimalism, we’re not talking extremes — you don’t have to fit your entire life into a backpack or own just one fork. You can go that lean if it helps you live more intentionally, but what matters most is defining minimalism in a way that serves you. A life free of excess — whether that’s clutter, overspending, comparing yourself to others, or too much screen time and not enough friend time — opens space for what really matters. Your minimalist strategy should bring you back to what you love and give you more time to focus on your goals. This chapter helps you get there.

Finding Your Balance between Less and Living

When you think about minimalism, you may envision a white room with a few pieces of carefully selected furniture, some plants, and a large abstract painting to quietly center your attention. Or you may think of a budget that allows you to bulk up your savings account while dining nightly on filling meals of popcorn and tomato soup. Or a life completely devoid of digital devices — no tv, no computer, no smart phone, just a hefty book for company. Yikes! We’re not sure that sounds appealing at all! (Well, we’ll take the plants.)

This vision is a broad stereotype of what minimalism looks like. And it surely sounds like a distraction-free world with nothing but our thoughts — a goal some of you may have (and we hope this book helps you achieve). To us, though, these images are too spare — we like having enough furniture for people to sit on (and can’t afford to replace all that we own anyway), we enjoy purchasing items that help us enjoy life, and we can’t imagine not keeping up with family and friends on social media.

The good news is you get to decide your own definition of minimalism and how you get there. Your experience may be plumper or leaner than someone else’s, and that’s just fine — what’s important is that you’re shifting to a mindset of “just what you need” and intentionality.

Creating your own definition of wealth

Tune into most media outlets, social media, websites, magazines, and newspapers, and you’ll often see our culture’s obsession with financial wealth and material possessions. The more money and bling financial executives, movie stars, or professional athletes have, the more publicity and attention they seem to get. In fact, some publications go as far as ranking those people who earn the most or have amassed the greatest wealth!

We’ve found there’s surprisingly little correlation between financial wealth and emotional wealth. That’s why a definition of “wealth” is limiting if you forget to remember the emotional side in addition to financial wealth and security. The following sections help you gain some perspective.

Acknowledging what money and things can’t buy

Recall the handful of best moments in your life. Odds are these memories don’t include that time you bought a car, found a great price online for a designer handbag, or upgraded to a full-house speaker system. The old saying is true: The most enjoyable and precious things of value in your life can’t be bought.

You know that money and “stuff” can’t buy happiness. A great deal of thoughtful research suggests that little relationship exists between these things.

Despite myriad technological gadgets and communication devices, cheap air travel, microwaves, personal computers, voice mail, smartphones, and all the other stuff that’s supposed to make life easier and more enjoyable, Americans aren’t any happier than they were four, five, or six decades ago, according to research conducted by multiple outfits including Gallup and the National Opinion Research Center. These results occur even though incomes, after being adjusted for inflation, have more than doubled during that time.

Understanding how focus helps with priorities

Ask yourself what you want in your life. Freedom? Space? Happiness? Peace? These are the usual responses that we hear when asking people about their life goals. Excess possessions, a focus on accumulating material things, and substantial time in front of digital screens can keep us from achieving those very goals! We spend our money and time buying more than what we need, when what we should be doing instead is evaluating our lifestyles and examining how they do (and don’t) support our goals.

Having “too much” can cause anxiety (am I keeping up with the Joneses?), worry (how will we pay these high credit card bills?), inefficiency (why am I always rifling through piles, drawers, and baskets for stuff I know I own but can’t find?), and a skewed view of the world and where we fit into it (is everyone but us on a group vacation right now? Did we miss the memo?).

Enter minimalism.

With fewer possessions and a deliberate effort to focus on what really matters to us, we’re choosing to cease comparing ourselves to others — we’re letting the Joneses get to the top of Mount MostStuff by themselves. We’re creating open space where our bodies can relax without the distractions of clutter, stacks, and piles. Minimalism helps us reduce our mental loads by simplifying our lives and focusing on what we’ve decided is important to us. That’s key to our definition of minimalism — it’s driven by what each of us decides is “enough.”

Through this book, we give you chances to determine what minimal means to you — in terms of possessions, your true financial needs and the path you take to fulfill them, and the amount of mental energy you expend on your digital life.

Setting Your Goals and Seeing How Minimalism Supports Them

We want you to accomplish all your life goals, including your goals around minimalism. You’ve probably never written down any goals about minimalism. Now’s your chance!

One goal we want you to set right now is that this is the year you will finally be free of “too much” — too much stuff, living beyond your means, and spending too much time with technology instead of with people and things in real life. Make minimalism a part of your lifestyle. This is the year that you will do it, and we promise it’ll make all aspects of your life lighter, less stressed, and hopefully more fun.

Defining some longer-term goals and figuring out how to achieve them

Now is the time to get real and relate minimalism to all your life goals. Being intentional about what you own, how you spend your money, and how you spend your time, can support broader goals you have in your life. Take a look at Figure 3-1 , which is a goal sheet we encourage you to fill out to relate your goals to minimalism, within the following time frames:

  • 90 days
  • One year
  • Five years

When establishing goals, start big (five years), then think about what medium-sized goals you need to accomplish (in one year) that will help you achieve the biggies, and then think about what small goals you need to meet (in 90 days) that will help you make meaningful strides toward accomplishing the medium-sized ones.

After you write down your 5-year, 1-year, and 90-day goals, think about how excess in your current lifestyle is impeding your ability to hit your 90-day goals. Fill out the remaining questions in Figure 3-1 ’s worksheet, and you can see how a lack of intentionality — in your physical space, your financial decisions, and/or your digital choices — may be impacting your ability to achieve your short-term goals and therefore your longer-term ones.

Being able to see how minimalism can help you achieve your tangible life goals will further motivate you to make minimalist choices. We’re huge believers in goal setting, and we’re often amazed at how our written-down goals actually happen. The power of writing them down seems to firm the commitment and allow your mindset and the universe to take note.

Evaluating your lifestyle

After you’ve spent some time reviewing your goals and seeing how minimalism fits, the next step is to make sure they align with your current lifestyle and your future projections.

For example, if your goal in the next year is to move to the city for the new company you’re launching, but you currently have a 3,000-square-foot home full of stuff, you can bet you’ll need to start assessing and paring back your possessions. Perhaps the stress of downsizing is already on your mind and maybe even holding you back from moving sooner! Don’t wait until moving day to eliminate the extras you can’t bring with you. Every time you get rid of an item, make a mental note that doing so is helping you achieve your dream goal.

Maybe your goals are more financially related, and you’re hoping to save a certain amount of money this year. Paring back your possessions and creating a budget both fit into this goal, because you want to be saving money instead of buying stuff you don’t need.

Whenever we do this goal-setting exercise with clients, we’re amazed at the similar responses most share. Usually everyone wants more freedom in their lives. Sometimes the word freedom doesn’t show up in words but in the goals themselves — clients want to have a more flexible schedule, travel more often, move to a different location, or have more money and therefore more choices. Taking steps to minimalize your life can help with all of these goals — freeing yourself from “stuff” frees your mind and your time, which creates room to work towards your other goals.

Getting Clear on What You Need

Getting clear on what you need goes to the heart of minimalism. We can’t tell you what items you should put in the trash or what habits you should retain, both because we can’t physically reach out of this book and help you move items or give you support, but also because we have no emotional attachment to your items, so it would be easier for us than for you to decide what excess you should get rid of. But we can help you get clear on what you need. Up until this point, maybe you’ve had problems with letting go of things or changing behaviors that you think are working for you as is.

Try something new to help you get really clear on what you need:

  1. Identify all the physical, financial, and digital clutter that’s stealing your energy.

    This is the time to get real. Look at each and dig deep. Your space or your behaviors may look okay on the surface, but we want you to really acknowledge where disasters lie. Hidden drawers, piled magazines from three years ago, too many food delivery charges, not enough savings, and hours of doomscrolling each night definitely steal your energy. Acknowledging this is an important first step.

  2. Plan a time to go through all the things you really need.

    Yes, this advice you will see over and over again in this book — every day we see the value in planning. (“Plan your work and work your plan” is a great approach to apply to all sorts of things in life.)

  3. Plan time for minimalizing sessions.

    Decluttering your space, wrangling your finances, and organizing your digital life can be intimidating, especially if you have a lot of excess or habits to get rid of. Allow yourself to spend no more than 60 minutes for each session — set a timer! Give yourself a treat after, or some type of reward for your hard work.

  4. Gather your minimalism supplies.

    No, don’t go and buy more stuff — supplies or boxes, expensive software, or behavior-changing phone apps. Find two boxes you already have and label one “In” and the other “Out,” create a basic spreadsheet, or figure out how you can use cloud-based storage that you probably already have access to (for example, Google Drive, OneDrive, or iCloud). We dig into details throughout the book.

  5. Move your items, get started on a budget, or start decluttering digital files.

    Following the tips in later chapters, get started. Don’t think too much about it; just do it. Then do keep going until your 60-minute timer goes off.

Letting Go of Stuff

After a life of excess, it can be difficult to let go of things and habits that have been important to you (even if you’re now realizing they didn’t serve you very well). Why is it so hard? Many of us have an emotional attachment to items that have sentimental value. We may prioritize feelings over practicality, which makes it difficult to get rid of things we own. We may purchase items to fill a void, even if we need to spend beyond our means to buy these things — we think having more will make us more happy/satisfied/interesting/desirable.

Becoming a minimalist and letting go of excess brings freedom and space for focus. Here are some tips on how to shift out of a mindset of “much:”

  • Realize you are not what you own. We often worry that getting rid of our stuff is like getting rid of a piece of ourselves. However, you are not stuff, and your memories, dreams, goals, and ambitions are not contained in objects; they are inside of you already! Your “stuff” is simply getting in the way of you living a more intentional life and achieving your dreams.
  • Make purchases only after extensive questioning. Another constant theme in this book is to make sure you buy consciously. The following are all good questions to ask before making a purchase: “Do I really need this?” “Will this add value to my life?” “Is this item worth the space I’m going to give it?”
  • Focus on the rewards of fewer distractions. If you can get rid of excess stuff, you can find the real freedom to dive into your life goals, deepen your relationships with family, friends, and your community, and discover the life you want.

Releasing yesterday to make room for tomorrow

Dealing with emotional items is often the most challenging part of going minimal, and we struggle with this ourselves. Getting rid of stuff and habits is difficult for most people but especially for those who treasure memories connected with possessions or activities you’ve done together (our kids love seeing Instagram reels we’ve saved from scrolling sessions). The possessions contain different memories for different people, and while some see them as trash, others revel in the memories those possessions provide them. Throughout this book we spend lots of time talking about how you can still keep your sentimental items while helping you determine what is sentimental and what isn’t. For now, we want you to acknowledge that emotions may arise during the decluttering process (especially when paring back your possessions), and that sometimes you’re going to need to accept that you no longer need many of the objects you’ve been keeping. Your memories will stay forever, but your stuff does not have to.

Be gentle with yourself during this process and acknowledge all the feelings that arise.

Busting through the “I might need it” syndrome

Operating with scarcity beliefs, which is a reason many people accumulate and hold onto stuff, not only holds you back from getting rid of things you don’t need, but it also produces lingering stress because it makes you think of what could happen.

From a possessions point of view, if you’re the type of person to hang onto something because one day you might use it, you need to bust through the “I might need this someday” belief today — this very second — because if you have this mentality forever, you will be holding onto unnecessary material things.

We’re not going to lie; this is the hardest problem that most of us have when it comes to clutter and too much stuff; hence, why we keep bringing it up. And this need to hold onto things is often linked to emotional reasons. For example, if you grew up not having a lot of money, perhaps you were conditioned to keep everything. As an adult, you hold onto things that may one day come in handy, even though they never do — a scarcity mindset drives you to accumulate and save. No matter how much money you have or don’t have, if you’re not going to use an item in your current lifestyle, then discard it swiftly.

Having random items also is characteristic of this syndrome. Maybe you look at that laminated hand painting you got at an art fair years ago and think, “I can never buy it again, and what if someday I want to frame it and hang it in my house?” Chances are if you haven’t hung it up yet, you won’t.

And that’s not to mention sentimental items, which are also tough to get rid of, as you may not need them, but you want them to hold on to memories of the past. But think hard about those broken eyeglasses your grandmother wore during your youth — are they worth keeping, or can you look at old photos of the two of you together where she’s wearing the glasses instead?

So, bust through this mindset and get rid of the excess you own. Have you used it in a year? Do you have an actual memory of the item that brings you so much joy that you want to permanently display it in your home? Ask yourself the difficult questions and then make some decisions.

Parents sometimes are known for having a “someday stash.” If you’re a parent reading this, now is the perfect time to get rid of the “I might” syndrome — your children will probably enjoy new toys more than the ones you had as a child, and they may not want your flowery china when they leave home. And if the shoe’s on the other foot and you’re helping your parents declutter, then use the techniques in this book to help them get over the “I might need it someday” syndrome.

Less stuff, less stress.

Fending Off Clutter of All Types

The quicker you are at dealing with your physical, financial, and digital excess, the easier it is to sustain your new behaviors. The next big hurdle is the sunk costs associated with the clutter, especially for possessions. The value an item once had is substantially gone, although in some cases it can be recuperated when you sell that item, but the lessons should be to be more wary of future clutter that may make its way into your home, and to know how to deal with it when it does.

Everything should have a place in your space. If you found a plate in the bedroom, you’d immediately return it to the kitchen without a second thought because you knew it wasn’t where it belonged. That should be the case with all of your belongings. If you have trouble deciding where something goes, it may be clutter that you need to let go of — and do it fast.

Being proactive versus reactive

By anticipating and dealing with things proactively, you can achieve higher results than by simply waiting to react when things come to you. In the context of minimalizing, being proactive instead of reactive means that instead of waiting to let clutter build up or hoping your old habits don’t creep back, you take constant steps toward keeping your space and habits minimal.

We’re still big fans of the Franklin Covey group and the late author Dr. Stephen Covey, who wrote The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People (Simon & Schuster, 1989). The number one habit is being proactive. As the book states,

Reactive people believe they are not responsible for what they say and do — they have no choice. Instead of reacting to or worrying about conditions over which they have little or no control, proactive people focus their time and energy on things they can control.

And we know all of us are responsible for controlling our own clutter, whatever shape it may take.

Keeping a proactive mindset and creating a routine and systems can make keeping clutter away an automatic part of your life.

Creating a system that you stick to

Creating systems and routines that are an organic part of your life makes keeping minimal not only sustainable but also something you don’t have to think about because doing so is just part of your routine.

When you’re tempted to buy something new or fall into an old habit, ask yourself these questions each time:

  • Does this item or behavior add value to my life?
  • Is this item or behavior bringing me something brand new?
  • Do I love this item, or will I love how I feel after I’m done with the activity?
  • If I buy this thing or participate in this activity, will I go over my monthly expense budget or my daily allocation of digital time?
  • Will this item take up valuable space better used by something else? Will engaging in this digital behavior make me feel bad about myself when I’m done?
  • If an item will take up empty space, will missing that emptiness cause me stress?

If an item or purchase or behavior brings you negativity or reduces your sense of peace, it’s probably best to avoid it and stick to your minimalist commitment.

Maximizing efficiency

Regardless of how much you can spend on minimalizing, you want to be as efficient as possible when doing it. You want to feel in control of the process as much as the result.

Here are a few tips to maximize your decluttering efficiency:

  • Start with one area. This is advice that we give everyone who starts on any minimalizing challenge, especially since at the beginning you may not find purging or analyzing pleasant. You may even find it stressful; therefore, start small. Start with one area. It should be your highest priority or the area that causes you the most stress or is the least efficient area of your home, workplace, financial, or digital space.
  • Give yourself a timeline. As with any goal, you need to have an attainable date to finish so you don’t get frustrated and give up.
  • Plan time. In addition to completion dates, you need to plan the time you will perform minimalizing activities. Shorter time spans can actually increase your chances of tackling a project. It’s amazing what you can accomplish in a set 15-minute minimalism challenge. It may also feel more like a game or challenge, for those of us who have a bit of a competitive edge. Experiment with different amounts of time that work for you and then stay consistent!
  • Use timers to keep you on track. Maximizing your time is so important that we recommend using a timer to promote efficiency. You could use a timer on your phone or computer, one built into your stove or microwave, a time cube gadget, or a wind-up chicken egg. We use timers every day. Today we’re so distracted by social media, emails, streaming videos, kids, and so forth — our lives are fast and busy, and distractions are always lingering — that timers can help us stay laser-focused.
  • When purging material items, have your Donate, Sell, Toss, and Repurpose boxes ready. You can go low-budget and repurpose boxes from merchandise you’ve had shipped to your home, or you can spend some money on nicer bins or baskets, if that’ll motivate you to always have them ready. In addition to continually reminding you to Donate, Sell, Toss, and Repurpose, these boxes will also aid in creating a decluttering sorting system that you can use to maintain your minimalist space.

Avoiding the all-or-nothing approach

The all-or-nothing approach to minimalism can be intimidating and overwhelming. This section discusses how to avoid these stumbling blocks.

Feeling intimidated

Choose a point of attack and then start with one item that you want to minimalize, and decide on it as quickly as possible. Then move onto the next item. If you think of the act of minimalizing in terms of “I have to do it all today,” then yes, assessing and reducing (or changing your behaviors) can be intimidating. To avoid this, be kind to yourself and start small, in sections or categories.

Feeling overwhelmed

Along with being intimidated, feeling overwhelmed is also a common emotion when a person hears the words “decluttering,” “purging,” or “limiting excess.” And that feeling of being overwhelmed can cause us to avoid getting started with something. We can get overwhelmed with the amount of work it takes to start a small business, so we drag our feet getting going. We’re overwhelmed with how hard it is to change our diets to healthier foods, so we stick to our unhealthy habits. And we can get overwhelmed just thinking about where to begin our minimalist journey, so we avoid getting started while we continue to feel frustrated with our excess. All of these examples are proof that something great could come if you started and were not held back by being overwhelmed. So, again, you don’t have to minimalize your life in one day — ease into minimalism the same way you would any other big changes. Schedule daily and/or weekly minimalizing sessions into your calendar to hold yourself accountable and beat the feelings of overwhelm.
