Glossary

ABC analysis :
systematic planning in OB modification, examining antecedents, behaviors, and consequences
absenteeism :
regularly staying away from work or school without good reason
abusive supervision :
hostile behavior toward followers that may include ridiculing, spreading rumors, taking credit for work done by followers, giving the “silent treatment,” or withholding information
achievement-oriented leadership :
challenging followers to perform at high levels, setting standards for excellence, showing confidence in followers’ ability to reach goals
action research :
the process of problem specification followed by interventions (i.e., actions) until the researcher understands how the intervention is affecting the organization
active listening :
way of listening that is “a creative, active, sensitive, accurate, empathic, nonjudgmental listening” 1
active or passive :
two different responses to job dissatisfaction; active is something the person can do something about, whereas passive is when the person chooses not to respond in an active way
adaptability :
the ability to transfer the demands of the market into organizational actions
additive task :
a performance goal that can be met by adding up individual contributions
adjourning :
when the team finalizes its work and disbands
affect :
one’s attitude, composed of both emotions and moods
affect relabeling :
verbally labeling the initial reaction of something negative (e.g., I am angry) and then relabeling it to be less intense (e.g., I am annoyed)
affect spin :
the ability to vary responses to emotional events by knowing which people are more reactive than others to both internal and external events
affective :
the emotional part of an attitude
affective climate :
refers to the shared affective experience of a work group or team 2
affective commitment :
employee’s emotional attachment to an organization
affective conflict :
conflict that engenders strong emotions such as anger or disgust
affective events theory :
a framework that integrates personality, emotions, and moods, and considers the impact of the work environment and events that may trigger emotional reactions
agreeableness :
a characteristic in which one is affable, tolerant, sensitive, trusting, kind, and warm
all-channel (or star) network :
more decentralized network that allows a free flow of information among all group members
alternative dispute resolution :
methods to resolve conflict that both parties agree to without involving litigation
ambient impact :
collective deviant behavior that creates a hostile working environment
applied social psychology :
the study of how people interact in groups
appreciative inquiry (AI) :
an OD intervention in which people reflect on peak experiences and visualize the future
apprising :
persuading the target of influence that complying with the influencer will advance their career
arbitration :
both parties in a disagreement agree in advance to accept a decision, and it is made by a neutral third party
artificial intelligence-mediated communication (AI-MC) :
communication in which an intelligent computational agent operates on behalf of a communicator by modifying, augmenting, or generating messages to accomplish communication or interpersonal goals
assimilation :
relinquishing cultural heritage and adopting the beliefs and behaviors of the new culture
attitude :
“a psychological tendency that is expressed by evaluating a particular entity with some degree of favor or disfavor” 3
attribution theory :
proposes that “the attributions people make about events and behavior can be either internal or external” 4
attributions :
a person’s attempt to assign a cause to a behavior or event they observe 5
authentic leadership :
knowing oneself and behaving in a way that is consistent with what is intuitively right; this includes the four dimensions of self-awareness, relational transparency, internalized moral perspectives, and balanced processing
autonomy :
ability to work alone without supervision
availability bias :
commonly occurs when readily available information comes to a person’s mind and affects a decision
avoiding :
conflict resolution approach where a person is low on their own concerns and the concerns of others
baby boomers :
people born during the baby boom after World War II, between 1946 and 1964
behavioral :
an intention to act based upon the cognitions and affect experienced
behavioral CQ :
the ability to adjust to others’ cultural practices
behavioral methods :
problem-solving behaviors
behaviorally anchored rating scales (BARS) :
a vertical scale that rates specific examples of performance
belief updating :
initial information affects the conclusion one draws, and this conclusion then impacts later judgments
best alternative to a negotiated agreement (BATNA) :
an alternative that negotiators will accept if the negotiation reaches an impasse and they can’t get their ideal outcome
big data :
the large volume of data being generated from numerous sources that is pouring into organizations every day, which includes five characteristics: volume, velocity, variety, veracity, and value
big data analytics :
a complex process of examining large, diverse data sets (or “big data”), looking for patterns, correlations, trends, and preferences that can help leaders make informed business decisions
boomers :
the generation born between 1946 and 1964; called this due to the baby boom that occurred after World War II
boundary spanners :
individuals in an organization who link the organization’s internal networks or external constituents together
bounded ethicality :
an unconscious psychological process that hinders the quality of decision-making
bounded rationality :
when decision-makers have limits on their ability to assimilate large amounts of information
brainstorming :
generating a large quantity of ideas in a face-to-face team meeting
broaden-and-build model :
a process by which emotions serve to both broaden employee experiences and allow them to build better-functioning organizations
buffering effect :
support during a stress episode that prevents stress
bureaucratic control :
when legitimate authority governs social interactions
business environment :
an environment that consists of various stakeholders, such as shareholders, customers, suppliers, distributors, the government, and the employees
calculus-based trust (CBT) :
a form of trust based upon keeping records of what another person does for you and what you do for them
career adaptability :
defined as “a psychosocial construct that denotes an individual’s resources for coping with current and anticipated tasks, transitions, traumas in their occupational roles” 6
career indecision :
“the difficulties preventing individuals from making a career decision” 7
case study :
a description of a situation in an organization
central tendency error :
giving ratings that center in the middle of a scale
centralization :
occurs when most or all key decisions are made by an organization’s top leadership rather than by the managers or employees of individual work units
chain network :
gives a flow of information among members, although the people are at the end of the chain
chain-of-command :
the flow of authority in an organization
challenge-related stress :
when work demands lead a person to be energized and work harder, resulting in higher performance
change uncertainty :
uncertainty due to strategic, structural, or job-related change
changing :
represents movement toward a new desired state
chief executive officer (CEO) :
the highest-ranking person in a company
chief operating officer (COO) :
a senior executive in a company tasked with overseeing day-to-day operations
circle network :
each person can communicate with two others located adjacent to them
clan control :
occurs when shared values and beliefs govern how people interact socially
climcult :
culture and climate work together to influence how people experience their work environment
coalition tactics :
a proactive influence tactic that involves seeking the aid or support of others as a reason for the target to agree
coding :
linking the information you need to remember to something familiar and easily retrievable
coercive power :
the ability to punish
cognitive :
describes a statement of belief about something
cognitive bias :
an irrational belief that influences the ability to make a specific decision based on facts and evidence
cognitive CQ :
refers to self-awareness and the ability to detect cultural patterns
cognitive dissonance :
incompatibility between two or more attitudes or between attitudes and behavior
cognitive evaluation theory :
another term for self-determination theory
cognitive methods :
managing thoughts and emotions
cohesion :
team spirit experienced in high-performing teams
collaboration :
offer to provide assistance or resources to the person being asked to do something
collective effort :
a work project that reflects the contributions of everyone on the team
collectivism :
group orientation
combining tasks :
creating natural work units by putting tasks together to create a more challenging and complex work assignment
commitment to change :
employees support change and help the organization implement it
communication apprehension (CA) :
“an individual’s level of fear or anxiety with either real or anticipated communication with another person or persons” 8
compassionate leadership :
noticing, feeling, and understanding the suffering of a follower
competence :
“a cluster of related knowledge, skills and attitudes that affects a major part of one’s job (a role or responsibility), that correlates with performance on the job, that can be measured against well-accepted standards, and that can be improved via training and development” 9
complementary linkage :
one individual identifies with another person, and this identification causes the emotions to spread, as in “misery loves company”
compliance :
when employees simply go along with the change but secretly hope that it is a program that will come to an end soon
complexity :
a dimension of organizational design that represents the range of activities conducted within an organization
compromising :
conflict resolution approach that reflects a moderate concern for the self and for others
conciliation :
calling in a neutral third party to attempt to resolve a conflict
conflict :
“the process that begins when one party perceives that the other has negatively affected, or is about to negatively affect, something that he or she cares about” 10
conglomerates :
a type of product organization structure often seen in large companies with different products in which product organization is broken down further into brands 11
conscientiousness :
being organized, systematic, punctual, achievement oriented, and dependable
consensus :
discussing ideas and deferring a final decision until everyone can say they have been heard and will support the final decision
consideration :
the degree to which the leader shows trust, respect, and sensitivity to employees’ feelings
consistency :
defining values and subsystems that are the basis of a strong culture
constructive or destructive :
a positive or negative response to job dissatisfaction
consultation :
invites the person to be involved with a proposed idea, and may be used in any direction as well
consultative :
asking for input from team members one-on-one or as a group and then making the final decision
contingency contract :
contracts that bridge concerns about future events that are brought up during the negotiation, and they become part of the agreement
continuance commitment :
degree to which an employee is aware of the costs of leaving the organization
contrast effect :
evaluation of a characteristic of an object or person affected by comparisons with other objects or people ranking higher or lower on the characteristic
corporation :
a legal entity that is created to conduct business
correlational field study :
data are gathered by having organizational members complete survey forms, and then data are analyzed using statistical methods
creativity :
“production of a novel and appropriate response, product, or solution to an open-ended task” 12
critical thinking :
the objective analysis and evaluation of an issue in order to form a judgment
cross-cultural communication :
compares one culture to another to examine how people communicate in similar and different ways
crossover stress effect :
a phenomenon that occurs when employees carry stress home with them from the workplace, which affects family interactions
C-suite :
refers to the top management in an organization
cultural intelligence (CQ) :
an individual’s capabilities to function and manage effectively in culturally diverse settings
cultural looseness :
associated with social disorganization, deviance, innovation, and openness to change
culturally endorsed implicit leadership theory (CLT) :
a theory that identifies leadership behaviors perceived as effective and ineffective across cultures
cultural retooling :
the psychological process of adaptation to another culture
cultural tightness :
associated with order and efficiency, conformity, and low rates of change
cultural tightness–looseness :
the strength of social norms and the level of sanctioning within societies
culture shock :
the distress experienced by a traveler from the loss of familiar patterns of social interaction
cybersecurity subculture :
the shared values, beliefs, and actions of an organization’s employees emphasizing the importance of safeguarding digital assets, data, and systems against cyber threats
cyberslacking :
using the internet for personal reasons during working hours
Dark Triad :
composed of Machiavellianism, narcissism, and psychopathy 13
data-driven decision-making (DDDM) :
the process of making organizational decisions based on actual data rather than intuition or observation alone
deep acting :
when a desired emotional expression is achieved by changing one’s underlying felt emotion
deep-level diversity :
“differences among members’ attitudes, beliefs, and values” 14
delegating :
allowing a team (or individual) to make a final decision
demands–abilities (DA) fit :
the compatibility between the employee’s knowledge, skills, and abilities, and the demands of the job. In other words, the job characteristics are neither too easy nor too difficult for the abilities of the employee; they match
determinism :
the belief that people should not try to change the paths their lives are destined to take
deviant behavior :
“aggression, bullying, harassment, incivility and social undermining” 15
digital transformation (DT) :
a fundamental change process, enabled by the innovative use of digital technologies accompanied by the strategic leverage of key resources and capabilities, aiming to radically improve an entity and redefine its value proposition for its stakeholders
digital workplace :
the broad set of connected technologies that employees use on a daily basis to do their jobs
direct effects :
refers to when an employee is the target of a coworker’s deviant act
directive leadership :
giving followers specific instructions about their tasks, providing deadlines, setting standards for performance, and explaining rules
direct reports :
individuals who report to another person higher in the organization
distributive justice :
perceived fairness of how rewards are distributed
disturbance handler :
one who resolves conflict and chooses strategic alternatives
diversity :
differences between individuals on any attribute that may evoke the perception that the other person is different from the self

employees’ shared perceptions of the policies and practices that communicate the extent to which fostering and maintaining diversity and eliminating discrimination is a priority in the organization
division of labor :
see specialization
divisional organization :
form of organizational structure that is organized according to specific products, product groups, services, markets, customers, or major programs
dominating :
conflict resolution approach where a person is high with respect to their own concerns but low with respect to the concerns of others
dotted-line relationships :
relationships that occur when an employee can provide useful information to a manager in a different part of an organization’s structure 16
downsizing :
reduction in the labor force by laying off employees
downward communication :
the process by which employees communicate with others who are lower in the organizational hierarchy
economies of scale :
when more units of a good or a service are produced on a larger scale, yet with (on average) fewer input costs
e-harassment :
email or other electronic communication directed at a specific person that causes substantial emotional distress and serves no legitimate purpose
elaborative interrogation :
when people are asked to generate their own explanations of factual statements presented to them
emotional contagion :
the negative mood of one employee spreading to others in their group
emotional culture :
shared affective values, norms, artifacts, and assumptions that govern how people feel and which express they emotions at work and which ones they are better off suppressing
emotional dissonance :
the result of the difference between the organizationally expected emotions and an employee’s inner or “real” emotions 17
emotional division of labor (EDOL) :
defined as any explicit or implicit division of roles in which individuals vary in their requirement to use emotional abilities 18
emotional intelligence (EI) :
“ability to monitor one’s own and others’ feelings and emotions, to discriminate among them and to use this information to guide one’s thinking and actions” 19
emotional labor :
the effort required to effectively manage emotions to be successful on the job
emotion regulation :
ability to manage emotion in the self and others (e.g., detaching from fear states that interfere with one’s functioning)
emotions :
brief but intense feelings triggered by specific events that disrupt a person’s thinking
employability :
“an attribution employers make about the probability that job candidates will make positive contributions to their organizations” 20
employee assistance program (EAP) :
program “designed to help employees deal with problems that seriously affect job performance” 21

“the investments of an individual’s complete self into a role” 22

lack of emotional exhaustion, psychosomatic health complaints, and physical health symptoms
enacted values :
values that are acted out
engineers :
the group that focuses on designing systems to support the work of operations, such as employees who design a manufacturing facility
entrepreneur :
one who looks for new ideas and opportunities
entry :
when a new member starts work
equity theory :
a theory that looks at how people compare their inputs to their outcomes

when individuals continue a failing course of action after receiving feedback that shows it isn’t working
espoused values :
values that are stated
ethical climate :
the moral atmosphere of the work environment and the level of ethics practiced within an organization

leadership that promotes honesty and acts based on moral values and beliefs

a normative ethical theory that holds interpersonal relationships and care or benevolence as a virtue central to moral action
evidence-based approach :
integrating individual clinical expertise with the best available external clinical evidence from systematic research
evidence-based management (EBM) :
the ability to translate research into practice
exchange :
persuasion based on a quid pro quo
executives :
employees who have worked their way up organizational career ladders and are financially responsible to their board of directors and shareholders
exemplification :
when people self-sacrifice or go above and beyond the call of duty in order to gain the attribution of dedication from observers
expanding pie :
enlarging the pool of resources so that a negotiation can end in a “win–win” agreement where both sides feel that they got the best possible outcome
experimental :
describes rigorous studies conducted in controlled settings
expert power :
the ability to influence others due to knowledge or a special skill set or expertise
external attribution :
when people believe that a person’s behavior is due to situational factors
external communication :
information that is shared with the public through marketing and public relations efforts
extraversion :
a trait of a person who is outgoing, talkative, sociable, and enjoys social situations
extrinsic motivation :
involves the performance → outcome instrumentality between the task and a tangible reward

refers to how extrinsic rewards may support an employee’s sense of competence if they don’t undermine autonomy
facilitation :
when a leader intervenes to resolve a conflict

one who helps the team make a decision by asking questions and reflecting statements but not influencing the final decision
family-to-work conflict :
family problems crossing over to work
feedback :
knowledge of results of a person’s efforts

an individual’s general level of proactive activity with respect to obtaining feedback from the work environment

the result of human evolution in which the response was needed to attack predators or run away from them

model of team development that includes forming, storming, norming, performing, and adjourning
fixed :
a specified amount of time or number of responses
fixed pie :
when only a limited amount of goods are divided, and the goal is to get the largest share

flexible hours that may be formal (i.e., allowing employees to arrive later to work and stay later) or informal (i.e., a supervisor being flexible regarding an employee’s need to pick up children from school)

when a person experiences a challenging opportunity aligned with their skills

behaviors a person engages in while interacting with leaders to meet organizational objectives; followers are viewed as partners with leaders

analyzing the forces for and against a change

the amount of rules, regulations, and procedures an organization requires its employees to follow
forming :
the first stage of team development when the team members meet for the first time
framing :
whether questions are presented as gains or losses
functional organization chart :
also known as the departmentalized structure; the most common type of organizational chart, which organizes work into different functional areas based upon specialized expertise

compensation tied to unit-level performance, where revenue increases (or cost savings) are shared with employees
Galatea effect :
when individuals set high expectations for themselves and then perform to these expectations
Gaming :
manipulation of information to make performance look better than it actually is
Generation X, or Gen Xers :
generation born between 1965 and 1980; sometimes referred to as the “baby busters” or “latchkey kids”
Generation Z, or Gen Z :
generation that became adults during the second decade of the 21st century and have been familiar with the internet from a very young age
geographical organization chart :
chart of the type of organizational structure used when organizations need to stay close to their customers and the delivery of products is important
Ghosting :
the termination of all communication without any explanation or reason
Global Leadership and Organizational Behavior Effectiveness (GLOBE) project :
a large-scale research program that sought to understand differences in leader behaviors and relationships with relevant organizational outcomes worldwide
global mindset :
a set of individual attributes that enhance a manager’s ability to influence others who are different from them
global mobility :
occurs when individuals, and often their families, are relocated from one country to another by an employer, generally from a familiar situation (a home country) to a novel one (a host country) for a fixed period of time

when individuals set low expectations for themselves and then perform to these expectations
grapevine :
the unofficial circulation of information in an organization
gratitude :
“a generalized tendency to recognize and respond with grateful emotion to the roles of other people’s benevolence in the positive experiences and outcomes that one obtains” 23
green human resource management (GHRM) :
integrating environmental sustainability into all human resources functions to create an environmentally sustainable workplace culture

the conformity-seeking tendency of a group; term coined in the 1970s by Irving Janis
growth need strength :
refers to a person’s need to learn new things, grow, and develop from working

heuristic :
decision rules
high-context cultures :
cultures that rely heavily on situational cues for meaning when perceiving and communicating with others

excessive or undesirable constraints that interfere with an individual’s ability to achieve goals, creating “negative stress”

the tendency for individuals with outcome knowledge (hindsight) to claim they would have estimated a probability of occurrence for the reported outcome that is higher than they would have estimated in foresight (without the outcome information)
horizontal :
adding different tasks at the same level

humble leadership :
a style of leadership where leaders tend to view themselves more objectively, others more appreciatively, and new information or ideas more openly
hygienes :
things like supervision, pay, company policies, and the working conditions
idea evaluation :
discussing the strengths and weaknesses of ideas
idea generation :
creating new ideas

form of trust characterized by the leader and follower sharing the same goals and objectives
imaging :
linking verbal information to visual images
implicit leadership schemas :
traits and characteristics a person thinks are linked to being a leader
impression management :
set of behaviors that people use to protect their self-image or change the way they are seen by others (or both)
inclusion :
the act of creating a workplace environment where all employees feel valued, respected, and have equal opportunities

a leadership approach that suggests that all leaders help all team members feel part of the group while retaining their sense of individuality so they contribute to the team atmosphere

an employee being affected by learning of another coworker’s deviant behaviors

the most basic level within organizational behavior such as personality or behavior
individual rights :
rights that protect an individual within an organization and guide ethical decision-making

the aggregate of productive enterprises in a particular field, often named after its principal product or service (for example, the health care industry)
influence :
the exercise of power to change the behavior, attitudes, or values of an individual or group

refers to the perceived fairness of the communications made by leaders during a process

compliment giving or acting deferential

initiating structure :
defining tasks for employees and focusing on goals
innovation :
the generation, acceptance, and implementation of new ideas, processes, products, or services

an appeal made to followers’ values and ideals or that seeks to arouse the target person’s emotions to gain commitment for a request or proposal

focuses on problem-solving during a stress episode

maintaining one’s cultural heritage and adopting a new cultural identity
integrative acculturation :
becoming bicultural

when parties do not see the bargaining process as a zero-sum game, and they believe that an agreement can be reached that satisfies all concerns

“the degree to which a person accepts the reasonableness of different cultural perspectives on how to live, both at the micro interpersonal level and at more macro organizational-societal levels and, consequently, is motivated to develop integrative schemas that specify when to activate different worldviews and/or how to blend them together into a coherent holistic mental representation” 24

focuses on the behavior of two individuals’ communication patterns
interdependent :
employees who depend on one another to get things done in the group and organization

when people infer that an event or a person’s behavior is due to their own character traits or abilities

“the communications transactions between individuals and/or groups at various levels and in different areas of specialization that is intended to design and redesign organizations, to implement designs, and to co-ordinate day-to-day activities” 25
interpersonal justice :
refers to how employees are treated by their leaders, including respect and propriety
interval :
provision of reinforcement based on time
intimidation :
where people signal their power or potential to punish in order to be seen as dangerous by observers
intrinsic motivation :
when someone works on a task because they find it interesting and gains satisfaction from the task itself
involvement :
building human capability, ownership, and responsibility
job :
set of task elements grouped together under one job and performed by one individual

“a prolonged response to chronic stressors on the job” 26

“the degree to which an individual perceives that s/he can control where, when, and how s/he works” 27

extent to which individuals can demonstrate initiative in designing their own work

redesigning jobs so that they are more challenging to the employee and have less repetitive work

how much an employee identifies with their job and views their performance at work as an essential part of their self-esteem

can be actual performance as collected in organizational records (e.g., the number of forms correctly processed in an insurance company), or it may be rated by supervisors or peers

involves cross-training or allowing workers to do different jobs

how content an individual is with their job—whether or not they like the job or aspects of it, such as the nature of work or supervision

a situational form of envy specific to the job search domain 28

regular—possibly daily—fluctuation in the experience of job stress
justice :
emphasizes fair treatment and the right to pursue happiness

the need to believe that the world is fair and that people get what they deserve
knowledge-based trust (KBT) :
trust grounded in experience of how predictable the other person is
labor relations :
the study and practice of managing unionized employment situations
language :

the process by which employees communicate with others at the same level in the organizational hierarchy

when the agent seeks to establish the legitimacy of a request or to verify authority to make it by referring to rules, policies, contracts, or precedent

cultures in which written and spoken words carry the burden of shared meanings

see machine bureaucracy

the cognitive processing necessary to recognize and understand expectations appropriate for different cultural situations

also known as multidivisional form, this is the most common divisional structure found in large organizations with a variety of brands and products
middle line :
middle managers responsible for translating general strategic plans into detailed action plans, specifying managerial responsibilities for particular tasks and how resources are distributed
millennials :
generation born between 1981 and 1999; they typically look for flexibility and choice in employment
mindfulness :
a state of open attention on what is happening in the present without thinking about the past or worrying about the future

studies conducted from 1979 to 1999 that followed identical and fraternal twins who were separated at an early age
mission :
defining the meaningful, long-term direction for the organization
mixed-methods research :
research that combines qualitative (e.g., interviews) and quantitative (e.g., surveys) in a single study
moods :
general feeling states that are not related to something that happens to a person but are not intense enough to interrupt regular thought patterns or work
