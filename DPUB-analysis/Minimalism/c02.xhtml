Realizing How Less Really Is More

IN THIS CHAPTER

Understanding why too much stuff is bad for you

Appreciating how minimalism can help you bring calm and order to your life

Turning your minimalist desires into new habits that last

Doesn’t it feel like you have much more stuff than you actually need? If you’re reading this book, you probably feel that way. Many of us live in an era of quantity. The success of businesses such as The Container Store and the aisles of empty red totes found in mass-market retailers in January reflect this — we have a lot of stuff to store. But why do we have so much stuff? We’re not sure there’s a single answer for this question.

Many of us aren’t very organized, so we forget about or can’t find what we thought we had, and we buy an item. Or we avoid getting rid of things — life can feel very full, contain lots of uncertainty or overwhelm, and include a scarcity mindset that compels us to hold onto items, or dissociate by doomscrolling or binge-watching TV. Our mental loads feel heavier than ever; we’re no longer bound by the confines of storage space (digital folders can hold so much more than a manila folder ever could) and time (gone are the days when phone calls would generally end between dinner and bedtime, and the only mail we got was predictably delivered at the same time each day). Today, the sheer volume of digital incoming (and the attention it requires) can be copious, and the timing is 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

This chapter helps you realize how minimalism and a leaner life can help you reduce the stress and chaos that a life filled with “too much” provides.

Contemplating the Consequences of Too Much Stuff

We’ve done enough research and have enough experience helping people to know that too much — whether your medicine cabinet or your inbox or your accumulating pile of bills — can make life feel noisy, chaotic, out of control, and overwhelming. We’re asked to make so many decisions every day that even choosing a protein and two veg for dinner can feel difficult. When clutter (of any type) exists, you can feel overwhelmed, drained, and defeated, and your energy is affected physically, mentally, and emotionally.

One of the biggest effects of too much clutter is stress and the increased cortisol levels that comes with it. A study done by UCLA observed 32 middle-class LA families and found that all of the moms’ stress hormones spiked during the time they spent dealing with their belongings.

Clutter can harm relationships, and studies have shown that where the word “clutter” is used to describe couples’ current households, a higher percentage of divorces ensue. Clutter can often be the root of disagreements that can lead to larger problems. Whatever you living situation is — whether you’re living with a friend, significant other, family member, or your hairdresser’s cousin’s best friend — chances are, you and those you live with define clutter differently, and that affects your living situation and your daily moods.

And clutter always reveals your mindset. If you operate with low self-esteem, you may own more expensive clothing than necessary or you may carry lots of credit card debt because you use retail therapy as a means to feel happy. If you’re worried about money, you may keep items that you don’t necessarily need and you may avoid spending money on even inexpensive, simple joys. If you’ve dealt with loss and grief, you may be more attached to sentimental items than someone who hasn’t faced similar experiences. The bottom line is that clutter is a symptom of deeper things going on in your psyche, and when you deal with clutter, you can transform into the person you’re meant to be.

WHY CLUTTER CREATES FRICTION AT HOME

Having too much stuff in a crowded space can cause some unintended consequences, to you and to those surrounding you, as we describe in the following list:

  • Your clutter may be causing misalignment with your partner, or your decision to declutter may disrupt your alignment. Not everyone has the same view of clutter. Some people aren’t bothered by stacks of stuff and a closet so full you can barely fit another hanger. Others have a nearly visceral response to a messy, crowded living space.

    As you consider starting your minimalist journey, talk to your partner about their feelings about the clutter each of you leaves in the house. Make sure you share your minimalism and decluttering goals, including your reasons for wanting to reduce the clutter chaos and the strategies you plan to use to do so. Your partner may hear “minimalist” or “declutter” and get worried that you’re going to get rid of everything . Be willing to listen to your partner’s concerns, and ask for — and offer — support throughout the process.

  • Clutter impedes your focus. Clutter can compete for your brain’s focus, thereby not allowing you to give your full attention to what you deem important. Remember that it takes more energy to actively avoid and ignore clutter than it does to actually deal with it, even if at a slow but steady pace. People sharing your living space face this same challenge as you do.
  • Clutter negatively influences behavior. A large part of your energy can be attributed to your surroundings. Waking up to an efficient home filled with things you use every day versus waking up to a big pile of junk makes a difference. A cluttered house can also affect the moods and behavior of your kids, friends, and even fur babies — they’re just as influenced by a chaotic environment as you are. Think about how you feel when entering a doctor’s office that’s so busy you can barely find a seat to fill out the paperwork you forgot to do online the night before — your mood is completely different than when you enter a quiet, clutter-free waiting room. Never discount the powerful effect of clutter on your mental and emotional well-being.

Understanding How Minimalism Can Help You Stress Less

Research has proved the positive effects of reducing, removing, and being organized. However, in a fast-paced life, it’s impossible to avoid all stress. You’re constantly stimulated by notifications, incoming emails, 24-hour news cycles, social media of all types, on-demand streaming services, and people wanting your attention and a response time measured in nanoseconds. The more stuff you have, both physical and mental, the more your stress is triggered.

While avoiding all stress may not be a real option, how you deal with your stuff can help manage one aspect of your stress. Minimalism can help you manage what’s within your control, and if even a portion of your stress is made more manageable, the stress driven by what you have less control over becomes less impactful.

Knowing why minimalism works

How does minimalism help? Minimalism can act as a pressure release valve when the feelings of chaos and stress threaten to boil over. It can help you focus, because a more minimalist life — by design — contains less, so you have fewer distractions and can more easily home in on what matters. Decluttering your physical or digital space is a good start. Once you begin decluttering and feeling the benefits, continued decluttering can even feel therapeutic.

Minimalism doesn’t mean you have to get rid of everything or that you have to live in a sterile, all-white home with one expensive couch in the middle of the living room. That may be what you’ve been trained to believe, but minimalism in this book simply means being intentional about what you have. Given the excess most of us live with, your definition of minimalism will likely mean (at least a little) less of everything. Less physical and digital stuff, fewer bills, less time spent staring at or interacting with devices, possibly even smaller dwellings — you get the point.

Besides lowering your stress level and helping you retain a sense of control and calm over your life, minimalism can help you save time and money, and it can free up some of your financial resources. When you choose to live simply, you have less stuff to maintain, which saves money. You create an environment with fewer distractions, which helps you be more productive, and greater productivity often turns into financial benefits. You waste less time looking for items you’re certain you own but just can’t put your finger on, and you waste less time on passive or virtual digital activities, thus creating space for more in-person interactions and creative pursuits.

In conjunction with more time and money, you’ll have more energy as your refocused time can be available for other activities. The reduction of a highly materialist lifestyle can prove to be healthier and make you feel better as you focus intentionally on what you’ve decided is important to you.

We want to reiterate that you don’t have to keep only one pair of shoes or one book to adopt a minimalist lifestyle. The great thing about minimalism is that the choices are yours to make, and you can do what works for you. You can choose what and how you want to minimize or downsize, and there’s no right or wrong way to go about it. You don’t have to figure it all out right away, either; the process can take some trial and error. Once you start on this journey, you may find that you want to embrace it even more after seeing the benefits.

Determining your “too much” threshold

Many people ask us for some concrete rules to help them figure out whether they have too much stuff. Although this differs for everyone, here are some guidelines to help you think through your situation:

  • Are you constantly misplacing things? When you’re wasting time looking for items from keys to shoes to wrenches, this could be a sign you have too much clutter.
  • Do you have piles of stuff? Clothing may be the most obvious thing to think about, but the concept applies to stuff in general.
  • Are you replacing stuff you’ve lost and can’t find? This is a clear sign of having too much clutter.
  • Are you purchasing items and then realizing you already had them? When you start to have doubles and even triples of items, chances are you have too much stuff. Two copies of the same digital file? Five pairs of similar shoes? Twenty nearly identical photos?
  • Are you always worrying about stuff? For example, do you stress about having people over because they have to walk through your crowded entranceway? Are you worried about losing files because you’re nearly at your cloud storage limit and you don’t have the budget or desire to add more?
  • Do you have a difficult time knowing where to start? Do you have so much clutter in so many areas of your life that you can’t even choose one spot to start minimalizing?
  • Do you feel overwhelmed just thinking about making your life more minimalist? Similar to not knowing where to start, do you feel overwhelmed about the entire minimalizing process?
  • Are you always late paying bills and arriving at appointments? This is another clear sign of clutter buildup.
  • Are there locations in your home that you can’t use as they were designed? For example, can you not park your car in the garage because there’s no room?
  • Does the idea of completely starting over sound more doable than bringing minimalism to your current spaces? Maybe you’re tempted to say “Forget it, I’m moving to Indonesia.”
  • Are you renting a storage unit that you never retrieve items from? If you’re considering renting a storage locker (or you already have one), you have too much stuff. A storage unit is just a location to accumulate even more, and the longer you keep the unit, the more stuff accumulates. When you’d rather pay to store your stuff miles away than let go of what’s excess, you’ve reached a turning point. A storage unit is also a decisive waste of time (traveling to and from) and money (monthly rent).
  • Do your kids have so many toys they never know what to play with? If you’re overwhelmed by clutter, chances are your kids may be as well.
  • Do you often throw away food because you over-shop and it goes bad? This is easily done, especially in North America where we have massive refrigerators. Europeans, on the other hand, often shop daily and have less storage space for food, which automatically cuts down on food waste.
  • Do you have to move things to sit down? This is an obvious sign that you have too much stuff.

Too much of anything can be considered clutter. As you determine your minimalist goals and your definition of minimalism as it pertains to possessions, your financial life, and your digital life (see Chapter 3 for help with goals), you’ll probably find yourself doing some serious soul-searching to figure out what’s considered too much.

Discovering the Secret to Change

Every year millions of people make new year resolutions. “I will go to the gym several times a week.” “I’ll stop eating chocolate cake.” And even “I’ll be more intentional about what I spend my money on.” And yet within just a couple of weeks, most people fail to stick to their resolutions and give up. Have you had that experience? We certainly have!

Although most people consider changing their daily habits to be hard, it’s actually not as difficult as you think — once you know how. Learning to change your behavior is like learning to swim — it looks really difficult at first, but it’s easy once you know how! And then it gets really exciting to put your new skills to work.

In this section, we tell you how you can change your behaviors so that you stick to the minimalist principles that you want to guide your life.

Designing your life for minimalism

If you try to adopt a minimalist lifestyle without a well-designed plan, you’re unlikely to succeed in these new habits long term. Why? Because you’re relying on your motivation. And your motivation doesn’t stay high all the time: It goes up and down.

For example, after reading some chapters of this book, you may feel highly motivated to make every decision with complete intentionality and mindfulness. You may say to yourself, “This is brilliant stuff. I’m going to declutter my closets and my computer for half an hour every day. This review-and-purge is going to be my new habit. I can do this!”

This is an example of what most people do. They use determination and sheer willpower to try to drive change into their life. The first few days go fine: you remember to do some decluttering and are pleased with yourself. Then a few days in and you wake up with a headache. No way do you feel like reviewing and making decisions and purging. That’s out of the question! You’re not motivated to practice your new behaviors. You’d rather stay in bed and sleep. The next day you wake up late. Motivation is still down. Then the next day you forget. A few days pass, and you realize you haven’t decluttered any of your spaces or you’ve mindlessly purchased items without first thoughtfully considering them, so you give up.

How do you think you’d feel? Probably not that great. Perhaps you may even think of yourself as a failure — someone who can’t stick to new habits. Next time you realize you need to create a new habit, you’ll be less likely to have a go because you won’t want to fail again.

Most people, at this point, blame themselves. You may think, “If only I tried harder” or “If only I were more disciplined.” Blaming yourself doesn’t help you, nor does just giving up. So what’s the solution? Read on!

EXPLORING YOUR INTENTIONS

The word “intention” comes from the Latin intendere , meaning to direct attention. Intention is purpose — what you hope to achieve from a certain action. If you’re driving to work and your intention is to get there on time even if you leave late or encounter heavy traffic, you may drive recklessly and dangerously. If you’re driving to work and your intention is to get there safely, you probably drive more defensively and at a safe and reasonable speed.

Intention shapes the nature of an action. Although the action may be the same, the intention itself strongly influences your moment-by-moment experience and state of mind. For this reason, the right intention is vitally important to successfully living a life of minimalism.

Starting small: The secret to creating new habits

As motivation goes up and down in humans, rather than starting habits with a big commitment that requires lots of willpower, why not start small — really small? What if you just began with a five-minute decluttering session, or you decide and write down the maximum number of screentime minutes you’ll allow yourself that day?

If you start small, you don’t need lots of motivation to stick to the new habit. Even if you wake up with a headache, or oversleep, or whatever other excuse you may have, you always have five minutes to review and get rid of a few items in your closet, or monitor your spending against your budget, and so on. And you get a sense of success each time you perform an activity that supports your minimalist goals.

In this way, you have a much better chance of success. After a week or so, you can gradually increase the length of time you engage in an activity supporting your new minimalist lifestyle.

Over time, you don’t need to worry about motivation because your minimalist actions become a habit. Once an action becomes a habit in your daily life, high levels of motivation are no longer necessary. The new activity becomes almost automatic — like brushing your teeth or having a shower. You’ve cleverly side-stepped the need for motivation.

THE POWER OF SMALL STEPS

The following amazing experiment has been repeated from the United States to Ireland. Researchers pick two random groups of office workers that don’t exercise regularly. With one group, the researchers hand out gift vouchers to buy gym shoes and clothes and offer them free access to a gym for six months. The workers get everything they need to get started at the gym. With the other group, the researchers just say, “On Monday, walk up one flight of stairs. On Tuesday, do the same but add just one extra step. On Wednesday, one more step again. In the same way, keep adding an extra step every single day.”

Which group do you think were fitter, happier, and had healthier blood pressure three years later? The walking group.

This experiment shows you the power of taking small steps — literally! Big change happens through taking small, consistent steps every day.

Minimalism is about living more mindfully, not automatically. So turning minimalist activities into an automatic habit may sound counterintuitive. But the opposite is true. If you don’t make minimalism-based decisions part of your routine, you’re much less likely to remember to be intentional, and your whole day could pass by automatically.

Generating Minimalism Motivation with Some Quick Wins

If you’re like most of us, you have a lot of physical and digital stuff, and you spend a lot of time looking at that little screen you hold in your hands. Desiring to live more intentionally is a goal you feel strongly about, but getting going may feel intimidating or overwhelming. Here are a few strategies to help you jumpstart your minimalist journey:

  • Stop making impulse buys. An easy step toward minimalism is to stop accumulating more stuff! Not adding to the pile, so to speak, means you have one less item to assess and decide to keep or toss. When you’re thinking about making an impulse purchase, ask yourself a few questions: Will your life be improved by having the item or diminished by not having it? Do you already have an item that looks or acts similarly? Will you have to give up something in order to make this purchase? Taking a beat to consider these questions will help you slow down and make an intentional decision.
  • Make your bed and tidy up your bedroom. Where you wake up has a big impact on how your day is affected. And walking into a tidy bedroom with a neatly made bed gives you a sense of accomplishment and calm at the order it reflects.
  • Select tomorrow’s outfit the night before. You don’t want to start your day stressed out because you don’t know what to wear or you waste time in your closet looking for items buried or hidden behind other stuff. Start your day on a chaos-free note by waking up to a pre-selected outfit.
  • Clean out your car. It’s amazing how great a clean car can feel during an otherwise stressful drive. Even just getting rid of trash and wiping down the dashboard, steering wheel, and console can make the small space feel more organized and calm. We’re always surprised at the quick spark of joy we feel when getting into a tidy front seat. And you don’t have to feel hesitant about giving a friend or colleague a ride when your car is clean.
  • Remove unwanted apps on your phone. If you’re like most people, your phone is with you all day every day, and you spend hours staring at the screen. Take 15 minutes to delete what you don’t need (and if you have a lot of apps, take as many short sessions as you need to cull the herd). Lickety split, your phone’s home screens look less like the app store barfed on them, and you can more easily find the apps you’ve decided to keep.

  • Unsubscribe to emails you’ve requested but don’t read. The less junk mail you get, the less cluttered your inbox and the less stress you feel. Review your inbox for mailing lists you’ve asked to be on, and unsubscribe to any you don’t regularly read.

    Ignore the spam emails you never signed up for. Many are phishing attempts, and clicking their Unsubscribe links simply alerts the senders that your email address is a valid one, which will probably increase the amount of spam you get ( Chapter 15 goes into more detail on unsubscribing).

We know you juggle so much every day — from personal and business obligations to family and friendship commitments, the struggle is real to fit everything in. How successfully you juggle your many activities and commitments can heavily depend on the clutter you have lingering in your home, your workspace, and your mind. To really be more focused and productive, start relating clutter to stress, and slowly reduce what you’ve accumulated. Tackle one day or category at a time, be consistent, and work at whatever steady pace you can commit to. Done is better than perfect, and even 1 percent progress makes a difference over time.

NAVIGATING NERVES ABOUT PARED-BACK POSSESSIONS

You’ve probably heard it said before, “If you don’t use it, toss it.” This is easy to say. However, we want to give you some quick tips on how to implement this advice. It requires a mindset change: You need to program your mind to say no a lot more. We don’t mean to party invites or customer inquiries; we mean to stuff.

Say no when you want to buy something simply because it’s your favorite color or it’s on sale for half price. The common sale purchase only works if you would actually have paid full price for the item in the first place; otherwise, it will likely end up being clutter.

Before investing in potentially more clutter, always ask yourself these questions: Why do I need this? Do I already have this? Will it serve a purpose?

Decluttering does not mean going without. Losing clutter should be a relief, not something to be scared about. When you picked up this book, you may have been worried you’d have to give up too much stuff, or we’d recommend you live so minimally that you’d be able to fit your life’s possessions into a backpack and you’d need to do laundry twice a week to keep up with demand. That is completely not the case.

Removing clutter all around should make you feel more comfortable in your home, office, car, mind, and wherever else you spend your time. If the act of decluttering makes you scared, don’t give up. You simply need to change your mindset. You also may be a little too ambitious in your initial minimalist goals, or you need to slow down how quickly you’re adopting new habits.

When you let go of something, keep track of how much it cost originally. Don’t do it to justify keeping anything. (Decluttering doesn’t focus on how much an item costs, but whether or not an item is useful.) This trick is simply to help give you perspective on how much you’ve been spending on things you don’t need, and to help prevent you from buying unneeded things in the future.

Discovering how much money you’re now donating or throwing in the trash may be depressing, but this exercise will help you save money in the future! You’ve already paid for the items, so the money is gone anyway. Use this tip to help keep you motivated to buy less and live better. We call the money you realize is gone the “sunk costs” of decluttering. You have to move on and do it swiftly. The trick is to remember your new goals and the techniques we discuss throughout this book, so you don’t make the same purchasing mistakes again.
