Information Literacy and Critical Thinking

Imagine preparing a class presentation on social media and its impact on society. On a blog, you see a statistic: The average time someone spends on social media each day is 2.5 hours. That number sounds low to you, and you know your professor will expect you to cite more formal sources. As you dig deeper, conflicting claims emerge. Now you’re stuck: Which source can you trust?

xliii When you evaluate information , you are determining whether a source or content deserves your time and trust and how or whether to use the information you find. The process involves critical thinking skills that you exercise as you consider factors like currency, quality, accuracy, and relevance. Evaluating information helps you make better decisions in school, work, and life.

This section guides you through that decision-making process. You’ll learn how to evaluate source reliability; recognize misinformation, hallucination, and disinformation; and understand how AI contributes to each.

Transparency and Verification

Before you decide what information to believe or repeat, you must know where it comes from. You can’t begin to evaluate information until you understand its source—through a process called verification —and you can’t identify the source without transparency. Transparency means disclosing who created the content, how it was produced, and which sources informed its development.

In a world where facts, opinions, and falsehoods mix freely, transparency and verification are essential habits. These habits include showing where data, images, and ideas originate; separating fact from opinion; and being honest when you’re uncertain about the source of something. As a consumer of information, you probably notice AI-generated TikTok or YouTube videos. That’s a verification habit. A transparency habit refers to your choice to share the video but say “It’s AI” when you do. We’ll discuss best practices for transparency around your own AI use later in the chapter. Practiced regularly, these habits shape responsible communication.

Misinformation, Hallucination, and Disinformation

Transparent attribution slows the spread of false information. Studies show that false information travels faster and farther than the truth. 1 In your own research, you will encounter three main types of false information—misinformation, hallucination, and disinformation—with important distinctions between them (see Figure 1 ).

Misinformation

Misinformation is false or misleading content shared without the intent to deceive. It often sounds familiar or plausible but lacks context or accuracy. It may include out-of-context facts, misunderstood statistics, outdated information, or someone repeating something they simply misunderstand. Examples include:

  • “Everyone should drink eight glasses of water a day.” That’s not true and oversimplifies individual needs. A baby needs far less water than a marathon runner does.
  • “Vikings wore horned helmets.” Despite movie depictions, archeological evidence shows they didn’t. 2

Misinformation spreads easily because it sounds familiar or “right.” Pausing, questioning, and verifying help prevent its spread.

Hallucination

“According to Donaldson (2025), AI tools invent false citations in more than 20% of all generated bibliographies.” Is that an odd data point? That statistic is fake. AI hallucinated the percentage and its source. AI’s accuracy depends on your prompts, your usage, and the tool itself.

AI hallucinations are false outputs generated by AI tools. These may include fabricated quotes, events, or statistics. Early AI chatbots made up quotes, citations, and facts. One AI tool suggested melting whole, in-shell eggs in the microwave (don’t do it). 3 Microsoft suggested visiting the Ottawa Food Bank as a tourist destination. 4 A professor gave his entire class an “X” or “incomplete” on their papers because ChatGPT had incorrectly identified that everyone used AI unethically. 5 Hallucinations have shifted from obvious fiction to subtle falsehoods and errors. Who is responsible for catching these errors? You, the user and consumer, are responsible for verifying AI-generated content.

xliv Hallucinations may lead to misinformation when shared unchecked, and disinformation if intentionally shared.

Disinformation

Disinformation is false content deliberately crafted to mislead. For example, a viral tweet impersonated a pharmaceutical company and falsely claimed insulin was free, causing confusion and financial fallout. The tweet mimicked authenticity to provoke a reaction, making it a clear case of disinformation. 6 AI tools that generate deepfakes and large amounts of content amplify the risks of disinformation. Deepfakes include sounds, videos, and images altered or created with AI tools to mislead media consumers about people, events, and more. 7

The best way to uncover misinformation, hallucination, and disinformation is to follow a trusted process for evaluating information and its sources. We must all remain vigilant to potential manipulation, whether human or AI-generated.

Steps to Evaluate Sources and Information

The SIFT method outlines four practical actions to guide your evaluation of sources and information (see Figure 2 ). 8

  1. Stop
  2. Investigate the Source
  3. Find Better Coverage
  4. xlv Trace Claims, Quotes, and Media

SIFT: Stop

Before sharing a video, clicking on a sensational headline, or using AI-generated content in your own work, pause to consider the consequences. The “Stop” step breaks the habit of treating online content as ready-to-use. Ask: 9

  • What do I already know about this topic?
  • Am I reacting emotionally?
  • What do I know about this source?

Stopping shifts your mindset from an impulsive reaction to a deliberate judgment.

SIFT: Investigate the Source

Research the author, organization, or platform behind the content. Ask:

  • Who created this? Who published the content? What are their affiliations (e.g., nonprofit, news outlet, company, university, political group)?
  • Why did they publish this? Are they trying to inform, persuade, or entertain their audience? Are they trying to sell something?
  • Are they qualified and trustworthy? What is their reputation, or what are their credentials?

Sideways reading or lateral reading involves cross-checking sources to help you assess their trustworthiness and credibility. Lateral reading means examining information beyond a single source to determine whether it is current, relevant, accurate, and presented honestly.

Lateral reading involves four steps: 10

  1. Note the source (author or organization) of the information you want to investigate.
  2. Leave the website and search for the author or organization in a new tab. For print sources, go online. Doing so shifts you from vertical reading (scrolling the source for information) to lateral, or sideways, reading.
  3. Check what reputable sources say. Review news outlets, fact-checking sites, academic sources, and Wikipedia.
  4. Compare information across sources. Examine what multiple places say about the source’s credibility and goals before trusting it. Don’t just see what one source says or trust an AI-generated description.

Through lateral reading, you may learn that the author has been discredited or that more precise information exists. Lateral reading can also help you identify hidden agendas and misinformation, and it can prevent you from falling for surface-level credibility markers like polished websites or.org domains. 11

SIFT: Find Better Coverage

Next, use lateral reading to find two or three credible sources. Check whether they agree or contradict each other. Does your original source seem biased or inaccurate?

Watch for:

  • Single-source stories. If only one site reports a claim and seems unreliable, question its accuracy.
  • xlvi Echo chambers. If multiple articles or posts trace back to one origin, you may be hearing the same voice repeated.

This step of lateral reading is particularly effective for AI-generated content. Ask the AI tool for multiple sources on the same topic, then verify them using these steps.

SIFT: Trace Claims, Quotes, and Media to Their Original Context

Even credible sources may misquote or distort information. Trace claims, quotes, and media to their original context, then:

  1. Check the full context. Review the original material to see if the meaning has been changed or exaggerated in the version you saw.
  2. Look for edits or omissions. Has the information, image, or quote been changed in a way that alters its meaning?

Using the SIFT method improves—and speeds up—your ability to evaluate information effectively. Write down or screenshot Table 1 for reference.

Key Takeaways: Why Evaluating Information Matters

Evaluating information means asking the right questions at the right times. Skilled evaluators slow down, verify sources, and stay curious. The same habits that help you spot an AI deepfake also support academic and professional credibility. Transparency and judgment form the foundation of academic integrity.

Exercising Academic Integrity and Intellectual Honesty

Imagine writing a novel and sharing your first draft with a friend, hoping to get their feedback. How would you feel if you later saw that novel appear on a self-publishing platform, supposedly written by your friend? You’d feel furious and call it theft. You might even pursue legal action. Intellectual honesty protects ideas, ownership, and individual hard work.

In college and throughout your career, intellectual honesty means avoiding plagiarism, accurately paraphrasing, and properly citing sources. These habits protect your reputation and reduce legal risk.

Integrity in Practice

Integrity is a personal and professional value, not just a set of rules. Integrity means choosing the right action, even when no one is watching. It reflects a mindset and personal standard that defines how you work, study, and collaborate. People trust those who take responsibility for their actions and follow through on commitments. Integrity enables others to trust your academic work and safeguards your future career.

Academic Integrity

Academic integrity includes a commitment to honesty, trust, fairness, respect, responsibility, and courage. 12 Maintaining integrity involves paraphrasing, citing sources, avoiding dishonesty or prohibited shortcuts, and completing your own work.

For example, during her senior research project, sociology major Lila noticed that a few of her survey participants had submitted identical answers, likely due to people taking the survey more than once. Deleting the duplicate responses would weaken her results, but keeping them would mean misrepresenting her data. She reported the issue to her professor and documented the correction. Her honesty upheld academic standards and demonstrated professional maturity, thereby strengthening the credibility of her study.

Integrity in the Workplace

Integrity protects your professional reputation. Employers consistently rank integrity as one of the top traits they look for in new hires. Cheating in school may seem minor, but it shapes habits that erode trust in the workplace.

Consider two recent professional cases:

  • A lawyer in California submitted a court appeal containing 21 fake, AI-generated legal citations. The court fined her $10,000 for failing to verify the information. 13
  • Deloitte refunded part of a $440,000 contract with the Australian government after a report included fabricated quotations generated by AI. The firm failed to disclose its use of the tool. 14

xlviii In both cases, professionals faced penalties not for using AI but for misrepresenting its output and violating trust. College holds the same expectations: Verify sources, disclose tool use, and ensure your work meets both ethical and professional standards. At work and school, AI may be banned. Check your workplace or school’s specific policies with your supervisor or professor.

Responsible Collaboration

Integrity also applies to how we work with others. Many people have experienced a team member who lied about a deadline or used AI to generate their section of a project without disclosing it. Responsible collaboration is part of personal integrity. Collaboration encompasses group work, study aids, and the use of AI tools. Responsible collaboration means:

  • Crediting teammates for their contributions
  • Using study aids and AI tools ethically
  • Communicating openly about expectations and roles

How to Paraphrase and Cite Sources

You are already familiar with paraphrasing and citing. You use these tools in everyday conversation. If your friend who missed Thursday’s class asks you what your professor covered that day, and you summarize it in your own words, that’s paraphrasing. If you paraphrase or quote a friend, you’d say, “Rubina said…” That’s a citation.

Whether you’re presenting or writing, paraphrasing and citing involve supporting your ideas, giving credit to others, and explaining things in your own voice. These practices help build credibility and prevent academic dishonesty, such as plagiarism. Plagiarism refers to using someone else’s words, ideas, or other content as your own without citing them or using quotation marks, if needed. Plagiarism includes:

  • Direct copying : Word-for-word copying without quotation marks or citations
  • Patchwriting : Changing a few words but keeping the original structure

Avoiding plagiarism builds trust and credibility with professors, peers, and colleagues.

Paraphrasing

Paraphrasing means restating ideas in your own words while keeping the original meaning. Paraphrasing includes altering the sentence structure, the words, and sometimes the tone of your source material. Even when paraphrased, the idea still belongs to the original author, so you must cite the source.

These examples show the difference between plagiarized content and paraphrased content:

  • Original sentence: Effective communication in teams requires trust and shared goals.
  • Direct copying: Effective communication in teams requires trust and shared goals. (direct copy, no quotation marks, no citation)
  • Patchwriting: Good communication in teams needs trust and common goals. (too close to the original version; no citation)
  • Paraphrased: Teams communicate well when members trust each other and work toward the same objectives [citation listed here].

Citing

Citing gives credit to the sources you use, whether you’re writing a paper or giving a speech. Cite:

  • xlix Direct quotes
  • Paraphrased ideas
  • Data, statistics, or research findings
  • AI-generated content (if used)

Citation style determines how to cite. Your class or degree may require one style or several. Each style has specific rules for formatting, in-text citations, and reference lists (see Table 2 ).

Table 2 Citation Styles
Citation Style Used in Values Emphasized What It Teaches You
MLA (9th ed.) Humanities, literature, arts, languages Authorship and close reading Pay attention to original language, meaning, and interpretation.
APA (7th ed.) Psychology, business, education, social sciences Recency, evidence, and precision Evaluate sources by credibility and date. Communicate findings clearly.
Chicago, Notes & Bibliography (17th ed.) History, art history, theology Context and interpretation Explain how ideas connect across time and sources.
Chicago, Author-Date (17th ed.) Sciences, social sciences, economics Balance and synthesis Integrate multiple studies efficiently while maintaining clarity and coherence.
IEEE Engineering, computer science, technical fields Precision and efficiency Communicate technical data quickly and accurately using numbered sources.

These differences reflect the distinct values and priorities of each discipline. For example, MLA emphasizes authorship and page numbers, ideal for analyzing texts. APA highlights the author and publication date, which are essential for conducting up-to-date research.

In each class, ask your professor or check your syllabus for the required style. You can purchase style guides, but you can also use reliable citation websites or your university library’s citation guide for examples and resources.

In each style, the author appears first, whether in in-text citations (MLA, APA) or in full citations using footnotes or bracketed numbers (Chicago, IEEE). In all styles, full references include details like the title and publication date of each source. Each style has its own rules for citing AI-generated content.

Key Takeaways: Why Academic Integrity Matters

Employers value integrity as a top trait, and lapses in honesty can cost careers. Practicing integrity protects your credibility, fosters responsible collaboration, and ensures your ideas shine. In short, own your work, give credit where it’s due, and commit to ethical standards.

Understanding AI: Types, Capabilities, and Limitations

Artificial intelligence is everywhere, from writing assistants to data analysis tools. But what exactly can it do, and where does it fall short? Understanding AI, including its types, capabilities, and limitations, informs decision-making and use. This section explores how AI excels, where it struggles, and why recognizing these boundaries is critical for academic and professional integrity and success.

How AI Works

Artificial intelligence (AI) describes when computers perform tasks we typically associate with human intelligence. AI systems operate through algorithms. An algorithm is a set of instructions that a computer follows to complete a task. 17 From spell check to Netflix recommendations, algorithms have been a part of everyday tools for years. Algorithms sort, rank, classify, or predict outcomes based on rules and data.

Most AI relies on machine learning. Machine learning is the process whereby algorithms learn from data rather than relying on a static set of rules to complete tasks. 18 Machine learning analyzes thousands or millions of examples and adjusts its internal “rules.” As you may have experienced when using AI, the model improves over time as it tries to avoid errors.

li When you enter a prompt into a generative AI tool, the system predicts the output you expect based on the millions of examples it has learned. The response is based on probability, not understanding or intent. AI can seem self-aware, but it learned everything it knows from content created by people online, ranging from academic journal articles to Facebook posts. Have you ever heard the phrase “What goes in must come out?” AI’s outputs depend on its training data. That data includes books, social media, coding websites, and other free online resources. For example, some AI tools have gathered information from Reddit. If you ask AI for relationship advice, you might get a Reddit-influenced list of tips.

Gaps, biases, and errors can occur in AI responses, but helpful and accurate information can result, too. Distinguishing between the former and the latter requires your human judgment and awareness.

Types of AI

Numerous websites, browsers, and software offer access to AI. Popular generative AIs include ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and Perplexity. Other tools generate images, computer code, and videos.

AI tools fall into three major categories:

  • Traditional AI is designed to complete specific tasks that involve pattern recognition and decision-making. Examples include email filters, voice assistants (e.g., Siri), and autonomous technologies (e.g., robot vacuums and self-driving cars). Traditional AI solves specific problems, such as math equations or translations.
  • Generative AI creates new content, including text, images, audio, video, and code. Examples include ChatGPT, Claude, SORA, and DALL·E. These tools can help you create study aids, give feedback on your writing, and generate written or visual content. The use of these tools is likely to be guided by your instructor, school, or employer.
  • Agentic AI goes beyond content creation to take goal-oriented action. Agentic AI generates content, conducts advanced research, and integrates disparate systems, files, and websites. For example, an AI agent could access your course syllabi, sync with your calendars, set reminders, and email the major deadlines to a friend.

Experts predict the future development of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) systems, which aim to match human intelligence, or even Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI), which would surpass human intelligence (see Figure 3 ). However, whether these systems can truly be considered “smart” depends on how one defines intelligence.

What AI Can Do Well

To utilize AI effectively, it’s important to recognize its strengths and limitations. AI excels at drafting, summarizing, and generating content. In the workplace, AI is most often used to write emails or messages. 19 AI can also process large datasets and provide quick outputs, and it can speed up tasks like slide creation and meeting scheduling.

AI in the Workplace

Many corporations rank AI skills as a top hiring need. Companies expect new graduates to know how and when to use AI—and when not to use it. Some expect job displacement due to AI; in other words, those unable to use AI or unwilling to try may lose their positions in the coming years.

In addition to tasks you might do in college, AI supports work tasks like analyzing data, automating processes, rewriting emails or reports, preparing for meetings, improving content, and generating content.

While AI use may be encouraged or expected, employees still need to be able to explain their use of AI and maintain responsibility for the outputs. Some employers may limit AI use or have policies about which AI tools can be used and how. Employers want honesty, creativity, and accuracy whether you use AI or not.

Where AI Falls Short

AI use requires a balance of creativity and caution. As you determine the best ways to use AI in your work and school lives, keep in mind the potential for inaccurate outputs, the pitfalls of AI overreliance, and the human touch that you might be missing.

AI (In)accuracy and Biases

Like websites or search engines, AI can be inaccurate or outdated. It is also prone to hallucinations, as we discussed earlier in the chapter. Always verify AI-generated facts and references through lateral reading with trusted and reliable non-AI sources.

AI reflects the biases and preferences of its training data. Some AI systems were trained on websites like Reddit or corporate email archives, which shape their tone and content output. AI may present a limited view or block responses on sensitive topics to avoid the appearance of bias. Because AI doesn’t know your own experiences, classroom norms, or local context, its responses often default to a generic tone, which weakens assignments meant to show and develop your authentic voice.

When AI makes mistakes, it can’t take responsibility. This has led to huge issues in workplaces and schools. People are more likely to act dishonestly when they feel detached, like when they’re using generative AI. 20 In school, some students treat AI-generated outputs as “nobody’s fault,” which can lead to a lower sense of accountability.

Whether you use AI or not, you are ultimately responsible for the final product. Think critically and double-check your work.

AI Overreliance

AI overreliance occurs when AI tools replace human thinking rather than supporting learning, collaboration, or work. The use of AI for drafting, summarizing, and problem-solving eliminates opportunities for practicing these skills. For example, writing skills develop through the hard work of trying, failing, getting feedback, rewriting, and trying again. AI can shortcut that process. The shortcut might save a tired student time or a business money, but over time, excessive use can hinder skill development and growth. As humans, we need to maintain our ability to think and make decisions.

AI Tensions

Many people utilize AI to save time and enhance the quality of their projects. However, most people also do not prefer to be evaluated by AI or to feel like they are “just talking to a bot.” AI lacks the human touch people expect when building and maintaining relationships.

For example, would you prefer getting an AI-written text from a friend, or a normal text they wrote themselves? If you said normal text, you’re not alone: 74% of people prefer human-generated content. 21

AI users must strike a balance between the ease of AI and readers’ discomfort with AI-generated content. This significant tension means core skills like communication become even more important for school, work, and even texting a friend. Most importantly, AI lacks empathy and judgment. In group projects, peer reviews, or class discussions, these human skills matter.

Key Takeaways: Why Understanding AI Matters

Knowing what AI can and cannot do helps you make informed decisions and avoid overdependence and improper use. In short, learn what AI does well, recognize where it falls short, and apply that knowledge to enhance, not replace, your creativity and judgment.

Using AI Responsibly

How can you use AI while maintaining your integrity? Responsible AI use is a lifelong skill. Employers rank AI ethics and creativity as top skills. 22 We must learn to use AI responsibly while showing independent thinking.

How to Build Good AI Habits

Responsible AI use supports learning; it doesn’t replace it. Use AI to learn, not to skip learning. AI should benefit and empower learners and teachers. 23 Responsible use helps you maintain integrity, develop skills, and form good habits that contribute to professional credibility.

Use AI as a Helper—Not a Thinker

Think of AI as a helper. Copying and pasting AI-generated content and passing it off as your own skips the most important part: your own unique thinking. Learning research shows that your brain remembers more when you wrestle with material yourself. 24 AI can help you get started or proofread (if allowed), but you need to do the analysis and provide the insights. Using AI this way in college prepares you for the same reality at work.

Think Critically About AI Data

AI generates responses based on its training data; it doesn’t “know” facts. Ask questions like:

  • Who trained or built this tool? What data is used for training? Is the company behind this large language model transparent about its data sources and limitations?
  • lv Where did the output come from? Ask the AI to cite its sources, then evaluate them using lateral reading.

Responsible use entails being accountable for privacy. Privacy has to do with the collection, storage, and usage of personal or sensitive information that can be traced back to and identified with individual people. Generative AI relies on you providing information; privacy focuses on who owns this information and how it might be used. Uploading your transcripts, student records, or emails with a professor into public AI tools can expose sensitive data.

How to Prompt AI Effectively

Prompting refers to giving instructions to an AI tool. A good prompt produces the desired output. A poor prompt may lead to a lack of details, vague or inappropriate content, and wasted time and effort.

Eventually, prompting should be automated and optimized, making AI easier to use. For now, learning approaches and frameworks for prompting can improve AI outputs.

Prompting Approaches

Prompting can be divided into three approaches: direct instructions, open-ended instructions, and task-specific instructions. 25

  • Direct instructions tell the AI what to do. For example, “Write a song about love.”
  • Open-ended instructions support brainstorming and exploring topics. For example, “Help me understand the role of AI at work.”
  • Task-specific instructions focus on achieving a goal. For example, “Reformat this citation in APA format.”

Beyond those major categories, prompting can range from zero-shot prompting, where you put in one prompt and hope for the best, to meta prompting, which is asking AI to create the prompt. With meta prompting, AI users can ask the AI to ask them questions until the AI knows what to do. As AI tools become more advanced, more meta prompting will occur.

lvi

Prompting Framework

AI performs only as well as your instructions, and all prompting approaches depend on your ability to provide the AI with enough information to achieve a goal. Use the PAC framework to improve your prompting:

  • Purpose. Begin with a clear action and tell AI what to do. What is your goal? What do you want to achieve? For example, you might be drafting an email, generating study questions, or rewriting a paragraph. Use verbs like “write,” “revise,” “summarize,” “explain,” or “compare.”
  • Audience. Identify who will read the output and what they expect. Tone, word choice, and format depend on the audience (professor, boss, friend, etc.). AI struggles with tone and nuance. For instance, you write casually to a classmate and formally to a professor. Specify the level of formality and professionalism.
  • Context. Provide relevant details or background, or upload examples or samples. Context might include deadlines, rules or policies, formatting, or details needed for an accurate output. For example, ask not for “ch 10 review questions” but instead for “ch 10 multiple-choice review questions from Northouse Introduction to Leadership for a freshman-level class.”
Example PAC Prompt

“Write a short, polite email to my professor asking for an extension on an assignment. The purpose is to request more time. The audience is a professor, so the tone should be formal. The context is that I was sick and needed two extra days. I have a doctor’s note for documentation. I will attach it to the email.”

Prompting Process

Treat prompting like a process. Many people default to zero-shot prompting: They put in one prompt and hope for the best. Effective prompting means having a conversation with an AI tool. You can use casual language or voice mode to talk through the purpose, audience, and context first, and then ask for revisions to the AI output.

Revising Outputs

Revising AI outputs means more than just changing the font. You should spend most of your time revising and verifying the output of an AI. Professors hate seeing papers that are simply copied and pasted from ChatGPT.

Revise for clarity, accuracy, tone, and originality. Check the facts and sources. Replace vague language or overly positive phrasing. Ensure that the final content reflects your purpose consistently throughout.

How to Put AI Guidelines Into Practice

Responsible AI use means using tools wisely. Your judgment is your edge in an AI-driven world. Combine AI with your critical thinking to strengthen integrity and career readiness. In school, the tool isn’t graded; you are. After graduation, you remain fully responsible. If a report has a significant error, AI isn’t being fired; you are.

Use AI to draft, review, or brainstorm, but ensure that the final work accurately reflects your understanding and effort. Before you create anything with the help of AI, consider whether your use is aligned with your purpose, transparent, accurate, and ethical (see Table 5 ).

lvii

Purpose

Start by clarifying why you want to use AI before you even use it. If you focus on your purpose, you can guide responsible use. For example, if your goal is to support learning, you might use AI for brainstorming, organizing your notes, or identifying gaps in your understanding. Avoid using AI in ways that remove practice with thinking, writing, or problem-solving. You want to get the most out of your education.

Before using AI, ask yourself, “What do I need help with? What needs to be done by me?” A clear purpose can help you stay within academic and professional expectations.

Transparency

At school or work, you remain responsible for AI-generated content, and you may be expected to disclose how AI was used. Students often have a higher bar for disclosing or avoiding AI use than employees do.

Track your AI use and be ready to explain it. Being able to explain how you used (or didn’t use) AI is an expected skill after graduation. AI disclosure refers to explaining how and when you utilized AI. A short statement builds transparency and trust and may be required. A strong statement distinguishes human and AI contributions. In school, disclosure clarifies the process behind your work.

Sample AI Disclosure Statement

“I used ChatGPT to outline my essay and refine the introduction. I reviewed and edited all content for accuracy and style myself. I did not generate any whole sentences or ideas. I did use AI to put my sources in APA format.”

One final tip: Track your work. Many companies now offer ways to track changes. You can easily track in Word, Google Docs, or Grammarly. AI detection tools can generate false positives; take ownership and prove your original work rather than waiting to see what the detectors say.

Accuracy

AI cannot be trusted as a fact checker. That’s your job. Check names, dates, citations, statistics, quotations, and, well, everything. Confirm claims using the SIFT approach. AI can sound very confident yet be very wrong.

Submitting inaccurate or incorrect work harms your reputation and credibility. While AI continues to improve, accuracy remains a human responsibility.

Ethics and Policy

AI policies vary by course and workplace. Always confirm expectations and ask when you’re unsure. When guidelines are unstated or unclear, asking questions protects you, especially if you can get those expectations in writing. Seeking clarification shows professionalism and responsibility.

Key Takeaways: Why Responsible AI Use Matters

AI should support, not replace, your effort. Always review its outputs. Add personal examples and explanations. Follow your school’s or professor’s AI use policies. These exact expectations apply in the workplace.

Human skills make your work original and credible. AI assists with routine tasks, but your judgment, imagination, and relationships shape your education.

Closing Reflection

Evaluating information, practicing academic integrity, and using AI responsibly shape how you think, learn, and earn trust. Slow down, verify sources, cite honestly, and balance AI with personal judgment.

These practices strengthen critical thinking:

  1. lix Use the SIFT method to verify sources, trace claims, and mitigate misinformation, especially with AI-generated content.

Glossary

  • Academic integrity: Ethical principles like honesty and responsibility that guide actions like giving proper credit in academic work
  • Agentic AI: AI that conducts goal-oriented actions
  • AI hallucinations: Fabricated outputs generated by an AI system
  • Algorithm: Instructions a computer follows to complete a task
  • Artificial intelligence (AI): Computers that perform tasks traditionally associated with human intelligence
  • Citing: Giving credit to the sources used
  • Deepfakes: Media (e.g., images, videos, or audio) that have been digitally manipulated or generated with AI to appear authentic; intended to mislead media consumers
  • Direct copying: Word-for-word copying of a given source material without quotation marks or citations
  • Disinformation: False content deliberately created to mislead or deceive
  • Generative AI: AI systems that can generate new text, images, code, or other content
  • Integrity: Choosing the right action, even when no one is watching
  • Lateral reading: Cross-checking information credibility by leaving a website and searching for more context about the author or publisher
  • Machine learning: The process where algorithms learn from data rather than relying on a set of rules
  • Misinformation: False or misleading information shared without intent to deceive
  • PAC framework: Adding purpose, audience, and context to an AI prompt
  • Paraphrasing: Restating ideas derived from a source material in your own words while keeping the original meaning
  • Patchwriting: Changing a few words but keeping the original structure of a given source material
  • Plagiarism: Using someone else’s words, ideas, or AI-generated text as your own without citation
  • Privacy: The safe collection, storage, and use of personal or sensitive information that can be traced back to and identified with individual people
  • Prompting: Giving instructions to an AI tool
  • Traditional AI: AI designed to complete specific tasks that involve pattern recognition and decision-making
  • Transparency: Disclosing who created content, how it was produced, and which sources informed its development
  • Verification: Identifying and understanding the source of information

Put the Chapter to Work

  1. Hallucination Hunt: Use an AI tool to generate content on any topic. Find one false claim or citation, verify it, and share how you identified the error.
  2. Expert Check: Ask AI a question about a topic you know well, like a hobby or niche interest. Evaluate the accuracy of its answer and the credibility of any sources it cites.
  3. SIFT in Action: Pick a trending claim online. Apply the four SIFT steps and summarize what you learned.
  4. Lateral Reading Challenge: Choose an article on a controversial topic. Leave the page, find two other sources, and compare what you discovered.
  5. Trace It Back: Find a quote or statistic in an article. Track it to its original source and note any changes in meaning.
  6. Spot the Bias: Locate an article or AI-generated text with biased language. Highlight the bias and rewrite the sentence neutrally.
  7. Paraphrase Practice: Select a short paragraph on a topic you like. Create three versions: paraphrase, direct quote, and plagiarized version. Label each clearly.
  8. Cheating or Acceptable?: Write down one way you’ve seen AI used in school. Decide if it’s acceptable or cheating and explain why.
  9. Disclosure Statement: Draft a five-sentence AI disclosure statement you could use to tell your professor you used AI responsibly. What information should be included and why?
  10. Integrity Case Study: Find a real news story about academic or professional dishonesty related to AI use. Summarize what happened and what could have prevented it.
  11. Bias Scan: Ask AI to generate an image of “a professional in [field].” Compare your image with those generated by your classmates and discuss the patterns or stereotypes you notice.
  12. Prompt Rewrite: Start with a vague prompt (e.g., “Write about good study habits”). Rewrite it using PAC (purpose, audience, context) for better results tailored to your needs and field. Compare the two outputs. Decide which is better and why.
  13. lxii PAC Speed Round: Pretend to write an email explaining you will be late submitting a report. Write three AI prompts for three audiences: professor, classmate, and employer. Compare how they differ.
  14. Responsible or Risky?: List three ways you might use AI for schoolwork. Mark each as responsible or risky and explain why.
  15. Fact Versus Fiction: Ask AI for a statistic or quote on a topic. Verify if it’s real by finding the original source. How easy was it to confirm?

Case Study: Fact or Fiction? When AI Gets It Wrong

Jordan, a student in public health, is preparing a presentation on nutrition trends. The professor allows the use of AI and keeps reminding the class that verifying sources is part of academic integrity.

To save time, Jordan uses an AI tool to draft slides and summarize key research. The AI-generated slides look polished and include statistics and citations. One claim stands out: “A 2022 study found that 70% of adults who follow plant-based diets have lower cholesterol levels.” The slide lists a reference for a journal article.

Jordan feels confident and presents to the class. After the presentation, his professor asks for a link to the journal article. When Jordan searches for the article, he realizes it doesn’t exist. In fact, none of the statistics can be verified. The AI fabricated both the numbers and the references. Jordan realizes the presentation included multiple hallucinations.

Jordan wonders: Was using AI without checking its facts an intentional violation? Or just an unintentional mistake? Jordan now faces the challenge of correcting the misinformation and deciding what to do about his presentation.
