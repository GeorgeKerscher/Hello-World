Living well doesn’t require an 800-page cookbook, a drawer full of supplements, or a fitness routine that sounds like a military operation. A minimalist approach to health is about bringing intentionality to the decisions you make every single day — what you eat, how you move, and how you rest — while using what you already have whenever possible. Instead of chasing the newest gadget or the most intense workout, you focus on the habits that actually support your energy, mood, and long-term well-being.

Eating with Intention

A minimalist diet isn’t slim pickings of pottage and vegetables. It’s simply eating in a way that reduces decision fatigue, uses what you purchase, and keeps your body fueled without making food prep your part-time job.

Here are some pointers for minimalist eating:

  • Use fewer ingredients and enjoy your favorites often. Rotate 10–15 staple foods without shame. Maybe that means a few go-to breakfasts, lunches, and dinners, or having specific ingredients on repeat when grocery shopping. This approach isn’t boring, it’s freeing, and it lets you save your creativity for things that matter.

  • Eat whole foods over hyper-processed stuff. Minimalist eating means eating what’s good for your body so your body stays fueled and healthy. This means lots of colorful vegetables, fruits, lean proteins, whole grains, beans, eggs, nuts, yogurt and low-fat dairy, and simple sauces.

    The Mediterranean diet (or MedDiet) is a great plan to follow, not necessarily to lose weight (although it will help you do so if you need to shed a few pounds), but to help stave off illnesses. Take a look at the sidebar “ Going Minimalist at the Med ” for more information.

  • Keep prep simple, which helps keep cleanup simple. Ideas include sheet-pan meals, where you roast protein and veggies together on a baking sheet, one-pot soups, and stir-fries.

    “Protein + vegetable + grain” is a minimalist formula you can replicate forever.

  • Follow mindful portions, not rigid rules. Minimalist doesn’t mean restrictive. It means you eat to feel good. You don’t beat yourself up — you simply check in: “Does this food, and this portion, support how I want to feel today?”
  • Eat smart snacks to get maximum satisfaction. Minimalism doesn’t mean no snacks; it means choosing snacks that help your body feel its best. A combo of protein (to fill you up) and carbs (to give you energy) is a great duo, and when either has a little fat (as protein often does), it helps you be satisfied. Some examples include fruit and nuts, yogurt with a sprinkle of granola, or cheese and crackers.
  • Buy what you’ll eat, eat what you buy, and waste less through your more intentional choices. Minimalist eating means you buy what you expect to actually use, and then you use what you buy. Meaning, you throw away less. You grocery shop with a plan instead of stress-hunting the aisles like it’s the Hunger Games. You get creative with leftovers.

GOING MINIMALIST AT THE MED

As you’re reassessing your pantry and fridge to cut out excess and make it simpler to decide what’s for dinner, consider stocking items that make up the MedDiet. The MedDiet, rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, olive oil, and fish (plus a little red wine), has been linked to numerous health benefits like lower risks of heart disease, diabetes, obesity, dementia, and improved mental health. Its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant properties make it ideal for reducing obesity and, with its plant-based focus, it has a low environmental impact, offering both health and sustainability benefits.

Rather than being a strict optimization protocol, the MedDiet is like a set of guiding principles that influence how you select, prepare, eat, and enjoy food. Here’s a quick summary of what you should eat on a Mediterranean-style diet — pantry staples that align well with a minimalist approach to eating:

  • Veggies and fruits: Load up on a variety of colorful vegetables and fruits.
  • Extra virgin olive oil: Use this as your main source of fat — it’s both tasty and healthy!
  • Wholegrain breads and cereals: Think wholegrain goodness such as whole wheat bread and hearty cereals.
  • Legumes and beans: Chickpeas, kidney beans, lentils — these are your new best friends.
  • Nuts and seeds: Snack on a handful of nuts or sprinkle seeds over your meals.
  • Fish and seafood: Treat yourself to fish and seafood regularly.
  • Herbs and spices: Flavor your dishes with onion, garlic, and a variety of herbs and spices such as oregano, coriander, and cumin.

Wondering about meat and dairy? No worries! You can definitely include:

  • Yogurt, cheese, and milk: Enjoy these in moderation.
  • Lean proteins: Chicken, turkey, and eggs are all great choices.
  • Red meat and sweets: Keep these to small amounts and occasional treats.
  • Processed meats and packaged foods: Try to limit these to rare occasions.

And what about alcohol? If you enjoy a drink, wine (especially red wine) is a traditional part of this diet. Just remember, it’s all about moderation (one glass per day for women, two glasses at most for men) and enjoying it with meals.

Meal Planning Made Easy

Who doesn’t love easy? As a minimalist, there’s no need to be intimidated by the idea of meal planning. Meal planning may be more detailed one week and less detailed another day or week. Or it may include the same meals for every breakfast and lunch, and then rotate through seven choices for dinner. Whatever works for you is okay — the meals you plan work around your life and your priorities, not the other way around.

Key parts of meal planning include deciding on what you want to eat (and when you want to eat it), knowing what ingredients you already have at home, and shopping for the other ingredients you need.

Determining when and what to eat at home

Each week may be different, as your schedule changes. Think about how much time you’ll have and what activities you have going on each week to determine how many meals you’ll actually be home to cook or assemble.

Then consider a weekly grocery plan based on the week’s schedule. You can shop less often, but if you plan only one week at a time, you may find it easier to buy what you need, use it that week for meals, and then better plan the following week’s grocery list. On the other hand, if weekly shopping doesn’t fit your schedule, do what does.

Once you know how many meals you’ll eat at home in a given week, create a zero-waste grocery list that reflects the ingredients your household will eat! Otherwise, you’ll be wasting money on unused or spoiled food. Take a look at the recipes you’d like to cook over the week, and check your pantry before you make the shopping list.

Considering the ingredients already in your kitchen

Maybe you’ve already begun organizing your kitchen by examining your pantry and thinking about your shopping habits. When shopping as a minimalist, it’s important to see what you already have before you make your list. For example, maybe you have some frozen chicken you can defrost, some rice, and a can of artichokes — great! You can make chicken with rice and artichokes from a recipe called Chicken with Orzo, Artichokes, and Zucchini you found online.

Wait, what? How can you make that dish if you don’t have orzo or zucchini, you ask? That’s the benefit of minimalist cooking with a focus on using what you have and wasting little. You can easily swap things in or out. On the other hand, if you want to make the recipe as is, you’ll add orzo and zucchini to your shopping list and won’t overbuy other ingredients.

When you’ve checked out what you already have in your kitchen, it’s time to make a shopping list of ingredients and food items needed to make the recipes or meals you’re planning.

Making a grocery list and sticking to it

Making a grocery list isn’t brain surgery, but sometimes you may forget to do it. Perhaps you need only a few items and think, “I can remember what I need.” Sometimes that works. Sometimes it doesn’t, and you either forget an ingredient or end up spending an extra 50 bucks on impulse buys. Not only can impulse buys cost you, but they can also go to waste if you have no plan for them, or you just end up with more food than your family can eat that week or two.

Consider keeping a running list posted on your cabinet, adding items to it as you need. If more than one person in your household does the grocery shopping, consider sharing a digital list with each other using an app such as Mealime or AnyList. Grocery apps can help you keep track of your shopping list, share it with others in the household, and even hold your favorite recipes, making planning for the week easier to track. We find that we sometimes write a list on paper and then forget it, but we always have our phones with us.

Here are a couple of other tips for making your minimalist grocery list:

  • Once you make your list, stick to it. A list helps you reason with yourself when you think about buying something that looks good but you won’t have time to make this week.
  • Break up your list into pantry staples, fresh and frozen food, and then things you’ll need weekly versus those you need to buy less often. Depending on the size of your household and your budget, the types of ingredients within each group can fluctuate.

Pantry staples: Keeping basic ingredients on hand

Minimalist meal planning, and avoiding wasting food you end up not eating before it goes bad, is much easier when you routinely keep a few staples on your shelf or in your freezer. These items can help you round out meals with fresh ingredients or create simple pantry meals.

These basic items are common ingredients for cooking and baking. Most of them will be shelf stable, with some needing refrigeration after opening. Everyone’s cooking style and cultural preferences are different; this is just a suggested list to get you thinking about your minimalist pantry:

  • Oils and vinegars: We like to keep a neutral oil (such as soybean or canola) on the shelf for baking and an olive oil for cooking and salads. We like to keep at least two vinegars on hand as well for salad dressings and marinades. There are so many vinegars to choose from — apple cider, red wine, rice wine, balsamic, as well as flavor-infused vinegars.
  • Canned foods: Tuna, salmon, chicken, tomato puree, diced tomatoes, an assortment of canned beans, corn, or other vegetable, as well as canned fruit. A container of stock is also a handy item.
  • Grains: A variety of pasta and other noodles (including small pastas like orzo and couscous), rice, farro, barley, quinoa, crackers, bread crumbs or panko, cereal, and oats.
  • Spices and herbs: A few staple spices are garlic powder, chili powder, curry powder, cumin, paprika, red pepper, black pepper, and cinnamon. Dried herbs may include basil, oregano, thyme, rosemary, Herbs de Provence, and any other dried herb or spice blends that you prefer.
  • Baking products: Flour, white sugar, brown sugar, honey, baking powder, baking soda, salt, vanilla extract (and other extracts if desired), dried fruit, and chocolate chips.
  • Frozen items: Peas, shrimp, fish, chicken breasts, frozen fruit, vegetables, and pre-cooked grains.
  • Condiments: Mustard, ketchup, pickles, olives, capers, soy sauce, Worcestershire sauce, salsa, hot sauce, tahini, and other sauces.
  • Other shelved items: Peanut butter and other shelf-stable butters, fruit jam, and nuts.

Perishable items: Buying for the week to avoid food waste

Perishable items are things you may need to pick up weekly. Some examples include:

  • Milk, cream, yogurt, and cheese
  • Cuts of meat that you’ll either cook that week or freeze
  • Eggs
  • Seasonal fruits and vegetables

Deciding what to eat can be surprisingly frustrating — it’s one more decision to make, and it can be disheartening if you don’t know how to combine foods into something tasty or if your fridge and pantry include more condiments and paper goods than filling food. Freezer bingeing

Like fridge foraging, freezer bingeing helps you put together minimalist meals and stretches out the time between shopping trips. If you stock vegetables and lean proteins in your freezer, you can combine them with whole grains, legumes, and seasonings from the pantry to pull together flavorful meals quickly. You can also keep individual portions of soups, stews, and sauces in the freezer to defrost and enjoy when you’re not up to cooking.

The following foods hold up well in the freezer:

  • Cooked soups and stews
  • Sauces
  • Raw meat cubes and chicken breast
  • Fresh fruits and vegetables

Fast food the minimalist way

The completeness of a meal, nutritionally speaking, has nothing to do with how complex the recipe is. It also has nothing to do with whether other people consider a particular combination of foods to be a meal. What matters is you’re eating what you like, you’re using what you have, and what you’re preparing is pretty healthy (including healthful fat, complex carbohydrates, and lean protein) so you’re satisfied.

It’s better to eat fresh foods that require no cooking — that is, “fast food the wholesome way” — than to eat unhealthy, quick-ready prepared food. Keep these ideas on hand when you need to nourish yourself when you have little time or aren’t interested in making a full meal:

  • Plain, whole-milk Greek yogurt with EVOO and seeds or spices or fresh fruit, honey, and almonds
  • Whole wheat pita bread, fresh raw veggies, and hummus
  • Almonds, dried fruit, cheese, or a hard-boiled egg
  • Fresh salad with a variety of vegetables, edamame beans, avocado, and walnuts dressed with good-quality EVOO and lemon juice

Becoming a Minimalist in Motion

A minimalist approach to fitness starts with a simple truth: Your body was built to move, and it doesn’t need a complicated plan, fancy accessories, or an expensive gym membership to do so. Consistent physical activity is better for your long-term health than occasional intense workouts, making healthy movement easy to add to a minimalist routine. A 30-minute walk delivers cardiovascular benefits, improves mood, supports weight management, and helps regulate sleep. That’s a pretty solid return on investment for something you can do in sneakers and a jacket.

Daily movement also cuts down on decision fatigue. If you walk every day, there’s no negotiating with yourself. It’s just part of your life — like brushing your teeth or checking emails. Some days you’ll be active for 30 minutes straight; other days you may get your 30 minutes via a couple of 15-minute bursts. Both deliver the same health benefits, giving you the flexibility to insert activity into your life where it suits you.

A minimalist activity plan also leans on activities that require minimal equipment and planning. Walking, hiking, gentle cycling, tending a garden, performing household tasks, or short bodyweight sessions count. Daily movement is about staying active in a way that feels accessible and sustainable, without having to organize your entire life around workouts.

Building strength and staying supple

A principle of minimalism is intentionality, where every decision or action has meaning and where activity happens with efficiency. And nothing in fitness is more efficient than compound, functional movements. These are exercises that use multiple muscles at once, such as squats, lunges, push-ups, and overhead presses. These movements mimic real life and help you stay strong for all the things you actually want to do, like carry groceries without strain, move furniture without fear, travel with less fatigue, and picking up kids without a chiropractor on standby.

As a minimalist, you don’t need a complicated strength training workout. The most successful plans are easy to remember and repeat. This might be three 20-30-minute strength workouts per week, alternating upper body plus abs with lower body plus abs. Or a whole-body workout two times per week that uses just your body weight for resistance. Or whatever works for you, which is key — variety may be the spice of life, but if a simple, repetitive strength plan one you actually do regularly, then it’s a great plan. As long as you’re not working out the same muscles two days in a row (rest is important for repair), you can do the same routine regularly, which removes the friction you might feel when faced with a rack of equipment.

A minimalist doesn’t need access to a full gym (at home or otherwise). With a pair of dumbbells, some resistance bands, or even just your bodyweight, you can build meaningful strength. For more information, see Weight Training For Dummies, 4th Edition (John Wiley & Sons, Inc., 2014).

Simplifying your mindset

Minimalist fitness isn’t about chasing perfection or fitness domination. It’s rooted in intentionality and mental clarity. The minimalist mindset helps you remove unhelpful activities and thinking, eliminating complex routines, performance pressure, guilt over skipped days, and the compare-and-despair trap of fitness influencers. With a minimalist approach to fitness, you don’t need to count the calories you burned after each session or worry over wearables. Instead, you can monitor what actually matters:

  • Did you move today?
  • Did you strength train 2–3 times this week?
  • Are you feeling stronger or more energetic?

These more forgiving questions still help you assess whether you’ve taken care of your body, and gently remind you to do so if that to-do hasn’t yet been moved to the done pile.

Living Like a Minimalist but Sleeping Like a Maximalist

Every night, billions of people close their eyes, surrendering to a state that has puzzled and fascinated scientists, philosophers, and poets alike for centuries: sleep. For something so universal, sleep remains one of the most complex and poorly understood processes of the human body. Sleep is not just a nightly shutdown; it involves a complex dance of chemical messengers from the brain that interact with biological rhythms. Sleep is a vital activity that underpins all bodily processes — from memory to immunity.

Sleep’s role in people’s lives is more crucial now than ever. Modern life — with its constant barrage of screens, deadlines, and stress — has turned sleep into an elusive commodity. For many people, getting the recommended seven to nine hours of sleep feels like a distant dream that’s been replaced by exhaustion and caffeine-fueled mornings. In our view, “minimalist” means prioritizing the sleep you need and eliminating the distractions and barriers to doing so.

Stages and states of sleep

When you fall asleep, your brain doesn’t simply turn off. Instead, it moves through two distinct states: Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) sleep and Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep.

Altogether, the states and stages of sleep form a roughly 90-minute cycle that repeats four to six times during an average night. Each cycle shifts in composition as the night progresses, with deep sleep (N3) dominating earlier cycles and REM sleep taking over later on.

NREM sleep

NREM sleep forms the foundation of restorative sleep and dominates most of the sleep cycle. It consists of three stages that serve unique purposes and repeat in cycles throughout the night:

  • Stage N1: This is the gateway to sleep — a light, transitional stage marked by slower brain waves. Your muscles relax and eye movements slow down. People often don’t realize they’re asleep during this stage.
  • Stage N2: Often called intermediate sleep , this stage makes up 40 to 55 percent of your nightly rest. It features hallmark brain wave patterns, which help consolidate memories and suppress external stimuli.
  • Stage N3: Also known as slow-wave sleep or deep sleep , this stage is essential for physical recovery. During the N3 stage, your body repairs tissues, clears brain toxins, and strengthens the immune system.

REM sleep

After NREM, you enter the state of REM sleep, which occurs about 90 minutes after you fall asleep and recurs multiple times throughout the night. Each period of REM sleep grows longer. During REM, your brain activity mimics wakefulness, but your body remains in a state of temporary paralysis. During this sleep stage, the majority of your dreams occur, and experiencing it is critical for emotional regulation and memory consolidation.

Sleep specialists often refer to REM sleep as a state rather than a stage of sleep because it is so different from NREM stages of sleep.

Getting enough rest

Life as an adult often feels like a juggling act. Between work, family, and personal responsibilities, sleep is often one of the first sacrifices. But even minimalist adults need seven to nine hours of sleep per night to function optimally.

To claim better sleep, you can

  • Make your sleep timing consistent. Minimalists love routines because they eliminate the need for decisions. Go to bed and wake up around the same time every day.
  • Establish boundaries with technology. Minimalist sleep means minimal stimulation. Avoid checking emails or scrolling social media close to bedtime.
  • Invest in sleep-friendly spaces. You don’t need 12 pillows, but you do need a comfy sleeping space. Optimize your bedroom with supportive bedding, blackout curtains, and a comfortable mattress.
  • Make sleep a priority. Treat rest as intentionally as you treat other decisions in your life, and as a nonnegotiable part of your health routine.
  • Stay active during the day. Regular physical activity promotes deeper sleep at night.
  • Practice a bedtime routine. Here’s another opportunity to eliminate decisions — a consistent bedtime routine is ideal for preparing your body to sleep. A pre-sleep routine may include activities such as light stretching, meditation, or listening to calming music.

Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to a host of health problems, including impaired memory, weight gain, and increased risk of heart disease.
