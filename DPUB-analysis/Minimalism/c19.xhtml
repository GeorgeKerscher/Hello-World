Chapter 19

Ten Benefits of Minimalism

IN THIS CHAPTER

Freeing up your time and money for more rewarding pursuits

Reducing stress while optimizing your health and fitness

Cutting down on distractions so you can focus on what matters

Adopting a minimalist life isn’t always easy, especially in the consumer-oriented society that many of us live in today. Getting along with less takes some adjusting, bad habits sometimes create the least difficult route, and living intentionally requires mindfulness and attention even when you’re tired. So we encourage you to keep your eyes on the prize. When you’ve fully adopted a minimalist approach to your life, you’ll more than reap the benefits of all the time and effort you put into it.

You’ll get benefits as you work through your minimalist journey — life will start to feel more calm, and you’ll feel less like a racoon in a room full of shiny things. But some days you’ll need some help staying motivated, and one of the best ways to do so is to remind yourself of the many benefits of a minimalist life.

In this chapter, we present ten of those benefits — ten good reasons to minimalize.

Spending Time and Money on What Really Matters

Living a minimalist life isn’t about owning nothing — it’s about clearing the noise so your time and money go where they actually matter. Fewer possessions, financial decisions driven by purposeful goals, and a digitally simpler life free you from clutter, impulse spending, and pressure, giving you space to focus on the people, goals, and experiences that truly matter to you.

As you get started on your minimalist journey, or when you’re in the middle of paring back excess and wonder when the decluttering process will finally end, we encourage you to envision a richer, fuller life due to fewer possessions weighing you down and dictating what you do with the time, money, and other assets you have.

If you have a lot of stuff, or until recently spent a lot of time in a digital world, paring back your possessions or weaning yourself off digital interactions can take a while and may be a little more challenging than you thought! But every step that helps you change your habits is one step closer to your simpler, less chaotic, more enjoyable life.

Saving Time and Effort Cleaning and Maintaining What You Own

People who are filthy rich can afford to buy and maintain large homes packed with possessions, because they can afford to pay other people to clean, maintain, and repair everything they own. If you’re not filthy rich, all the time and effort required to clean, maintain, and repair what you own falls on you. You must do it yourself or work longer hours so that you can hire someone to do it for you. In many ways, you become a servant to your possessions.

Minimalism provides a path to freedom from possessions. With fewer possessions, you have less to clean, maintain, and repair and more time and energy to spend on enjoyable and rewarding activities. Even if you hire someone to do the work for you, you’ll be paying less for housecleaning services, maintenance, and repairs. Time is money!

Reducing Stress

After your children leave the nest, you may experience persistent low-level stress, knowing that you eventually need to reduce your possessions. You may find yourself living in too much house for one or two people, with too many closets that are too full of stuff you don’t use regularly. This stress is mostly self-imposed.

For one of this book’s authors, paring back her possessions was stressful (dealing with many years of memories, deciding what loved-but-seldom-used items would go, and so on), but as soon as it was over, she and her partner felt as though a huge weight had been lifted from their shoulders. They ended up with far fewer possessions, everything they retained was well organized, and what they still owned was what they love and enjoy frequently.

Reclaiming Focus from Digital Distractions

Digital minimalism frees you from the constant tug of online noise (just check the number of phone pick-ups you have in a day to appreciate that constant tug). When you cut back on social media, you spend less time comparing your life to everyone else’s highlight reel, giving you more time to actually live your own. Streamlining email and deleting subscriptions you never read clears mental clutter and reduces the great wall of messages you see when firing up your email, and trimming your apps to the ones you truly use helps reduce your phone’s ability to hijack your attention. And we can’t forget the shopping cart: scaling back your online shopping curbs impulse buys that drain your money and your focus (and helps you avoid building back up the possessions you’re trying hard to reduce).

When you step back from primarily digital interactions — whether constant gaming or endless group chats or frequent comparison-shopping — you create space for deeper, real-life connections.

Spending More Time with Loved Ones

Minimizing your possessions, finances, and digital life frees up your time, money, energy, and other resources, and one way to redirect those resources is to spend more time with loved ones. With fewer possessions, a budget that covers all your needs (not just today’s day to day, but tomorrow’s savings), and a quieter digital life, your mind is also freer, which gives you the opportunity to be more present with your family and friends.

Having fewer possessions may require that you take a different approach to family gatherings. You may need to gather at someone else’s home, or have everyone contribute place settings, flatware, and temporary seating like card tables so everyone has what they need to enjoy an event or meal. Don’t let that get you down. You may find that your family gatherings are even more enjoyable when the entertainment to-do’s are shared, and everyone’s contributing to the fun.

Gaining More Financial Breathing Room

When you shift into a minimalist, goals-oriented financial life, the first thing you may feel is space — real, almost tangible breathing room. You’re no longer drowning in bills for things you don’t actually care about or scrambling to cover impulse purchases made in a moment of stress or boredom. Instead, your money starts flowing toward the priorities you choose: paying off debt, building savings, investing for the future, or funding experiences that genuinely matter to you.

Because you’re intentionally buying less, you naturally spend less. And that drop in spending isn’t about deprivation; it’s about alignment. You’re no longer leaking money on subscription traps, random online-shopping hauls, or upgrades that don’t improve your life. Instead, you keep more of what you earn. Your budget starts to feel like a supportive tool instead of a punishment.

Over time, that breathing room compounds. You build an emergency fund. Debt shrinks. You have the freedom to handle surprises without panic. You can take trips, make career changes, or weather financial storms with more confidence. Money turns from a source of stress into a source of stability, opportunity, and choice. Minimalism doesn’t just tidy your space; it gives your finances room to grow.

Focusing on Quality over Quantity

Minimizing what you own drives home the fact that more isn’t necessarily better. Health and fitness, freedom to do what you want to do, and having lower stress levels can significantly increase the quality of your life. Material possessions don’t necessarily improve the quality of your life, but if they’re curated with an intentional eye, they can create great experiences and memories with less to worry about.

Life is all about being, doing, and having. Sometimes, by having less, you have the potential to be more and do more. You have more time, energy, and resources to work on self-development and on enjoying memorable experiences and adventures. As you pursue a minimalist life, look for opportunities to create a more fulfilling balance among who you are, what you do, and what you have, with less significantly less emphasis on what you have.

Optimizing Your Health and Fitness

Many people sacrifice their health and fitness for their homes and lifestyle. They’re so singularly focused on supporting the lifestyle they’ve grown used to that they neglect diet, exercise, leisure, and even sleep. A minimalist life provides an opportunity to make diet and lifestyle changes that can have a huge positive impact on your health and fitness and even extend your life. Seize that opportunity.

After minimizing your possessions, if you want easy access to fitness equipment, designate a small area of your home as an exercise space or take advantage of your condo- or apartment-complex’s shared fitness room. You can go online to find plenty of exercises for building strength and endurance that require no specialized machinery or even much space. Walking is also an excellent form of exercise that requires nothing but a supportive pair of shoes.

Becoming More Flexible in How You Live

Minimalism gives you flexibility because you’re no longer tied down by stuff you don’t need or habits that drain your time and energy. When you’re not managing closets full of possessions, or numbing yourself with endless shopping, scrolling, or gaming, you gain mental space to spend time intentionally. You can say yes to new opportunities (even if that simply means doing the paint-by-numbers watercolor you bought three years ago), and you can make choices based on what you actually want, not what your clutter or coping mechanisms demand. A simpler life means you’re lighter on your feet, mentally and financially, and far more able to move in the direction that matters most to you.

Alleviating the Future Burden on Loved Ones

If you own a ranch like John Dutton in the hit series Yellowstone, you may want to preserve your home for future generations. Otherwise, maintaining a home that’s packed with belongings beyond the age of 70 or 80 is probably a disservice to your heirs. They’re the ones who will be stuck with disposing of your belongings when you’re no longer able to live on your own or when you pass away.

If you’re retired or nearing that age, we encourage you to minimize your stuff even if you choose to remain in your current home. Reduce the clutter, sort your possessions, get rid of anything you don’t need, and organize everything, so that if you die or become incapacitated, the person in charge of dealing with your home will have a much easier job.
