Does Lack of Sleep Make You Grumpy?

Have you ever stayed up late studying and then noticed that the next day everything seemed to go wrong? You get stuck in traffic, and you are annoyed by your classmate who asks to see your notes. However, this might be more due to your own emotions and mood than other people. Research has shown that the lack of sleep influences emotions. Sleep researchers conduct experiments to figure out how the lack of sleep affects emotions and moods. Researchers bring people into their laboratories and keep them up all night. Would you do this for science? When people are sleep deprived, they feel more irritable, angry, and hostile. Loss of sleep is even associated with feeling more depressed. 1 Also, sleep deprivation results in people reacting more emotionally—especially when something doesn’t go well for them. Sleep loss leads to increased negative mood and a reduction in the ability to regulate anger due to biochemical changes that occur in the brain. 2 Even small changes in normal nightly sleep inhibit the ability to regulate emotions at work the next day. 3

66 We have all heard stories about employees who didn’t get enough sleep and then subjected their coworkers to a cranky mood the following workday. 4 Organizational behavior (OB) research has found that sleep loss results in lower self-control and higher workplace deviance (e.g., dragging out work to get more overtime pay). 5 Managers who don’t get enough sleep are more likely to abuse their followers. 6 Insomnia has been related to lower job satisfaction. 7 A study found that the use of smartphones late at night rather than sleeping soundly resulted in lower work engagement the next day. 8

The COVID-19 pandemic exacerbated the negative effects of the lack of sleep and work performance. Studies demonstrated that disruptions to daily routines, increased stress, and changes in work environments during the pandemic contributed to sleep disturbances and decreased productivity. 9

Research reviews focused on the remedies to mitigate the negative effects of sleep deprivation on work performance postpandemic. Organizations have implemented health programs, expressive writing, and meditation workshops to improve relaxation and productivity. These interventions improved presenteeism (coming to work, but exhibiting decreased productivity due to health problems). 10 Overall, recent research underscores the importance of addressing sleep-related issues in the workplace to enhance employee well-being and productivity. Employers continue to examine possible interventions to promote healthy sleep habits and supportive work environments.

So how much sleep should you be getting? According to the National Sleep Foundation, adults aged 18 to 64 should aim to get around 7 to 9 hours of sleep each night. 11 So put that smartphone or computer down at night and start getting your Zs!

Emotions and Moods at Work

Unfortunately, we have all had the experience of being treated rudely by someone—a coworker, supervisor, or a clerk in a retail store. We wonder, “Why is this person such a crab?” One reason, as noted earlier, might be that they didn’t get enough sleep. But what are other reasons for negative emotions and moods at work? Research on emotions at work seeks to address these questions.

Despite the clear implications of employee feelings about the experience of work, emotions and moods were largely ignored in most early OB literature. 12 This is likely because societal expectations of leaders are that—in order to be most effective—they should be logical, detached, and rational decision-makers. Showing one’s feelings or caring about the feelings of followers and coworkers was attributed to weakness and not sound leadership. However, in the mid-1980s and the 1990s, organizational researchers began studying the effects of emotions and moods on behavior in organizations. By 2003, scholars referred to this research as “the affective revolution” in OB. 13

Critical Thinking Questions: Do you think a leader should suppress their emotions and moods to be effective? Why or why not?

67 Affect is a general term that refers to the range of feelings that employees experience at work. Affect is composed of emotions and moods. 14 State affect refers to feelings experienced in the short term that fluctuate over time, whereas trait affect refers to stable individual differences. Emotions are triggered by specific events and are brief but intense enough to disrupt a person’s thinking—lasting only seconds or minutes. For example, a person may experience pride when they achieve a goal. 15 Other emotions emerge in relationships with others, such as shame and guilt. 16 Moods , on the other hand, are general feeling states that are not related to a specific event, and they are not intense enough to interrupt regular thought patterns or work. 17 Emotions are more fleeting than moods. In other words, a felt emotion, such as anger at your boss, may pass. But being in a foul mood may last for hours. Moods aren’t typically caused by a person or something that happens to us. Research has shown that moods can affect job performance. Two studies focused on moods were as inputs to motivation. Defensive pessimism is a strategy that can lead to poor moods and lower performance in the workplace. Strategic optimism is not affected by mood change. These two studies highlight the relationship between performance, thought patterns, and mood. 18

Obviously, emotions and moods are related—being in a good mood can result in the experience of feeling happy (an emotion). The relationship between affect and emotions and mood is shown in Figure 3.1 .

The following example should help clarify the distinction between emotions and moods. Clarence just received praise and recognition from his manager for successfully completing a challenging project. He feels a surge of emotions of happiness, pride, and satisfaction. These emotions are specific, intense, and tied to a particular event or trigger (in this case, the recognition from his manager). On a different day, his mood affected his experience at work. He had a rough morning dealing with technical issues, followed by a disagreement with a coworker during a meeting. As a result, Clarence felt irritable, frustrated, and stressed out all day. You can see that, unlike emotions, moods are more diffuse, less intense, and can persist over a more extended period without being tied to a specific event. Emotions are the immediate reactions to specific events (praise from a manager), while moods represent more general, ongoing states (irritability and frustration).

68 As noted earlier, the study of emotions and moods has revolutionized thinking about OB. Next, we turn to the key foundation for studying emotions at work: the affective events theory (AET). This framework will be a useful reference for you to follow as you read further about research on emotions and moods.

Affective Events Theory

Affective events theory provides a useful road map for the material we cover in this chapter. 19 This framework integrates personality, emotions, and moods, and it considers the impact of the work environment and events that may trigger emotional reactions (positive and negative affect). Areas in the work environment that leaders should pay close attention to are the characteristics of the job (e.g., is it boring or interesting?), the job demands (e.g., is the job just too difficult for the person to handle?), and the requirements for emotional labor (e.g., does the person have to interact with the public and be courteous to irate customers?). Notice that in Figure 3.2 , these work environment factors lead to work events such as daily hassles and uplifts (uplifts are moments when everything is going great). Personality and moods play a role in how people react to hassles and uplifts. For example, a person low on emotional stability may have a stronger reaction to a daily hassle. Taken together, the work environment, events, personality, and moods combine to evoke emotional responses—positive or negative. This, in turn, leads to job satisfaction and performance. Affective events explain the development of effective working relationships between leaders and followers. 20

Research has continued with respect to how leaders’ emotions affect their followers, especially when it comes to negative emotions. While negative emotions from leaders may momentarily activate followers, they can have long-term negative effects on their engagement, loyalty, and well-being. To counteract these detrimental effects, leaders need to be mindful of how they express negative emotions. Leaders’ self-directed negative emotions may be less harmful to followers’ well-being compared to negative emotions directed at followers. Leaders should act as “emotion shields” for their followers, protecting them from the toxic effects of negative emotions. Leader emotions thus impact the quality of leader–follower relationships. 21

A review of AET discusses the large number of studies that have supported AET and considers it to be a “classic” organizational behavior model. 22 This review summarizes what we know about affective events at work:

  • Satisfaction is not emotion. Emotions influence outcomes such as job performance and turnover independent of job satisfaction. Fluctuating experiences of emotions and moods are not the same thing as a judgment of job satisfaction. For example, a person can be satisfied with their work in general, but negative emotions can reduce the ability to attend to details in their work.
  • 69 Events cause emotions. This is a distinct aspect of AET compared to other OB theories. Both job-related and non-job-related events can instigate emotional states at work and therefore have work consequences. For example, being treated negatively by a coworker can cause an employee to have negative affect (job-related). Also, getting into an argument with a person’s spouse can cause an employee to experience negative affect all day at work (non-job-related).
  • Affect-driven behaviors are different from judgment-driven behaviors. Affect-driven behaviors are decisions and judgments that have (relatively) immediate consequences. In comparison, judgment-driven behaviors are decisions, or judgments, that are driven by more long-term attitudes about the job or organization. For example, an affect-driven behavior occurs when a person feels negatively about being treated badly by a customer and then raids the department’s refrigerator and eats a coworker’s doughnut (immediate consequence).
  • Affective experiences change over time. A person is more likely to perform better when they are happy than when they are less happy. 23 For example, a person could come to work experiencing negative affect, but they receive a compliment from their boss in the late morning that lifts them up and they perform better in the afternoon.
  • Affect is structured as emotions and moods. As noted earlier, AET specifies that emotions are distinct from moods. This distinction is important since moods lack a specific causal event, whereas emotions are reactions to something that provokes a person at work. For example, a person can be in a bad mood for no reason, but when they get into an argument with a coworker, they also experience negative affect.

Another review study looked at the connection between emotions and job satisfaction, and refined the AET using the appraisal theory of emotion. The authors conducted a review of 235 samples and over 99,000 individuals to test their ideas. They compared specific emotion experiences to general emotion experiences and found that emotions like depression and happiness, when accompanied by an employee’s assessment of the cause of the feelings (i.e, appraisals), strongly influence job satisfaction. They also noted more variability in the relationship between negative emotions and job satisfaction compared to positive emotions. Other factors that make a difference were emotion intensity and time lapse in emotion recall. Overall, the study improves understanding of how and why emotions impact job satisfaction. 24

Research on AET sometimes asks employees to keep diaries of their emotions and moods at work. An example of such a study 25 asked 218 employees to keep diaries of negative and positive events at work. Positive events were when they were enthusiastic, and negative events were when they were worried or angry. Most positive events were related to goal attainment and task success. Also, praise and appreciation generated enthusiasm. Negative events were most related to hindrances and obstacles encountered at work. Also, employees reported managerial problems and poor organizational climate as generating worry or anger.

Decades of research supports the relationships shown in Figure 3.2 . AET serves as an evidence-based and useful way to summarize what is covered in this chapter. This theory really puts it all together for us.

Moods

70 Moods are generally more enduring than emotions. Positive and negative state affects are the most studied moods at the workplace (i.e., being happy or sad). Positive state affect (PA) is defined as the extent to which a person feels enthusiastic, active, and alert. High PA is a state of high energy, full concentration, and pleasurable engagement. In contrast, negative state affect is defined as a general dimension of subjective distress and unpleasant engagement that subsumes a variety of aversive mood states. These states may be anger, contempt, disgust, guilt, fear, or nervousness. 26 Studies show that happier people perform better and have higher incomes. 27 , 28 , 29 Feeling happy and positive at work matters to the organization as well. When employees feel happy, they tend to be more creative, healthy, and work better in teams. Managers should create a workplace where people feel positive. They can do this by being positive leaders, giving employees what they need to do their jobs well, and helping them manage stress. It’s also important for companies to support workers in their personal lives and families. Managers that create a culture where people feel happy and supported may very well make the organization more successful in the long run. 30

71 Mood at the start of the workday (i.e., “waking up on the wrong side of the bed”) in one study was related to perceptions of customer emotions in a call center, and this affected the employees’ moods after the calls. 31 Positive affect, in turn, was related to performance quality, whereas negative affect was negatively related to productivity. The results of this research study are summarized in Figure 3.3 . So, if you get up on the wrong side of the bed in the morning, pay attention to your mood. You just might be less productive that day.

Affective Climate

72 Affective climate refers to the shared affective experience of a work group or team. 32 The climate, or tone, is a result of the feelings that arise from, or in, groups. 33 Affective climates are typically referred to as being affectively “positive” or “negative.” 34 For example, a positive affective climate includes “participation, warmth, social rewards, cooperation.” 35 The range of emotions that may be experienced has been summarized in the circumplex model of affect shown in Figure 3.4 . This model locates specific emotions in the conceptual space defined by two orthogonal primary dimensions: pleasantness (pleasure–displeasure) and arousal (low activation–high activation). Affective climate is described as having different facets as shown in the figure. 36 A study using the circumplex model examined team conflict in 156 bank branches and found that disagreements about the work (task conflict) and difficulties with other team members (relationship conflict) were significantly related to the creation of a tension affective climate. This climate is characterized as the group mood being nervous, tense, and anxious rather than enthusiastic. 37

Research suggests that affective climate reduces burnout in the workplace. The term job stress spin refers to regular—possibly daily—fluctuation in the experience of job stress. In the study, job stress spin and its effect on employee burnout was analyzed using the circumplex model of affect. The study demonstrated that job stress spin was a major factor leading to burnout at work, but maintaining a positive affective climate mitigated this effect. 38

73 Positive leader–member relationships result in more friendships at work, and the positive affective climate in the work group enhances this relationship. Feeling that team members are friends results in more effective teamwork. 39 Feelings of compassion and pride within teams result in more ethical behavior. 40 Affective climate is related to team creativity, especially when the groups work together effectively (for example, by asking a lot of questions of one another). 41

Emotions can have a positive impact within a team. They may also build over time into positive cycles of emotions. This happens because positive emotions change a person’s outlook and how they see other people with whom they work. Moral emotions serve to support the moral standards enacted by society and help team members evaluate their own and others’ behaviors in terms of contributing to, or detracting from, a well‐functioning world. Moral emotions also play a key role in circumventing unethical conduct and encouraging team members to help one another. 42 Team norms regarding moral perspective-taking influence team members’ proscriptive (e.g., fear, guilt, shame) and prescriptive (e.g., compassion, pride) moral emotions. In turn, each of these moral emotions has unique action tendencies linked to one or more of three different types of ethical behaviors witnessed in teams: compliance, helping others, and doing the right thing. 43 This is further explained in the broaden-and-build model of emotions.

The Broaden-and-Build Model of Emotions

Emotions serve to both broaden employee experiences and allow them to build better functioning in organizations. This is known as the broaden-and-build model . 44 Positive emotions, such as pride in one’s work, can transform organizations and the people in them. 45 This is because positive emotions open people’s minds, and they begin to build personal and social resources, which enables them to work more effectively. Positive emotions can be contagious: A person’s positive outlook at work affects the emotional reactions of their coworkers. 46 Also, positive workers are more likely to have positive interactions with customers.

Research employed the broaden-and-build model to explore the relationship of mindfulness and stress in the workplace. The research involved 193 full-time employees in a Fortune 100 company in the United States. The results showed that two different types of mindfulness—“nonreacting” and “nonjudging”—significantly reduced stress levels. Thus, practicing mindfulness may reduce negative affect in agreement with the broaden-and-build theory because mindfulness can lead to more positive emotions and then better coping skills. 47

The broadening and building of emotions have also been linked to creativity at work (see the Research in Action box).

Research in Action
Affect and Creativity

74 Creativity is affectively charged, and complex cognitive processes are shaped by both emotions and moods. Creativity may be particularly susceptible to affective influence, mainly because positive affect leads to the sort of thought processes that stimulate creativity. Consider these emotional reactions to creative outputs cited in a research study headed by Teresa Amabile at Harvard:

Both individuals are expressing positive emotions resulting from their creativity. Positive affect results in an affect–creativity cycle. This research identified positive affect as an antecedent of creative thought, with incubation periods of up to 2 days. In other words, creativity both evokes and accompanies creative performance. Positive affect has three primary effects on creativity. First, positive affect makes additional information available for processing by increasing the number of associations that can be made. Second, it leads to more complex contexts by defocusing people’s attention on one solution, and this increases the number of things that are treated as relevant to the problem. Third, it increases cognitive flexibility by increasing the probability that diverse cognitive elements will become associated with one another. Also, the ability to control one’s time enhances positive affect, which then increases creativity.

The COVID-19 pandemic caused significant uncertainty for instructors. Research conducted during the pandemic found that instructor and student creative beliefs and affect played a supportive role in adaptively managing stress, happiness, and resilience. This study found that school shutdowns and how instructors adapted creatively to distance learning related to instructors’ positive affect in teaching and helped students stay engaged. 48

The broaden-and-build model suggests that positive emotions, such as joy and pride, broaden a person’s available repertoire of thoughts and actions. The experience of positive emotions leads people to pursue more novel ideas. Positive emotions broaden the scope of attention and the scope of ideas during the creative problem-solving process. Thus, positive affect leads to greater variation and therefore increases the probability of creativity occurring.

75 Being grateful is an example of an emotion that may broaden and build spirals of positive emotion and behaviors at work. 49 Gratitude has been studied as a trait (in other words, some people are more grateful than others). Trait gratitude is defined as “a generalized tendency to recognize and respond with grateful emotion to the roles of other people’s benevolence in the positive experiences and outcomes that one obtains.” 50 Gratitude may also be a state that is related to being in a particular work situation. For example, state gratitude at work would result from a person having a job that gives them a sense of purpose, which evokes positive emotions. 51 Feeling grateful for having a purpose in your work creates additional positive emotions that result in higher motivation and performance. Employers may benefit from adopting mindfulness interventions to increase employee gratitude and, in turn, motivate their helping behavior. Research suggests that mindfulness may promote greater positive emotions and increase employees feeling the other-oriented emotion of gratitude. 52

An example of gratitude in action happened when Sherilyn Joseph, who works at World Duty Free Group’s Tampa gift shop at Tampa International Airport, was on duty, and a passenger came in asking for help finding her coat and gloves. The passenger was about to board a flight to New York when she realized she had lost them. After airport officials were unable to find the coat, Sherilyn gave her own jacket to the woman. “I just felt I should do something to help her,” Sherilyn said in a news release. “I wanted her to be warm and have a blessed trip.” Company officials said Joseph showed exceptional customer service with her compassionate deed. Joseph received a letter of gratitude from the customer: “If not for your giving nature and helping another in a time of need, I would have walked into LaGuardia Airport shivering and looking to purchase a coat in the middle of the night!” In addition to the gratitude from the customer, the company expressed its gratitude as well. Sherilyn was rewarded with a trip to London, including round-trip airfare, accommodations, and $1,000 in spending money. 53

Sherilyn may not have expected to receive gratitude, but this may create positive expectations of being thanked for her and other employees. The expectation that others will show gratitude at work motivates employees to help others. 54 Thus, it seems that gratitude is a two-way street. Research sheds light on the importance of expressed gratitude from service beneficiaries (i.e., patients or customers), particularly for employees in health care or service occupations who typically suffer from high levels of stress. Expressed gratitude energizes service providers during the course of the workday, enhancing their subsequent interactions with customers or patience. Managers and organizations should thus provide more frequent opportunities for service beneficiaries to offer positive feedback and express their gratitude to their service providers. For example, annual events such as International Nurses Day encourage patients to acknowledge the efforts of their heath care providers. 55 Employees who have an orientation toward the well-being of others are more likely to expect gratitude, and this expectation increases their job performance. 56 A review found that the positive emotion of gratitude is enhanced by training in which people practice feeling grateful for what they have, and this increases their well-being. 57 You can learn to practice feeling the emotion of gratitude by following the steps in the 5-minute gratitude exercise in the toolkit at the end of this chapter (Activity 3.1).

Emotional Labor

76 As noted earlier, employees feel a range of emotions and moods during the course of the workday. However, they don’t always show it. For example, if you have ever had a service job such as a waiter or waitress, you know that you had to smile and be pleasant regardless of your emotions (“I am disgusted by your table manners”) or moods (“It’s raining outside and I just feel blah”). Jobs that require employees to suppress affect (emotions and moods) are said to require emotional labor. Other jobs require you to express affect. Emotional labor is defined as “the management of feeling to create a publicly observable facial and bodily display.” 58 In other words, it is the effort required to effectively manage emotions to be successful on the job. The feeling of having to act differently from one’s true emotions creates emotional dissonance for employees. Emotional dissonance is the result of the difference between the organizationally expected emotions and an employee’s inner or “real” emotions. 59 In other words, employees are told to “fake it until you make it.” All jobs have this requirement to some extent, but some have higher requirements than others (e.g., flight attendants, salespeople, customer service representatives, and nurses).

One notable trend in recent research is the examination of emotional labor in the context of remote work and virtual interactions, especially considering the increased reliance on digital communication platforms. This has raised questions about the nature of emotional labor in online environments and its implications for employee well-being and performance. As work shifted to remote and virtual settings due to COVID-19, emotional labor—the effort to manage emotions at work—took on new dimensions. Brodsky’s research found that how emotions are managed varies across different forms of virtual communication, like email, chat, or video calls. While chat and email can hide emotions better, video calls may feel more authentic but also risk showing unwanted feelings. Phone calls were suggested as a middle ground, where emotions are hidden but effort is perceived as higher, potentially making interactions more genuine. The impact of emotional labor on well-being in virtual settings remains uncertain. Virtual meetings, especially when on camera, can add to fatigue, particularly for women and new employees. It’s unclear if this fatigue is from added pressure to present oneself positively or from regulating emotions. Working from home might ease some emotional labor stressors but could introduce new ones, like managing home appearances on camera. Jobs in the gig economy present their own emotional labor challenges, as workers may lack clear guidelines for interactions. As work evolves, understanding emotional labor across various contexts becomes increasingly important. 60

Acting at Work

77 The concept of emotional labor was introduced by sociologist Arlie Hochschild, 61 who investigated flight attendants and demonstrated that a significant part of their job was attending to the emotions of passengers. Emotional labor also has been studied with convenience store clerks and customers. Clerks had differing expectations of their roles and followed scripts (like actors in a role) to control customer service interactions. 62 This is known as “deep acting” since the employees actually feel the emotions they are acting out. Deep acting happens when a desired emotional expression is achieved by changing one’s underlying felt emotion. For example, a college professor may “psych themselves up” to present a lecture to students after learning of a family member’s illness. The professor becomes more enthusiastic. In contrast, surface acting refers to producing a desired outward emotional expression without modifying the underlying emotions. 63 Research has, however, shown that surface and deep-level acting occur simultaneously. 64 Such acting may come with costs, however. Surface acting emotional labor (acting out service roles) has been related to emotional exhaustion and burnout. 65 , 66 The emotional exhaustion from surface acting may be reduced by team members’ positive actions.

Emotional Division of Labor

When team members engage in deep acting, it spreads to their team members. Thus, not every person in a team needs to deep act—there is an emotional division of labor that occurs in teams. Emotional division of labor is defined as any explicit or implicit division of roles in which individuals vary in their requirement to use emotional abilities. 67 For example, in a car dealership, the general manager, customer service representatives, and sales representatives need to have high emotional abilities, but the service technicians do not. 68

Team leaders should engage in positive deep acting and encourage key team members to do the same. Research has demonstrated that some people are better at emotional labor (surface and deep acting) than others. 69 However, training programs can discuss the benefits of deep acting and provide strategies for genuinely changing one’s feelings during difficult situations to reduce the stress of emotional labor. 70 Training can also help individuals be more aware of their emotions and employ different emotional labor strategies. 71 Emotional labor has a bright side: It may have positive outcomes when organizations grant more autonomy and adopt norms that call for the expression of positive emotions. 72 For example, allowing a call center employee to work without being watched increases his motivation to help customers.

This overview of emotional labor further illustrates that emotions and feelings at work do matter. Emotional labor causes stress; however, when properly managed, it increases performance. To engage in emotional labor, an employee needs to be sensitive to their own emotions and those of others. Some people are more adept than others at reading the emotions or moods of coworkers or customers. There has been a great deal of research and practitioner interest in recent years on understanding emotional intelligence (EI) , and next we review EI research and practice.

Emotional Intelligence

Research from the field of psychology shows emotional regulation may be a form of intelligence. 73 , 74 Organizational leaders and human resource professionals find this concept, EI, to be relevant to the workplace. In fact, having EI abilities may be essential to being an effective leader. 75 EI is considered to have four aspects:

  1. Ability to perceive emotion in self and others (e.g., correctly identifying a perceived emotional expression as fear)
  2. Ability to use emotion to facilitate cognitive activities like thinking and problem-solving (e.g., knowing how to capitalize on a happy mood swing to engage in a creative task)
  3. 78 Ability to understand emotional information (e.g., understanding how two emotions can blend into a third emotion)
  4. Ability to manage emotion in self and others (e.g., detaching from fear states that interfere with one’s functioning) 76

EI is related to job performance. 77 This is especially true in jobs that have high emotional labor requirements. 78 However, EI becomes a stronger predictor of performance and organizational citizenship behavior (OCB) when intelligence is lower. 79 In other words, employees with lower intelligence perform tasks correctly and engage in OCBs frequently if they are emotionally intelligent. This may be explained by the results of a study that found that employees with high EI are better able to control and reduce the counterproductive outcomes of challenging developmental job experiences (EI reduced the unpleasant feelings associated with demanding tasks that required new learning). Taking on these difficult tasks resulted in employees being seen as having more advancement potential. 80 In a study of retail stores, store managers’ EI increased team cohesiveness, which resulted in higher sales volume. 81 A study conducted with U.S. Air Force recruiters found that EI was related to success in meeting recruiting quotas. 82 In addition, a study of more than 300 managers at Johnson & Johnson found that managers who scored higher on a measure of EI were rated as more effective by their followers. 83 Reviews show that leaders’ EI relates to follower job satisfaction and performance. 84 , 85 , 86 Other benefits of EI demonstrated by research are enhanced employee creativity, 87 teamwork effectiveness, 88 and the ability to resolve conflict. 89 Research continued on EI after these criticisms and a review concluded that evidence accumulated in recent years shows that organizational researchers have made great strides in the field of EI and leadership. Proponents of EI argue that it is essential for effective leadership. Leaders having higher EI may result in improved performance variables, including task performance, helping, job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and lower turnover. 90

Given these positive results, organizations are interested in whether employees can be trained to be more emotionally intelligent.

Can Emotional Intelligence Be Learned?

Can we send employees to a training program to increase their EI and improve their ability to get along with others? Many scholars believe that EI can be learned. First, people need to develop emotional literacy and be able to label their emotions. Second, they need to learn how to manage or regulate their emotions. 91 Research has compared managers who received EI training to a group receiving no training (a control group). After the training, managers showed higher EI, and they also reported lower work-related stress, experienced higher morale, and treated one another in a more civil manner. 92 , 93

79 EI training programs vary in their content; however, they all include both emotional awareness exercises and emotion regulation strategy practice. For example, people are shown a series of photos of faces and then asked to identify the emotions expressed in the photos (emotional awareness). An example of an emotion regulation strategy exercise is the Stop! technique in which participants are asked to shout the word stop in their mind whenever an anxious or troubling thought appears. Then, they replace it with a word like calm . 94 While the research on training interventions in organizations is not extensive, a review of training interventions from diverse fields, including OB, education, mental health, and sports, concluded that “it is possible to increase emotional intelligence and that such training has the potential to lead to other positive outcomes.” 95

Limitations of Emotional Intelligence

EI has supporters, yet researchers disagree on its definition. Some researchers believe that EI is a trait or ability similar to IQ. 96 Others argue that EI is a mixed combination of intelligence, personality traits, and affect (they use grab-bag measures of EI that have a number of different measures of personality traits and EI combined). 97 A third approach is that EI is an ability that can be learned. 98 Due to differences in definitions and a great number of researchers interested in EI, there are various measures of EI, but they do not converge. 99 Some scholars even argue that the concept is too vague and cannot be measured at all. 100 , 101 , 102 A critical review of the literature on EI in the workplace concluded that initial claims of the predictive value of EI may have been overstated. 103

How Emotional Intelligence Is Used in Organizations

Despite its critics, the EI concept has definitely impacted the workplace through EI training programs, specialized EI consultants, and articles in the business press and popular press. 104 , 105 For example, the FedEx Global Leadership Institute is charged with continuously updating and innovating in keeping with CEO Fred Smith’s call for continuously “raising the standards.” FedEx Express implemented a training program for new managers in EI that had three steps for using emotional intelligence on a day-to-day basis:

  • Know Yourself —Increase self-awareness of emotions and reactions
  • Choose Yourself —Shift from unconscious reaction to intentional response
  • Give Yourself —Align the moment-to-moment decisions with a larger sense of purpose

Results of the training at FedEx indicated an 8% to 11% increase in EI competencies from before to after the training, which was a statistically significant difference. By supporting new managers in this way, FedEx gains by having more competent leaders and by showing employees that the company puts its values into action. This training models the kind of people-centered leadership that FedEx expects from all managers. One of the key principles of the EI training at FedEx is “Emotions drive people; people drive performance.” 106

Here’s what we can safely conclude about EI:

  1. EI is distinct from, but positively related to, other intelligences (such as IQ).
  2. EI is an individual difference, where some people are more endowed and others are less so.
  3. 80 EI develops over a person’s life span and can be enhanced through training.
  4. EI involves, at least in part, individuals’ ability to effectively identify and perceive emotion (in themselves and others), as well as possession of the skills to understand and manage those emotions successfully. 107

EI may also improve employees’ ability to cope with stressful situations, even when the responses appear maladaptive. A study conducted in a law enforcement setting found that coping strategies such as venting, denial, and disengagement might be adaptive for short-term performance. Organizations could manage employee emotions by educating employees about more appropriate coping responses for jobs that involve emotions (i.e., jobs that require emotional labor). 108 Research has demonstrated that leaders that show variability in emotions create negative feelings in their employees, and this reduces work engagement. Leaders’ knowledge about emotions may help in improving leaders’ ability to understand how employees react to different emotions. Leadership training increases leaders’ awareness that employees pay close attention to their expressions of affect and also its variability, which impact employees’ own emotions and work engagement. 109

Given the continued interest in EI, the question arises as to whether employees’ emotional expressions in the workplace are becoming more accepted. The What’s #Trending in OB? box discusses whether or not employees should express emotions at work.

What’s #Trending in OB?
Is It Okay to Cry at Work?

The Great Recession of 2008 hit Starbucks hard. Company founder Howard Schultz was at the helm during this challenging time for the company. More than 600 Starbucks stores closed, and about 12,000 workers lost their jobs. He cried at a team meeting when he made the announcement. Schultz then outlined a detailed plan for addressing the challenges. Following the meeting, he received email from over 5,000 employees thanking him for showing his emotions. He later told Oprah that vulnerability is important to transparency as a leader. When he stepped down as CEO, he said to his employees, “You’re going to make me cry before I start.”

81 Would you cry at work? Workplace crying is “any audible or visible crying expression (from being choked up to outright sobbing) by one individual (crier) that is directly observed by another individual (observer) in a work setting.” 110 A survey of more than 1,000 U.S. employees working in an office environment and more than 2,200 executives from 20 of the largest U.S. metropolitan areas found that 45% of workers admitted to crying at work. That said, some employees worry that it will affect their career. Lower-level employees are more concerned about showing their emotions at work. In fact, 32% said it is never okay to cry at work compared to 26% of executives. Mature workers (55 and older) are less likely to worry that crying will affect their reputation compared to younger workers. People typically associate crying with loss, but it can be a reaction to anger as well. Many people cry when they feel frustrated, anxious, or deeply passionate about their work. Employees today experience more stress and burnout. Emotions—and the likelihood of tears—are increasing at the workplace. The new version of crying in the bathroom has become turning off your video to regain your composure during a Zoom meeting.

You will likely encounter challenging work situations that make you feel as if you want to cry. For example, you may have a boss who is constantly criticizing you, or you may have a family emergency that you learn about while working. How can you manage your emotions?

Managing Your Emotions

Take note of this 6-step process:

  1. Accept sad feelings. Assess the situation. Determine if it is okay to cry in front of others or find a private place.
  2. Note how you are talking to yourself about the situation.
  3. Pay attention to physical responses such as shaking or looking down.
  4. Tell yourself that you are in control.
  5. Stop the negative self-talk and replace it with positive affirmations.
  6. Plan your communication strategy.

For example, a coworker is promoted, but you know you deserved it. You feel let down by your boss and are sad. Your self-talk is, “This is unfair. I am frustrated at not being appreciated.” Your physical response is that you have a stomachache, and your face is flushed. Replace the self-talk with a positive response like, “I need to stand up for myself and talk to my boss about this.” You take control by breathing deeply a few times and walk around the block after lunch. This calms you down, and you feel more in control. Your strategic communication is to set up a face-to-face meeting with your boss, where you will assertively state your feelings about the situation.

It’s all about keeping your composure and holding back the tendency to do something impulsive, such as sending a nasty email to your boss or resigning on the spot.

Regulating Emotions

82 Emotion regulation comprises “the processes by which individuals influence which emotions they have, when they have them, and how they experience and express these emotions.” 111 Emotional regulation theory focuses on the causes, responses, or mechanisms through which employees respond to emotional events at work. 112 For example, a bill collector may encounter an angry person on the phone, which raises the emotional labor required to interact with the person. Suppressing emotions in responding to the angry person is needed for the bill collector to perform her job well. Research has shown that customer service employees can reduce feelings of burnout by engaging in affect spin. Affect spin is the ability to vary responses to emotional events by knowing which people are more reactive than others to both internal and external events. In other words, employees with higher affect spin ability can read other people’s emotions and change their reaction to fit the person’s expressed emotions. A study of restaurant servers found that affect spin buffered the servers from the fatigue caused by difficult customers. 113 Another effective emotion regulation strategy is to relabel and reappraise an undesirable situation. Affect relabeling is verbally labeling the initial reaction of something negative (e.g., I am angry) and then relabeling it to be less intense (e.g., I am annoyed). Reappraisal is intentionally decreasing the intensity of an emotional response to a situation or reinterpreting it in a positive way. For example, reappraisal occurs when the person “looks for the silver lining” in a bad situation. 114

This line of research has clear evidence-based best-practice implications. An individual can regulate their emotional responses to work by reading others and adapting to their emotional response to reduce the impact of fatigue (affect spin). Also, when encountering a negative or undesirable situation, they can relabel it as something less upsetting and then look for something good that may come from the situation (i.e., treat it as a learning experience). As noted already, emotion regulation has been shown to be an important component of EI, and it is also necessary for the surface-level acting requirements of emotional labor. Emotional regulation matters in the workplace, especially for leaders. According to a recent CoachHub survey, emotional regulation is the second-most important focus area for respondents worldwide (second only to conflict management). This statistic indicates that professionals understand and appreciate the need for the ability to respond calmly under pressure. 115

Emotional Contagion

83 Even groups can be in a bad mood. Emotional labor is essential for high performance in certain jobs but may also result in stress for employees who must suppress their own emotions and moods. For example, consider whether the magical people at Walt Disney World always feel magical. Such stress may cause coworkers to complain to one another because they must hold in negative emotions toward customers. When this happens, an emotional contagion effect may occur, which is defined as the negative mood of one employee spreading to others in the group. Negativity spreads like a virus; employees “catch” the negative moods of others. 116 Positive moods are also contagious.

By adding up responses to measures of mood from individuals in groups, researchers have discovered that mood can be defined for a group. In other words, groups can be characterized as having a negative mood. 117 Also, the “group mood” spreads to the moods of individuals in the group—that is, if a group is positive, then members of the group typically experience positive mood states as well. To assess the degree that emotional contagions may be affecting a work group, leaders should ask the following questions:

  • To what extent are people on the same side of the table?
  • What events and environmental conditions do they tend to face together?
  • What are the hot buttons to which a lot of people are reacting?
  • Even when people share the same viewpoint, how do they interpret what is going on around them?
  • Do people converge in their interpretation, or do people disagree in terms of their reactions? 118

Leaders have a strong influence on how emotional contagions emerge and spread from individuals to teams and to the organization as a whole. 119 The spread of negative emotions in groups has a “ripple effect,” and the emotional contagion reduces team cooperation and creates conflict. 120 But keep in mind that positive moods are also infectious—when an employee smiles at a coworker, their positive mood spreads. 121 , 122 Research has demonstrated that the contagion of positive emotions is associated with higher teamwork engagement. The sharing of positive emotions between team members leads the team to feel fully absorbed in their work, elevating feelings of pride and joy. 123 A study found that leaders created positive emotional contagion in their work groups, and followers responded by spreading the positive emotions. 124 In longitudinal studies of large social networks, researchers have shown that the spread of positive emotions is likened to infectious diseases, influencing the experience of positive affect for large numbers of individuals. In other words, the spread of happiness moves through social networks via social ties. 125

84 This effect also happens in social networking on digital media, when people are constantly exposed to the emotional expressions of others. This can lead their own emotion expressions becoming more similar to those of others. In fact, one of the goals of social media is exactly that—to increase the frequency and intensity of user emotion. A study of Facebook users found that social media amplifies emotional contagion. The prevalence of “likes” and positive comments results in more positive responses. 126 A study of superstar social media influencers on YouTube found that emotional contagion was a central route for influencers that increased their performance (increases in views and subscribers to their channel). Influencers conveyed strong emotion through their videos with nonverbal cues (e.g., facial expressions, varied tones, rich body language) and verbal expressions (saying things like “We are excited . . .”). When exposed to influencers who express certain emotions, viewers change in their own emotional state because they automatically mimic the emotions of others subconsciously. 127

The same happens for negative emotions. For example, one study found that when employees feel a climate of job insecurity, safety outcomes were negatively impacted (i.e., there were more injuries and accidents). 128

Leadership Implications: Mindful Coaching

We have all had the experience of asking someone what’s wrong and they reply, “Nothing, I’m fine.” But we know from their tone of voice and body language that they are upset about something. People tend to deny their emotional pain. For example, they may feel worthless, rejected, not listened to, or even invisible. There are several reasons why people conceal their hurt emotions, but what these reasons all have in common is fear. In other words, people conceal their emotions because they don’t want to feel weak and powerless. People don’t trust others to respond in caring, supportive ways. 129 What are organizations doing to increase the levels of trust so that employees can discuss their emotions? And what can we do as leaders?

Companies such as Aetna, Google, General Mills, and Goldman Sachs now provide mindfulness training and meditation rooms for their employees. David Gelles, author of the book Mindful Work , states that mindfulness meditation programs resulted in a 28% reduction in employee stress levels, a 20% improvement in sleep quality, and a 19% reduction in physical pain. Employees who participated in the programs also became more effective on the job, gaining an average of 62 minutes per week of productivity each, which Aetna estimates is worth $3,000 per employee per year. This may not seem like a lot, but considering that about one fourth of Aetna’s 50,000 employees have participated in the program, this works out to be around $37,500,000 per year. The classes continue to be full and are overbooked. 130

Mindfulness is a new area of research in psychology, and it is being applied to the study of emotion regulation in the workplace. A study of employees who engaged in surface acting found that mindfulness training reduced emotional exhaustion. 131 Mindfulness is a state of open attention on what is happening in the present without thinking about the past or worrying about the future. When a person is in a mindful state, they look at their emotions and moods without labeling them as bad or good.

Being mindful is significantly related to well-being. Are you mindful or mindless in how you go about your study, work, or life in general? Being mindful is related to the EI aspect of being self-aware (recognizing emotions when you experience them), but it is more than this. By focusing on the present, we can learn about the individual differences of followers and also be attentive to their emotions and moods. Research has shown that mindfulness training increases empathy (the ability to understand what another person is feeling). 132 A review of research on leader mindfulness found that it is beneficial factor for both leaders and their employees. This review of 54 samples and 9,414 leaders suggests that leader mindfulness significantly relates to leaders’ well-being (e.g., stress), relationships (e.g., relationship qualities, leadership styles), and job performance, as well as followers’ well-being and job performance. There was support for the effectiveness of mindfulness training in improving leaders’ well-being. 133

85 A mindful coaching session involves listening in a nonjudgmental way. Begin a mindful coaching session by following steps from the Center for Creative Leadership for mindful leadership coaching: 134

  1. Based on your preparation, start with an empty mind. Allow yourself not to judge or think about what the person should have done in the past or should do in the future.
  2. Be nonreactive. Remember that during this coaching session, “no reaction is required, regardless of the provocation.” Create a safe emotional space for that person’s self-expression.
  3. Practice permissive attention. Allow yourself to draw the person into moments of connection where distractions disappear (cell phones, street noise, or anything else that might impair their ability to focus). Allow yourself to stay focused for more than a moment on one serious line of thought, perception, judgment, or action that you might observe. Draw the person’s attention to what is important (but not in a coercive way).

With a bit of practice, leaders should be able to create an affective coaching environment in which they attend with a focus on their followers’ emotions. Athletes refer to this as being “in the zone” where you are focused and behavior seems effortless. 135 A great coach can evoke this state from a follower and maintain it so that the person can hear their feedback without becoming defensive. Thus, mindfulness coaching is about the preparation and execution of a few steps in each phase. Research has shown that people can be trained to experience mindfulness and that this state is related to increased attention (alerting, orienting, and conflict monitoring). 136 Like any skill, with practice, a leader can become more mindful and then use this skill to understand the entire range of the emotions and moods expressed by their followers. Leaders can then develop the ability to react and do so in an appropriate way to reduce emotional exhaustion and enhance their followers’ well-being.

In this chapter, you have learned about the importance of affect (emotions and moods) to job performance and employee well-being. AET serves as a guide and overall framework to understand how emotions and moods influence workplace outcomes. Emotional intelligence has emerged as a major area of research, as well as practice applications through EI training such as that conducted at FedEx Express. Emotions (both positive and negative) can spread through groups and organizations via the emotional contagion process, so it’s important to understand them and keep them positive. Finally, the role of brain science and the potential for affective neuroscience to shed light on how emotions and moods influence OB have been discussed. In sum, emotions are a relatively new area of OB, but an “affective revolution” has taken place in the field, which has led to new implications for effective leadership.

Toolkit Activity 3.1 The 5-Minute Gratitude Exercise

Everyone experiences problems that consume them. Here are some examples:

  • Schoolwork challenges
  • Work problems
  • Health issues
  • Financial challenges

The list is endless. These issues grab hold and overwhelm you and your energy, sometimes so much that you are exhausted from the problem. They grab so tightly that you wonder if you will ever move forward. How do you lessen the grasp your challenges have on you and your energy? What if something could have you seeing a way to positive change? The 5-Minute Gratitude Exercise just may be what you have been looking for. The practice of acknowledging gratitude lessens the heavy impact issues have on you, your energy, and your inability to see through your challenges. By releasing the pressure on the problem, you create space for new and more harmonious views on the situation.

The exercise takes 5 minutes and has 7 steps:

  1. For 1 minute, ponder the issue that has consumed your energy.
  2. Focus on how you are feeling as you think about the problem.
  3. For the second minute, think of something you are grateful for around that issue. Some examples might be as follows:
    • My best friend and I are having difficulties at the moment. I am grateful for my best friend, as I can call and talk with her and feel I have someone with whom I can share my feelings.
    • I received a C on an exam. I felt that I studied hard, but I don’t do well on multiple-choice exams. I am grateful that the course has other assignments where I can shine. I am good at writing papers.
    • The workload at my job has been heavy, and I am stressed about it. I worry about whether I will be able to keep up. I am thankful to have a job that pays my bills and gives me the opportunity to learn new things.

87 These are just examples to get you going. Think of what is in your life around the issue that you can honestly be grateful for, even though the issue seems to have a hold on you. There’s always something to be grateful for, even in a difficult situation. It may require a bit of searching, but it’s there.

  1. Once you have identified what you are grateful for, close your eyes and focus on it. Let your thoughts wander, and soon other things you are grateful for in other areas of your life will begin to speak up. Positive thoughts attract positive thoughts.
  2. As these thoughts speak to you, imagine there is a volume knob inside your head, a physical knob like the one on a stereo receiver or an old-time radio. Now, envision reaching out and turning up the volume on these thoughts of gratitude to a point where it is comfortable but stretches you a little bit.
  3. Just like that song that you crank up the volume to because it feels good, let this volume of gratitude fill you with that same sense.
  4. Notice how you feel. Hold onto this feeling, and when you feel yourself stuck by the situation, think of this feeling and come up with another thought of gratitude to add. When you find yourself being sucked down into the “woe is me” syndrome or are consumed by your problems, stop for 5 minutes and practice gratitude with this exercise.

Case Study 3.1 Managing Your Boss’s Moods and Emotions

Ted is a forensic accountant at the law firm of Chambers, Bergweitz, and Rowe. He has worked there for 10 years and is called in on cases when his unique skills are needed by the different attorneys and partners. He reports to Margaret, who has been his boss for 8 years. Margaret is responsible for all the specialized personnel used on cases, not just the forensic accountants. She has been a decent boss—fair, with clear standards for performance and what it takes to get promotions, raises, and bonuses. However, Margaret suffered a medical scare last month and is going to be taking early retirement in order to take care of her health. As a result, the specialists under Margaret are now being divided among the different department heads they serve until a replacement can be hired in a few months (as Margaret is on medical leave, she can’t be replaced in her position for legal reasons at this time).

88 Ted now reports to Richard, the head of the divorce department, and finds him a difficult boss to work for on most days. He always seems to start his day in a bad mood, denying any subordinates’ requests or finding that the requests (as well as requestor) are inconsiderate if he is approached before 11 a.m. Whereas Margaret had a clear process for assigning cases to the accountants, Richard seems to let his moods guide his choices. For instance, when Seeru, another forensic accountant, was late getting Richard a report because she had to get an emergency dental crown repair, Richard was angry with her. He seemingly retaliated by assigning three last-minute cases to her in 1 week and left Ted with nothing to do. He also required Seeru to complete her analysis of all the cases by Monday, thus requiring her to work the weekend. However, when Ted and Seeru’s work helped a client get a large divorce settlement, and thus the firm a large cut of the settlement, he was pleased and gave both of them Friday off on a whim so they could enjoy a long weekend. In addition, Ted has found him yelling on the phone in his office on more than one occasion when he has gone to get files or clarification on cases from him. To make matters worse, Richard often belittles his assistant and other employees outside of his office, which disturbs those in nearby offices and cubicles.

Richard’s emotionally charged behaviors are very disruptive to Ted and Seeru’s work. It is hard for them to concentrate when he is yelling, and they do not feel comfortable working in their cubicles because they never know when he is going to have an outburst that makes it impossible to have a phone conversation or to have clients, clients’ financial managers, or other attorneys over for meetings. They also cannot get information they need from him when he is in a bad mood, as they don’t dare go near him.

Ted would like to request a transfer to the partner that handles corporate trials and investigations. He has talked to some of the attorneys in that department, and it seems a much nicer working environment. However, until he can get transferred over, he and Seeru are employing a number of strategies to deal with their boss and his volatile moods. These include the following:

  • Avoidance. The two accountants work out of the office when possible by meeting with clients and their financial managers at their homes or offices. When they have to work in the office, they try to work in the archives, libraries, or conference rooms away from Richard.
  • Gray rocking. By not emoting, even when Richard’s emotions are affecting their own feelings, Ted and Seeru are not fueling his fire. Often, they have seen him react even more harshly if someone cried or yelled back and so do their best to not only say nothing but also avoid facial expressions that could communicate how they are feeling.
  • Gifting and complimenting. In the last few weeks, the two accountants have brought what they consider peace offerings whenever they go see Richard—from coffees to bagels or bars of chocolate. Whenever he gets these little things, Richard’s mood improves, and he is a bit easier to get information from or permission to use company resources.
  • Overdelivering. After getting that Friday off for doing good work and knowing what can happen if they don’t deliver on time, the accountants work hard to ensure they do excellent work and beat deadlines.
